Itamar

Itamar Ben-Gvir (born 6 May 1976) is an Israeli lawyer and politician serving as Minister of National Security, a position he has held since November 2022 following his party's entry into the governing coalition, with a brief interruption in early 2025 before reappointment in March.[1][2] He leads Otzma Yehudit (Jewish Power), a party he has headed since 2019 that emphasizes expanded Jewish sovereignty over contested territories, increased civilian armament for self-defense, and stringent enforcement against Palestinian terrorism and incitement.[3] Elected to the Knesset in 2021 as part of a joint list, Ben-Gvir's rise marked the mainstreaming of previously marginalized nationalist factions, enabling policies such as bolstering police powers and promoting settlement expansion amid ongoing security challenges.[3] His tenure has involved direct interventions in policing operations, including directives to intensify arrests of suspected militants, though it has drawn domestic and international scrutiny for statements perceived as provocative toward Arab citizens and Palestinian leadership.[4][2] Earlier in life, Ben-Gvir faced multiple convictions for incitement and support for a banned terrorist organization, experiences he cites as formative to his commitment to legal advocacy for Jewish rights under threat.[3]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Itamar Ben-Gvir was born on May 6, 1976, in Mevaseret Zion, a middle-class suburb on the outskirts of Jerusalem.[3][5] He grew up in a secular family of Mizrahi Jewish descent, with his mother originating from Iraqi Kurdistan as a Kurdish Jew who immigrated to Mandatory Palestine as a teenager and had been involved with the Irgun underground militia.[3][5] His mother worked as a homemaker, while his father was employed at a gasoline company and occasionally engaged in writing.[3] The family's household was religiously traditional but not strictly observant, reflecting a moderate level of Jewish practice common among some Mizrahi communities in Israel at the time.[5] Ben-Gvir's upbringing occurred during a period of heightened national tensions, including the First Intifada (1987â€“1993), which influenced his later ideological shift toward more fervent religious and nationalist views, diverging from his family's secular orientation.[3]

Formal education and early influences

Ben-Gvir was born on May 6, 1976, in Mevaseret Zion, a suburb west of Jerusalem, to parents of Iraqi Jewish descent; his father worked for a gasoline company while pursuing writing on the side.[3] He completed secondary education in the Israeli public school system, after which, at an age when most peers enlisted in the Israel Defense Forces, he opted instead for extended study at a yeshiva, where he encountered and internalized radical ideological currents associated with the teachings of Rabbi Meir Kahane and the Kach movement.[6] This period marked his initial immersion in Kahanist principles, which emphasized Jewish sovereignty over biblical Israel and opposition to perceived threats from Arab populations, shaping his worldview amid the ideological fervor of post-Oslo Accords Israel.[7] In the early 2000s, Ben-Gvir pursued formal secular higher education by enrolling in the law program at Ono Academic College near Kiryat Ono, completing a Bachelor of Arts in Law.[8][1] His legal training occurred concurrently with ongoing activism testing boundaries of Israeli law, though he later faced scrutiny from the Israel Bar Association during bar admission due to prior convictions for incitement and support of banned organizations.[9] These academic pursuits provided him with tools for a future career in advocacy, while his yeshiva background reinforced early commitments to religious-nationalist ideology over conventional military service paths.[6]

Ideological development and activism

Embrace of Kahanist principles

Ben-Gvir's ideological alignment with Kahanism emerged in his teenage years, when he joined youth movements inspired by Rabbi Meir Kahane's teachings on Jewish self-defense, opposition to Arab nationalism, and the demographic threat posed by Israel's Arab population.[10] As a follower of Kahane's banned Kach party and its successor Kahane Chaiâ€”designated terrorist organizations by Israel in 1994 and the U.S. in 2001â€”he participated in protests advocating for the expulsion of "disloyal" Arabs and the establishment of a halakhic state prioritizing Jewish sovereignty.[11] This commitment manifested in symbolic acts, such as displaying a portrait of Baruch Goldsteinâ€”a Kahanist who massacred 29 Palestinian worshippers at the Cave of the Patriarchs in Hebron on February 25, 1994â€”in his living room until January 2020, when he agreed to remove it amid political pressure.[12] Ben-Gvir has praised Goldstein as a hero in past statements, reflecting Kahane's emphasis on uncompromising resistance to perceived Arab aggression. He also kept a sign reading "Kahane was right" in his office, echoing the slogan popularized by Kahanist supporters to affirm Kahane's predictions of Arab disloyalty and calls for transfer.[13] Public endorsements reinforced this embrace. On November 18, 2016, Ben-Gvir stated, "Everyone knows that Rabbi Kahane was right. Rabbi Kahane was a man of truth, he meant every word, every comma, every statement."[14] At a November 4, 2015, memorial for Kahane, he described the event as "a pledge of allegiance... to continue his way," adding, "We see and hear more and more people who believe that Kahane was right... We want the ideas of Rabbi Kahane in power."[14] In 2017, he advocated hanging Kahane's picture in synagogues, calling his words "never stop being relevant."[14] These positions align with core Kahanist tenets, including loyalty tests for Arab citizens and prioritizing Jewish rights over democratic pluralism. Even as Ben-Gvir moderated rhetoric ahead of electoral viability, his attendance at a November 2022 Kahane memorialâ€”where he eulogized Kahane's "love of Israel"â€”signaled enduring affinity, despite explicit rejection of mass expulsion or segregated beaches.[15] He pledged instead to deport "terrorists," encompassing stone-throwers and "disloyal" Arab parliamentarians, adapting Kahanist transfer ideology to target perceived threats selectively.[15] This evolution reflects pragmatic restraint while retaining foundational principles of Jewish primacy and deterrence against Arab militancy.[16]

Youth activism and legal troubles

Ben-Gvir's youth activism centered on opposition to the Oslo Accords and support for Kahanist ideology, which advocated Jewish supremacy and expulsion of Arabs from Israel. As a teenager in the early 1990s, he participated in protests against Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin's peace policies, aligning with groups inspired by Rabbi Meir Kahane's banned Kach party and its successor, Kahane Chai.[3] These activities often involved public demonstrations and inflammatory rhetoric targeting Arab citizens and the peace process.[17] A notable incident occurred on March 5, 1995, when 18-year-old Ben-Gvir approached Rabin's official Cadillac during a protest in Jerusalem, removed the hood ornament as a symbolic act of defiance, and stated, "We got to his car; we'll get to him too," which prosecutors viewed as incitement and a personal threat against the prime minister. He was arrested shortly thereafter and convicted of assaulting a police officer in connection with the event.[18] [19] This episode exemplified his early confrontational style, blending symbolic vandalism with verbal provocation amid heightened tensions following Rabin's signing of the Oslo II agreement.[20] Throughout his late teens and early twenties, Ben-Gvir faced repeated arrests for disrupting public order and interfering with law enforcement during anti-Oslo rallies. He accumulated several convictions, including for incitement to racism and obstructing police duties, stemming from chants and signs promoting Kahanist slogans like "Death to Arabs."[3] [17] By his early adulthood, these encounters had resulted in over a dozen indictments, though many cases ended in suspended sentences or fines rather than imprisonment, reflecting the minor nature of some offenses amid Israel's lenient handling of ideological protests at the time.[21]

Legal career

Advocacy for Jewish defendants

Ben-Gvir focused his legal practice on defending Jewish Israelis accused of nationalist-motivated offenses, including vandalism, assaults, and arson attacks classified by authorities as "price tag" actions or Jewish terrorism. These cases often involved settlers or "hilltop youth" retaliating against perceived Palestinian violence or state policies restricting settlement expansion. He argued that such prosecutions reflected systemic bias against Jewish defenders amid asymmetrical threats from Arab terrorism, frequently challenging evidence obtained through interrogations by the Shin Bet security service.[18][22] A key example was his representation of multiple suspects in the July 31, 2015, arson attack on the Dawabsheh family home in the West Bank village of Duma, which killed parents Saad and Riham Dawabsheh along with their 18-month-old son Ali, leaving four-year-old Ahmad severely injured. Ben-Gvir served as counsel for Amiram Ben-Uliel, indicted in January 2016 for the murders, as well as other initial suspects like Aaron Klein and Asher Frederick. He maintained from the outset that Ben-Uliel's confession was coerced via torture, including severe physical abuse during Shin Bet detention, and sought to disqualify it in court. Ben-Uliel was convicted in May 2020 and sentenced to life imprisonment, though Ben-Gvir continued appeals asserting investigative misconduct.[23][24][25] Ben-Gvir's advocacy extended to broader critiques of administrative detention and solitary confinement applied to Jewish suspects, which he likened to practices used against Palestinian militants but claimed were disproportionately harsh for Jews lacking equivalent security threats. In 2019, he publicly accused the Shin Bet of torturing a Jewish terror suspect under his representation, demanding investigations into interrogation methods. His firm collaborated with organizations like Honenu, providing pro bono defense to over 100 such cases annually, emphasizing due process amid what he described as politicized pursuits by law enforcement. Outcomes varied, with some acquittals on procedural grounds, but convictions in major incidents underscored tensions between legal defense and public perceptions of extremism.[26][25]

Notable cases and convictions

Ben-Gvir established his legal practice focusing on the defense of Jewish suspects accused of involvement in extremist violence, including "price tag" attacksâ€”retaliatory acts against Palestinian property or individuals following perceived provocations or Israeli government actions against settlements. These cases often involved allegations of vandalism, arson, or assault, with Ben-Gvir challenging the proportionality of charges and the methods used by Israel's Shin Bet security service to obtain confessions.[18] A landmark case was his representation of suspects linked to the July 31, 2015, arson attack in the West Bank village of Duma, where masked assailants firebombed the home of the Palestinian Dawabsheh family, killing toddler Ali Dawabsheh (aged 18 months), his father Saad, and his mother Riham, while critically injuring their 4-year-old son Ahmad. Ben-Gvir defended two teenage suspects initially implicated in the attack, arguing against the evidence presented by prosecutors.[18][23] Ben-Gvir also represented Amiram Ben-Uliel, the primary perpetrator convicted in the Duma case. On May 18, 2020, an Israeli court found Ben-Uliel guilty of murder, attempted murder, arson, and membership in a terrorist organization, sentencing him to three life terms plus 20 years in prison. Throughout the proceedings, Ben-Gvir maintained that Ben-Uliel's confession had been coerced through prolonged and severe interrogation tactics by the Shin Bet, including physical abuse, which he claimed invalidated the evidence and highlighted systemic bias against Jewish defendants in terror-related probes.[27][28] In broader advocacy, Ben-Gvir handled multiple defenses for individuals charged with hate crimes or incitement tied to settler activism, often securing reduced sentences or acquittals by contesting the intent element of the offenses and emphasizing contextual retaliation amid ongoing Palestinian terrorism. However, convictions in these cases underscored the courts' determination to treat Jewish extremist acts as serious security threats, with sentences reflecting the lethal outcomes in high-profile incidents like Duma.[18]

Entry into electoral politics

Formation and leadership of Otzma Yehudit

Otzma Yehudit, meaning "Jewish Power," originated as an ultranationalist Israeli political party established on November 13, 2012, through the merger of the Jewish National Front and the Hatikva faction, both ideological successors to the banned Kach movement founded by Rabbi Meir Kahane in the 1970s.[29] The initial formation, led by Michael Ben-Ari and Aryeh Eldad after their split from the National Union party, emphasized policies such as the annexation of the West Bank, the expulsion of Arabs deemed disloyal to Israel, and the promotion of Jewish sovereignty over the entire Land of Israel.[29] In the 2013 Knesset election, the party received 1.09% of the vote, falling short of the electoral threshold and failing to secure seats, after which it became largely inactive.[29] Itamar Ben-Gvir assumed leadership of Otzma Yehudit in 2019, revitalizing the party by aligning it more closely with his personal advocacy for aggressive national security measures and Jewish self-defense against perceived Arab threats.[30] Under his direction, the party maintained its Kahanist foundationsâ€”rooted in Kahane's teachings on Jewish supremacy and separation from Arabsâ€”while Ben-Gvir sought to broaden its appeal beyond fringe elements by focusing on issues like crime in Arab communities and opposition to left-wing influences in law enforcement.[31] Ben-Gvir, a longtime Kahane supporter who had previously run under allied banners, positioned himself as the party's unchallenged head, registering it independently for the April 2019 election where it garnered 0.42% of the vote but built momentum through alliances and media presence.[32] As leader, Ben-Gvir centralized decision-making within Otzma Yehudit, recruiting candidates from his legal and activist networks who shared his hardline views on sovereignty and deterrence against terrorism.[33] The party's platform under his tenure explicitly called for amending Israel's Nation-State Law to prioritize Jewish rights, dismantling unauthorized Arab outposts, and executing terrorists convicted of murdering Jews, reflecting Ben-Gvir's emphasis on causal links between perceived weakness and increased violence.[30] Despite early electoral setbacks, Ben-Gvir's leadership transformed Otzma Yehudit from a marginal entity into a viable contender by 2021, leveraging public frustration with security lapses to advocate for unyielding Jewish power as the foundation of national survival.[34]

Pre-2022 electoral efforts

Otzma Yehudit, under Itamar Ben-Gvir's leadership starting in 2019, contested the April 2019 Knesset election independently, receiving approximately 0.42% of the valid votes (around 18,000 votes) and failing to surpass the 3.25% electoral threshold required for representation.[35] In the subsequent September 2019 election, the party achieved a higher but still insufficient 1.93% (84,000 votes), bolstered by public endorsements from Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, who urged right-wing voters to support Otzma Yehudit to consolidate the right-wing bloc and block potential left-Arab coalitions.[36] Despite this tactical push, the party secured no seats, as the threshold demanded over 140,000 votes given the total valid ballots cast.[37] The March 2020 election yielded similar results for Otzma Yehudit, with vote share remaining below 2% and no Knesset entry, reflecting persistent challenges in broadening appeal beyond a niche ultranationalist base amid fragmented right-wing politics.[38] Ben-Gvir's campaigns emphasized themes of Jewish sovereignty, stringent anti-terrorism measures, and criticism of perceived government weakness on security, often leveraging provocative rhetoric to maintain media visibility despite electoral marginalization. In the March 2021 election, the party slightly improved to about 1.82% (roughly 100,000 votes), yet again fell short of the threshold, highlighting incremental growth in support from hardline voters disillusioned with mainstream parties but insufficient for parliamentary breakthrough.[39] During the November 2021 election, Otzma Yehudit ran independently once more, garnering around 1.2% of the vote (approximately 86,000 votes) and continuing its pattern of threshold failure, though Ben-Gvir positioned the party as a purer alternative to diluted right-wing alliances.[40] These repeated efforts, spanning four elections from 2019 to 2021, resulted in no Knesset seats for the party and underscored Ben-Gvir's strategy of ideological consistency over compromise, gradually cultivating loyalty among ultranationalist constituencies in advance of future coalitions.[41]

Rise to prominence

2022 election victory and coalition role

In the 2022 Israeli legislative election held on November 1, Otzma Yehudit, under Ben-Gvir's leadership, joined a technical alliance with the Religious Zionist Party (led by Bezalel Smotrich) and Noam to form the Religious Zionism electoral list, enabling smaller parties to meet the 3.25% electoral threshold.[42] This alliance secured 14 seats in the 120-member Knesset, with Otzma Yehudit receiving approximately 5.8% of the vote and translating to 6 seats, marking the party's first entry into parliament after failing to cross the threshold in prior elections in 2019 and 2021.[43] The right-wing bloc, including Likud (32 seats), Religious Zionism (14 seats), Shas (11 seats), and United Torah Judaism (7 seats), achieved a slim 64-seat majority, positioning Ben-Gvir's faction as pivotal in Netanyahu's return to power.[42] Post-election coalition negotiations highlighted Ben-Gvir's influence, as his demands for control over national security portfolios were central to deal-making. On November 25, 2022, Netanyahu's Likud party signed an agreement granting Ben-Gvir the role of Minister of National Security upon government formation, overseeing the Israel Police, Israel Prison Service, and border security units.[44] This positioned him to shape policies on law enforcement and public order, reflecting Otzma Yehudit's emphasis on tough measures against terrorism and crime. The 37th government was sworn in on December 29, 2022, with Ben-Gvir's appointment confirming his bloc's leverage in securing key concessions amid broader agreements on judicial reforms and settlement expansion.[45] Ben-Gvir's coalition role amplified Otzma Yehudit's platform, as the alliance's seats proved indispensable for maintaining the government's majority against opposition challenges. While the partnership with Religious Zionism facilitated electoral success, it also involved compromises, such as shared influence over religious and settlement issues, underscoring the tactical nature of the union to consolidate right-wing votes.[44]

Appointment as Minister of National Security

Following the November 1, 2022, Israeli legislative election, in which Otzma Yehudit, led by Ben-Gvir, secured six seats within the Religious Zionism electoral alliance, Prime Minister-designate Benjamin Netanyahu's Likud party finalized a coalition agreement with Otzma Yehudit on November 25, 2022.[44] This pact explicitly allocated the position of Minister of National Security to Ben-Gvir, reflecting his party's leverage in enabling Netanyahu to form a majority coalition of 64 Knesset seats.[46] The role involved heading a newly restructured Ministry of National Security, which absorbed and expanded the responsibilities of the prior Ministry of Public Security.[47] The portfolio encompassed administrative oversight of the Israel Police (with approximately 30,000 officers), the Israel Prison Service (managing over 40 facilities and around 20,000 inmates as of late 2022), and the Border Police's Judea and Samaria division (previously under the Defense Ministry's purview, involving thousands of personnel focused on counter-terrorism and area control in the West Bank).[44] [47] This shift transferred direct policy-setting authority for internal law enforcement and border security from military to civilian leadership, a change codified in the coalition deal to align with Ben-Gvir's emphasis on enhanced deterrence against terrorism.[44] Ben-Gvir was formally sworn in on December 29, 2022, coinciding with the inauguration of Israel's thirty-seventh government, which held 72 seats including ultra-Orthodox and other right-wing partners.[48] [49] The appointment proceeded despite opposition from figures like Attorney General Gali Baharav-Miara, who raised concerns over Ben-Gvir's prior convictions for incitement to racism (from the 2007 conviction under Israel's anti-incitement laws), though no legal barrier prevented it under Knesset eligibility rules.[50] In his inaugural statements, Ben-Gvir prioritized combating Arab-Israeli crime waves, which had resulted in over 100 homicides in Israel's Arab communities in 2022 alone, and arming civilian security squads in settlements.[44]

Policies and actions as minister

Police and prison reforms

As Minister of National Security, Itamar Ben-Gvir prioritized bolstering police resources through substantial budgetary expansions and personnel growth. In May 2023, his ministry achieved an allocation of NIS 9 billion, incorporating 4,850 additional staffing positions to enhance operational capacity.[51] He advocated for recruiting 4,000 new officers over two years starting in early 2023, alongside immediate hiring of 350 combatants for Border Police units in Jerusalem by March 2023 to strengthen urban security.[52][53] These efforts included salary hikes, such as a NIS 1,000 monthly increase for police and correctional officers formalized in July 2023, and further raises announced in December 2024 despite initial finance ministry disputes, which helped stem resignations and boost recruitment.[54][55] Ben-Gvir also pursued legislative changes to empower police operations. The December 2022 amendment to the Police Ordinance, known as the "Ben-Gvir Law," explicitly granted him authority to set general police policy and principles of action, shifting oversight dynamics.[56] A 2023 law co-sponsored by his Otzma Yehudit party enabled warrantless home searches under specified conditions, which he credited with facilitating a "change of course" in law enforcement.[57] He publicly pledged full support for officers using lethal force in self-defense, emphasizing operational backing amid rising threats.[57] In prisons, Ben-Gvir addressed overcrowding exacerbated by post-October 7, 2023, arrests of security prisoners through measures allowing denser confinement. In October 2023, he advanced a temporary bill granting authority to waive minimum living-space standards for such inmates, permitting floor sleeping to accommodate Hamas-linked detainees without releasing others; the Knesset approved this for three months on October 18.[58][59] This included removing beds from cells housing convicted terrorists to free space, framed as a response to wartime influxes rather than humanitarian concessions.[60] By July 2024, facing persistent capacity strains, he proposed capital punishment for certain Palestinian detainees as a "simpler solution" to alleviate pressure, though it remained unimplemented.[61] These policies aligned with his stated goal of ending perceived leniency, declaring prisons would cease functioning as "summer camps" for inmates.[62] An additional $500 million was allocated to the ministry in November 2023 to support expanded detention infrastructure.[63]

Security measures post-October 7, 2023

In the immediate aftermath of the Hamas-led attack on October 7, 2023, National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir prioritized enhancing civilian self-defense by streamlining firearms licensing procedures, which had already been liberalized under his tenure but accelerated sharply post-attack to address the demonstrated risks to isolated communities. His ministry approved over 100,000 new gun permits in the ensuing year, including allowances for assault rifles previously restricted to military use, enabling broader arming of settlers and residents near conflict zones. This policy was justified as a direct response to the attack's exposure of delays in military response times, with Ben-Gvir publicly distributing rifles at events to symbolize heightened readiness.[64][65][66] Ben-Gvir also ordered the Israel Prison Service to implement punitive restrictions on Palestinian security prisoners, particularly those affiliated with Hamas and other groups involved in the October 7 assault, revoking privileges such as family visits, legal consultations, and canteen access starting October 8, 2023. These measures included the rapid construction of facilities like the Sde Teiman detention site for high-risk detainees and extensions of visitation bans into 2024, framed by Ben-Gvir as essential deterrence against ongoing hostage-holding and terrorism. By mid-2024, the prisoner population had surged to over 10,000, with reports of overcrowding and isolation tactics enforced under his oversight.[67][68] To mitigate risks of intercommunal violence echoing the 2021 riots, Ben-Gvir directed increased police deployments to Arab-majority cities and mixed communities, instructing the police chief on October 11, 2023, to prepare for potential unrest amid heightened tensions. This involved bolstering patrols and enforcement against illegal weapons and incitement, resulting in hundreds of arrests for public disorder and terror-related activities in the weeks following the attack. Such actions aligned with his pre-existing push for a national guard auxiliary force, though implementation focused initially on reallocating existing police resources to enforce sovereignty in volatile areas.[69][70]

Recent initiatives (2024-2025)

In October 2025, Ben-Gvir supported a bill approved by the Ministerial Committee on Legislation to designate certain organized crime groups operating primarily in Arab communities as terrorist organizations, enabling the Shin Bet to employ counter-terrorism tools such as administrative detention against them.[71][72] The measure, advanced by his Otzma Yehudit colleague MK Tzvika Foghel, aims to address escalating violence by allowing enhanced investigative powers typically reserved for national security threats, though critics argue it risks blurring lines between criminality and ideological terrorism without sufficient safeguards.[73] Ben-Gvir intensified efforts in October 2025 to enact legislation imposing the death penalty on terrorists convicted of murdering Israelis, issuing a three-week ultimatum to the coalition to advance the bill or face Otzma Yehudit's withdrawal of support for other votes.[74][75] The proposal, which had cleared initial committee hurdles in September 2025 under MK Limor Son Har-Melech's sponsorship, mandates capital punishment in such cases following a panel recommendation, building on prior failed attempts amid debates over its constitutionality and international implications.[76] Throughout 2024, Ben-Gvir championed expansions to police surveillance capabilities, including preliminary Knesset approval in November for a bill permitting secret access to suspects' computers and data under ministerial oversight, framed as essential for combating severe crimes like terrorism and organized violence.[77] Earlier drafts in March sought to authorize spyware for wiretapping in high-stakes investigations, though limited by Attorney General conditions to exclude routine corruption probes.[78] These initiatives reflect ongoing reforms to bolster law enforcement tools, despite judicial pushback and concerns from civil liberties advocates over potential misuse.[79]

Political positions

Views on Arab citizens and terrorism

Ben-Gvir maintains that his positions target terrorism and disloyalty rather than Arabs as an ethnic group, stating in an October 2022 interview that "I do not hate Arabs; I hate terrorists."[80] He has proposed legislation to enable the deportation of Arab Israeli citizens convicted of nationalist-motivated attacks on IDF soldiers, such as throwing rocks or Molotov cocktails, as well as "disloyal" politicians like Joint List MKs Ayman Odeh and Ofer Cassif.[81] The rationale emphasizes a distinction "between those who want to eliminate the Jewish state and those who donâ€™t," with deportation options including relocation to Europe via "trains, planes, whatever you want," while Jewish offenders would face imprisonment rather than expulsion.[81] Concerning Palestinian Arabs in the West Bank, Ben-Gvir prioritizes Jewish Israeli security over their freedom of movement to mitigate terror risks, declaring in an August 2023 Channel 12 interview: "My right, the right of my wife and my children to move around Judea and Samaria is more important than freedom of movement for the Arabs."[82] This stance reflects a view that unrestricted Arab travel facilitates attacks, with Jewish safety prevailing in contested areas.[83] On terrorism, Ben-Gvir equates public displays of Palestinian symbols with support for violence, ordering police in January 2023 to remove Palestinian flags from public spaces as "an act in support of terrorism" and incitement.[84] He has advocated dismantling the Palestinian Authority, labeling it a "terror authority" enabling attacks in an August 2025 statement responding to PA Chairman Mahmoud Abbas's statehood remarks.[85] Otzma Yehudit's platform, rooted in countering Arab terrorism, calls for security measures unbound by standard democratic limits in response to threats, framing Arab sector crimeâ€”including shootings and clan violenceâ€”as a national security issue intertwined with terrorism.[31]

Stance on settlements and Temple Mount

Itamar Ben-Gvir has consistently advocated for the expansion of Israeli settlements in Judea and Samaria, viewing them as essential for Jewish historical claims and national security. In November 2024, following Donald Trump's U.S. presidential election victory, Ben-Gvir joined settlement leaders in calling for the immediate application of Israeli sovereignty over the West Bank, arguing that the moment was opportune to extend settlements and end administrative detentions of Palestinians.[86] He reiterated this position in September 2025, proposing "immediate" annexation in response to Western recognitions of a Palestinian state, framing it as a corrective measure to assert control over what he describes as the Jewish people's historic homeland.[87] In May 2025, despite International Criminal Court arrest warrant concerns, Ben-Gvir vowed to continue settlement growth undeterred, emphasizing sovereignty application as a long-overdue policy.[88] As National Security Minister, Ben-Gvir has implemented measures supporting settlement security, including the formation in July 2025 of a new police unit staffed by armed settlers to patrol the West Bank, which critics described as deepening de facto annexation but which he defended as enhancing law enforcement in the territory.[89] In October 2025, he backed Knesset bills advancing West Bank annexation, stating that applying sovereignty would rectify historical oversights and secure the region against terrorism.[90] Ben-Gvir's stance aligns with his Otzma Yehudit party's platform, which prioritizes Jewish settlement as a deterrent to Palestinian violence, rooted in biblical inheritance rather than international diplomatic concessions. Regarding the Temple Mount, Ben-Gvir opposes the longstanding status quoâ€”established after Israel's 1967 capture of East Jerusalemâ€”that permits Jewish visitation but prohibits overt prayer to avoid inflaming Muslim sensitivities at the Al-Aqsa Mosque compound. He has repeatedly declared Jewish prayer permissible, arguing that the restriction constitutes discrimination against Jews at their holiest site. In July 2024, during a Knesset event, Ben-Gvir stated, "The political echelon allows Jewish prayer at the Temple Mount," defying the arrangement and encouraging worshippers.[91] Ben-Gvir has personally ascended the Temple Mount multiple times to lead or participate in Jewish prayers, escalating tensions. On August 3, 2025, he prayed aloud with a group, singing and prostrating in violation of the status quo, under a policy he influenced that has permitted expanded Jewish activities like singing and bowing since his ministerial tenure began.[92] [93] In August 2024, he expressed willingness to construct a synagogue on the compound, asserting Jewish rights to worship alongside Muslims.[94] His position holds that equal access and prayer rights for Jews would affirm Israeli sovereignty over the site, countering what he terms as Waqf dominance, while maintaining that such changes promote stability through strength rather than appeasement.[95]

Broader ideological commitments

Ben-Gvir's ideological framework draws heavily from the teachings of Rabbi Meir Kahane, founder of the banned Kach party, whom he has described as a guiding influence and whose portrait he displayed in his office until 2022.[96][6] While Ben-Gvir has publicly distanced himself from Kahane's most explicit calls for mass expulsion of Arabs, he endorses core Kahanist principles such as prioritizing Jewish national interests and sovereignty over the entirety of biblical Israel, including Judea and Samaria.[96][29] This commitment manifests in advocacy for annexing occupied territories and rejecting any Palestinian state, viewing such entities as existential threats to Jewish self-determination.[29][97] At the heart of his worldview is a form of religious nationalism that emphasizes Israel's identity as an exclusively Jewish state, where loyalty to Zionism and Jewish law supersedes egalitarian multiculturalism.[29] Ben-Gvir's Otzma Yehudit party platform articulates this through demands for Jewish settlement across all regions of the historic Land of Israel and education systems that instill unwavering devotion to the nation.[29] He argues that Jewish rights, particularly in security matters, take precedence over those of non-Jews, as evidenced by his 2023 statement that "the right of Jews to live and not to be murdered in terror attacks prevails over the right of Arabs in Judea and Samaria to travel on the roads."[98] This hierarchical approach, rooted in a causal understanding of Arab-Israeli conflict as driven by irredentist ideologies rather than Israeli actions, justifies policies like encouraging the emigration of "disloyal" Arab citizens.[99] Socially, Ben-Gvir aligns with conservative religious values, opposing abortion and LGBTQ+ rights expansions, which he sees as erosions of traditional Jewish family structures and national cohesion.[99] His ideology rejects assimilation or compromise with liberal democratic norms that dilute Jewish particularism, instead favoring a robust state apparatus to enforce deterrence against perceived internal and external enemies.[100] This stance has gained traction amid repeated security failures, such as the October 7, 2023, attacks, where empirical data on terror incidents underscores the need for unyielding Jewish primacy in governance and defense.[101]

Controversies

Rhetoric and incitement allegations

Ben-Gvir faced multiple legal convictions prior to entering politics, including for incitement to racism. In June 2007, a Jerusalem court convicted him of incitement to racism and supporting a terrorist organization based on his display of a placard reading "My honor sanctifies its blood," interpreted as endorsing violence, alongside possession of Kach movement materials, which Israel designated as terrorist.[102] He received a one-year suspended sentence and community service. Additional convictions included supporting terrorism and vandalism, totaling at least eight prior offenses, though these predated his 2021 Knesset entry and did not bar his ministerial appointment under Israeli law.[21] During his early activism, Ben-Gvir's rhetoric drew incitement charges for praising Baruch Goldstein, the perpetrator of the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre, whom he defended as a hero in public statements and by displaying a portrait in his officeâ€”later modified to remove an explicit incitement slogan. Courts viewed such actions as promoting racial animosity, yet Ben-Gvir maintained they reflected ideological support for Jewish self-defense rather than calls for violence. In 2024, Israel's High Court rejected petitions to disqualify him as National Security Minister, ruling his past convictions insufficient to override electoral mandates absent ongoing violations.[21] As a lawmaker and minister since 2021, Ben-Gvir has faced renewed allegations of inflammatory rhetoric, particularly post-October 7, 2023, amid heightened Israel-Hamas conflict. Critics, including opposition figures and international officials, accused him of inciting against Palestinians, citing statements opposing humanitarian aid to Gaza and calling for harsher West Bank measures as potentially fueling violence. In July 2024, Israel's state prosecutor sought a police probe into Ben-Gvir for alleged incitement against Gazans over remarks framing aid as supporting terrorism, though no charges resulted. European diplomats, such as EU foreign policy chief Josep Borrell, labeled similar comments as "incitement to war crimes," prompting sanction calls, but these assessments relied on interpretations contested by Ben-Gvir's defenders as legitimate security advocacy amid ongoing attacks.[103][104] Allegations often emanate from sources with documented ideological opposition to Ben-Gvir's Otzma Yehudit platform, which prioritizes Jewish sovereignty and counters perceived Arab incitementâ€”itself a point of legal action under his ministry. No convictions for incitement have occurred during his tenure, and supporters argue the claims reflect selective scrutiny amid broader Palestinian incitement documented by Israeli intelligence, such as PA-funded programs glorifying attackers. Empirical outcomes show no causal link established in court between his rhetoric and specific violence, contrasting with upheld cases against Arab leaders for direct terror endorsements.[105]

Temple Mount provocations

Itamar Ben-Gvir has repeatedly visited the Temple Mount, Judaism's holiest site and the location of the Al-Aqsa Mosque compound, advocating for the right of Jews to pray there in defiance of the longstanding status quo established after Israel's 1967 capture of East Jerusalem, under which non-Muslim prayer is prohibited to preserve calm, with the Jordanian Waqf overseeing Muslim administration while Israel controls security.[106][107] As National Security Minister, Ben-Gvir has directed police to enforce a more permissive approach toward Jewish visitors, including allowing prostration in prayerâ€”a practice previously restrictedâ€”and has timed many ascents to Jewish fast days or holidays, framing them as assertions of Israeli sovereignty.[108][107] His first visit as minister occurred on January 3, 2023, coinciding with the 10th of Tevet fast day, lasting under 15 minutes during which he stated that "the Israeli government is in charge" of the site, prompting protests from Palestinian groups and criticism from the Biden administration for risking escalation.[109][110] On May 21, 2023, he ascended again, declaring Israel "in charge" and emphasizing Jewish rights, which drew condemnation from Jordan as a violation of international agreements.[106] Subsequent visits intensified scrutiny. On July 18, 2024, Ben-Gvir entered the compound amid ongoing Gaza hostilities, highlighting his influence in security policy.[111] An August 13, 2024, ascent sparked intra-coalition debate, with some Israeli officials warning of diplomatic fallout.[112] In April 2025, ahead of Passover, he visited to underscore Jewish historical ties.[113] The most overt challenges came in 2025. On August 3, during Tisha B'Av, Ben-Gvir led dozens in aloud recitation of the Amida prayerâ€”the first such open group Jewish worship documented under his tenureâ€”explicitly violating the no-prayer norm and calling for Israeli sovereignty over Gaza, actions decried by Jordan as "unacceptable escalation" and by U.S. Secretary of State Antony Blinken as provocations undermining stability.[114][92][115] On October 8, 2025, he returned, proclaiming Israel the "victorious landlord" and leading further prayers, prompting Hamas to label it a "deliberately provocative fascist" act amid heightened regional tensions.[116][117] These actions correlate with a documented surge in Jewish visitsâ€”reaching record numbers under Ben-Gvir's oversightâ€”reflecting his long-held ideological push for equal access, though critics, including some Orthodox rabbis who prohibit Jewish entry due to ritual impurity concerns, argue they heighten risks of violence without altering underlying legal control.[107][118] Ben-Gvir maintains that the status quo discriminates against Jews, asserting that police enforcement now aligns with Israel's sovereign authority rather than external sensitivities.[119]

International and domestic backlash

Domestic critics, including opposition lawmakers and families of hostages held by Hamas, have accused Ben-Gvir of exacerbating mistreatment of captives through public statements perceived as provocative. On October 27, 2025, during a Knesset briefing, Ben-Gvir clashed with opposition MKs who claimed his rhetoric encouraged Hamas abuses, prompting him to label them "Hamas spokespeople."[4][2] The brother of a former hostage similarly blamed Ben-Gvir's approach for inflaming captors, citing warnings from Israel's security establishment that such declarations could harm negotiations and prisoner welfare.[120] Ben-Gvir rejected these allegations, denying any causal link and framing the criticism as politically motivated.[121] Additional domestic backlash has targeted Ben-Gvir's oversight of police operations, with Attorney General Gali Baharav-Miara and legal experts accusing him of politicizing promotions and interfering in investigations. In July 2025, following the appointment of a deputy commissioner, the AG criticized the move as undue influence, while Ben-Gvir countered that it reflected her "political meddling" in law enforcement.[122] Public opinion polls in January 2025 indicated majority Israeli support for his potential dismissal from the Knesset after a separate altercation, though Likud voters were divided.[123] These disputes highlight tensions between Ben-Gvir's hardline security policies and institutional checks, often amplified by left-leaning outlets like Haaretz, which have historically critiqued ultranationalist figures. Internationally, Ben-Gvir faced condemnation for remarks prioritizing Jewish rights in the West Bank, with the U.S. State Department labeling them "inflammatory" on August 25, 2023, amid concerns over escalating tensions.[124] In June 2025, the UK imposed sanctions on Ben-Gvir alongside Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich, citing their "incitement of violence" against Palestinians and opposition to Gaza aid, measures echoed by some EU states but disputed by Israel as biased interference.[125] His April 2025 U.S. visit drew protests, including Orthodox Jewish groups calling him a "war criminal" over settlement policies.[126] Ben-Gvir's August 4, 2025, visit to the Al-Aqsa Mosque compound, where he prayed in violation of the longstanding status quo, provoked widespread regional outrage, including from Jordan and Palestinian authorities, who viewed it as provocative amid Gaza hostilities.[127][118] Global media reported over 27 deaths at a Gaza aid site coinciding with the event, fueling accusations of escalation, though Ben-Gvir defended it as asserting sovereignty.[128] In October 2025, his threats to intercept a Gaza flotillaâ€”denouncing activists as "killers"â€”drew Western sanctions calls and European protests, underscoring patterns of backlash from outlets critical of Israel's right-wing government.[129] Ben-Gvir has consistently dismissed such responses as antisemitic or supportive of terrorism, aligning with Israeli government rebuttals to international bodies like the ICC.[130]

Reception and legacy

Support base and achievements

Ben-Gvir's core support emanates from nationalist constituencies emphasizing Jewish self-defense, territorial integrity, and aggressive countermeasures to Palestinian terrorism and Arab crime. Otzma Yehudit voters are disproportionately drawn from peripheral communities, including development towns and West Bank settlements, where dissatisfaction with prior governments' security policies has fueled allegiance to his unyielding platform.[41] Analysis of electoral patterns shows the Religious Zionist blocâ€”encompassing Otzma Yehuditâ€”achieving elevated turnout and vote shares in settlement-heavy districts during the 2022 Knesset elections, underscoring a demographic tilt toward ideological hardliners prioritizing sovereignty over concessions.[41] Among broader right-wing voters, Ben-Gvir commands notable loyalty, with a July 2024 poll indicating 24% of Netanyahu coalition backers viewing him as the prime minister's preferred successor, ahead of other figures.[131] The party's electoral ascent represents a key achievement for Ben-Gvir, evolving from repeated failures to meet the 3.25% threshold in 2019 and 2020 elections to securing six Knesset seats within the Religious Zionist alliance in November 2022, which collectively garnered 14 seats and 10.8% of the national vote.[132] This breakthrough propelled Otzma Yehudit into coalition governance, granting Ben-Gvir oversight of the National Security Ministryâ€”encompassing police, prisons, and border forcesâ€”since December 2022.[133] Recent polling as of April 2025 forecasts further growth, projecting 11 seats for Otzma Yehudit amid persistent security threats, reflecting voter validation of his tenure.[134] In office, Ben-Gvir secured a record NIS 9 billion budget for the ministry in 2023, incorporating 4,850 new positions to expand police recruitment and infrastructure, which he credits with enhancing officer salaries and operational capacity against urban violence and terrorism.[51] [135] He expedited civilian firearm permits post-October 2023 attacks, raising monthly issuances from approximately 2,000 to 10,000, arguing this bolsters personal and communal deterrence in high-risk areas.[136] These initiatives, coupled with directives for heightened police enforcement in Arab-majority locales and against protesters, have been hailed by supporters as causal steps toward restoring public order, though empirical data on homicide reductions remains contested amid ongoing spikes in Arab sector violence.[137] Ben-Gvir's reappointment to the ministry in March 2025, following a January resignation over Gaza policy disputes, affirms his pivotal role in sustaining the coalition's 64-seat majority.[138]

Criticisms and empirical outcomes

Ben-Gvir has faced criticism for past convictions related to incitement to racism and support for a terrorist organization, including a 2007 guilty plea for incitement after possessing a poster praising Baruch Goldstein, the perpetrator of the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre.[6] Critics, including Israel's Attorney General Gali Baharav-Miara, have accused him of undermining the professional and politically neutral character of the police during his tenure as National Security Minister, alleging interference that prioritizes political loyalty over operational effectiveness.[139] His advocacy for policies such as deporting "disloyal" Arab citizens and restricting Palestinian freedom of movement in the West Bank has drawn international condemnation, with the U.S. State Department labeling his August 2023 statements as inflammatory and inconsistent with shared values.[99][124] Empirical data on domestic security outcomes under Ben-Gvir's leadership, which began in November 2022, indicate a marked deterioration in crime control, particularly in Israel's Arab sector. Police statistics reported a more than doubling of the Arab homicide rate in 2023, Ben-Gvir's first full year in office, with criminal homicides rising sharply overall and the percentage of solved cases leading to indictments declining.[140][141] In the first four months of 2023, homicides totaled 78, double the same period in 2022, amid claims by police sources that officers were overburdened with political directives rather than core policing.[142] By 2024 and into 2025, intra-Arab violence continued to surge, with a 122% increase in murders reported for 2023 alone by advocacy groups tracking the issue, and parallel rises in Jewish sector killings, contradicting Ben-Gvir's campaign pledges to enhance personal safety.[143][144] On terrorism and public security measures, Ben-Gvir's push to arm civilians and encourage bystander intervention has yielded isolated successes, such as in the July 2023 Tel Aviv attack where a licensed civilian neutralized the assailant, but broader data shows no commensurate decline in terror incidents or overall violence.[145] Policies targeting Arab communities, including heightened scrutiny of alleged "disloyalty," have coincided with reports of repressed speech and political persecution among Palestinian citizens, exacerbating tensions without evidence of reduced radicalization or improved integration outcomes.[146] Analysts have described these results as the worst performance by any Israeli police minister in history, with unsolved murder rates climbing and institutional trust eroding due to perceived politicization.[147]
