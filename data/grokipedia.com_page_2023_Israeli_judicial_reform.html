2023 Israeli judicial reform

The 2023 Israeli judicial reform comprised a series of bills proposed by Justice Minister Yariv Levin and the Netanyahu-led coalition government, following the November 2022 elections, to curtail the Supreme Court's expansive judicial review powers, which had evolved without explicit constitutional foundation, by abolishing the "reasonableness" standard for invalidating government decisions, granting elected officials a majority on the judicial selection committee, and subordinating the attorney general to the government.[1][2] Proponents, including conservative legal scholars, argued the reforms addressed an imbalance where unelected judges overridden democratically enacted policies on subjective grounds, restoring legislative supremacy akin to systems in many democracies without strong constitutional courts.[1] Critics, primarily from opposition parties, civil society organizations, and segments of the military and tech sectors, contended the changes would undermine checks against executive overreach and entrench corruption, igniting Israel's largest sustained protests in history, with weekly demonstrations drawing hundreds of thousands and instances of reservist refusals to serve.[3] The Knesset enacted the reasonableness clause amendment in July 2023 amid chaotic debates, but the Supreme Court overturned it on January 1, 2024, in an 8-7 rulingâ€”the first invalidation of a Basic Law provisionâ€”asserting its quasi-constitutional authority to safeguard democracy.[3] Subsequent components stalled due to public division and the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks, which shifted national focus and prompted a de facto moratorium on further reforms, though underlying tensions persist.[2]

Historical and Institutional Background

Origins of Judicial Power in Israel

The judicial system of the State of Israel was established in the immediate aftermath of its declaration of independence on May 14, 1948, building on the framework inherited from the British Mandate period.[4] The Provisional Council of State, functioning as the interim legislative authority, enacted the Law and Administration Ordinance No. 1 on May 19, 1948, which preserved the laws in force in Palestine as of May 5, 1948â€”the day before the Mandate's terminationâ€”subject to necessary adaptations for the sovereign Jewish state.[5] This ordinance ensured legal continuity while subordinating existing laws to the directives of the Provisional Government, thereby laying the groundwork for an independent judiciary free from prior colonial oversight.[6] On June 24, 1948, the Provisional State Council passed the Courts Ordinance, which reorganized the inherited Mandatory court structure into a unified national system.[7] This legislation formally established the Supreme Court of Israel as the apex judicial body, alongside district courts and magistrates' courts, vesting it with both appellate jurisdiction over lower courts in civil and criminal matters and original jurisdiction as the High Court of Justice (HCJ) for reviewing administrative actions.[8] The HCJ's powers, modeled on the English High Court's prerogative writs such as habeas corpus, mandamus, and certiorari, allowed it to supervise government decisions and ensure they conformed to existing law, though without explicit authority to invalidate primary legislation enacted by the Knesset.[9] The Supreme Court's initial bench consisted of five justices appointed in 1948 by the Provisional Council of State, including Moshe Smoira as the first president, alongside figures such as Meir Sussman and Shneor Zalman Cheshin.[10] These appointments, overseen by Justice Minister Pinhas Rosen, prioritized legal expertise from the pre-state Yishuv community, drawing judges primarily from European Jewish immigrant backgrounds familiar with common law traditions.[11] Judicial independence was enshrined from the outset, with judges serving for life until age 70 and insulated from political interference, reflecting a deliberate design to embed rule-of-law principles amid the nascent state's wartime exigencies.[12] At its founding, the judiciary's power was circumscribed by the absence of a formal written constitution, with authority derived from ordinances and later Basic Laws rather than inherent supremacy over the legislative branch.[13] Early cases, such as the 1948 Ziv decision, affirmed that the Declaration of Independence held declarative rather than justiciable status, underscoring the Court's initial deference to parliamentary sovereignty.[14] This foundational structure emphasized adjudication under a hybrid legal systemâ€”incorporating Ottoman civil codes, British common law procedures, and religious law for personal statusâ€”while prioritizing practical governance over expansive review mechanisms.[15] Over time, these origins set the stage for incremental expansions, but the 1948 framework prioritized stability and administrative oversight in a state forged under existential threats.[16]

The 1992 Constitutional Revolution and Its Long-Term Effects

In March 1992, the Knesset enacted two foundational Basic Lawsâ€”Basic Law: Human Dignity and Liberty, which safeguards rights including life, bodily integrity, privacy, property protection, and freedom from unreasonable searches, and Basic Law: Freedom of Occupation, which protects vocational libertyâ€”without explicit provisions for judicial supremacy over ordinary legislation.[17] [18] These laws emerged amid incremental constitutional development, as Israel lacks a single formal constitution, relying instead on Basic Laws as quasi-constitutional chapters.[19] Then-Deputy Supreme Court President Aharon Barak proclaimed these enactments a "constitutional revolution," asserting they elevated the judiciary's role in interpreting and enforcing rights against legislative and executive actions.[20] [21] This view crystallized in the landmark 1995 United Mizrahi Bank v. Migdal Cooperative Village ruling, where an expanded nine-justice panel, led by Barak, held that the 1992 Basic Laws possess supra-legal status akin to a constitution, empowering the court to strike down inconsistent Knesset statutesâ€”a power not expressly granted by the laws themselves but inferred from their entrenchment clauses and the implicit will of the enacting majority.[22] [13] [23] Over subsequent decades, this framework catalyzed expansive judicial authority, enabling the High Court of Justice (HCJ) to void primary legislation on substantive grounds, relax locus standi requirements to permit public interest petitions without direct injury, and broaden justiciability to encompass "political questions" like security policy and governmental appointments.[24] [25] For instance, the court invalidated laws restricting Palestinian family unification (2000), intervened in ministerial selections (e.g., disqualifying appointees deemed unfit under reasonableness tests), and shaped administrative law through doctrines like proportionality, often overriding executive discretion in defense and immigration matters.[26] [27] These developments engendered a perceived "juristocracy," where an unelected judiciary accrued veto power over democratically enacted policies, fostering tensions between judicial guardianship of rights and legislative sovereignty.[28] Critics, including legal scholars, contend the revolution's judicial self-empowerment lacked explicit Knesset consent, leading to overreach in policy domains traditionally reserved for elected branches and eroding public trust amid rising HCJ interventionsâ€”over 1,000 annual petitions by the 2010sâ€”while proponents credit it with bolstering minority protections absent a full constitution.[29] [30] This imbalance, evident in the court's frequent nullification of laws on vague "dignity" grounds, precipitated long-simmering demands for recalibration, culminating in reform efforts to restore democratic primacy.[31][32]

Evidence of Judicial Overreach and Public Grievances

Critics of the Israeli judiciary highlighted the Supreme Court's expansive use of the "reasonableness" standard, a common law doctrine imported into administrative law, which empowered justices to annul executive and ministerial decisions deemed "extremely unreasonable," even absent explicit statutory violation. This tool was invoked in numerous cases to override elected officials' choices, fostering perceptions of judicial substitution for democratic will. For instance, in 1993, the High Court of Justice disqualified Shas leader Aryeh Deri from serving as a minister and deputy minister, ruling Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin's appointment unreasonable due to Deri's ongoing criminal investigations, despite no legal prohibition at the time.[33] Similar reasoning annulled a deputy minister's appointment in the same rulings, setting a precedent for repeated interventions in personnel matters.[33] The doctrine's application extended to high-profile post-2022 election cases, amplifying grievances. On January 18, 2023, an 11-justice panel unanimously voided Deri's appointment as interior and health ministerâ€”sworn in just weeks priorâ€”citing extreme unreasonableness tied to his recent tax fraud conviction and prior imprisonment, overriding a Knesset-backed sentence commutation.[34] Proponents of reform, including Justice Minister Yariv Levin, argued this exemplified overreach, as the court preempted legislative overrides and imposed policy preferences on coalition formations, effectively dictating cabinet composition in a fragmented parliamentary system.[35] Such rulings, Levin contended in pre-reform speeches, undermined governmental stability, with the court annulling dozens of administrative decisions annually under reasonableness since the 1980s, often in politically sensitive areas like security and appointments.[35] Public discontent crystallized around the judiciary's structural insulation from electoral accountability. The nine-member Judicial Selection Committee granted sitting justices, alongside two Bar Association representatives, veto power over appointmentsâ€”effectively requiring supermajorities for non-consensus candidatesâ€”resulting in a judiciary perceived as ideologically uniform and resistant to diverse inputs.[36] Right-wing and religious voters, who polled higher rates of dissatisfaction (up to 70% in some sectors viewing the court as overly interventionist), resented blocks on policies like settlement expansions or migrant deportations, where reasonableness claims clashed with security cabinet discretions. The November 2022 elections, in which pro-reform parties secured 64 Knesset seats on platforms explicitly targeting these imbalances, reflected accumulated grievances, with figures like Netanyahu decrying the court's evolution from post-1992 "constitutional revolution" into an unelected veto over majoritarian governance.[36] While mainstream outlets often framed such critiques as threats to checks and balances, empirical patterns of annulmentsâ€”23 regular laws struck since 1995, plus extensive administrative reviewsâ€”substantiated claims of disproportionate judicial sway relative to the Knesset's legislative primacy.[37]

Political Catalysts Leading to Reform

Netanyahu Indictments and the 2018â€“2022 Stalemate

In November 2019, Israeli Attorney General Avichai Mandelblit formally indicted Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu on charges of bribery, fraud, and breach of trust across three separate cases. Case 1000 alleged that Netanyahu and his wife accepted illicit giftsâ€”including cigars, champagne, and jewelry valued at approximately 700,000 shekels (about $200,000)â€”from wealthy businessmen like Arnon Milchan in exchange for political favors, such as visa assistance and tax benefits.[38][39] Case 2000 involved recorded conversations where Netanyahu purportedly negotiated favorable coverage from Yedioth Ahronoth publisher Arnon Mozes in return for legislation harming rival Israel Hayom. Case 4000 centered on regulatory benefits granted to Bezeq telecom in exchange for positive coverage on the company's news site, Walla!, owned by a Netanyahu ally.[40][41] Netanyahu maintained his innocence, portraying the prosecutions as a "witch hunt" orchestrated by left-leaning elements within the judiciary, police, and media to undermine his right-wing government, a view echoed by his supporters who cited the attorney general's prior ties to Netanyahu's administration as evidence of internal betrayal rather than impartiality.[39] The indictments, announced on November 21, 2019, shortly after Netanyahu's Likud party secured the most seats in the April 9, 2019, Knesset election but failed to form a coalition, exacerbated existing political fragmentation.[42] Netanyahu conditioned coalition negotiations on legislation granting him immunity from prosecution while in office, a move opposition leaders, including Benny Gantz of the Blue and White alliance, rejected as self-serving, insisting an indicted prime minister could not lead ethically. This impasse triggered a repeat election on September 17, 2019, which again yielded no stable majority, as centrist and right-wing parties splintered over Netanyahu's legal entanglements and demands for judicial protections.[42] The resulting deadlock prevented government formation, leading to a third election on March 2, 2020; although Netanyahu eventually partnered with Gantz in a rotational unity government on May 17, 2020, amid the COVID-19 crisis, mutual distrustâ€”fueled by Netanyahu's alleged breaches of coalition agreements, such as budget disputesâ€”caused its collapse by December 2020.[42] A fourth election on March 23, 2021, empowered an anti-Netanyahu bloc under Naftali Bennett and Yair Lapid, forming a diverse "government of change" on June 13, 2021, that explicitly barred Netanyahu from participation and advanced his trial without immunity barriers.[42] This coalition, spanning right, center, left, and Arab parties, unraveled by June 2022 over internal policy rifts, culminating in a fifth election on November 1, 2022. Throughout 2018â€“2022, these repeated pollsâ€”unprecedented in Israeli historyâ€”stemmed primarily from Netanyahu's refusal to resign amid charges and opposition insistence on his sidelining, paralyzing legislative output on issues like budgets and security while amplifying grievances over judicial influence.[42] Netanyahu's allies, including figures like Justice Minister Yariv Levin, increasingly argued that the prosecutions exemplified systemic judicial overreach, where unelected officials wielded disproportionate power to target conservative leaders, a critique rooted in prior High Court interventions but intensified by personal stakes.[43] Critics, including former attorney generals, countered that such rhetoric masked efforts to evade accountability, though empirical data on conviction rates in high-profile political cases remained limited and contested.[43] The era's instability, with governance costs exceeding 5 billion shekels in added election expenses, underscored causal links between legal proceedings and electoral gridlock, priming demands for recalibrating judicial-executive balances post-2022.[42]

2022 Election Outcomes and Coalition Formation

The fifth Israeli legislative election in less than four years took place on November 1, 2022, to elect the 120 members of the 25th Knesset, following the collapse of the previous unity government in June 2022. Voter turnout reached 70.6%, the highest since 2015, reflecting heightened public engagement amid political deadlock and ongoing issues such as security threats and economic pressures.[44][45] The right-wing bloc aligned with Benjamin Netanyahu's Likud party emerged victorious, securing a slim majority with 64 seats: Likud won 32 seats (23.4% of the vote), Shas obtained 11 seats, United Torah voter shift toward parties promising decisive governance and policy shifts on issues like judicial authority and settlement expansion.[48] Coalition negotiations, which Netanyahu framed as prioritizing national stability over personal legal battles, extended over seven weeks due to disputes over ministerial portfolios, military exemptions for ultra-Orthodox Jews, and legislative priorities.[49] On December 29, 2022, the thirty-seventh government was sworn in, marking Netanyahu's return as prime minister for a record sixth term and the first stable right-wing coalition since cohesion on curbing perceived judicial overreach contrasted with the fragmented opposition, setting the stage for the reform's rapid advancement despite warnings from legal scholars and international observers about risks to democratic checks.[49]

Specific Components of the Reform Package

Reforms to Judicial Selection Process

The Judicial Selection Committee in Israel, established under Basic Law: The Judiciary, traditionally comprises nine members: three Supreme Court justices (including the court president), two cabinet ministers (with the justice minister as chair), two Knesset members (one from the coalition and one from the opposition), and two representatives from the Israel Bar Association.[52] To appoint Supreme Court justices, the committee requires at least seven members present and a simple majority vote, though in practice, the three justices and two Bar representativesâ€”five membersâ€”hold veto power over candidates, as they can block approval if unified.[52] This structure has been criticized for granting disproportionate influence to the judiciary and legal profession over appointments, potentially insulating the court from broader democratic input.[53] As part of the 2023 judicial reform package introduced on January 11, 2023, Justice Minister Yariv Levin proposed amending Basic Law: The Judiciary to restructure the committee into 11 members: three cabinet ministers (including the justice minister as chair), three Supreme Court justices (the president plus two elected by the court's judges), three Knesset committee chairpersons (from the Constitution, Law and Justice Committee, State Control Committee, and House Committee), and two public representatives selected by the justice minister (one lawyer and one non-lawyer).[53][52] This would eliminate the Bar Association's direct seats, replacing them with minister-appointed public figures, while the Knesset chairpersonsâ€”positions typically held by coalition members in a majority governmentâ€”would enhance elected officials' leverage. Appointments of Supreme Court justices would require a simple majority of committee members, followed by ratification via a public hearing and majority vote in the Knesset Constitution, Law and Justice Committee within 21 days (or automatic progression if delayed).[53] The proposal also allowed for selecting the Supreme Court president and deputy from outside sitting justices, ending the seniority convention, and raised the dismissal threshold to nine of 11 members.[52] The amendment advanced to preliminary discussions and was forwarded for second and third readings in the Knesset on March 27, 2023, but was paused amid widespread protests and not enacted that year.[52] Proponents argued the changes would democratize selections by balancing judicial self-selection with political accountability, reflecting the elected branches' role in a parliamentary system.[53] Critics, including legal scholars and opposition figures, contended it risked politicizing the judiciary, enabling the government to appoint ideologically aligned judges without sufficient checks.[52] The minimum quorum was proposed to drop to five members, facilitating operations under government control.[53]

Abolition of the Reasonableness Standard

The reasonableness standard in Israeli administrative law empowered courts to invalidate government decisions, ministerial appointments, or regulations deemed extremely unreasonable, serving as a judicial check on executive discretion derived from common law traditions.[36][54] This doctrine, applied sparingly but notably in cases like disqualifying senior appointments for lack of proportionality or arbitrariness, was criticized by reform proponents for enabling unelected judges to substitute their judgment for that of elected officials on policy matters.[55] The abolition targeted Basic Law: The Judiciary through Amendment No. 3, which explicitly prohibited courts from reviewing the reasonableness of decisions by the government, prime minister, ministers, or the Knesset itself, while preserving review for local authorities.[37] Introduced as part of the broader judicial reform, the bill passed its first reading on July 11, 2023, by a 64-56 Knesset vote, followed by second and third readings on July 24, 2023, succeeding 64-0 after opposition members walked out in protest.[36][56] Proponents, including Justice Minister Yariv Levin, contended that the standard facilitated judicial overreach, undermining democratic accountability by allowing courts to veto reasonable executive choices without clear constitutional grounding, and argued it restored balance to favor elected branches.[57] Opponents, including legal scholars and opposition lawmakers, warned that eliminating this tool would permit arbitrary or capricious governance, particularly in appointments and regulations, eroding judicial oversight essential for preventing abuse of power in Israel's quasi-parliamentary system lacking a formal constitution.[58] They highlighted its role in landmark rulings, such as voiding appointments lacking expertise, asserting that without it, no equivalent mechanism existed to enforce administrative rationality, potentially damaging democratic checks amid coalition dynamics prone to factional influence.[59] Petitions challenging the amendment culminated in High Court of Justice case HCJ 5658/23, Movement for Quality Government in Israel v. Knesset, where on January 1, 2024, an 8-7 majority struck down the law, ruling it inflicted severe harm to Israel's democratic framework by unduly curtailing core judicial review principles embedded in Basic Laws.[60][61][62] The decision marked the first invalidation of a Basic Law amendment, with the majority emphasizing the standard's necessity for upholding rule of law, while the minority viewed the abolition as a legitimate legislative recalibration of powers not requiring judicial override.[63] This ruling reinstated the doctrine but intensified debates over judicial versus legislative supremacy.[64]

Introduction of an Override Clause

The override clause, formally proposed as an amendment to Israel's Basic Law: The Judiciary, would have empowered the Knesset to reinstate any statute invalidated by the Supreme Court as unconstitutional through a simple majority vote of 61 out of 120 members, effectively limiting the finality of judicial review over primary legislation.[3][65] Introduced by Justice Minister Yariv Levin on January 11, 2023, as part of the broader judicial reform package, the mechanism drew inspiration from override provisions in other democracies, such as Canada's notwithstanding clause, but controversially omitted any supermajority requirement or time limitation that might constrain its use.[52] Proponents, including members of the Netanyahu coalition, argued that it addressed an imbalance where the unelected judiciary could nullify democratically enacted laws without reciprocal legislative recourse, citing instances like the court's 2021 invalidation of a citizenship law provision as evidence of overreach.[66] The clause's text specified that upon a Knesset resolution declaring a law's applicability "notwithstanding" a judicial ruling, the decision would bind all authorities, including the courts, thereby subordinating judicial interpretations of Basic Laws to parliamentary will in policy matters.[67] This proposal advanced to preliminary Knesset approval stages by March 2023, alongside other reforms, but faced immediate backlash for potentially eroding protections for minorities and rights entrenched via judicial doctrine since the 1990s constitutional developments.[67] Critics, including legal scholars and opposition lawmakers, contended it would democratize tyranny by enabling transient majorities to override constitutional limits, pointing to the rarity of prior judicial invalidationsâ€”fewer than 20 Knesset laws struck down in over 70 yearsâ€”as evidence against claims of systemic judicial excess.[3][66] By June 2023, amid escalating protests, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu announced the postponement of the override clause's advancement, signaling a shift toward requiring a supermajority (potentially 70 votes) for future iterations to mitigate concerns over abuse, though no such revised bill progressed to enactment before the reform package was effectively frozen following the October 7 Hamas attacks.[68] As of late 2023, the clause remained unlegislated, preserving the Supreme Court's exclusive authority to interpret Basic Laws without statutory override, though coalition figures continued advocating its necessity for legislative sovereignty.[69] This deferral highlighted tensions between parliamentary democracy and judicial guardianship in Israel's Basic Laws framework, where the absence of a formal constitution amplifies interpretive disputes.[52]

Alterations to Government Legal Advisors and Attorney General Role

The proposed alterations to the roles of government legal advisors and the Attorney General sought to codify their advisory function in statute, shifting from a binding gatekeeper roleâ€”established through Supreme Court rulings and administrative practiceâ€”to a non-binding advisory capacity, allowing the elected government greater discretion in legal interpretations while remaining subject to judicial review for legality.[52][70] Currently, without explicit statutory basis, the Attorney General's opinions bind government decisions on policy, appointments, and legislation, effectively granting veto power over ministerial actions deemed unlawful or unreasonable by the officeholder; ministerial legal advisors, as civil servants appointed via public tender and supervised by the Attorney General, similarly enforce binding compliance, requiring their consent for ministry operations and blocking initiatives conflicting with their assessments.[66][71] Under the reform package, introduced by Justice Minister Yariv Levin on January 11, 2023, the Attorney General's stance would become one legal opinion among others, enabling the government to override it, hire private counsel for state representation in court, or proceed with actions subject only to post-hoc High Court scrutiny of statutory compliance rather than deference to the Attorney General's view.[52][70] For ministerial legal advisors, a specific draft billâ€”Proposed Civil Service Law (Appointments) (Amendment), 5773-2023, sponsored by Knesset member David Amsalemâ€”would transfer appointment authority to politically appointed ministry director-generals, subordinate advisors directly to ministers, eliminate the binding effect of their opinions, and simplify dismissals without Attorney General approval, framing their primary duty as facilitating policy implementation alongside legality checks.[52] Proponents, including reform advocates, argued this corrects an imbalance where unelected officials, empowered by judicial precedents like those from the Agranat (1962) and Shamgar (2000) commissions (which affirmed governmental deviation rights but were not followed in practice), impede democratic governance; the changes would align Israel with systems where executive legal advice informs but does not dictate elected decisions.[70][72] Opponents, such as the Israel Democracy Institute, contended the shifts would politicize advisorsâ€”potentially turning them into compliant enablers of executive overreachâ€”and erode internal rule-of-law safeguards, increasing risks of actions evading legal vetting, though the proposals preserved the Attorney General's criminal prosecution authority and public corruption oversight unchanged.[71] These components were not enacted in 2023, as the broader package stalled amid protests, with only the reasonableness standard amendment passing in July; subsequent 2025 efforts to curtail Attorney General authority, including dismissal attempts, faced Supreme Court blocks, underscoring ongoing tensions over the office's conventional powers.[52][66]

Legislative Process and Partial Enactment

Timeline of Knesset Deliberations and Votes

The Knesset began deliberations on components of the judicial reform package following the government's formation on December 29, 2022. On January 11, 2023, Justice Minister Yariv Levin formally introduced the legislative proposals, including amendments to judicial selection, the reasonableness standard, and other judicial review mechanisms.[52] On February 20, 2023, the Knesset plenum approved the first reading of a bill to overhaul the Judicial Selection Committee by granting the coalition majority control over appointments, eliminating veto power held by judges and bar association representatives. The vote passed 63â€“47, with the bill referred to the Constitution, Law and Justice Committee for further deliberation.[73] In early March 2023, the committee under Chairman Simcha Rothman (Religious Zionism Party) held initial hearings on multiple reform bills, including those addressing judicial selection and override mechanisms. On March 13â€“14, 2023, the plenum passed first readings on related amendments, such as one requiring 12 of 15 Supreme Court justices to agree to strike down laws as unconstitutional, with votes reflecting coalition majorities amid opposition boycotts.[74][75] Deliberations on the reasonableness standard amendment intensified in the committee during June and early July 2023. On July 4, 2023, the committee approved advancing the bill to second and third readings by a 9â€“6 margin, following sessions marked by opposition challenges and procedural disputes.[76] The reasonableness bill's first reading passed the plenum on July 11, 2023, by 64â€“56, primarily along coalition lines.[36] On July 24, 2023, the Knesset enacted the amendment abolishing the Supreme Court's use of the reasonableness standard for reviewing government decisions, combining second and third readings in a single 64â€“0 vote after opposition members exited the chamber in protest. This marked the only major provision passed into law from the 2023 package, with other bills like judicial selection reforms stalling in committee without advancing to final votes.[77][36]

Enactment of Key Provisions in 2023

On July 24, 2023, the Knesset passed Amendment No. 3 to Basic Law: The Judiciary, which abolished the Supreme Court's authority to invalidate administrative decisions, ministerial actions, or laws enacted by the Knesset on grounds of unreasonableness.[77][78] The legislation targeted a judicial standard that had enabled the court to strike down executive or legislative measures deemed extremely unreasonable, a tool frequently invoked in cases involving government appointments and policy decisions.[36][79] Proponents, including Justice Minister Yariv Levin, argued the standard represented judicial overreach, allowing unelected judges to substitute their judgment for that of elected officials.[80] The bill advanced through three readings in the Knesset plenum following committee deliberations that began earlier in the year.[56] It secured passage with 64 votes in favor and 0 against, as all 56 opposition members exited the chamber in boycott, refusing to participate amid ongoing nationwide protests.[80][77] The coalition's unified support reflected the government's slim 64-seat majority in the 120-member Knesset, enabling the measure despite internal debates over timing and a last-minute compromise proposal that failed.[78][80] This enactment marked the firstâ€”and, as of late 2023, onlyâ€”major component of the broader judicial reform package to become law, with subsequent elements such as changes to judicial selection and an override clause stalling amid escalating domestic opposition and security concerns following the October 7 Hamas attacks.[56][64] The law's immediate effects included removing a check on executive discretion, potentially facilitating decisions previously quashed by the court, such as certain high-level appointments.[36] Critics, including legal scholars and opposition leaders, contended it undermined democratic safeguards by insulating arbitrary governance from judicial review, though government officials maintained it restored balance to a system where the court had overturned decisions in approximately 20 cases over decades using this standard.[79][64] Multiple petitions challenging the amendment's constitutionality were promptly filed with the High Court of Justice, setting the stage for prolonged litigation.[77]

Societal and Domestic Reactions

Mass Protests and Their Scale

Mass protests against the proposed judicial reforms began on January 14, 2023, with approximately 80,000 participants gathering in Tel Aviv and other cities, marking one of the largest demonstrations in recent Israeli history up to that point.[81] These early rallies were organized in response to the new government's initial legislative proposals, drawing crowds primarily from urban centers and opposition strongholds. By January 21, attendance in Tel Aviv alone reached an estimated 100,000, according to reports from multiple outlets, signaling rapid escalation.[82] The demonstrations became a weekly occurrence, primarily on Saturday evenings, continuing for nearly 30 consecutive weeks until the partial enactment of reforms in July 2023 and subsequent events shifted national focus.[83] Peak turnout occurred in March 2023, with organizers claiming half a million participants nationwide on March 11, described as the largest in Israeli history, though police estimates for specific cities like Haifa were lower at 50,000.[84] [85] Discrepancies between organizer claims and police figures were common; for instance, on February 13, police reported 100,000 total attendees while organizers asserted at least 250,000.[86] Later protests in July, ahead of key votes, drew hundreds of thousands in Tel Aviv and tens of thousands marching to Jerusalem on July 22.[87] Over the course of the movement, Israel's police chief estimated that between 6 and 7 million Israelis participated in protests across 4,400 locations nationwide, reflecting broad but not universal engagement from the population of about 9.7 million.[88] Events frequently centered on major highways like the Ayalon in Tel Aviv, with demonstrators blocking roads to amplify visibility, leading to tensions with law enforcement but minimal widespread violence.[89] The scale underscored deep societal divisions, though participation remained concentrated among secular, left-leaning demographics, contrasting with the electoral mandate secured by the reform-supporting coalition in November 2022.[90]

Supportive Demonstrations and Counter-Narratives

Supporters of the 2023 judicial reform organized rallies to counter the larger-scale opposition protests, emphasizing the democratic mandate of the elected government and the need to curb perceived judicial overreach. On March 27, 2023, an estimated 100,000 individuals gathered in Jerusalem to endorse the overhaul, framing it as essential for strengthening Israel's democratic and Jewish character by aligning judicial power with elected institutions.[91] Similar events occurred in April and July 2023, with organizers claiming up to 200,000 attendees in Jerusalem on April 27 and in Tel Aviv on July 23, where participants urged continuation of the legislative process despite opposition disruptions.[92][93] These demonstrations, while smaller and less frequent than anti-reform gatherings that drew hundreds of thousands weekly, highlighted divisions along ideological lines, with pro-reform crowds often including coalition lawmakers who argued that the electorate's choiceâ€”over two million votes for right-wing partiesâ€”should prevail over unelected judges.[90] Counter-narratives from reform advocates portrayed the Israeli judiciary, particularly the Supreme Court, as having accumulated excessive authority without a formal constitution to constrain it, enabling interventions in policy domains like security and settlements that undermined legislative sovereignty.[94] Proponents contended that the "reasonableness" standard allowed vague judicial vetoes over executive and Knesset decisions, citing instances where the court struck down laws on national security grounds despite elected majorities, thus restoring balance by empowering the political branches as in most democracies.[95] They further argued that the judicial selection committee's structure, dominated by sitting judges and bar representatives, perpetuated an insular elite unresponsive to public will, and reforms would democratize appointments without eliminating oversight.[66] Critics of the opposition narrative dismissed claims of democratic erosion as exaggerated by media and academic elites, noting that similar judicial reforms exist in countries like the United States and Germany, where elected bodies hold primacy, and that halting the process equated to minority veto over electoral outcomes.[96] These arguments gained traction among coalition supporters, who viewed the overhaul as correcting a decades-long shift where the judiciary assumed quasi-constitutional powers, potentially exacerbating policy gridlock on issues like immigration and defense.[97]

Responses from Military, Economy, and Elite Institutions

In the Israeli Defense Forces (IDF), opposition to the judicial reform emerged prominently from reservists, particularly in elite units such as the Air Force. On July 19, 2023, hundreds of reservists publicly vowed to refuse volunteer service if the overhaul proceeded, citing concerns over democratic erosion and its potential impact on national security.[98] By late July 2023, the number of threatening non-compliance exceeded 1,000, including pilots and intelligence officers, prompting the IDF to assess operational readiness amid fears of degraded capabilities.[99] Defense Minister Yoav Gallant, on July 24, 2023, urged a pause in the legislative process, warning of damage to the IDF's strength, a stance that led to his temporary dismissal by Prime Minister Netanyahu before partial reinstatement.[87] While some active-duty personnel and coalition-aligned figures expressed support for the reforms as necessary checks on judicial overreach, documented instances of pro-reform military endorsements remained limited compared to the widespread reservist dissent.[100] Economic responses highlighted risks of instability and reduced investment. The Bank of Israel, in analyses released throughout 2023, linked the reform-induced uncertainty to elevated risk premiums, export declines, and potential GDP contraction of up to several percentage points, projecting growth below 3% for the year amid prior 6.4% expansion in 2022.[101][102] Finance Ministry officials echoed these warnings on March 21, 2023, forecasting backlash including capital flight and weakened foreign confidence.[101] Credit rating agencies responded swiftly: S&P Global identified the reforms as a "downside risk" to Israel's rating as early as January 12, 2023, while Moody's shifted the outlook from positive to stable in April 2023, attributing it to governance strains from the overhaul.[103] Business leaders, including tech sector executives, joined protests, with demonstrations in Tel Aviv underscoring fears of eroded rule of law deterring innovation in Israel's high-tech economy.[104] Elite institutions, including academia and legal bodies, largely voiced opposition, framing the reforms as threats to judicial independence. University presidents and faculty from institutions like Hebrew University and Tel Aviv University participated in strikes and petitions starting in February 2023, arguing the changes would undermine academic freedom and institutional autonomy intertwined with legal protections.[105] The Israel Bar Association, led by Chairman Amit Becher, condemned the proposals as politicizing the judiciary, with public statements and legal challenges emphasizing risks to due process.[106] A minority of academics, numbering around 120 affiliated with right-leaning groups like Professors for a Strong Israel, endorsed the overhaul on March 2, 2023, contending it corrected judicial activism without compromising core democratic functions.[105] These institutional critiques often aligned with broader elite concerns over power concentration, though supporters among legal scholars highlighted empirical data on comparable systems in other democracies functioning without equivalent judicial veto powers.

Judicial Counteractions

High Court Petitions and Proceedings

Following the Knesset's enactment of Amendment No. 3 to Basic Law: The Judiciary on July 24, 2023â€”which eliminated the reasonableness standard for judicial reviewâ€”multiple petitions were promptly filed with the High Court of Justice (HCJ) seeking to declare the law unconstitutional. Petitioners, including civil rights organizations, argued that the amendment undermined core democratic principles by stripping courts of a key tool to check arbitrary or extreme executive and legislative actions, potentially enabling corruption and eroding separation of powers.[60] The petitions contended that Basic Laws, while quasi-constitutional, are not immune from review if they fundamentally damage Israel's constitutional framework as established by prior jurisprudence. The cases were consolidated primarily under HCJ 5658/23 Movement for Quality Government in Israel v. Knesset, with additional petitions from entities such as the Israel Bar Association and individual jurists joining the docket. Respondents included the Knesset and government ministers, who defended the amendment as a necessary correction to judicial overreach, asserting that the reasonableness doctrine had been expanded beyond legislative intent to encroach on elected branches' authority. Attorney General Gali Baharav-Miara, despite her role in the executive branch, opposed the reform and supported the petitioners, citing risks to administrative legality and public trust in governance.[107] ![HCJ 5658/23 case document][float-right] Proceedings commenced on September 12, 2023, marking the first time the HCJ convened as a full plenum of all 15 justices to hear a single matter, reflecting the amendment's perceived existential threat to judicial independence.[108] Oral arguments unfolded over four days, with petitioners emphasizing empirical evidence of the doctrine's role in prior rulings invalidating unreasonable decisions, such as ministerial appointments lacking any rational basis. Government representatives countered that the doctrine's "extremeness" threshold allowed unelected judges to substitute their policy preferences for democratically enacted laws, citing over 20 annual reasonableness-based interventions as evidence of imbalance. The court grappled with foundational questions of its authority to review Basic Law amendments, invoking the "constitutional revolution" of the 1990s that entrenched judicial review under the Basic Laws framework. Subsequent procedural developments included requests for interim relief, which the court denied, allowing the amendment to remain in force pending full deliberation.[62] Hearings extended into late 2023 amid heightened national tensions, with justices probing the amendment's timingâ€”passed via a slim majority during mass protestsâ€”and its potential to impair checks on coalition-driven decisions.[60] No further en banc sessions occurred before deliberations concluded, though amicus briefs from legal scholars underscored divisions: some viewed the reform as restoring parliamentary sovereignty, while others warned of a slide toward unchecked majoritarianism absent robust review mechanisms.

The 2024 Ruling Striking Down Core Elements

On January 1, 2024, Israel's Supreme Court, sitting as the High Court of Justice, ruled 8-7 to strike down Amendment No. 3 to Basic Law: The Judiciary, a key provision of the 2023 judicial reform enacted on July 24, 2023.[62][109] The amendment had removed the "reasonableness" standard, preventing courts from invalidating government or ministerial decisions deemed extremely unreasonable.[60] This marked the first instance in which the court annulled a Basic Law, Israel's quasi-constitutional statutes.[62] In the case HCJ 5658/23 Movement for Quality Government in Israel v. Knesset, a 12-3 majority of the 15-justice panel affirmed the court's authority to conduct judicial review of Basic Laws in exceptional circumstances where they deviate substantially from Israel's democratic foundations.[109] The majority opinion, authored by retiring President Esther Hayut, held that the amendment represented an extreme and unprecedented infringement on judicial independence and the core democratic tenets of the state, justifying intervention despite the Knesset's constituent role.[109][62] The seven dissenting justices contended that the court lacked jurisdiction to review Basic Laws, viewing such authority as an overreach that undermines the Knesset's sovereign legislative power as the people's elected representative.[109] Dissenters argued the amendment merely recalibrated the balance of powers by limiting an expansive judicial tool, without fundamentally altering Israel's democratic structure.[109] The ruling restored the reasonableness doctrine to administrative law, reinstating courts' ability to scrutinize executive actions for extreme irrationality.[60]

International Perspectives and Alleged Connections

Global Media and Governmental Reactions

The United States government under President Joe Biden expressed significant concern over the judicial reform proposals starting in early 2023. On March 20, 2023, Biden conveyed "deep concern" about the plans, stating they could push Israeli democracy into "dangerous waters" and urging Netanyahu to seek broad consensus rather than unilateral action.[110] Following the Knesset's passage of the reasonableness clause amendment on July 24, 2023, the White House described the vote as "unfortunate," reiterating calls for dialogue amid fears of diminished judicial independence.[111] Netanyahu responded by defending Israel's sovereignty, rejecting external pressure and emphasizing that the reforms addressed judicial overreach without ending democracy.[112] European governments and institutions echoed similar criticisms, viewing the reforms as a risk to democratic checks and balances. The European Union issued a statement on July 25, 2023, expressing "great concern" over the developments and monitoring them closely, while stressing the importance of judicial independence for Israel's democratic stability.[113] France, Germany, and the United Kingdom released joint or individual remarks aligning with U.S. positions, with German Foreign Minister Annalena Baerbock warning on July 24, 2023, that the changes could erode the separation of powers.[114] These responses contrasted with limited explicit governmental support elsewhere; Hungarian Prime Minister Viktor OrbÃ¡n, a Netanyahu ally, maintained strong bilateral ties but did not publicly endorse the specific overhaul measures amid Hungary's own history of judicial restructuring.[115] Global media coverage, particularly from Western outlets, predominantly framed the reforms as a threat to Israel's democracy, amplifying narratives of internal division and judicial weakening. Outlets such as The New York Times, BBC, and CNN emphasized mass protests against the legislation, with headlines like "Thousands Protest in Israel Over Netanyahu's Judicial Overhaul" on February 13, 2023, highlighting opposition scale while giving less prominence to proponents' arguments on rebalancing executive and judicial powers.[116] Coverage often drew parallels to authoritarian shifts, as in The Guardian's July 24, 2023, analysis portraying the overhaul as enabling executive dominance, though analyses from Israeli perspectives critiqued this as disproportionate focus on anti-reform voices over empirical critiques of prior judicial activism.[117] Mainstream reporting tended to align with domestic liberal opposition, reflecting institutional biases toward preserving unelected judicial authority in liberal democracies.[118]

Claims Linking Reform to Broader Conflicts

Security officials and analysts claimed that the societal divisions sparked by the judicial reform proposals eroded Israel's national unity and deterrence posture, potentially emboldening adversaries like Hamas in the broader Israeli-Palestinian conflict. On March 25, 2023, Defense Minister Yoav Gallant warned that the reform's advancement had "created an internal rift that poses a clear and immediate threat to Israel's national security," urging a halt to the legislation to preserve cohesion amid external threats.[119] Similarly, on July 28, 2023, following the Knesset's passage of the reasonableness clause limitation, Israeli Air Force Commander Maj. Gen. Tomer Bar stated that the ongoing crisis could prompt enemies to "test the frontiers," emphasizing the need for restored societal solidarity to maintain operational readiness.[120] These assertions were echoed by former IDF Military Intelligence chief Aharon Haliva, who in April 2023 described the reform-induced discord as inflicting potentially irreversible damage to national security by undermining institutional trust and focus on existential threats.[121] Post-October 7, 2023, several observers linked the reform controversy's distractions to intelligence oversights that facilitated Hamas's attack, arguing that the intense domestic polarization diverted attention from Gaza border vulnerabilities. Reservist officers, in letters circulated during the peak protest period in early 2023, cautioned Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu that the judicial crisis was weakening deterrence, with Hamas and other groups monitoring Israel's internal fractures as opportunities for aggression; Netanyahu later rejected claims that specific October 7 warnings were conveyed amid these protests.[122] Opposition leader Yair Lapid asserted in August 2024 that Netanyahu had been informed for months prior that the reform-fueled rifts diminished deterrence, with terror organizations exploiting the perceived disarray.[123] Analyses from security think tanks, such as the Institute for National Security Studies, contended that the reform debates amplified broader threats by impairing unified threat assessment, contributing to a conceptual failure where Hamas's preparations were underestimated amid internal strife.[124] While direct causation remains debated, these claims highlight how proponents of the reform viewed protesters' actions as fracturing the societal fabric essential for confronting persistent conflicts with Hamas and Iran-backed proxies.[125] Critics of the reform, conversely, alleged that the government's push itself compromised democratic checks, indirectly heightening vulnerabilities in the Arab-Israeli arena by eroding judicial oversight of security decisions. However, empirical assessments of the October 7 intelligence lapses primarily attribute them to doctrinal misjudgmentsâ€”such as overreliance on Hamas's economic incentives and underestimation of its attack capacityâ€”rather than solely the reform's divisiveness, though the latter exacerbated resource and attentional strains on military reserves and intelligence prioritization.[126][127] These divergent attributions underscore the reform's role in amplifying pre-existing tensions between domestic governance debates and external security imperatives.

Consequences and Subsequent Developments

Pre-October 7 Divisions and National Security Implications

The 2023 judicial reform protests exacerbated longstanding societal divisions in Israel, pitting supporters of the government's efforts to curb perceived judicial overreach against opponents who viewed the changes as an existential threat to democratic checks and balances. Weekly demonstrations drew tens of thousands of participants nationwide, with peaks exceeding 500,000 attendees in Tel Aviv alone by March 2023, including actions such as highway blockades and airport disruptions that strained public order.[128][129] Public opinion polls reflected this polarization, with the Israel Democracy Index plummeting to 4.4 out of 10 in June 2023 amid the unrest, as coalition voters largely backed the reforms while opposition supporters mobilized en masse.[130] These fissures extended to ethnic and sectoral lines, though Arab Israelis largely abstained from the protests, highlighting uneven civic engagement.[131] Within the Israel Defense Forces (IDF), the protests precipitated a crisis of cohesion, as elite reservistsâ€”particularly from air force squadrons, intelligence units, and cyber commandsâ€”publicly threatened service refusals. By July 2023, hundreds of reservists had signed petitions vowing to suspend voluntary duty if the reform advanced, organized by groups like Brothers in Arms, with estimates of up to 10,000 signatures from active-duty personnel and veterans signaling broader discontent.[98][99] Tens of thousands of reservists participated in demonstrations, amplifying internal military dissent and prompting warnings from IDF leaders about eroded unit morale and operational readiness.[132] While proponents argued that actual refusals would cease under genuine threat, the rhetoric nonetheless conveyed signals of vulnerability to adversaries monitoring Israel's domestic turmoil.[99] National security implications materialized through perceived deterrence erosion, as foreign intelligence assessments, including from Iran, interpreted the divisions as signs of Israeli weakness ripe for exploitation. IDF intelligence reports prior to October 7, 2023, alerted Prime Minister Netanyahu that regional actors viewed the unrest as diminishing Israel's resolve, with multiple fronts potentially aligning against it.[133] In the weeks leading up to the Hamas attack, IDF Chief of Staff Herzi Halevi explicitly cautioned Netanyahu that enemies had grown bolder in risk-taking due to internal strife, underscoring how the reform debate distracted from unified threat vigilance.[134] Adversaries like Hamas and Hezbollah capitalized on this narrative in propaganda, framing the protests as evidence of governmental illegitimacy and societal fracture, which analysts linked to heightened preemptive aggression incentives.[135] Though direct causation to specific intelligence lapses remains debated, the protests' scale and military involvement objectively strained Israel's projection of strength, contributing to a pre-war environment of divided priorities.[126]

Post-War Pause and Resumed Efforts in 2024â€“2025

Following the Hamas attacks on October 7, 2023, which initiated the Israel-Hamas war, both proponents and opponents of the judicial reform suspended their activities in a display of national unity. Protests against the reform, which had mobilized hundreds of thousands weekly earlier in the year, ceased almost immediately as public focus shifted to military mobilization and defense efforts. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's government likewise halted advancement of pending legislation, including bills on judicial selection and government legal advisors, to prioritize wartime coordination.[136][137] During the initial phases of the war in late 2023 and throughout 2024, judicial reform remained dormant amid ongoing hostilities and internal coalition strains. Justice Minister Yariv Levin declined to convene the Judicial Selection Committee, resulting in a freeze on senior judicial appointments; this inaction affected at least 20 vacant positions on district and appellate courts by mid-2024. The Supreme Court's January 1, 2024, ruling invalidating the 2023 amendment to the Basic Law: Judiciary's reasonableness clauseâ€”by an 8-7 marginâ€”further sidelined legislative pushes, as the government avoided immediate countermeasures to maintain war cabinet stability, which included opposition figures Benny Gantz and Yair Lapid until its dissolution in June 2024.[35][138][139] Efforts resumed in earnest by late 2024, with the coalition introducing narrower bills rather than comprehensive packages. In December 2024, the Knesset began debating amendments to enhance political influence over judicial processes, reflecting coalition demands from parties like Shas and United Torah Judaism for overrides to the reasonableness ruling. A pivotal development occurred on March 27, 2025, when the Knesset approved legislation altering the Judicial Selection Committee's composition: it increased elected officials' representation from three to seven out of nine members, granting the coalition majority control over judge appointments for the first time. The bill passed 67-1 after opposition members boycotted the final votes, prompting renewed street protestsâ€”the largest since the war began, drawing tens of thousands in Tel Aviv on March 25, 2025â€”and warnings from legal experts of eroded judicial independence.[136][140][141][142] By October 2025, additional proposals circulated, including mechanisms for the Knesset to override High Court decisions with simple majorities, though wartime constraints and public fatigue limited progress. Supporters, including Netanyahu, argued these changes restored democratic balance by empowering elected representatives over unelected judges, citing pre-war precedents where the judiciary blocked government policies on security grounds. Critics, including former military leaders and the Israel Bar Association, contended the reforms risked politicizing the courts, potentially undermining checks on executive power amid ongoing Gaza operations. No further major enactments occurred by late 2025, as coalition focus shifted to postwar reconstruction and hostage negotiations.[143][136][144]

Long-Term Debate on Democratic Balance in Israel

The debate over democratic balance in Israel predates the 2023 judicial reform proposals, tracing back to the "constitutional revolution" of the early 1990s, when the Supreme Court, under Chief Justice Aharon Barak, unilaterally expanded its powers by declaring Basic Laws as a de facto constitution and asserting broad judicial review over Knesset legislation, including the "reasonableness" standard to invalidate executive and legislative decisions.[145] This shift enabled the court to strike down approximately 20 primary laws between 1995 and 2023, often on grounds of proportionality or administrative reasonableness, which critics argued substituted judicial policy preferences for elected majorities.[146] Proponents of reform, including legal scholars affiliated with the Kohelet Policy Forum, contend that such interventions undermine democratic sovereignty, as the Knessetâ€”Israel's unicameral parliament elected by proportional representationâ€”lacks a formal written constitution to constrain its authority, making unelected judges an anomalous veto power in a parliamentary system.[147] Opponents, drawing from liberal democratic theory, maintain that the judiciary serves as an essential check against majoritarian excesses in Israel's fragmented political landscape, where coalition governments frequently include ultra-Orthodox and nationalist parties that could prioritize sectoral interests over minority rights or rule of law.[148] For instance, the court's pre-2023 rulings, such as the 2002 invalidation of the Tal Law exempting ultra-Orthodox men from military service on reasonableness grounds, highlighted its role in enforcing universal obligations, though reformers viewed this as judicial overreach into security and cultural policy domains traditionally reserved for the elected branches.[149] Empirical analyses, including those from the Israel Democracy Institute, note that while the court has protected civil liberties in cases like striking discriminatory admissions policies, its interpretive expansionâ€”lacking explicit textual anchorsâ€”has fueled perceptions of elite capture, with justices selected via a committee where sitting judges hold veto power, perpetuating ideological continuity.[3] The 2023 reforms crystallized these tensions by targeting mechanisms like the reasonableness doctrine and judicial appointments, aiming to realign power toward the Knesset through majority rule and an override clause allowing legislative reassertion of struck-down laws.[146] Post the January 1, 2024, Supreme Court decision (8-7) striking down the reasonableness amendment as exceeding the court's quasi-constitutional authority, the debate persisted into 2025, with renewed legislative efforts focusing on incremental changes to the Judicial Selection Committee to enhance political input without full politicization.[136] Advocates argue this restores causal balance in a system where judicial dominance has empirically correlated with policy gridlock, such as repeated interventions in settlement regulations and security decisions, while detractors warn of risks to institutional independence amid ongoing national security inquiries post-October 7, 2023.[137] As of October 2025, no comprehensive resolution has emerged, with public opinion polls showing persistent divisions: roughly 50% favoring curbs on judicial power per Israel Democracy Institute surveys, reflecting deeper questions on whether Israel's "basic laws" framework adequately sustains democratic accountability without formal constitutional entrenchment.[3]
