CityPlex Towers

CityPlex Towers is a three-tower high-rise complex in south Tulsa, Oklahoma, consisting of 20-, 30-, and 60-story buildings totaling 2.2 million square feet of space.[1][2] The central tower stands at 648 feet (197.5 meters), ranking as the second-tallest building in Tulsa and featuring distinctive golden pyramidal roofs atop dramatic, futuristic-modern architecture.[2] Originally developed by Oral Roberts University from 1977 to 1981 as the City of Faith Medical and Research Center, the facility was constructed at a cost of $250 million as a faith-based hospital and medical school intended to blend conventional treatment with prayer, following televangelist Oral Roberts' reported vision from God.[1] Despite opening with 777 beds in 1981 as the world's largest poured-in-place concrete structure, the center incurred annual losses exceeding $10 million due to operational inefficiencies, regulatory hurdles for Medicare reimbursement, and insufficient patient volume, leading to its closure to patients in 1989 and contributing to ORU's broader financial distress.[1] Repurposed in 1991 into office space under the CityPlex Towers name, the complex underwent management changes, including oversight by Trinity Commercial Properties from 2006, and shifted toward mixed-use tenancy with a focus on medical offices, achieving approximately 70% occupancy by the late 2000s and earning recognition as an outstanding building in industry awards.[1][3] Today, it serves as Oklahoma's largest office and medical campus, equipped with modern amenities such as a remodeled fitness center, conference facilities, and robust security systems, situated adjacent to Oral Roberts University and upscale retail areas.[4][5]

Origins and Vision

Founding Concept and Oral Roberts' Role

The City of Faith Medical and Research Center, later repurposed as CityPlex Towers, originated from evangelist Oral Roberts' vision to create a pioneering facility integrating conventional medical treatment with charismatic Christian faith healing practices. Roberts announced the project on September 7, 1977, describing it as a divine mandate to build a comprehensive complex on the campus of Oral Roberts University in Tulsa, Oklahoma, with an initial estimated cost of $100 million, later revised to $250 million.[6][7] The concept emphasized a holistic approach where physicians, researchers, and ministers would collaborate to treat patients, focusing on conditions such as heart disease, cancer, and geriatrics, while serving as a teaching hospital affiliated with ORU's proposed medical school.[6] The architectural core of the project comprised three towers: a 60-story clinic and diagnostic center, a 30-story hospital designed for 777 beds (symbolizing biblical completeness through the number seven, which Roberts deemed spiritually significant), and a 20-story research facility.[6][8] Groundbreaking occurred on January 24, 1978, following regulatory approvals despite initial skepticism from state health planners regarding market feasibility in Tulsa.[7] The design incorporated symbolic elements, such as 60-foot sculptured hands and a tree-lined stream with a fountain, intended to evoke prayer and renewal, aiming to attract international patients seeking combined medical and spiritual intervention.[6] Oral Roberts played a central role as the project's visionary and chief proponent, claiming the foundational inspiration came during a February 1977 prayer retreat in the California desert, shortly after the deaths of his daughter and son-in-law, where he said God instructed him to outline the entire complex in 30-40 minutes on legal pads.[6][9] Through his television program Oral Roberts and You, he detailed the vision in broadcasts, such as one on December 3, 1977, and mobilized fundraising by soliciting donations in increments tied to the number seven (e.g., $7, $77, $777), securing $27 million toward initial phases by early 1978 without incurring debt.[10][6] Roberts personally lobbied authorities for approvals and later cited additional visions, including one in 1980 of a 900-foot figure of Jesus affirming completion, to sustain donor momentum amid construction delays.[9]

Planning and Divine Claims

In September 1977, Oral Roberts, founder of Oral Roberts University, publicly announced plans for the City of Faith Medical and Research Center, a $100 million complex in Tulsa, Oklahoma, intended to merge conventional medicine with prayer and faith healing practices.[11] Roberts described the project as a divine mandate, stating that God had instructed him to construct it as a facility where medical treatment and spiritual intervention would integrate to achieve holistic healing.[12] This revelation, which Roberts claimed occurred prior to the announcement, emphasized a biblically inspired model drawing on the number sevenâ€”symbolizing divine perfection in his theologyâ€”including plans for 777 hospital beds, seven-foot-wide corridors, and a one-seventh-mile prayer walkway.[1] The core of the City of Faith planning centered on two 15-story towers (later known as CityPlex Towers), designed for administrative, research, and outpatient functions, alongside envisioned taller structures for inpatient care that were ultimately not realized due to financial constraints.[1] Roberts attributed the project's inception to direct communication from God, positioning it as an extension of his "seed-faith" theology, where donations from followers would yield supernatural returns, including breakthroughs in treating incurable diseases through combined medical and prayer modalities.[13] Groundbreaking occurred on January 24, 1978, coinciding with Roberts' 60th birthday, marking the formal start of construction amid local medical community opposition over potential redundancy with existing Tulsa hospitals.[6] Roberts reinforced these divine claims during planning challenges with additional visions; in 1980, he reported seeing a 900-foot-tall figure of Jesus Christ, who urged him to complete the project despite fundraising difficulties and regulatory hurdles.[9] These revelations were disseminated through Roberts' television broadcasts and university publications to solicit donations, framing the endeavor as a prophetic fulfillment rather than a conventional development initiative.[13] While Roberts presented the plans as empirically grounded in emerging holistic health trends, critics within medical and religious circles questioned the verifiability of the divine directives, noting their alignment with his prosperity gospel emphasis on tangible miracles tied to financial giving.[12]

Construction and Development

Site Selection and Architectural Design

The site for the CityPlex Towers complex, initially developed as the City of Faith Medical and Research Center, was selected immediately south of the main Oral Roberts University campus along 81st Street in south Tulsa, Oklahoma.[14] This positioning enabled direct integration with the university's existing infrastructure and student body, supporting Oral Roberts' goal of combining medical education, research, and charismatic Christian ministry.[15] Construction faced legal challenges from competing hospitals citing oversupply and low occupancy risks in the local market, but a district court judge approved proceeding on May 25, 1978.[16] Architect Frank William Wallace, responsible for multiple Oral Roberts University structures, designed the towers in a futuristic modern style characterized by sleek, vertical forms and reflective golden exteriors.[17] The ensemble comprises a 60-story central tower reaching 648 feet, flanked by a 30-story western tower and a 20-story eastern tower, encompassing approximately 2.2 million square feet across the three buildings.[2] Wallace's layout included over five miles of interior walls and 8,000 doors to accommodate extensive medical and office spaces.[1] Roberts influenced the design with numerological symbolism drawn from the biblical number seven, viewed as denoting divine perfection, incorporating features such as seven-foot-wide corridors and a one-seventh-mile interior walking track.[8] The planned capacity included 777 hospital beds, aligning with this motif to symbolize faith-integrated healing.[1] These elements underscored the project's intent to merge conventional healthcare with prayer-based practices, though the ambitious scale strained feasibility from inception.[18]

Building Phases and Completion

Construction of the City of Faith Medical and Research Center, later known as CityPlex Towers, commenced on April 26, 1978, shortly after receiving approval from the Oklahoma Health Planning Commission.[7] The project involved erecting three interconnected towersâ€”a 60-story outpatient clinic as the central structure, flanked by a 30-story hospital tower and a 20-story research centerâ€”designed to integrate medical care, research, and faith-based healing on a 60-acre site adjacent to Oral Roberts University.[9] The build progressed rapidly over three years, with up to five concurrent construction stages employing specialized crews that operated year-round in varying weather conditions to meet the ambitious timeline.[19] This parallel approach minimized delays despite logistical challenges, including local opposition to the scale of the development and regulatory hurdles related to hospital bed approvals.[20] Funding, totaling approximately $120 million, derived primarily from donations solicited through Oral Roberts' ministry appeals.[21] By October 1981, the complex neared full operational readiness, with the towers standing as Tulsa's tallest structures at the timeâ€”the central tower reaching 648 feet.[9] Dedication ceremonies occurred on November 1, 1981, marking the official completion and opening of the facility, which was intended to accommodate 777 beds, research labs, and outpatient services under a unified faith-medical model.[21][9]

Operational History as City of Faith

Early Challenges and Expansion Attempts

The City of Faith Medical and Research Center encountered significant operational difficulties shortly after its dedication on October 25, 1981, primarily stemming from insufficient patient occupancy in a Tulsa market already burdened by excess hospital capacity. Local healthcare authorities, including the Tulsa Area Hospital Council, had contested the project's certificate of need, arguing that the region had approximately 1,200 surplus beds, which limited the facility's ability to draw patients and contributed to chronic underutilization from inception.[28][29] By late 1982, after one year of operations, the complex had failed to activate its planned 777 beds, operating at a fraction of capacity amid serious financial strains that required substantial subsidies from the Oral Roberts Evangelistic Association to sustain basic functions. Officials acknowledged disappointment over the shortfall but projected confidence in ramping up admissions through enhanced promotion of its hybrid model combining conventional medicine with prayer and faith healing, though early revenue projections fell short of the $120 million annual requirement for the integrated university-hospital-evangelistic operations.[29][29] Expansion efforts in the initial years focused on incrementally increasing bed availability and broadening service lines, including outpatient clinics and research initiatives tied to the adjacent Oral Roberts University School of Medicine, with the goal of achieving full operational scale by attracting faith-oriented patients from across the U.S. However, these initiatives yielded limited results, as patient revenues declined further by 1984 despite an infusion of nearly $50 million in donor funds, leading to mounting debts from unoccupied rooms and underscoring the challenges of the unorthodox faith-integrated approach in a competitive, reimbursement-driven healthcare landscape.[30][28][13] Persistent fundraising campaigns, often linked to Oral Roberts' reported divine visions urging persistence, represented another vector of expansion attempts, aiming to finance infrastructure upgrades and marketing to boost enrollment in the medical school and hospital alike, yet these met with skepticism from critics who highlighted the facility's divergence from standard medical accreditation norms and its reliance on charismatic appeals over empirical demand signals.[30][13]

Financial Decline and Closure

Revenue Shortfalls and Debt Accumulation

The City of Faith Medical and Research Center, operational from October 1981, experienced persistent revenue shortfalls primarily due to chronically low patient occupancy rates, which failed to cover operational expenses exceeding $1 million per month by mid-1984.[28] Despite an initial influx of donations totaling approximately $150 million for construction, the facility struggled to attract sufficient medical cases, with officials reporting disappointment over unmet patient volume targets as early as October 1982.[29][28] Contributing factors included competition from established Tulsa hospitals and skepticism toward the center's integration of faith healing with conventional medicine, resulting in underutilized beds across its 777-bed capacity in the three towers.[13] Donation revenues, which peaked at $88 million in 1980 during construction fundraising, began declining sharply in the early 1980s, exacerbating the shortfall as operational costs for the $250 million complexâ€” including staff salaries, maintenance, and debt service on bondsâ€”outpaced income from patient fees and contributions.[31][31] By 1987, Oral Roberts publicly appealed for $8 million from supporters, citing divine instruction and warning that failure to raise the funds would result in God "calling him home," amid broader ministry financial pressures tied to the center's deficits.[32] This plea highlighted the dependency on irregular charitable inflows, which proved insufficient to offset the facility's high fixed costs, leading to cumulative losses that drained resources from affiliated Oral Roberts University operations.[30] Debt accumulation intensified through the mid-to-late 1980s, culminating in $25 million in liabilities by September 1989, primarily from unpaid operational shortfalls and reduced donor support rather than initial construction loans.[33][34] The center's failure to achieve financial self-sufficiencyâ€”despite attempts at expansion and cost-cuttingâ€”stemmed from overambitious scale and optimistic projections of faith-based patient demand that did not materialize, forcing Roberts to announce closure of the hospital and medical donor-dependent ventures in healthcare.[30]

Shutdown and Asset Liquidation

On September 13, 1989, Oral Roberts announced the immediate closure of the City of Faith Medical and Research Center, including its hospital and medical school, attributing the decision to a mounting financial crisis that left the Oral Roberts Ministries facing a $25 million deficit it could not sustain.[35][36] Roberts stated that ongoing operational losses, exacerbated by insufficient patient volume and revenue shortfalls, rendered continued functioning impossible without further unsustainable debt.[37] The announcement included plans to sell personal assets, such as Roberts' home, to help offset ministry-wide obligations, though the core medical operations halted by late 1989, resulting in over 90% vacancy across the complex.[36][38] Asset liquidation efforts focused on disposing of the underutilized CityPlex Towers and surrounding facilities to recover value from the $150 million investment.[39] In September 1992, the property was offered at public auction to attract buyers for repurposing, but bids failed to materialize amid market disinterest in the oversized, specialized medical infrastructure.[39][38] By June 1993, after 20 months of unsuccessful marketing, Oral Roberts University withdrew the complex from sale, citing inadequate offers that undervalued the assets relative to maintenance costs and potential liabilities.[40] This delay prolonged financial strain, as holding costs for the vacant towersâ€”estimated in the millions annuallyâ€”continued to burden the university's budget without generating income.[40]

Ownership Transitions and Repurposing

Sale to Private Entities

Following the closure of the City of Faith Medical and Research Center in October 1989, Oral Roberts University (ORU) sought to divest the complex through sale to alleviate ongoing financial burdens from debt exceeding $25 million. In September 1992, ORU announced plans to sell the three-tower facility via sealed bid auction, emphasizing its potential for repurposing as office or medical space amid low occupancy and maintenance costs.[41] However, by June 1993, ORU withdrew the sale offer, citing insufficient bids and strategic decisions to retain the asset for potential revenue generation through leasing.[40] Subsequent efforts in the late 1990s also faltered; a 1996 negotiation collapsed, leading to a 1997 federal lawsuit where ORU prevailed against the prospective buyer over a disputed $1 million deposit, reinforcing ORU's retention of the property.[42] In April 2003, ORU entered talks with investor group CityPlex LLC to sell the towers, with proceeds intended to offset university debts, but the deal did not close, mirroring prior unsuccessful attempts driven by valuation disputes and market conditions.[43] No outright sale to private entities materialized, as confirmed by ORU's 2024 financial statements, which affirm continued university ownership and operational responsibility for CityPlex Towers.[44] Instead, ORU pursued hybrid transitions involving private management partnerships; in 2007, executives Mark Hollingsworth and David Predovic established Trinity Commercial Properties to oversee leasing, renovations, and occupancy improvements, boosting utilization from under 50% to over 65% by 2008 through targeted commercial tenant recruitment.[1] This approach preserved ORU control while leveraging private expertise to convert the site into a revenue-producing office complex, averting full divestiture amid persistent economic challenges for the oversized structure.

Conversion to Mixed-Use Complex

Following the 1989 closure of the City of Faith Medical and Research Center, the CityPlex Towers complex remained largely vacant until 1991, when Oral Roberts University initiated renovations to adapt the structures for commercial office use.[1] This conversion entailed reconfiguring hospital-specific features, such as extensive plumbing and mechanical systems, to support general office tenants while preserving potential for medical-related leasing due to the buildings' original design.[1] The initial major tenant was Cancer Treatment Centers of America, which occupied approximately 400,000 square feet across multiple floors starting in the early 1990s, marking the first significant step toward repurposing the 2.2 million-square-foot complex.[1] Subsequent efforts to stabilize and expand occupancy faced setbacks, including the departure of Commercial Financial Servicesâ€”a tenant that had leased up to 800,000 square feetâ€”amid its 2002 collapse due to accounting irregularities.[1] In 2006, ORU partnered with Trinity Commercial Properties, founded by principals Michael Predovic and Larry Hollingsworth, to manage leasing and revitalization; this included a $1 million lobby renovation completed in 2007, alongside upgrades to roofing, cooling systems, and energy efficiency measures.[1] By late 2008, these initiatives had boosted occupancy to about 70%, with an additional 200,000 square feet leased that year, positioning the site as Oklahoma's largest mixed-use commercial development combining office and medical office spaces, though some upper floors remained unfinished and vacant.[1] The mixed-use orientation capitalized on the towers' scale and south Tulsa location near Oral Roberts University, attracting federal agencies via a 2016 U.S. General Services Administration lease for 86,500 square feet over 20 years, valued at $32 million.[45] Ongoing adaptations emphasized flexible leasing for professional services, with building features like 24/7 security and on-site amenities supporting diverse tenants, though full utilization has been gradual given the property's oversized footprint relative to local demand.[1]

Architecture and Technical Specifications

Tower Structures and Heights

The CityPlex Towers complex consists of three interconnected high-rise buildings constructed on a shared four-story base that spans the entire footprint. This podium structure provides foundational support and connects the towers via internal corridors and skybridges, facilitating unified access and operations. The design, completed between 1979 and 1981, totals approximately 2.2 million square feet of floor space across the ensemble.[4][46] The dominant feature is the central tower, standing at 60 stories and 648 feet (198 meters) in height, which was the first to be completed in 1979 and ranks as the second-tallest building in Tulsa and third in Oklahoma.[2] Flanking it to the west is the 30-story West Tower, rising 348 feet (106 meters), completed in 1981.[47] To the east lies the 20-story East Tower, measuring 248 feet (76 meters), also finished in 1981.[48]

Current Operations and Tenants

Commercial and Medical Usage

The CityPlex Towers complex functions primarily as a mixed-use facility emphasizing medical offices and commercial leasing in south Tulsa, with approximately 2.2 million square feet of space across three towers.[4] The 30-story tower, originally designed for hospital operations, now hosts extensive medical infrastructure, including over 20 surgery suites distributed among three hospitals and numerous outpatient clinics.[51] This setup supports specialized healthcare services, leveraging the building's central location near other acute care facilities like Oklahoma Surgical Hospital and Saint Francis Hospital, both within a short drive.[5] Key medical tenants include Oklahoma Surgical Hospital, which provides advanced surgical treatments and patient care within the towers.[52] Advanced Orthopedics of Oklahoma operates a clinic at 2406 East 81st Street, Suite 290, offering MRI, X-ray, CT scans, and workers' compensation services.[53] Additional providers encompass Behavioral Medicine at Suite 3700 for mental health services, Surgical Associates at Suite 1100 for surgical consultations, and Tulsa Oklahoma Plastic Surgery on the 10th floor, featuring a surgery center and medical spa.[54][55][56] These entities utilize the complex's infrastructure for outpatient procedures, diagnostics, and comprehensive patient support, contributing to its role as a regional medical hub.[57] Commercially, the towers accommodate general office leasing with over 100 available spaces, marketed for businesses seeking contiguous blocks of space at competitive rates.[58] Oral Roberts University, which retains ownership ties, derives revenue from these leases, supporting ongoing maintenance and operations amid efforts to achieve higher occupancy.[59] The facility's amenities, including meeting auditoriums and energy-efficient upgrades, enhance its appeal for corporate tenants alongside medical users.[3]

relying on emotional urgency and promises of spiritual reciprocity to extract funds from a national audience of followers.[34] Empirically, these tactics yielded short-term inflows but masked Legal Disputes and Mismanagement Allegations

In 1995, Oral Roberts University (ORU) initiated legal action in federal court seeking a declaratory judgment regarding a failed agreement to sell CityPlex Towers to Metroplex Properties, after negotiations collapsed due to financing issues.[67] The court ruled in ORU's favor on January 13, 1997, affirming that no binding contract existed and allowing ORU to retain a $1 million earnest money deposit from Metroplex.[68] Metroplex subsequently countersued ORU in state court for negligence, tortious interference with contract, and fraud, alleging ORU misrepresented the property's condition and interfered with Metroplex's financing efforts.[42] The Oklahoma Court of Civil Appeals upheld the dismissal of Metroplex's claims in 1998, finding the petition failed to state viable causes of action under Oklahoma law.[69] During Richard Roberts' presidency of ORU from 1984 to 2007, a 2007 lawsuit filed by three former professors alleged systemic mismanagement of university assets, including specific misuse of CityPlex Towers resources.[70] The suit claimed Roberts authorized underage students to remove furnishings from CityPlex Towers for use in off-campus private apartments, violating university policies and contributing to broader financial irregularities such as unauthorized personal expenditures charged to ORU accounts.[71] These allegations formed part of wider accusations of lavish spending on items like luxury trips and home improvements, reportedly totaling hundreds of thousands of dollars, amid ORU's ongoing debt burdens from projects like CityPlex.[72] ORU settled the professors' wrongful termination claims in 2008 without admitting liability, following Richard Roberts' resignation amid the scandal.[73] Critics, including the suing professors, attributed CityPlex Towers' underutilization and ORU's repeated sale attempts to initial overambitious development under Oral Roberts' vision, which strained finances through heavy reliance on unfulfilled donor pledges for the complex's construction in the 1970s and 1980s.[1] No criminal charges resulted from these allegations, and ORU maintained that the claims stemmed from internal political disputes rather than proven malfeasance.[74] Subsequent federal scrutiny in 2019 led ORU to pay $303,502 to resolve unrelated False Claims Act violations involving improper student aid incentives, though this did not directly implicate CityPlex operations.[75]

Legacy and Cultural Impact

Architectural and Urban Significance

The CityPlex Towers complex, comprising three high-rise structures completed between 1979 and 1981, exemplifies futuristic modern architecture designed by Tulsa architect Frank William Wallace.[17] The central tower stands at 60 stories and 648 feet (197.5 meters), making it the second-tallest building in Oklahoma after the BOK Tower.[2] Across the three towers, the complex totals 110 floors and 2.2 million square feet, recognized at the time as the world's largest poured-in-place concrete structure.[1] Engineering features include foundations on piers 50 feet deep and 10 feet wide, each requiring 20 truckloads of cement and 17,000 pounds of steel, enabling the towers to sway less than 2 inches in 100-mile-per-hour winds.[1] The design incorporates biblical symbolism, with elements structured around the number sevenâ€”deemed spiritually significant by founder Oral Robertsâ€”reflecting a fusion of modernist engineering and charismatic Christian vision.[8] Additional attributes encompass over five miles of interior walls, 8,000 doors, and 44 elevators, underscoring the scale of construction that cost $250 million.[1] In Tulsa's urban landscape, the golden-hued towers serve as a prominent landmark in the south side, distinct from the downtown skyline and visible from afar due to their height and reflective metal plating.[1] Positioned adjacent to Oral Roberts University at 81st Street and Lewis Avenue, they contribute to the area's identity as a hub of religious and educational infrastructure, though their isolation in a suburban context highlights the challenges of integrating megascale projects into regional development.[8] Originally envisioned as a self-contained "City of Faith" medical center, the towers' repurposing into office and medical space demonstrates adaptive reuse amid shifting economic demands, influencing local commercial leasing patterns.[1]

The CityPlex Towers exemplify the hazards of religious megaprojects that prioritize visionary scale over empirical financial modeling, as Oral Roberts' City of Faith complex amassed $150 million in institutional insolvency rather than sustained growth.[77] Roberts' 1987 public appeal for $8 million to avert personal divine judgment raised the funds temporarily but did not prevent the complex's demise two years later, highlighting how charismatic appeals can inflate short-term capital while masking structural deficits like overleveraged debt and inadequate revenue diversification.[78][79] Analyses of similar projects, such as those by contemporaries like Jim Bakker's PTL Club, indicate that 80-90% of large-scale religious ventures post-1970s experienced comparable fiscal collapses due missionsâ€”Oral Roberts University accrued $52 million in debt by 2007 partly traceable to City of Faith overhangâ€”and eroding public trust in religious organizations when prophetic guarantees prove empirically void.[81][13] Effective alternatives emphasize scalable, low-overhead models grounded in verifiable outcomes, as evidenced by enduring ministries that prioritize operational transparency over monumental symbolism, thereby mitigating the causal chain from hype-driven funding to reputational and financial ruin.[82]
