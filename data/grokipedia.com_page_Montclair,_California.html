Montclair, California

Montclair is an incorporated city in the Pomona Valley region of southwestern San Bernardino County, California, approximately 35 miles east of downtown Los Angeles.[1] Incorporated on April 25, 1956, the city spans about 4.6 square miles and had a population of 37,650 as of July 2024.[1][2] Positioned at the convergence of Los Angeles, Orange, and San Bernardino counties, Montclair functions as a commercial and transit hub, featuring the expansive Montclair Place shopping center and the Montclair Transcenter, which connects regional rail and bus services.[1] The area was originally inhabited by the Serrano people, with European settlement tied to nearby Spanish missions in the late 18th century.[3]

History

Early Settlement and Development

The area now comprising Montclair was originally inhabited by the Serrano people, indigenous to the San Bernardino Mountains and surrounding valleys, who established villages along creeks lined with sycamore trees for access to water and resources.[1][3] These communities relied on hunting, gathering, and seasonal migration patterns adapted to the local semi-arid environment prior to European contact.[1] Following Spanish colonization efforts, including the establishment of Mission San Gabriel ArcÃ¡ngel in 1771, the region fell under Mexican control after 1821, with land grants facilitating ranching operations. In 1837, the Mexican government awarded Rancho San JosÃ©, encompassing much of the present-day Montclair area, to Ygnacio Palomares, a soldier who utilized the fertile valley for cattle grazing and agriculture.[1] After the Mexican-American War concluded in 1848 and California statehood in 1850, the rancho was subdivided among heirs and American settlers, transitioning to private ownership amid disputes resolved through U.S. land commission proceedings.[1] By the late 19th century, the landscape served primarily as open grazing land bridging early settlements like Pomona, incorporated in 1888, and Ontario in 1891, with limited permanent habitation beyond scattered ranchos. The arrival of the Santa Fe Railroad spurred initial modern development, including a short-lived settlement named Narod around the 1880s, honoring a railroad official, though it comprised only about 15 families.[4] In 1897, the Township of Marquette emerged as the first formal subdivision, promoted by Edward Fraser, marking the onset of organized land sales. Agricultural pursuits intensified in the early 1900s, exemplified by the Reeder Citrus Ranch established for orange cultivation, while developer Emil Firth acquired and surveyed approximately 1,000 acres by 1907, dubbing it the Monte Vista tract and dividing it into 10- to 40-acre lots to attract small-scale farmers.[5] This era laid the groundwork for citrus-dominated orchards that defined the area's economy through the mid-20th century.[6]

Incorporation and Mid-20th Century Growth

The Monte Vista Land Tract, fearing annexation by neighboring cities such as Ontario, Pomona, or Upland, incorporated as the City of Monte Vista on April 25, 1956, encompassing 4.2 square miles and a population of 8,008.[1][7] The incorporation followed a resident vote on April 10, 1956, establishing it as a general-law city amid post-World War II suburban pressures.[7] On April 8, 1958, voters approved renaming the city Montclair to resolve U.S. Postal Service restrictions stemming from name conflicts with another California community.[3] Postwar demand for affordable housing fueled rapid residential expansion in the 1950s and 1960s, converting citrus orchardsâ€”dominant in the area's first half of the 20th centuryâ€”into suburban neighborhoods.[3][1] This development aligned with broader Southern California trends, including federal housing initiatives and highway expansions that enhanced accessibility to Los Angeles employment centers.[6] Population surged from 13,546 in 1960 to 22,546 in 1970, a 66.4 percent increase, underscoring the tract's transformation into a bedroom community.[8] Commercial growth complemented residential trends, highlighted by the August 5, 1968, opening of Montclair Plaza, a $50 million single-level shopping center with 69 stores that anchored regional retail.[9] This facility, situated at the city's core, stimulated local economic activity and further population influx by providing convenient access to goods and services.[10]

Recent Developments

In the decades following incorporation, Montclair underwent significant commercial and infrastructural growth. The Montclair Plaza shopping center, construction of which began in 1964, opened on August 3, 1968, and quickly became a key economic driver, contributing 20% of the city's tax revenue in its inaugural year.[1] This development shifted regional commerce eastward from neighboring Pomona and supported ongoing residential expansion amid the broader Pomona Valley boom.[5] Public safety enhancements marked the early 1970s, with the dedication of two new fire stations in February 1970, including the headquarters at Station No. 1, reflecting the city's response to population increases and suburbanization pressures.[11] The plaza itself expanded with a second floor addition in 1985, sustaining its role as a retail hub despite emerging competition from newer centers like Victoria Gardens in the 2000s.[5] Into the 21st century, Montclair has focused on revitalization and mixed-use planning. The plaza underwent remodeling in 2008 and rebranding to Montclair Place in 2015 to adapt to retail shifts, followed by the removal of its historic neon sign in August 2019 as part of broader site updates.[5] Recent initiatives include the Montclair Place District Specific Plan, adopted to guide 104 acres of mixed-use redevelopment through 2040, alongside industrial projects such as an eight-building business park at Mission Boulevard and Ramona Avenue (draft environmental review in 2022) and warehouse construction at 5006-5010 Mission Boulevard.[12] These efforts align with the 2020 General Plan update, emphasizing sustainable growth in residential, commercial, and industrial sectors.[12]

Geography

Location and Physical Features


Montclair is located in southwestern San Bernardino County, California, at the westernmost edge of the county, bordering the Inland Empire, Los Angeles County, and Orange County regions.[1] The city occupies a position in the Pomona Valley, approximately 35 miles east of downtown Los Angeles, with primary access via Interstate 10.[13] It shares borders with Pomona to the west, Claremont and Upland to the north, Ontario to the east, and Chino to the south.[14]
The city encompasses 5.54 square miles of land area, with no measurable water bodies, reflecting its fully urbanized character.[15] Montclair's average elevation stands at 1,066 feet (325 meters) above sea level.[16] Physically, the terrain consists of flat to gently sloping alluvial plains typical of the Pomona Valley, an inland basin formed by sedimentary deposits from surrounding ranges.[17] To the north rise the San Gabriel Mountains, while the Chino Hills bound the area to the south, contributing to a sheltered valley geography that influences local microclimates and urban development patterns.[17]

Government and Politics

Municipal Government Structure

Montclair operates as a general law city under a council-manager form of government, with the City Council serving as the elected legislative body responsible for policy-making, ordinance adoption, budgeting, and oversight of municipal operations.[47][48] The council consists of five members: a mayor and four council members, all elected at-large by district voters to staggered four-year terms, with elections held in even-numbered years.[47] The mayor, elected separately from the council members, presides over meetings and represents the city in official capacities, while the council appoints a mayor pro tempore from among its members to act in the mayor's absence.[47] Council members are not compensated with salaries but receive a stipend for attendance at meetings and events.[47] The city manager, appointed by and serving at the pleasure of the council, functions as the chief executive officer, managing daily administration, implementing council policies, and supervising key departments including administrative services and human resources, city clerk, community development, economic development, finance, fire services, police, public works, and recreation.[48][49] Edward C. Starr has held the position since July 1, 2010, following prior roles within the city since 1984.[48] The council also appoints members to advisory bodies such as the Planning Commission and Community Activities Commission, and jointly oversees affiliated entities including the Successor Agency to the former redevelopment agency, Housing Authority, Housing Corporation, and Community Foundation, with regular meetings conducted for these groups.[47] Council committees and liaison assignments are updated biennially to address specific issues like finance, public safety, and infrastructure.[47]

Montclair operates under a council-manager form of government with non-partisan elections for its mayor and four at-large city council seats, each serving staggered four-year terms.[50] Elections coincide with even-numbered year general elections administered by the San Bernardino County Registrar of Voters, emphasizing local issues such as development, public safety, and infrastructure rather than national party platforms.[51] Voter turnout in municipal races varies, aligning with broader county trends where participation in local contests often trails presidential-year levels; Policy Debates and Controversies

In 2024, Montclair faced significant controversy over sexual harassment allegations against City Councilmember Ben Lopez, stemming from lawsuits filed by two city employees who claimed Lopez created a hostile work environment through inappropriate comments and advances. The city incurred over $700,000 in legal fees defending against the suits, which were settled out of court, prompting Montclair to countersue Lopez for reimbursement of those costs, alleging his actions necessitated the expenditures. Lopez, who was elected in 2020, denied the allegations, characterizing them as politically motivated by opponents seeking to remove him from office.[58][59] Lopez's tenure has also drawn criticism for his expressed views on social issues, particularly during his 2020 campaign, when local advocacy groups highlighted his opposition to LGBTQ rights, including past support for measures restricting same-sex marriage and transgender participation in sports aligned with biological sex. Opponents argued these positions were incompatible with Montclair's diverse community, while Lopez maintained they reflected traditional values held by many residents. The debate intensified electoral scrutiny, with calls from progressive organizations to reject his candidacy on grounds of potential discrimination, though he secured the seat amid a field of candidates emphasizing fiscal conservatism.[60] Public access to city meetings sparked debate in March 2024, when a City Council session on a proposed Gaza ceasefire resolution was disrupted by Zoom bombers delivering racist and anti-Semitic remarks during virtual public comments, leading the council to suspend remote oral participation indefinitely to prevent further abuse. Critics, including free speech advocates, contended this policy curtailed democratic engagement in a city with limited in-person attendance options, while officials defended it as necessary for maintaining order and safety post-pandemic. The incident underscored tensions over balancing virtual inclusivity with content moderation in local governance.[61][62] Earlier policy friction emerged in 2018, when the council, responding to a spike in pedestrian fatalitiesâ€”eight deaths in two yearsâ€”enacted an ordinance increasing fines for jaywalking from $100 to $197, aiming to enforce safer crossing habits amid heavy vehicle traffic on arterials like Holt Boulevard. Transportation safety experts praised the measure for addressing causal factors in collisions, where data showed 40% of local pedestrian incidents involved mid-block crossings, but pedestrian rights groups decried it as victim-blaming that ignored inadequate infrastructure like missing crosswalks and sidewalks. The policy highlighted ongoing debates over personal responsibility versus municipal investment in walkability in a car-dependent suburb.[63]

Education

School District Overview

The public education system in Montclair, California, is divided between two districts: the Ontario-Montclair School District (OMSD) for kindergarten through eighth grade and the Chaffey Joint Union High School District (CJUHSD) for ninth through twelfth grade.[64][65] OMSD, established as one of California's larger elementary districts, operates 33 schoolsâ€”including 22 elementary, six middle, and four K-8 schoolsâ€”across a 26-square-mile area encompassing all of Montclair, most of Ontario, and portions of Upland and unincorporated San Bernardino County.[66][67] OMSD enrolls approximately 17,944 to 18,500 students, with a student-teacher ratio of 21:1, reflecting a predominantly Hispanic student body (over 80% in many metrics) and near-total minority enrollment of 100%, alongside 84% economically disadvantaged students.[68][69][70] The district emphasizes programs for English learners and socioeconomically disadvantaged subgroups, operating under state oversight with a focus on compliance with federal Title I requirements.[66] Montclair's high school students attend Montclair High School within CJUHSD, which serves communities including Ontario, Montclair, Rancho Cucamonga, and parts of adjacent cities, totaling over 25,000 students district-wide across 11 comprehensive high schools.[65][71] Montclair High enrolls about 2,605 students, with the district governed by an elected board prioritizing career technical education and college preparatory pathways amid California's high school funding framework.[72][73] This bifurcated structure aligns with regional patterns in the Inland Empire, where elementary districts often separate from unified high school districts to manage localized growth and demographics.[64]

Academic Performance Metrics

The Ontario-Montclair School District (OMSD), which serves K-8 students in Montclair, exhibits proficiency rates on the California Assessment of Student Performance and Progress (CAASPP) that fall below state averages. For the 2022-23 school year, 38% of elementary students met or exceeded standards in English language arts, compared to the California average of 47%, while 27% achieved proficiency in mathematics against a statewide figure of 34%. Middle school performance mirrors this trend, with 37% proficient in English language arts. District-wide aggregates show 40% proficiency in reading/language arts and 30% in mathematics, reflecting challenges in core subjects amid a student population where over 80% qualify for free or reduced-price meals.[69][74] Montclair High School, part of the Chaffey Joint Union High School District and serving the city's secondary students, demonstrates stronger outcomes in certain metrics. On the 2022-23 CAASPP assessments, 63% of students met or exceeded standards in grade 11 English language arts, surpassing the state average of 55%. Mathematics proficiency remains lower, aligning with statewide trends for high schools where integrated math courses yield around 30% proficiency overall. The school's four-year adjusted cohort graduation rate stands at 87% to 91%, placing it in the top 50% of California high schools and exceeding the state average of 87%. College readiness indicators include average SAT scores of 1090 and ACT scores of 20, with participation in Advanced Placement courses available but completion rates varying by subject.[75][76][77][78]

The Ontario-Montclair School District (OMSD), which serves K-8 students in Montclair, contends with persistently low academic performance, as measured by the California Assessment of Student Performance and Progress (CAASPP). District-wide results on the California School Dashboard indicate English language arts proficiency significantly below state standards, with performance levels rated yellow and distances from standard exceeding 25 points in multiple reporting cycles. Mathematics outcomes similarly underperform, with chronic gaps in meeting grade-level expectations that hinder long-term student readiness.[79][80] A substantial English learner (EL) populationâ€”comprising a majority of students in many OMSD schoolsâ€”intensifies these challenges, as ELs face barriers in language acquisition and alignment with rigorous state academic standards. Law Enforcement and Community Programs

The Montclair Police Department (MPD) functions as the primary full-service law enforcement agency for the city, employing approximately 60 sworn officers dedicated to uniform patrol, crime prevention, and response operations.[86] Founded in 1956 with an initial force of six officers, MPD has developed into a community-oriented entity focused on disrupting criminal activity, resolving community concerns, and building resident partnerships through proactive initiatives.[87] [86] To strengthen ties with residents and enhance safety, MPD administers targeted programs emphasizing collaboration and volunteer involvement. The Neighborhood Watch program empowers community-led groups to monitor neighborhoods and report suspicious behavior, with the objective of deterring crime and elevating living standards; department personnel support these efforts by providing informational speakers but do not establish the groups themselves.[88] School Resource Officers partner with the Chaffey Joint Union High School District and Ontario-Montclair School District to cultivate secure school settings, delivering conflict resolution, law enforcement education in classrooms, rapid incident response, and relationship-building activities with students during regular campus hours and patrols.[88] The Chaplain Program, launched in January 1997, enlists ordained volunteers meeting criteria such as ecclesiastical endorsement, active local ministry involvement, clean criminal records, and valid California driver's licenses to furnish spiritual and emotional aid amid crises; responsibilities encompass officer ride-alongs, death notifications to families, and at least 18 hours of monthly counseling, with three active chaplains currently serving.[88] Reserve Police Officers augment regular staffing as unpaid volunteers committing at least 25 hours monthlyâ€”for which they receive $300 stipendâ€”handling duties like traffic enforcement, dispatch assistance, and evidence preservation following completion of mandatory Peace Officer Standards and Training (POST) certification; eligibility requires a high school diploma, age 18 or older, and absence of disqualifying criminal history.[88] Volunteers in Policing roles further extend departmental capacity by recruiting civilians for non-sworn support tasks, with ongoing efforts to expand participation in these auxiliary functions.[86]

Transportation

Roadways and Highways

Interstate 10 (I-10), a principal east-west freeway, traverses Montclair, serving as a critical artery for commuter traffic and freight movement between the Los Angeles Basin ports and eastern destinations across the nation.[89] The freeway marks the boundary at the Los Angeles-San Bernardino County line within the city, with ongoing infrastructure enhancements under the I-10 Corridor Project, which added two express lanes in each direction starting from Montclair eastward to Redlands, with initial construction phases targeting the segment to the I-15 interchange in Ontario beginning in 2018-2019.[89][90] Prior to I-10's development in the 1950s, Holt Boulevard and Mission Boulevard functioned as primary highways through the area, accommodating regional travel along what is now part of the historic U.S. Route 66 alignment.[6] Today, these and other arterials support local circulation, with the city's Traffic Program maintaining signals, striping, and signage to manage flow.[91] Designated truck routes include Arrow Highway from the western city limits to Benson Avenue, Palo Verde Street between Monte Vista Avenue and Central Avenue, and Holt Boulevard from Mills Avenue onward, directing heavy vehicles away from residential zones.[92] In July 2025, San Bernardino County finalized a comprehensive road rehabilitation initiative in Montclair and adjacent Chino, encompassing repaving and reconstruction of select streets, installation of ADA-compliant ramps, and upgrades to sidewalks, curbs, and gutters to enhance safety and accessibility.[93] The Central Avenue Bridge over I-10 underwent replacement to address structural deficiencies, improving overall infrastructure resilience as identified in state evaluations.[94]

Public Transit and Accessibility

Montclair's public transit infrastructure centers on the Montclair Transcenter, located at 5091 Richton Street, which serves as the city's primary intermodal hub for regional and local travel.[95] This facility accommodates Metrolink commuter rail on the San Bernardino Line, providing weekday service to downtown Los Angeles and San Bernardino with connections to Amtrak, as well as multiple bus operators including Omnitrans, Foothill Transit, and Riverside Transit Agency.[96] The Transcenter offers 1,600 free parking spaces to facilitate park-and-ride access.[96] Bus services are extensive, with Omnitrans operating several fixed routes through Montclair, such as Route 84 linking Chino, Ontario, Rancho Cucamonga, and Upland; Route 85 connecting Chino Transit Center to Chaffey College via Montclair; Route 88 serving Chino Hills along Ramona Avenue; Route 66 from Fontana via Foothill Boulevard; and express Route 290 to San Bernardino and Colton.[97][98][99][100][101] Foothill Transit provides additional connectivity, including the Silver Streak rapid bus from Montclair to El Monte and Los Angeles, and Line 699 express service from the Transcenter to downtown Los Angeles.[102][103] Intercity options include Greyhound buses departing from the Transcenter.[95] Accessibility features align with federal Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) requirements across fixed-route services. Metrolink stations, including Montclair, provide ramps, elevators, and accessible platforms for passengers with mobility impairments.[96] Omnitrans and Foothill Transit buses are equipped with low-floor designs, wheelchair lifts, and priority seating, while the agencies offer complementary paratransit: OmniAccess provides curb-to-curb ADA service within 3/4 mile of fixed routes for eligible riders unable to use standard buses.[104] Foothill Transit maintains similar ADA-compliant vehicles and paratransit eligibility processes.[105] Riders must register for paratransit and schedule trips in advance, with service hours matching fixed-route operations.[104] The Transcenter itself includes accessible pathways and signage to support independent navigation.[95] A planned extension of the Foothill Gold Line light rail to the Transcenter, part of Phase 2B, is anticipated to enhance regional rail accessibility upon completion, potentially by late 2025, integrating with existing bus and Metrolink services.[106]

Prominent Individuals

Nick Rimando (born June 17, 1979) is a retired professional soccer goalkeeper born in Montclair, California, who attended Montclair High School before playing college soccer at UCLA.[107][108] He appeared in 473 Major League Soccer matches across 18 seasons, primarily with D.C. United and Real Salt Lake, winning MLS Cups in 1999 and 2009, and earned 20 caps for the U.S. national team, including at the 2002 FIFA World Cup. Rimando was inducted into the National Soccer Hall of Fame in 2025.[109] Danny Califf (born March 17, 1980) is a former professional soccer defender born in Montclair, California.[110][111] He played 18 MLS seasons with teams including the Philadelphia Union and represented the U.S. national team 23 times, scoring once, while also competing in Europe with clubs like PSV Eindhoven.[112] Vince Velasquez (born June 7, 1992) is a Major League Baseball pitcher born in Montclair, California, who attended Garey High School in nearby Pomona.[113][114] Drafted by the Houston Astros in 2010, he has pitched for multiple teams including the Philadelphia Phillies, recording a career 4.54 ERA over nine seasons through 2023.[115] Addison Reed (born December 27, 1988) is a former MLB relief pitcher born in Montclair, California, who played college baseball at San Diego State University.[116][117] Selected by the Chicago White Sox in 2010, he appeared in 499 games across teams like the New York Mets, saving 40 games in 2014.[118] Bryan Barberena (born May 3, 1989) is a professional mixed martial artist born in Montclair, California, competing in the UFC welterweight division with a record of 18-12 as of 2024.[119] He trains in Arizona and has notable wins including against UFC veterans like Jason Witt. Dee Jay Daniels (born October 17, 1988) is an actor and rapper born in Montclair, California, best known for portraying Michael Hughley on the ABC sitcom The Hughleys from 1998 to 2002.[120][121] He also appeared in films like Sky High (2005).[122]
