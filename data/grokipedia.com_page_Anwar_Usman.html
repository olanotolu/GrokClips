Anwar Usman

Anwar Usman (born 31 December 1956) is an Indonesian jurist and sitting justice of the Constitutional Court of Indonesia, who previously served as its Chief Justice from April 2018 until his removal from that position in November 2023 following findings of serious ethical misconduct.[1][2] Usman's judicial career spanned decades, beginning as a district court judge before advancing to roles including judge at the Jakarta High Court in 2005 and Head of the Personnel Bureau at the Supreme Court.[3] He was appointed to the Constitutional Court prior to his election as Chief Justice by his fellow justices.[4] His tenure as Chief Justice became mired in controversy over his handling of case number 90/PUU-XXI/2023, which reinterpreted the constitutional age minimum for presidential and vice-presidential candidates to permit those who had served as elected regional headsâ€”effectively allowing his nephew by marriage, Gibran Rakabuming Raka (son of then-President Joko Widodo and husband to Usman's daughter), to circumvent the 40-year-old threshold and run for vice president.[5][6] Usman, whose wife is Joko Widodo's sister, failed to recuse himself despite the evident conflict of interest and was found by the Court's Ethics Council to have violated professional oaths, engaged in unbecoming conduct, and improperly influenced the adjudication panel's composition, constituting a gross breach of judicial ethics.[2][7] The panel demoted him from leadership but permitted him to retain his justiceship, barring him from deliberations for three years; this decision drew widespread criticism for perceived leniency amid accusations of enabling political dynasty-building.[5][6] Post-demotion, Usman pursued administrative litigation, securing a partial victory in 2024 from the Jakarta Administrative Court that annulled aspects of his sanction and questioned his successor's appointment, though without restoring his chief role, further highlighting tensions over institutional integrity.[8] As of 2025, he continues as an active justice while the Court's credibility remains scrutinized in light of these events.[9]

Early life and education

Birth and upbringing

Anwar Usman was born on 31 December 1956 in Bima, West Nusa Tenggara province, Indonesia.[10][3] He is the son of Usman A. Rahim and Hj. St. Ramlah, who raised him in a modest family environment.[11] Usman spent his early childhood and formative years in Rasabou Village, located in Bolo Subdistrict, Bima Regency, a rural area characterized by traditional community life and limited urban influences.[12] This upbringing in a close-knit village setting shaped his initial experiences before he later pursued opportunities beyond his hometown.[12]

Formal education and early influences

Anwar Usman commenced his formal education at State Elementary School (SDN) 03 Sila in Bima, graduating in 1969.[1][13] He subsequently attended the State Religious Teacher Training School (PGAN) in Bima, completing the program in 1975, which provided foundational training in religious education and pedagogy.[14] After finishing secondary-level studies, Usman began working as an honorary teacher in Bima, a role that shaped his early professional discipline and commitment to education amid limited resources in his rural upbringing in Desa Rasabou.[13][15] While employed, he demonstrated self-motivation by relocating from his village to pursue higher education, enrolling in the Faculty of Law at Universitas Islam Jakarta and earning a bachelor's degree (S.H.) in 1984.[3][15] This transition from teaching to legal studies was influenced by his religious educational background, as evidenced by his selection of an Islamic-affiliated institution, reflecting a blend of faith-based values and aspirations for professional advancement in a secular field like law.[16] Usman later advanced his qualifications with a master's degree (M.H.) in law from Sekolah Tinggi Ilmu Hukum (STIH) IBLAM Jakarta, graduating in 2001, further underscoring his persistent pursuit of legal expertise alongside practical experience.[17][18] His early rural origins and initial teaching career fostered a pragmatic approach, prioritizing self-reliant education over immediate financial stability, which informed his later judicial trajectory.[15][13]

Pre-judicial career

Teaching and other early professions

Anwar Usman commenced his professional career as a non-permanent (honorary) teacher at SD Kalibaru, an elementary school in Jakarta, immediately after relocating from West Nusa Tenggara to the capital in 1975, following his graduation from a teacher training program.[3][15] This initial role involved instructing primary-level students, marking the onset of his engagement in education amid limited resources as a young migrant.[19] While maintaining his teaching position, Usman pursued higher education, enrolling in the Faculty of Law at Jakarta Islamic University (now Universitas Islam Jakarta) and earning his bachelor's degree (S.H.) in 1984.[1] During this period, he balanced pedagogical duties with academic studies, though specific details on concurrent teaching assignments beyond the elementary level remain undocumented in primary records.[20] In subsequent years, prior to his formal entry into legal practice, Usman transitioned toward legal education and advocacy roles, eventually attaining recognition as a professor (Guru Besar) in law, including an honorary professorship from Universitas Islam Sultan Agung (Unissula) Semarang, reflecting his contributions to legal pedagogy.[21] However, these advanced academic positions emerged later in his career trajectory, post his initial undergraduate teaching phase.[22]

Involvement in acting and media

During his university years at Jakarta Islamic University, Anwar Usman actively participated in theater activities as a member of the Sanggar Aksara troupe, guided by director Ismail Soebarjo.[3] This involvement included performing in stage plays, which he later credited with helping him overcome stage fright and apply life philosophies learned from the experiences.[3] In 1980, Usman received an invitation from Soebarjo to appear in a minor role in the film Perempuan dalam Pasungan (Women in Shackles), which starred Nungki Kusumastuti, Frans Tumbuan, and Rini S. Bono.[3] [12] The film, directed by Soebarjo, earned the Citra Award for Best Film at the Indonesian Film Festival.[3] Usman has described this as a proud but limited foray into cinema, noting that despite opportunities to pursue acting furtherâ€”including competing with figures like Rano Karnoâ€”he prioritized his legal education over a potential entertainment career.[23] [3] No extensive media production or ongoing acting roles are documented from this period, with Usman's theater and film participation appearing confined to his student activities before shifting to academic and legal pursuits.[3]

Judicial career

Service on the Supreme Court

Anwar Usman commenced his tenure at the Supreme Court of Indonesia (Mahkamah Agung) in 1997, serving as an Assistant to a Supreme Court Justice until 2003, where he supported judicial operations and case handling.[15] This role involved administrative assistance in legal proceedings and contributed to his foundational experience in the apex judicial institution.[3] In 2003, Usman advanced to the position of Head of the Personnel Bureau at the Supreme Court, a role he held until 2006, overseeing staffing, recruitment, and human resources management for the judiciary.[15] Concurrently, in 2005, he was appointed as a judge at the Jakarta High Court, balancing adjudicatory duties with administrative leadership at the Supreme Court level.[3] From 2006 to 2011, he directed the Research, Education, and Training Center for Human Resource Development (Litbang Diklat Kumdil) at the Supreme Court, focusing on judicial training programs, legal research, and capacity building for judges and court personnel nationwide.[18] These positions at the Supreme Court emphasized administrative and developmental functions rather than direct adjudication as a Supreme Court justice, equipping Usman with institutional knowledge that later informed his nomination by the Supreme Court Chief Justice for appointment to the Constitutional Court in April 2011.[15]

Appointment and early tenure at the Constitutional Court

Anwar Usman was appointed as a justice to the Indonesian Constitutional Court (Mahkamah Konstitusi, MK) in April 2011 by the Supreme Court, replacing Arsyad Sanusi. This appointment followed his prior service as a judge at the Jakarta High Court since 2005 and as Head of the Personnel Bureau at the Supreme Court.[3] Justices of the MK are appointed for a single five-year term, renewable once, with appointments divided among the Supreme Court, the People's Representative Council (DPR), and the President, each nominating three justices.[24] Usman's initial term spanned from 2011 to 2016, during which he participated in the court's adjudication of constitutional disputes, including those related to electoral laws and governmental authority. In 2015, toward the end of his first term, he was elected as Deputy Chief Justice of the MK.[24] His reappointment for a second term was formalized through Presidential Decree No. 144/P of 2015, with the oath of office administered by President Joko Widodo on April 7, 2016.[25] During his early years on the bench, Usman contributed to the court's role in upholding constitutional amendments post-Reformation era, focusing on judicial review of laws for compliance with the 1945 Constitution. In September 2018, following the end of the prior chief's term, Usman was elected Chief Justice for the 2018-2020 period during a plenary session.[26] This elevation marked a progression in his tenure, emphasizing internal leadership amid the court's growing caseload on democratic processes.[27]

Elevation to Chief Justice

Anwar Usman was elected as Chief Justice of the Constitutional Court of Indonesia on April 2, 2018, succeeding Arief Hidayat whose term had concluded.[28] The election occurred during a plenary session of the court's nine justices, where Usman defeated fellow justice Suhartoyo by a narrow 5-4 majority vote.[26] This internal selection process, mandated by the court's regulations, requires justices to nominate and vote for the leadership positions among themselves, with the chief justice serving a fixed term typically aligned with the court's operational periods.[28] Prior to his elevation, Usman had been serving as Deputy Chief Justice since his election to that role in 2015, following his appointment to the Constitutional Court in 2011 by the Supreme Court of Indonesia.[24] His initial term as Chief Justice was set for 2018â€“2020, reflecting the court's practice of staggered leadership terms to ensure continuity.[28] The vote outcome underscored the competitive nature of the position, with Usman's prior experience as a deputy and his judicial tenure contributing to his support among peers.[26]

Key rulings and judicial philosophy

Notable decisions prior to controversies

During his tenure as Chief Justice beginning in 2022, Anwar Usman presided over the Constitutional Court's dismissal of petitions challenging the 20 percent presidential nomination threshold in Law No. 7 of 2017 on General Elections. In March 2023, the court rejected a petition from Partai Kebangkitan Nusantara, determining that the party lacked legal standing as it had not participated in prior elections and thus could not demonstrate concrete harm from the threshold requirement.[29] Similarly, in September 2023, a petition by the Partai Buruh was dismissed on identical grounds of insufficient legal standing, with Usman announcing the verdict.[30] These rulings upheld the threshold's constitutionality by enforcing strict procedural criteria for judicial review, preventing broader substantive challenges to election structures. In early October 2023, the court under Usman's leadership declared constitutional the Government Regulation in Lieu of Law No. 2 of 2022 on Job Creation, which revised the controversial 2020 omnibus law previously invalidated for flawed legislative processes.[31] The decision affirmed the regulation's alignment with constitutional mandates for economic policy flexibility, despite dissenting opinions from justices who argued procedural irregularities persisted.[31] This outcome facilitated legislative revisions to the Job Creation Law, emphasizing the court's role in balancing economic reform urgency against formal lawmaking standards. Usman also participated in earlier reviews of judicial institutions, including a June 2022 ruling invalidating provisions in the Constitutional Court Law amendments that permitted justices extended terms beyond standard limits, thereby reinforcing term restrictions to safeguard institutional independence.[24] These pre-controversy decisions reflected a consistent emphasis on procedural rigor and institutional integrity in constitutional adjudication.

Approach to constitutional interpretation

Anwar Usman has described the Indonesian Constitution (UUD 1945) as a dynamic instrument serving as a means for social change, requiring interpretation that aligns with both vertical and horizontal dimensions. Vertically, the constitution functions as the supreme norm, establishing a hierarchical framework where lower laws and state actions must conform to its principles, ensuring coherence in governance. Horizontally, it promotes equitable social relations, justice, and adaptation to evolving societal needs, emphasizing substantive values over rigid textualism.[32] This perspective reflects a purposive approach to constitutional interpretation, prioritizing the constitution's transformative purpose to address contemporary challenges, such as democratic participation and public welfare, rather than strict originalism confined to historical intent. Usman has advocated for judges to act as statesmen, making bold decisions oriented toward future generations' interests, which implies flexibility in applying constitutional provisions to unforeseen contexts.[33] In practice, this philosophy manifests in rulings favoring substantive equivalence over literal readings, as seen in interpretations allowing legislative experience to substitute for formal age requirements in eligibility clauses, justified by the need to enable capable leadership while upholding constitutional goals of representation and stability. Critics, however, argue such methods risk judicial overreach, potentially undermining textual clarity for policy-driven outcomes.[34][35]

Controversies and ethical investigations

Conflict of interest in the 2024 vice presidential age limit ruling

On October 16, 2023, Indonesia's Constitutional Court issued a 5-4 ruling in case 90/PUU-XXI/2023, interpreting Article 169(q) of Law No. 7/2017 on General Elections to allow presidential or vice-presidential candidates under the 40-year age minimum to qualify if they had prior experience as elected regional heads, such as mayors or governors.[36][37] This decision directly enabled Gibran Rakabuming Raka, then 36-year-old mayor of Surakarta and eldest son of President Joko Widodo, to register as vice-presidential candidate alongside Prabowo Subianto for the February 2024 election. Anwar Usman, as Chief Justice, chaired the nine-justice panel and authored the majority opinion, voting to grant the petition without recusing himself despite his familial ties to the beneficiary.[38] Usman is married to Rini Siti Marlina, the younger sister of First Lady Iriana Jokowi, making him the uncle by marriage to Gibran Rakabuming Raka.[39] Under the Constitutional Court's ethics code, justices are required to abstain from cases involving close relatives to avoid conflicts of interest, yet Usman participated fully, arguing publicly that his ruling adhered to legal merits and that family relations did not inherently compromise impartiality.[40] The decision sparked immediate allegations of judicial impropriety, with critics contending that the interpretationâ€”narrowly tailored to Gibran's profile as a recently elected mayorâ€”suggested orchestration to circumvent constitutional age safeguards amid speculation of a Jokowi political dynasty.[41] Four dissenting justices, including Arief Hidayat and Saldi Isra, rejected the petition outright, arguing it violated legislative intent and risked politicizing the court by enabling exceptions for incumbents' kin.[42] Public petitions to the court's Honorary Council accused Usman of nepotism, citing the petition's filing by a pro-Gibran group and the timing just before candidate registration deadlines, which amplified perceptions of executive influence over judicial independence.[43] Independent observers, including legal scholars, highlighted systemic risks to democratic checks, noting Indonesia's history of post-Suharto reforms aimed at insulating courts from elite capture.[44]

Ethics Council probe and dismissal from Chief Justice role

The Majelis Kehormatan Mahkamah Konstitusi (MKMK), Indonesia's Constitutional Court Ethics Council, initiated an investigation into Chief Justice Anwar Usman on October 23, 2023, following complaints from civil society groups and academics alleging ethical violations in the court's October 16, 2023, ruling on vice presidential age limits.[45][5] The allegations centered on Usman's failure to recuse himself despite his familial ties to President Joko Widodo, whose son benefited from the decision, and his involvement in altering a dissenting opinion to sway the vote outcome.[46][6] During the probe, the MKMK examined evidence including Usman's participation in deliberations and his last-minute revisions to Justice Saldi Isra's dissenting opinion, which shifted a potential 5-4 minority to a 5-4 majority in favor of relaxing the age requirement for candidates with prior regional leadership experience.[5][2] The council determined that Usman violated five core principles of the Constitutional Justices' Code of Ethics: impartiality, integrity, professionalism, independence, and equality, constituting a "serious" breach by prioritizing personal and political interests over judicial neutrality.[2][47] On November 7, 2023, the MKMK unanimously ruled Usman guilty, imposing the sanction of dismissal from the Chief Justice position effective immediately, while allowing him to retain his seat as a justice.[2][48] The decision directed the court's deputy chief justice to organize leadership elections within 48 hours and emphasized that Usman's actions undermined public trust in the institution's independence.[47][49] Dissenting opinions within the panel called for his full dishonorable dismissal as a justice, citing the severity of the interference, but the majority opted for the lesser penalty to avoid broader institutional disruption.[2]

Subsequent appeals and legal challenges

Following his dismissal from the position of Chief Justice by the Constitutional Court's Honorary Council (MKMK) on November 7, 2023, Anwar Usman initiated legal action in the State Administrative Court (PTUN) in Jakarta, challenging the decision as exceeding the council's authority and violating procedural norms.[50][51] The lawsuit sought annulment of the sanctions, including the removal from leadership and restrictions on participating in cases involving relatives, arguing that the MKMK lacked jurisdiction over judicial deliberations.[51] On August 14, 2024, the PTUN issued a partial ruling in Usman's favor, annulling portions of the MKMK decision related to the prohibition on his involvement in nepotism-linked cases and affirming his compliance with certain ethical principles during the October 2023 vice presidential age limit deliberation.[52][53] However, the court upheld the core sanction of his demotion from Chief Justice, rejecting his request for full reinstatement on grounds that the MKMK's authority to impose leadership removal was valid under internal regulations.[52] Usman subsequently appealed the PTUN's partial denial to the Supreme Court on August 27, 2024, contending that the ruling undermined judicial independence and failed to fully invalidate the MKMK's overreach.[44][53] Legal analysts have criticized the appeal as potentially eroding institutional accountability, given the internal nature of judicial ethics enforcement, though no final resolution has been reported as of late 2024.[44] In parallel, on March 28, 2024, the MKMK issued Usman a written warning for an additional ethics breachâ€”attending the October 2023 vice presidential inauguration of his nephew Gibran Rakabuming Raka without recusalâ€”further complicating his post-dismissal standing but not triggering further appeals at that stage.[54] Separately, the Constitutional Court rejected a November 2023 petition challenging the substantive validity of its age limit ruling (No. 90/PUU-XXI/2023), upholding the decision despite Usman's prior involvement.[55]

Post-dismissal activities

Continued role as Constitutional Court justice

Following the Constitutional Court's Ethics Council's decision on November 7, 2023, to dismiss Anwar Usman from his position as Chief Justice for violations of judicial independence and integrity in the 2024 vice presidential age limit ruling, he retained his role as a justice on the nine-member bench.[5][2] The council imposed sanctions limiting his participation, including a prohibition on adjudicating election-related disputes where conflicts of interest might arise, to mitigate ongoing concerns over impartiality.[47][56] Usman resumed duties in non-restricted cases, contributing to plenary sessions and deliberations as permitted under the sanctions.[57] In January 2025, after a brief hospitalization, the court's public relations head confirmed his expected return to sidang (hearings) absent further health issues, underscoring his ongoing operational status.[57] By October 2025, he actively engaged in public outreach, delivering a lecture on the Constitutional Court's role in Indonesia's state system at Dwijendra University's Faculty of Law on October 4, identifying himself as a sitting justice.[58] In 2024, the Ethics Council issued an additional ruling (Decision No. 01/MKMK/L/03/2024) examining Usman's post-dismissal press conference on November 8, 2023, for potential further breaches, but this did not result in his removal from the bench.[59] Critics, including legal scholars, argued that his retention eroded public trust in the court, with calls for voluntary resignation to preserve institutional integrity, yet Usman persisted in his position without facing outright dismissal as a justice.[60] His term as justice is set to conclude with mandatory retirement in December 2026, after which the court announced plans for replacement nominations.[17]

Public engagements and defenses

On November 8, 2023, the day after his dismissal from the Chief Justice position, Anwar Usman held a press conference to respond to the Constitutional Court Ethics Council's decision.[61] [62] He affirmed respect for the ruling while emphasizing his long career as a judge, stating that he had acted in accordance with statutory laws and his conscience.[61] Usman defended his refusal to recuse himself from case No. 90/PUU-XXI/2023, arguing that judicial review processes are matters of public interest rather than personal affairs, and that stepping aside would violate his judicial oath and beliefs.[61] [63] In the same statement, Usman rejected claims of personal conflict of interest, noting that the selection of political candidates, including his nephew Gibran Rakabuming Raka, ultimately depends on public votes rather than judicial decisions alone.[61] He compared the ethics allegations against him to routine practices in other jurisdictions, suggesting that familial ties do not inherently disqualify judges from handling related cases.[63] Usman described the Ethics Council's sanctions as themselves violating institutional norms, despite their stated intent to uphold the court's reputation, but clarified that he had not interfered in the proceedings.[61] Usman further remarked that positions of authority, including his former role, ultimately belong to divine will, framing his ouster as part of a broader scenario of attempted character assassination that he had anticipated.[62] [64] He expressed commitment to continuing his duties as an ordinary Constitutional Court justice without further public contention at that time.[61] Subsequent public appearances by Usman remained limited, with no major speeches or interviews documented beyond initial legal objections, such as his November 24, 2023, letter challenging the appointment of Suhartoyo as interim Chief Justice.[65]

Personal life and family connections

Marriage to Iriana Jokowi's sister

Anwar Usman married Idayati on May 26, 2022, in a private ceremony at Graha Saba Buana in Surakarta, Central Java, coinciding with her 56th birthday.[66][67] Idayati, born on May 26, 1966, is the younger sister of Indonesian President Joko Widodo, making Usman the president's brother-in-law following the union.[68][69] The ijab kabul (Islamic marriage contract) was conducted smoothly, with Usman serving as the groom and providing a set of prayer equipment and a gold watch as mahar (dowry).[70][67] Prior to the marriage, Idayati had been widowed since the death of her first husband, Hari Mulyono, in 2008 after 22 years of marriage; the couple had two children together.[71] Usman and Idayati reportedly met through family connections after Usman joined the Constitutional Court in 2018, though Usman has stated the relationship developed naturally without political motivations.[72] The event included a rehearsal the day prior and drew attendance from close family, including President Widodo, but was kept low-key amid public scrutiny over Usman's judicial role.[73]

Implications of familial ties on public perception

Anwar Usman's marriage to Rini Siti Hesty, the younger sister of First Lady Iriana Jokowi, positioned him as the brother-in-law of President Joko Widodo, amplifying scrutiny over potential conflicts of interest in judicial decisions. This familial connection drew widespread criticism during the October 16, 2023, Constitutional Court ruling that relaxed age requirements for vice presidential candidates, enabling Widodo's son, Gibran Rakabuming Raka, to run despite being under 40. Critics argued the decision exemplified nepotism, eroding public confidence in the court's impartiality and fueling perceptions of a Widodo-led political dynasty that prioritized family advancement over democratic norms.[74][75] Public backlash manifested in protests, media exposÃ©s, and academic analyses decrying the ruling as a symptom of cronyism within Indonesia's elite institutions. Legal experts and opposition figures highlighted how Usman's failure to recuse himselfâ€”despite his nephew-by-marriage benefiting directlyâ€”undermined the judiciary's credibility, with some labeling it "baby nepotism" that threatened long-term democratic stability. Surveys and commentary post-ruling indicated a dip in trust toward the Constitutional Court, though Widodo's overall approval ratings remained high, suggesting the issue resonated more among urban intellectuals and activists than the broader electorate.[69][76][77] The November 7, 2023, decision by the court's Ethics Council to demote Usman from Chief Justice for ethical breaches was framed by supporters as a corrective measure to restore institutional legitimacy, yet skeptics viewed it as insufficient, arguing it failed to address deeper systemic favoritism tied to his family links. This event intensified debates on judicial independence, with reports noting persistent public wariness that familial influences could recur, particularly as Gibran ascended to the vice presidency following the February 2024 election victory of the Prabowo-Gibran ticket. Despite these concerns, the lack of widespread electoral backlash implied that for many Indonesians, economic priorities overshadowed nepotism allegations, though the controversy lingers as a cautionary example of how personal ties can tarnish perceptions of elite accountability.[78][7]
