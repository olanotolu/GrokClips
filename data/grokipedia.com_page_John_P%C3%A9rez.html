John Pérez


John A. Pérez (born September 28, 1969) is an American labor leader and Democratic politician who served as the 68th Speaker of the California State Assembly from January 2010 to September 2012.[1][2] Elected to represent the 53rd Assembly District in 2008, Pérez became the first openly gay man to lead a state legislative chamber in the United States, a milestone that garnered national attention amid his tenure navigating California's budget crises and legislative reforms.[3][2] Prior to entering politics, Pérez was a prominent union organizer with United Teachers Los Angeles, rising through activist ranks in the state's largest teachers' union and advocating for education funding and labor rights.[4] As Speaker, he prioritized economic development initiatives, including authoring the bill that established the Governor's Office of Business and Economic Development (GO-Biz) to streamline state support for businesses, and played a key role in preserving child-care programs during fiscal shortfalls.[5][6] His leadership emphasized bipartisan compromises on budget matters, though it faced criticism for aligning closely with union interests.[6] Pérez's post-legislative career includes appointments as a Regent of the University of California, where he chaired the Board of Regents and supported expansions like the Middle Class Scholarship program, as well as serving as Vice President of the Los Angeles Board of Harbor Commissioners since 2024.[1][7][5] A notable controversy emerged in 2011 when reports questioned his repeated claims of graduating from UC Berkeley in 1991, revealing he had not completed his degree despite enrolling.[8] This incident highlighted discrepancies in his biographical accounts but did not derail his subsequent public roles.[8]

Early life and education

Family and upbringing

John A. Pérez was born as the youngest of five children to parents Felipe and Vera Pérez in Los Angeles, California.[9] His father, Felipe, had emigrated from Jalisco, Mexico, in 1951, learned English proficiently, but suffered a disabling industrial accident shortly after Pérez's birth, rendering him unable to work.[9] Vera Pérez then became the family's primary breadwinner, advancing from a receptionist role to director of a local community clinic, which exposed the family to the direct effects of public policy decisions on working-class households.[9] Pérez grew up in the working-class neighborhoods of Highland Park and El Sereno in northeastern Los Angeles, areas characterized by Mexican-American communities and economic challenges during the late 20th century.[9] He maintained a close relationship with extended family, including his cousin Antonio Villaraigosa, then mayor of Los Angeles, whom Pérez described as closer than typical siblings.[9] This familial environment, marked by his parents' resilience amid financial precarity—exacerbated by 1980s policy threats to disability benefits and clinic funding—instilled an early awareness of socioeconomic vulnerabilities in immigrant and laboring families.[9]

Academic background and controversies

Pérez enrolled at the University of California, Berkeley, in 1987, where he majored in Chicano studies.[10] Some profiles also noted his pursuit of sociology alongside this focus.[11] He left the university without earning a degree.[10] A significant controversy arose in May 2011 when reports revealed that Pérez had long misrepresented his educational attainment in official biographies, campaign materials, and media accounts, claiming a 1991 graduation from Berkeley with a bachelor's degree.[8] [12] This included a 2009 press release from Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger's office stating that Pérez had "earned a bachelor of science degree from the University of California, Berkeley," as well as similar assertions in Los Angeles Times profiles and other outlets dating back to at least 2005.[10] Pérez responded by confirming his attendance but admitting he did not graduate, attributing the discrepancies to errors for which he took full responsibility, and pledged to correct future references.[12] [13] Critics, including political opponents, questioned the implications for his credibility as Assembly Speaker, though supporters dismissed it as a minor biographical oversight without impact on his qualifications or performance.[13] No legal or formal disciplinary actions resulted from the matter.

Pre-political career

Union organizing roles

Pérez commenced his union organizing efforts shortly after departing the University of California, Berkeley, joining the International Union of Painters and Allied Trades District Council 16 as an organizer from January 1992 to December 1995, where he focused on membership recruitment and contract negotiations for trades workers in Southern California.[14] In 1995, at age 25, he transitioned to the United Food and Commercial Workers (UFCW), initially directing its Western States Council and advancing to executive director of the Regional 8 States Council by 1998, roles that encompassed coordinating regional organizing drives and political strategies to secure improved wages and conditions for food industry employees.[11] [15] From January 2001 to December 2008, Pérez served as Director of Political Affairs for UFCW Local 324, California's largest grocery workers local, overseeing campaigns that integrated electoral advocacy with workplace organizing, including support for the 2003–2004 Southern California supermarket strike, which mobilized over 70,000 UFCW members against Vons, Ralphs, and Albertsons in a dispute over healthcare costs and wages lasting 141 days. [16] Concurrently, Pérez engaged deeply with education labor, rising within United Teachers Los Angeles (UTLA), where he held positions such as Secondary Vice President before being elected president in 2002, leading efforts to represent over 48,000 Los Angeles Unified School District educators in negotiations amid budget shortfalls and reform debates.[4] [17] His tenure emphasized protecting collective bargaining rights and opposing certain charter school expansions perceived as undermining public sector jobs.[18]

Advocacy and community involvement

Prior to his election to the California State Assembly, John Pérez served on the board of the Los Angeles Community Redevelopment Agency, where he led initiatives to require major construction projects in the city to hire at least 40% local residents, aiming to boost employment in economically disadvantaged neighborhoods.[11] [5] Pérez also engaged in environmental advocacy as a board member of the California League of Conservation Voters and, by 1998, the Los Angeles League of Conservation Voters, supporting policies for conservation and sustainable development.[11] [5] In the realm of public health, Pérez held leadership roles with the AIDS Project Los Angeles and was appointed by Presidents Bill Clinton and George W. Bush to the President's Advisory Council on HIV/AIDS, where he contributed to federal efforts addressing prevention, treatment, and policy responses to the epidemic.[19] [11] As an openly gay advocate, his work emphasized expanding access to HIV/AIDS services in vulnerable communities.[20]

Entry into politics

2008 Assembly election

In the June 3, 2008 Democratic primary for the open 53rd Assembly District seat—vacated due to term limits for incumbent Ron Calderon—Pérez, a longtime SEIU labor leader, competed against Arturo Chavez, Ricardo Lara, and Michael Aldapa.[21] He received 4,299 votes, or 55 percent of the total, advancing as the nominee in the heavily Democratic district covering East Los Angeles, Boyle Heights, and nearby areas.[21] The Republican primary was uncontested, with Manuel Aldana Jr. securing the nomination unopposed.[21] Pérez's campaign emphasized support for working families, public education funding, and affordable healthcare, drawing on his union organizing experience to mobilize Latino and labor voters in the district.[2] In the November 4 general election, he defeated Aldana with 51,556 votes (84.9 percent) to Aldana's 9,180 (15.1 percent), achieving a decisive win reflective of the district's partisan leanings.[22] This election made Pérez the first openly gay Latino member of the California State Assembly.[23]

Initial legislative activities and leadership ascent

Pérez was elected to the California State Assembly on November 4, 2008, representing the 46th district, which encompassed parts of downtown Los Angeles and East Los Angeles communities.[24] He was sworn into office on December 1, 2008, joining a Democratic supermajority amid a severe state budget crisis with a projected deficit exceeding $40 billion. As a freshman legislator with a background in union organizing, Pérez focused on bills advancing environmental stewardship and local infrastructure improvements, including AB 2398, which established a producer responsibility program for carpet recycling to reduce landfill waste and promote sustainable materials management.[25] He also championed measures addressing water quality in underserved areas, such as efforts to secure clean drinking water access for residents in Maywood, reflecting his emphasis on community health and equity issues tied to his district's urban and immigrant populations.[26] During the 2009-2010 legislative session, Pérez served on key committees including Appropriations and Budget, where he contributed to deliberations on fiscal reforms and spending cuts necessitated by the recession-driven shortfall, which had ballooned to approximately $64 billion by mid-year.[1] His advocacy drew on prior experience with the California Labor Federation, prioritizing worker protections and economic recovery initiatives, though many proposals faced resistance in a gridlocked environment requiring bipartisan compromises.[27] Pérez's rapid ascent to leadership began in late 2009, when, despite his freshman status, he garnered support from labor unions and Democratic caucus members to secure nomination as the next Speaker on December 10, 2009, bypassing more senior colleagues.[24] This selection followed the impending resignation of Speaker Karen Bass to pursue a congressional seat, positioning Pérez—cousin to Los Angeles Mayor Antonio Villaraigosa—as a compromise candidate backed by influential stakeholders.[28] He was unanimously elected Speaker by the Assembly on March 1, 2010, becoming the first openly gay man to hold the position and one of the youngest at age 40.[29] His elevation was attributed to strategic alliances forged through union networks and a willingness to break from established factions within the caucus, enabling him to navigate internal power dynamics effectively.[27]

Tenure as Speaker of the California Assembly (2010–2012)

Budget negotiations and fiscal policies

As Speaker of the California State Assembly from March 2010 to May 2012, John Pérez navigated acute fiscal challenges, including a projected $20.7 billion deficit at the onset of his tenure amid the lingering effects of the 2008 recession.[30] Negotiations for the 2010-11 budget under Republican Gov. Arnold Schwarzenegger extended beyond the June 15 constitutional deadline, resulting in passage on October 8, 2010, which addressed the shortfall via $7.7 billion in spending reductions, $5.3 billion in fund shifts and borrowing (including from the beverage recycling fund), and preservation of K-12 education funding without mid-year cuts.[31][32] Pérez emphasized the plan's avoidance of widespread layoffs and its reliance on temporary measures to bridge the gap, while criticizing California's supermajority voting requirement—needing two-thirds approval—as an "irrational" barrier contributing to delays.[33][34][32] Pérez championed structural reforms to expedite future budgeting, leading Democratic efforts to qualify Proposition 25 for the November 2010 ballot, which voters approved by 52.4% to eliminate the two-thirds threshold for budget bills, enabling simple majority passage while retaining it for tax increases.[6] This change facilitated on-time adoption of subsequent budgets, including the 2011-12 plan. He also pledged greater transparency in deliberations upon assuming the speakership, though implementation proved challenging amid partisan impasses.[35] In 2011, under newly elected Democratic Gov. Jerry Brown, Pérez coordinated with Senate President pro Tempore Darrell Steinberg on a $222 billion budget proposal facing a $26.6 billion gap. An initial Democratic-passed version on June 16 was vetoed by Brown the next day as unbalanced, citing gimmicks like delayed school payments, inflated building sales, and illegal local tax hikes that masked ongoing deficits and added debt.[36] Pérez countered that Brown had failed to deliver Republican votes for his preferred $12 billion tax extensions via voter referendum, limiting options in the Democrat-controlled Legislature.[36] A compromise $86 billion general fund budget passed on June 28—two days before the fiscal year deadline—encompassing $15 billion in cuts to health, welfare, and higher education, plus $9.6 billion in additional reductions, while forgoing new taxes and hinging on optimistic revenue forecasts from economic recovery.[37][37] Pérez joined Brown and Steinberg in announcing the deal, which Democrats framed as protecting core services despite the austerity.[37][38] Pérez's fiscal approach prioritized shielding education and social programs from deeper slashes, favoring revenue bets over permanent tax hikes without ballot validation, and temporary fixes like borrowing—measures that closed immediate shortfalls but perpetuated volatility tied to California's heavy dependence on progressive income and capital gains taxes. By 2012, he noted prior cuts had narrowed structural imbalances to $5-6 billion in ongoing obligations, crediting bipartisan restraint and Prop. 25 for enabling timely passage of the 2012-13 budget under Brown's proposal.[39][40]

Key legislative initiatives

Pérez led efforts to address California's fiscal challenges during his speakership, collaborating with Governor Jerry Brown and Senate leaders to enact budgets that eliminated the state's structural deficit, which had persisted for over a decade by balancing revenues and expenditures through spending cuts, temporary tax extensions, and reserve building measures in 2010 and 2011.[1] These agreements, finalized in June 2012 for the 2012-2013 fiscal year, included $8.3 billion in reserves and avoided further deep cuts to education and social services after earlier shortfalls exceeding $25 billion.[41] A signature initiative was the creation of the Governor's Office of Business and Economic Development (GO-Biz) through legislation he authored, aimed at consolidating state business promotion activities, streamlining permitting processes, and fostering job creation amid the post-2008 recession, with the office launching in 2011 to coordinate economic incentives across agencies.[5] Pérez also championed the Middle Class Scholarship Act (AB 955), enacted in 2012, which generated approximately $1 billion annually by closing a tax loophole exploited by multistate corporations through unitary business principles, redirecting funds to reduce tuition by up to 40% for nearly 100,000 students at California State University and University of California campuses starting in 2013-2014.[42][1] In health policy, Pérez sponsored AB 1602 in January 2010 to establish the California Health Benefit Exchange as a state-level implementation of the federal Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act, enabling enrollment in qualified health plans and subsidies for low-income residents by October 2013, with the exchange processing over 1.3 million applications in its first open enrollment period. He further advanced education reforms by securing increased K-12 funding restorations in the 2012 budget, reversing prior cuts of over $20 billion since 2008, and authoring AB 1967, signed September 26, 2012, to mandate organ and tissue donation awareness in high school health curricula to boost California's donor registry participation rates.[1][43]

Controversies and criticisms

During his tenure as Speaker, Pérez faced accusations of using coercive tactics to advance legislation, particularly in efforts to disincorporate the City of Vernon, a small industrial municipality criticized for corruption and environmental issues. Opponents claimed Pérez threatened to withhold support for bills sponsored by lawmakers who resisted the measure, AB 46, which aimed to abolish Vernon's charter city status in 2011.[6][44] Pérez dismissed characterizations of his approach as bullying, stating in a 2011 interview that if he were a bully, "I would get my way," amid failed passage of the bill despite reported arm-twisting.[6] Pérez's educational background drew scrutiny in May 2011 after reports revealed he had dropped out of the University of California, Berkeley, without earning a degree, contrary to some biographical descriptions portraying him as a graduate.[10][12] The discrepancy arose from earlier praises, such as in the Congressional Record by then-Rep. Hilda Solis, which lauded Pérez's academic achievements alongside his labor activism, prompting questions about the accuracy of public representations tied to his rapid rise to leadership.[10] In budget negotiations, Pérez encountered criticism for protracted delays and perceived inflexibility, exemplified by the June 2011 failure to pass a timely state budget, resulting in withheld paychecks for legislators under a law enforced by Controller John Chiang.[45] Pérez endorsed Chiang's action but joined Senate leaders in suing to restore pay, arguing it hindered bipartisan talks without advancing fiscal solutions.[46] Critics, including Republican lawmakers and fiscal watchdogs, faulted his management-labor negotiation style—shaped by his union background—for treating budget impasses like industrial disputes rather than prioritizing structural reforms amid California's $26 billion deficit.[47] Pérez's close ties to labor unions, including contributions from firms implicated in a New York state pension fund corruption probe during his time as a union official, raised questions about potential conflicts in his legislative priorities, such as resisting aggressive public pension overhauls despite statewide shortfalls exceeding $500 billion by some estimates.[48][49] While Pérez supported a 2012 bipartisan pension reform bill signed by Gov. Jerry Brown, projected to save $55 billion over 30 years per CalPERS, detractors argued it inadequately addressed long-term liabilities, reflecting union influence in diluting changes like hybrid plans or benefit caps.[50][49] Separately, Pérez's personal relationship with Eugene Frey, a Hollywood funeral director facing fraud charges in a $100 million Ponzi scheme, became public in 2013 but overlapped with his speakership, as the pair dated publicly for over a year starting around 2011.[51] No direct ethical violations were alleged against Pérez, but the association drew media attention to his judgment amid high-profile events attended together.[51]

Later Assembly service and term limits (2012–2014)

Redistricting and reelection

Following the 2010 United States Census, California's Citizens Redistricting Commission, established by Propositions 11 and 20, redrew the state's legislative district boundaries to reflect population changes, with final Assembly maps certified by the legislature on August 15, 2011.[52] These maps reconfigured Pérez's existing District 46 into the new District 53, which retained a heavily Democratic and Latino-majority composition centered in eastern Los Angeles areas including Boyle Heights and Vernon.[24] The redrawn districts were designed to comply with federal Voting Rights Act requirements and population equality standards, resulting in maps that preserved Democratic advantages in urban areas without evident partisan gerrymandering, though critics noted the outcomes aligned with the state's underlying voter demographics favoring Democrats.[53] Pérez, who had served as Assembly Speaker until December 2012, benefited from the maps' stability for incumbents in safe districts, as District 53 maintained an estimated Democratic performance of over 80% based on prior voting patterns.[54] The commission's work ensured Democratic control of at least 52 Assembly seats post-2012 elections, providing continuity for the party's legislative agenda despite Pérez's impending term limits.[53] In the June 5, 2012, top-two primary for District 53, Pérez secured the top spot with 13,944 votes (68.5%), advancing alongside Republican opponent Janet Dixon.[55] He won the general election on November 6, 2012, defeating Dixon with 66,357 votes to her 13,456 (83.1% to 16.9%).[56] This decisive victory extended his Assembly tenure through 2014, his final term under California's lifetime limit of 12 years in the legislature.[24]

Final legislative priorities

In his final term in the California State Assembly, representing the 53rd District from 2012 to 2014, John A. Pérez prioritized legislation aimed at economic development, student debt relief, and cybersecurity preparedness. These efforts reflected a shift toward long-term fiscal tools and emerging risks, following his tenure as Speaker, amid California's ongoing recovery from the 2008 recession. Pérez introduced measures to expand access to infrastructure financing and refinance options for student loans, seeking to bolster middle-class support and state competitiveness.[2] A key initiative was AB 701, introduced on February 21, 2013, which authorized the California Infrastructure and Economic Development Bank to issue revenue bonds for broadband deployment and other infrastructure projects, aiming to stimulate job creation and connectivity in underserved areas. The bill passed the Assembly Appropriations Committee on April 16, 2013, with a 6-2 vote, emphasizing public-private partnerships to address funding gaps without general fund reliance. Pérez also advanced AB 2377 in 2014, establishing the California Student Loan Refinancing Program under the ScholarShare Oversight Committee to enable eligible borrowers to refinance existing loans through state-backed mechanisms, potentially reducing interest burdens for California residents with incomes below specified thresholds. Enrolled on August 29, 2014, the measure targeted affordability for postsecondary education amid rising tuition costs, building on prior scholarship expansions. Addressing cybersecurity vulnerabilities, Pérez proposed legislation on February 27, 2014, to create a bipartisan California Cybersecurity Commission tasked with assessing threats to state infrastructure, recommending protections against foreign and domestic attacks, and coordinating with federal agencies. This reflected concerns over increasing digital risks to utilities, finance, and government systems, positioning California as proactive in a domain lacking comprehensive state-level oversight at the time.[60]

Post-legislative appointments and roles

University of California Regent (2014–2024)

John A. Pérez was appointed to the University of California Board of Regents by Governor Jerry Brown on November 17, 2014, for a term initially set to expire in 2020, with subsequent reappointments extending his eligibility through March 1, 2036.[1][14] As a regent, Pérez served on committees including Compliance and Audit, Finance and Capital Strategies Governance, and chaired the Health Services Committee from 2024 to 2025.[1] In May 2019, the board elected him as chair, a position he assumed on July 1, 2019, for a one-year term during which he oversaw key systemwide decisions.[61] During his chairmanship and subsequent service, Pérez contributed to policies on admissions and campus operations, including unanimous board approval in May 2020 to suspend SAT and ACT requirements for undergraduate admissions until at least fall 2024, amid disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic.[62] The board under his leadership also endorsed efforts to repeal Proposition 209, California's 1996 voter-approved ban on race, ethnicity, and gender considerations in public university admissions, arguing for renewed affirmative action to address diversity gaps.[63] Pérez supported responses to federal policies affecting students, such as welcoming the 2020 reversal of ICE rules that would have deported international students not attending in-person classes and advocating for DACA protections.[64][65] In 2024, amid campus protests over the Israel-Hamas war, Pérez publicly criticized disproportionate antisemitic incidents, including disruptions at events and chants targeting Jewish students, while distinguishing them from historical civil disobedience by emphasizing their focus on harassment over debate.[66] He argued for limits on free speech where it involved private space violations or identity-based targeting, opposing "both-sides-ism" in equating antisemitism reports with those on Islamophobia.[66] Pérez stepped down from the board in November 2024 after a decade of service, citing unresolved issues such as state budget negotiations and the search for a new UC president as factors influencing his decision to depart despite recent reappointment.[7]

Los Angeles Harbor Commission (2024–present)

John A. Pérez was appointed to the Los Angeles Board of Harbor Commissioners by Mayor Karen Bass on August 14, 2024, for a term expiring June 30, 2029, succeeding retired commissioner Diane Middleton.[67][5] The five-member board, which governs the Port of Los Angeles—the busiest container port in the Western Hemisphere—oversees maritime operations, infrastructure development, environmental compliance, and economic policies affecting annual cargo volumes exceeding 9 million twenty-foot equivalent units.[68] Pérez's nomination was unanimously confirmed by the Los Angeles City Council on September 20, 2024.[20][69] On October 3, 2024, Pérez was elected vice president of the board, positioning him to assist President Edward R. Renwick in leading commission meetings and strategic initiatives.[5] In this role, he has contributed to deliberations on port sustainability, including the extension of a $10 clean truck fund fee approved by the board on May 23, 2025, to support zero-emission vehicle transitions amid ongoing federal and state regulatory pressures.[70] The board under Pérez's tenure also voiced concerns over U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement operations on Terminal Island on July 17, 2025, highlighting potential disruptions to port labor and logistics without evidence of direct threats to maritime security.[71] These actions reflect the commission's emphasis on operational continuity and environmental mandates, though specific votes or statements by Pérez remain undocumented in public records as of October 2025.[72]

Private sector and advisory positions

Following his departure from the California State Assembly in 2014, Pérez founded Double Nickel Advisors, a Sacramento-based government relations and strategic consulting firm specializing in legislative advocacy, policy development, and lobbying campaigns.[73] The firm draws on Pérez's experience as former Speaker to assist clients in navigating California's political landscape, including coordinating messaging and building coalitions for regulatory and fiscal issues.[74] As principal, Pérez has emphasized targeted strategies for private sector clients seeking influence in state government processes.[75] In October 2023, Pérez joined the board of directors of Glass House Brands, a California-based cannabis cultivation and distribution company, bringing his expertise in state policy and economic development to guide corporate governance and expansion efforts amid evolving regulations.[76] This role represents his primary private sector board involvement post-legislature, focusing on oversight of operations in a heavily regulated industry.[75] No additional advisory or consulting positions in the private sector have been publicly documented beyond these engagements.

Personal life

Family and relationships

Pérez is the cousin of Antonio Villaraigosa, former Mayor of Los Angeles and Speaker of the California State Assembly.[9][6] His father immigrated from Mexico.[6] Pérez is openly homosexual, a fact he has publicly acknowledged since his political career began.[24][77] In a 2010 video message, he reflected that as a teenager, he "never thought [he'd] be able to live a happy and successful life as an openly gay man."[77] As of June 2010, he was single and had no children.[78] From approximately 2012 to 2013, Pérez maintained a romantic relationship with David Louis Cassity, a Hollywood funeral director and defendant in a 2009 federal lawsuit alleging the looting of millions from customer trusts at his family business.[51] During this period, Pérez attended political events with Cassity and arranged state meetings involving him, actions that associates later described as demonstrating poor judgment by blending personal and official duties.[51] No public records indicate Pérez has married or entered subsequent long-term partnerships.[14]

Health challenges and public identity

Pérez publicly identified as homosexual during his time at the University of California, Berkeley, coming out to his parents over Thanksgiving break in the late 1980s.[77] As a state assemblymember, he became the first openly homosexual person of color elected to the California legislature upon winning his seat in November 2008.[79] Pérez's election as Speaker of the Assembly in December 2009 marked him as the first openly homosexual man to hold that position, and he was sworn into the role on March 1, 2010, with participation from the Gay Men's Chorus of Los Angeles.[80] [28] In December 2016, Pérez withdrew from a special election for California's 34th congressional district, citing a newly diagnosed health condition that required his full attention, though he did not disclose specifics.[81] This decision ended his candidacy to succeed Xavier Becerra as U.S. Representative, a race in which he had garnered endorsements from figures including then-Senator-elect Kamala Harris and Lieutenant Governor Gavin Newsom.[81] No further public details on the condition or its resolution have been reported, and Pérez has continued in subsequent public roles without reference to ongoing health impediments.[20]
