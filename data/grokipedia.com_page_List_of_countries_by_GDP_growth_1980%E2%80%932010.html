List of countries by GDP growth 1980–2010

The list of countries by GDP growth 1980–2010 ranks sovereign states according to their average annual real gross domestic product (GDP) growth rates over the three-decade span, drawing primarily from national accounts data compiled by institutions such as the World Bank.[1] This period encompassed pivotal global shifts, including the widespread adoption of market-oriented reforms, deregulation, and integration into international trade networks, which propelled divergent economic outcomes across regions.[2] Emerging economies in Asia dominated the upper echelons, with China posting an average annual real GDP growth of approximately 9.8%, driven by post-1978 liberalization that shifted the country from central planning toward export manufacturing and foreign investment attraction.[3][2] India followed with around 5.8% average growth, accelerated by 1991 reforms that dismantled licensing regimes and opened sectors to private competition, enabling sustained expansion amid population pressures.[4] Other standouts included resource-dependent Botswana, averaging over 6% through prudent diamond revenue management and stable governance, and Southeast Asian tigers like Vietnam, which averaged about 7% following Đổi Mới reforms in 1986 that emphasized agricultural decollectivization and private enterprise.[5] In contrast, advanced economies such as the United States and Japan recorded more modest averages of roughly 3% and 2.5%, respectively, reflecting saturation in mature markets and structural challenges like Japan's asset bubble aftermath.[6] These rankings highlight how policies fostering capital accumulation, human capital investment, and institutional stability correlated with superior growth trajectories, often outpacing those reliant on state intervention or commodity windfalls alone.[1]

Methodology and Data Sources

Definition of GDP Growth Metrics

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) measures the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders over a specific time period, typically a year or quarter, using one of three approaches: production (value added by industries), expenditure (consumption plus investment plus government spending plus net exports), or income (wages, profits, rents, and taxes adjusted for subsidies).[7][8] Nominal GDP values output at current market prices, incorporating both changes in production volumes and price levels, whereas real GDP adjusts for inflation by valuing output at constant prices from a base year, isolating volume changes from price effects.[7][9] Real GDP growth rates are preferred for cross-country and intertemporal comparisons, as nominal rates can be distorted by varying inflation experiences, such as hyperinflation in some economies during the 1980s and 1990s.[8][10] The annual real GDP growth rate is calculated as the percentage change from the previous period: (Real GDPt−Real GDPt−1Real GDPt−1)×100\left( \frac{\text{Real GDP}_t - Primary Sources and Data Compilation

The primary sources for GDP growth data from 1980 to 2010 consist of official national accounts reported by countries' statistical agencies and central banks, which calculate real GDP growth as the annual percentage change in constant-price gross domestic product, adjusted for inflation using domestic price deflators or implicit GDP deflators.[1] These national figures are derived from production, expenditure, or income approaches under the System of National Accounts (SNA), with many countries adhering to SNA 1968 standards in the 1980s and transitioning to SNA 1993 by the 1990s and 2000s for improved comparability.[1] Data quality varies, with advanced economies like the United States and Japan providing robust, timely series from bureaus such as the Bureau of Economic Analysis or Cabinet Office, while emerging markets often faced challenges like incomplete coverage or revisions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa and post-communist states during the 1990s transition.[1] International organizations compile and standardize these national submissions into cross-country datasets. The World Bank's World Development Indicators (WDI) aggregates GDP growth data from official national statistics, supplemented by inputs from the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), United Nations Statistics Division (UNSD), and United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD), ensuring consistency in base years and methodological notes for each country's series.[14] Similarly, the International Monetary Fund's World Economic Outlook (WEO) database incorporates national accounts data from 1980 onward, with IMF staff applying adjustments for gaps or inconsistencies, such as estimating missing years based on proxy indicators like industrial production for select low-income countries.[15] Both datasets prioritize reported official figures but flag estimates where primary data is unavailable, as seen in WEO's handling of hyperinflation episodes in Latin America during the 1980s or Soviet bloc dissolutions in the early 1990s.[16] Compilation involves harmonization to local currency units at constant prices, with growth rates calculated as (GDPt−GDPt−1GDPt−1)×100\left( \frac{GDP_t - GDP_{t-1}}{GDP_{t-1}} \right) \times 100(GDPt−1​GDPt​−GDPt−1​​)×100, where ttt denotes the year and GDP is in chained or fixed base-year prices to minimize substitution bias.[1] For aggregate rankings over 1980–2010, analysts often average annual growth rates arithmetically or compute compound rates from endpoint GDP levels, drawing directly from these databases; for instance, WDI covers over 200 economies with annual data starting variably from 1960, achieving near-complete coverage by the 1990s.[14] Cross-verification between World Bank and IMF sources reveals minor discrepancies (typically under 0.5 percentage points annually) due to differing estimation methods, underscoring the value of consulting multiple compilers for robustness, especially for volatile periods like the 1997 Asian financial crisis.[17] National revisions, such as China's post-2010 adjustments to pre-1990s GDP baselines, can retroactively affect historical growth computations, necessitating reference to the latest vintages of these databases.[1]

Measurement Limitations and Controversies

GDP growth measurements for the period 1980–2010 face challenges from inconsistent national accounting practices across countries, which hinder direct comparability; for instance, many developing nations relied on outdated surveys with incomplete coverage of informal sectors, potentially understating true economic activity by excluding non-market transactions and shadow economies estimated at 20–50% of GDP in low-income countries.[18] International aggregators like the World Bank and IMF often impute missing data or apply estimates, leading to discrepancies between their series—for example, benchmark revisions in national accounts can alter historical growth rates by 1–2 percentage points annually, as seen in multiple economies during data harmonization efforts.[19] These revisions stem from improved methodologies or newly incorporated sectors, but they introduce uncertainty in long-term averages, with production-side and expenditure-side GDP estimates diverging by over 2% in nominal terms in some years for certain countries.[18] In transition economies of Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union, GDP series experienced significant data breaks around 1990–1992 due to the shift from planned to market systems, where pre-transition figures often overstated output by failing to account for quality declines and hidden inflation, while post-transition data initially underestimated activity amid institutional disruptions; empirical analyses indicate that initial GDP collapse estimates were exaggerated by 10–20% in countries like Russia and Ukraine as black markets and informal production were gradually captured.[20] Subsequent revisions in the mid-1990s adjusted growth trajectories upward, reflecting better coverage of private sector emergence, but persistent inconsistencies in deflators—often underestimated by 1–2 points—could inflate real growth rates by similar margins during stabilization phases.[21] Controversies intensified regarding fast-growing emerging economies like China, where official real GDP growth averaged over 9% annually from 1980–2010 but faced skepticism for potential overstatement driven by local government incentives to meet targets, evidenced by provincial aggregates exceeding national totals by 5–10% in years like 2004 and 2010, and discrepancies with alternative indicators such as electricity usage showing sharper slowdowns.[18] Independent estimates, such as those by Angus Maddison, pegged China's growth at roughly 2.4% annually from 1978–1995 versus official 7.5%, attributing gaps to incomplete service sector surveys and political smoothing that masked cyclical volatility, including during the 1998 Asian crisis.[18] While some econometric tests, including adherence to Benford's Law and correlations with freight and energy data, suggest no systematic falsification and alignment within 1 percentage point of alternatives, critics highlight persistent expenditure-production gaps and census-driven upward revisions (e.g., +16.8% in 2004) as signs of inflated figures to support policy narratives.[22][18] These debates underscore broader risks of politically motivated reporting in non-transparent systems, potentially biasing cross-country rankings.

Regional and Per Capita Variations

East Asia and the Pacific region exhibited the strongest aggregate GDP performance from 1980 to 2010, with average annual real GDP growth exceeding 7 percent in many subregions, fueled by market-oriented reforms, high savings rates, and integration into global supply chains. China's post-1978 opening, which accelerated industrial output and foreign direct investment inflows, contributed disproportionately, averaging nearly 10 percent annual growth over the period, while the "Asian Tigers" (South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Singapore) sustained rates above 5 percent through export manufacturing and human capital investments. In contrast, Latin America and the Caribbean averaged around 2.5 percent annual growth, marred by the 1980s debt crisis—triggered by oil shocks and unsustainable borrowing—that led to hyperinflation and contractions in countries like Argentina and Brazil, followed by uneven recovery via commodity exports in the 2000s.[32][33] Sub-Saharan Africa recorded the lowest regional average, approximately 2.6 percent annually, with growth hampered by commodity price volatility, civil conflicts, institutional weaknesses, and policy missteps such as Dutch disease in oil-dependent economies. South Asia improved from sluggish 1980s performance (around 4 percent) to over 6 percent post-1991 liberalization in India, driven by services sector expansion and remittances, though intra-regional variation persisted due to political instability in Pakistan and Bangladesh. Europe and Central Asia averaged about 2 percent, with Western Europe (e.g., EU members) at roughly 2.2 percent amid mature market dynamics and the 1990s transition shocks in Eastern Europe causing temporary declines before stabilization via EU integration.[34][35] Per capita GDP growth amplified these disparities, as population dynamics interacted with aggregate expansion. East Asia achieved around 5-6 percent annual per capita growth, enabling rapid poverty reduction and urbanization, since population growth averaged under 1.5 percent. Latin America saw per capita rates near 1 percent, reflecting high fertility rates (over 2.5 percent in the 1980s) outpacing GDP gains during lost decades. Sub-Saharan Africa's per capita growth hovered near zero overall, dipping negative in the 1980s-1990s amid 2.5-3 percent population increases and frequent recessions, underscoring failures in productivity-enhancing reforms. In Europe, per capita growth averaged 1.7 percent for the Euro Area, supported by low population growth (under 0.5 percent) but constrained by aging demographics and regulatory rigidities. South Asia's per capita gains reached about 4 percent post-reforms, though offset by 2 percent annual population growth. These patterns highlight how institutional quality and policy choices, rather than endowments alone, mediated demographic pressures on living standards.[32][36][37]

Decade-Specific Trends

1980–1990: Recovery from Stagflation, Debt Crises, and Early Market Reforms

The 1980s represented a period of uneven global recovery following the stagflation of the 1970s, where double-digit inflation coexisted with stagnant output in many advanced economies. Aggressive monetary policies, such as the U.S. Federal Reserve's federal funds rate hikes to over 20% in 1980-1981 under Chairman Paul Volcker, successfully curbed inflation from 13.5% in 1980 to 3.2% by 1983, but induced a severe recession with U.S. real GDP contracting 2.7% in 1982.[38] This approach, echoed in other OECD nations, prioritized price stability over short-term growth, yielding average annual real GDP expansion of about 2.8% across member countries from 1980 to 1990.[39] Japan outperformed with roughly 4.3% average growth, driven by manufacturing exports and domestic investment booms preceding its asset bubble.[40] In contrast, European economies like the UK and Germany averaged 2.2% and 2.1%, respectively, amid structural rigidities and initial resistance to deregulation.[41] Emerging markets exhibited stark divergences, with East Asian economies accelerating via export-led strategies and initial liberalization. China's post-1978 reforms under Deng Xiaoping, including rural decollectivization and special economic zones, propelled average annual real GDP growth to 9.9%, transforming it from agrarian stagnation to industrial takeoff despite political setbacks like the 1989 Tiananmen events.[24] South Korea sustained its "Miracle on the Han" with 9.3% average growth, fueled by chaebol-led heavy industry investments and trade surpluses, though not without authoritarian state direction.[42] Singapore and Taiwan similarly achieved 6.5% and 7.0% averages, respectively, through open trade policies and human capital focus.[43] Botswana, leveraging diamond revenues and prudent fiscal management, posted around 9.5% growth, an outlier in sub-Saharan Africa.[5] The decade's challenges centered on the Latin American debt crisis, triggered by Mexico's 1982 default amid soaring U.S. interest rates, oil price volatility, and commodity busts. Debtor nations, burdened by $300 billion in external debt, faced capital flight and hyperinflation; regional real GDP growth averaged -0.4% annually, marking a "lost decade" exacerbated by initial protectionist responses and delayed reforms.[44] IMF-led structural adjustment programs, emphasizing austerity, privatization, and trade openness, yielded mixed results—Chile's market-oriented shifts under Pinochet enabled 6.5% average growth post-1984, contrasting with Argentina's -2.5% amid Peronist populism.[45] In Africa, many oil-importing states stagnated at 1-2% growth, hampered by commodity dependence and governance failures.[44] Early market reforms in advanced economies, such as U.S. tax reductions from marginal rates of 70% to 28% under Reagan and UK privatizations under Thatcher, boosted productivity and investment, contributing to non-inflationary recoveries.[46] These shifts challenged Keynesian consensus, highlighting fiscal discipline and supply-side incentives, though critics in academia often downplayed their causal role amid left-leaning institutional biases toward demand management.[47] Overall, the period underscored causal links between sound monetary policy, institutional reforms, and sustained growth, with high performers exhibiting resource allocation toward tradables and away from rent-seeking.

1990–2000: Acceleration via Trade Liberalization and Technological Advancements

The 1990s marked a period of notable economic acceleration for several nations, propelled by extensive trade liberalization measures and the adoption of technological innovations, particularly in computing and communications. Trade barriers diminished globally following the establishment of the World Trade Organization in 1995, enabling increased export volumes and foreign investment inflows, which correlated with higher GDP growth in liberalizing economies. Empirical analyses indicate that post-liberalization growth rates exceeded pre-reform levels by approximately 1.2 percentage points on average, with gains amplified in per capita terms up to 2.6 points through expanded investment and manufacturing output.[49][50] In emerging markets, China's sustained reforms toward market integration and export promotion yielded an average annual real GDP growth of about 10 percent from 1990 to 2000, as manufacturing sectors capitalized on access to global markets and low-cost labor attracted multinational firms.[24][3] India's 1991 liberalization package, which reduced tariffs and deregulated industries, shifted average annual GDP growth to roughly 6 percent per capita during the decade, driven by service sector expansion and improved trade openness.[51][52] Similarly, Ireland's low corporate tax regime and European Union market access fostered the "Celtic Tiger" boom, with GDP expanding at an average of 9.4 percent annually from 1995 to 2000 via foreign direct investment in high-tech exports.[53][54] Technological advancements, especially information and communications technology (ICT), underpinned productivity surges in advanced economies. In the United States, ICT capital deepening and total factor productivity gains from IT diffusion accelerated labor productivity growth to 2.5 percent annually in the late 1990s, supporting overall GDP expansion of around 3.2 percent per year amid the dot-com era's innovation wave.[55][56] European nations like Finland benefited from comparable ICT investments, though aggregate impacts varied due to slower adoption rates compared to the U.S.[57] These factors collectively elevated global growth, though the 1997 Asian financial crisis temporarily disrupted momentum in affected regions, highlighting vulnerabilities in rapid liberalization without robust financial safeguards.[58]

2000–2010: Commodity-Driven Expansion and Policy Divergences in Emerging Markets

The period from 2000 to 2010 marked a phase of accelerated economic expansion in many emerging markets, propelled by a sustained commodity price supercycle that elevated global prices for oil, metals, and agricultural products, primarily due to surging demand from China's industrialization and urbanization. Real GDP growth in emerging and developing economies averaged approximately 6.5% annually, outpacing the 1.8% recorded in advanced economies, with commodity-exporting nations experiencing particularly pronounced gains from terms-of-trade improvements. This boom, peaking between 2003 and 2008 when crude oil prices rose from around $30 per barrel to over $140, translated into windfall revenues that financed public investment and consumption in resource-dependent states, though it also amplified vulnerabilities to price volatility.[59][60] China exemplified non-commodity-driven growth within emerging markets, achieving an average annual real GDP growth of 10.6% through export-oriented manufacturing, fixed-asset investment, and integration into global supply chains, which absorbed rural labor and boosted productivity via technology transfers. In contrast, commodity-reliant emerging markets like Angola and Azerbaijan posted even higher rates—Azerbaijan's oil sector propelled averages exceeding 15% in the mid-decade—while Russia's growth averaged 7.2%, heavily tied to energy exports amid rising prices. India sustained around 7.5% growth via services liberalization and domestic reforms, underscoring policy choices favoring market access over resource dependence. These patterns highlight causal links: empirical analyses show commodity price surges added 1-2 percentage points to GDP growth in exporters through direct revenue channels and multiplier effects on investment, though without accompanying diversification, such gains often masked underlying institutional rigidities.[61][62][63] Policy divergences amplified growth disparities, as some governments channeled commodity rents into productive infrastructure and sovereign wealth funds—evident in Kazakhstan's partial success with oil stabilization mechanisms—while others pursued populist redistribution or nationalization, delaying structural reforms. In Venezuela, for instance, oil-dependent growth averaged over 5% but relied on state-controlled pricing and spending, fostering inflation and inefficiency without building resilience. Brazil's commodity-fueled expansion, averaging 4.1%, benefited from export booms in soy and iron ore but suffered from regulatory bottlenecks and fiscal expansion that constrained non-resource sectors. The 2008 global financial crisis temporarily halted momentum, with emerging markets contracting less severely (average -0.5% in 2009) due to lower financial leverage and countercyclical policies, yet recovery trajectories diverged based on pre-crisis institutional quality: market-oriented reformers like those in East Asia rebounded faster than interventionist Latin American peers. This era underscores that while exogenous price shocks provided tailwinds, endogenous policies—such as trade openness and fiscal discipline—determined sustainable outcomes, with data indicating that diversified emerging markets grew 1.5-2% faster post-shock than pure commodity plays.[64][65]

Causal Factors and Policy Insights

Role of Market-Oriented Reforms and Deregulation

Market-oriented reforms, encompassing deregulation of product and labor markets, privatization of state-owned enterprises, and liberalization of trade and investment, played a pivotal role in accelerating GDP growth across numerous countries from 1980 to 2010 by enhancing resource allocation efficiency, incentivizing innovation, and attracting capital inflows.[66][67] Empirical analyses indicate that such reforms spurred investment and productivity, with entry liberalization in product markets particularly effective in reducing barriers that stifled competition.[66] Cross-country studies further demonstrate that deregulation's growth impact varied by implementation timing and context, but overall contributed positively, explaining divergences in performance among OECD and emerging economies.[68] The Fraser Institute's Economic Freedom of the World index, which quantifies policy dimensions like regulatory restrictions and government size, reveals a robust positive correlation between improvements in economic freedom scores and real GDP growth rates over the period.[69] Nations that advanced in areas of deregulation and market openness—such as reducing product market regulations—experienced higher per capita GDP growth, with top-quartile countries averaging annual rates exceeding those in lower quartiles by 1-2 percentage points when controlling for initial conditions.[69][70] This relationship holds in panel data analyses, underscoring causation through channels like increased total factor productivity from diminished state intervention.[71] In China, gradual market reforms initiated in the late 1970s and intensified in the 1980s— including decollectivization of agriculture, establishment of special economic zones in 1980, and progressive deregulation of prices and foreign investment—propelled average annual real GDP growth to approximately 9.8% from 1980 to 2010, transforming the economy from stagnation to rapid industrialization.[72] India's 1991 liberalization package, which dismantled the "License Raj" through deregulation of industry, trade openness, and privatization, shifted growth from a Hindu rate of around 3.5% pre-reform to over 6% annually in the subsequent decades, driven by surges in exports (from 5% of GDP in 1991 to 20% by 2010) and FDI inflows.[73] Similarly, Chile's earlier reforms under the 1975-1990 period, featuring extensive deregulation, privatization of over 200 state firms, and trade liberalization, yielded average growth of 7% from 1984 to 1998, outperforming regional peers and sustaining momentum into the 2000s through institutional stability.[67] These cases illustrate how reforms mitigated institutional failures, though outcomes depended on complementary factors like property rights enforcement, with heterogeneous effects observed in less stable environments such as post-Soviet transitions.[68][67]

Impact of External Shocks, Resource Endowments, and Institutional Failures

External shocks, including commodity price volatility, debt crises, and financial contagions, significantly influenced GDP growth trajectories across countries from 1980 to 2010. The 1982 Latin American debt crisis, triggered by rising U.S. interest rates and falling commodity prices, led to severe contractions; for instance, Mexico's GDP fell by 0.6% in 1982 and 4.2% in 1983, while Argentina experienced a 5.4% decline in 1982, with regional growth averaging below 1% annually in the early 1980s.[74] Empirical analysis indicates that external factors, such as terms-of-trade shocks, explained 50-60% of GDP growth variance in Latin America during this period, underscoring vulnerability in export-dependent economies. Similarly, the 1997 Asian financial crisis caused sharp downturns, with Indonesia's GDP contracting 13.1% in 1998 and Thailand's by 10.5%, though recoveries followed structural adjustments. The 2008 global financial crisis further disrupted trade, reducing Asian GDP growth (excluding China and India) by nearly 15% annualized in Q4 2008, highlighting the amplifying effect of integrated financial systems.[74][75] Resource endowments presented a mixed impact, often manifesting as a "curse" rather than a blessing due to volatility and misallocation. Cross-country evidence from 1970-1990, extended into later decades, shows that resource-rich economies grew 1-2% slower annually than resource-poor peers, attributed to Dutch disease effects where resource booms appreciated currencies, crowding out manufacturing; for example, oil-dependent Nigeria averaged 2.5% real GDP growth from 1980-2010, hampered by export concentration exceeding 90% in hydrocarbons.[76] The 2000s commodity supercycle temporarily boosted resource exporters like Venezuela (averaging 4.5% growth 2000-2008) and Brazil (4.1%), but subsequent busts exposed overreliance, with Venezuela's growth turning negative post-2010 amid mismanagement. In contrast, diversified resource users like Australia mitigated curses through institutional buffers, achieving 3.2% average growth. Empirical models confirm that resource abundance correlates negatively with growth absent strong governance, as rents foster rent-seeking over productive investment.[76] Institutional failures exacerbated shocks and squandered resource advantages, with weak rule of law, corruption, and policy rigidity impeding sustained growth. Cross-country regressions over 1980-2005 reveal that improvements in governance quality—measured by control of corruption and regulatory efficiency—boosted per capita GDP growth by 0.5-1% annually, while failures in sub-Saharan Africa (e.g., Zimbabwe's hyperinflation and expropriations) yielded near-zero average growth despite resource wealth. In Latin America, persistent interventionist policies prolonged debt crisis recoveries, contrasting East Asian tigers where institutional reforms post-1980s shocks enabled 6-8% growth. Studies attribute up to 75% of growth divergences to institutional factors, as poor property rights and fiscal profligacy amplified external vulnerabilities, evident in Argentina's repeated defaults versus Chile's stabilization yielding 5% average growth after 1985 reforms.[77][77] Overall, these elements interacted causally: shocks tested resilience, resources tempted waste, and institutional weaknesses determined long-term outcomes, with empirical data favoring market-oriented frameworks over statist ones in averting failures.[77]

Debunking Oversimplified Narratives on Global Convergence

The hypothesis of unconditional economic convergence posits that poorer countries, starting from lower GDP per capita levels, would exhibit faster growth rates during 1980–2010 due to diminishing returns to capital and technology transfer, thereby narrowing global income disparities. However, cross-country regressions of per capita GDP growth on lagged income levels produced statistically insignificant or even positive coefficients—indicative of divergence—for the aggregate sample through the 1990s, as initial poverty did not reliably predict superior performance.[78][79] This pattern reflected stark regional divergences rather than uniform catch-up: East Asia and Pacific economies averaged annual per capita growth exceeding 5% in the 1990s, driven by export-led industrialization, while Sub-Saharan Africa recorded near-zero or negative rates amid debt overhang and commodity dependence, widening the gap with high-income countries that grew at 1.5–2% annually.[80] Latin America endured a "lost decade" in the 1980s with per capita contraction averaging -0.5% yearly due to hyperinflation and default crises, followed by modest recovery insufficient to offset earlier losses.[79] Overall, global dispersion in log GDP per capita (sigma measure) rose through 2000, peaking amid bimodal distributions separating advanced economies from laggards.[81] Post-2000 accelerations in China (averaging 9% per capita growth) and India contributed to apparent aggregate convergence, but these masked persistent stagnation elsewhere, with total factor productivity gaps—rather than mere capital inflows—sustaining divergence in non-reformers.[81][80] Empirical tests confirm "club convergence," where subgroups sharing institutional traits (e.g., secure property rights and low corruption) formed intra-group catch-up, but cross-club gaps expanded, contradicting narratives of inevitable globalization-induced narrowing.[82] Oversimplifications attributing non-convergence to external barriers overlook first-order causal factors like policy-induced misallocation: protectionist regimes and weak governance in many low-income states perpetuated low productivity, while reformers prioritizing deregulation and human capital investment achieved sustained gains. Academic literature, often rooted in neoclassical assumptions, has underemphasized these institutional prerequisites, with some analyses selectively highlighting successes to imply broader trends despite aggregate evidence of conditional, not unconditional, dynamics.[83][84]
