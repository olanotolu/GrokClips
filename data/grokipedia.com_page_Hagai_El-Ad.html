Hagai El-Ad

Hagai El-Ad (born c. 1969) is an Israeli activist specializing in human rights and civil liberties, best known for serving as executive director of B'Tselem, the Israeli Information Center for Human Rights in the Occupied Territories, from May 2014 to June 2023.[1][2] Born in Israel shortly after the start of the Six-Day War, El-Ad holds an M.Sc. in physics from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and conducted pre-doctoral research at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics.[1] Prior to B'Tselem, he directed the Association for Civil Rights in Israel for six years, where he opposed legislation perceived as anti-democratic, and served as the founding director of the Jerusalem Open House for Pride and Tolerance, advancing LGBT rights in the capital.[1] Under El-Ad's leadership, B'Tselem shifted toward more confrontational advocacy, including testimonies before the United Nations Security Council on settlement expansion and violence in the West Bank and Gaza, which prompted condemnation from Israeli officials, including Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, who accused him of aiding Israel's adversaries.[3][4] In January 2021, the organization issued a report concluding that Israel maintains a single regime of Jewish supremacy across Israel and the occupied territories, amounting to apartheidâ€”a characterization rejected by the Israeli government as a distortion intended to delegitimize the state and echoed in similar assessments by groups like Human Rights Watch but contested by legal scholars and allies emphasizing Israel's democratic institutions for its citizens.[5][6]

Early life and background

Family and upbringing

Hagai El-Ad was born in Haifa, Israel, in 1969 to parents employed as journalists and academics.[7] He grew up on the slopes of Mount Carmel, an area in northern Israel where his father, a journalist and environmentalist, lived.[8] During his childhood, El-Ad had virtually no awareness of Arabs in his immediate environment.[8] Accounts describe him as a talented yet asthmatic boy.[7]

Education and early career

El-Ad was born in Haifa in 1969 to parents who worked as journalists and academics.[7] He earned a bachelor's degree and a master's degree in physics from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem.[1][9] Following his military service, he secured a scholarship to pursue a PhD in astrophysics at Harvard University but returned to Israel prior to completion.[7][10] In the early 2000s, El-Ad directed the Jerusalem Open House, a community center focused on lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, and queer issues, where he advocated for LGBT rights amid societal challenges in Israel.[11][10] He assumed the role of executive director at the Association for Civil Rights in Israel (ACRI) in July 2008, leading the organization until 2014 and emphasizing public advocacy on civil liberties.[9][12] During this period, ACRI under his leadership pursued legal and policy efforts to protect rights in areas including freedom of expression and equality.[9]

Military service

El-Ad performed his mandatory military service in the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) in Unit 504, an elite field intelligence unit within the Military Intelligence Directorate responsible for human intelligence operations, including the cultivation of Arab sources in Palestinian territories and Lebanon.[7][13] He opted for this non-combat intelligence role, leveraging his Arabic language skills learned in high school, as an alternative to service in a frontline combat unit.[7] Former comrades in Unit 504 have characterized him as an excellent soldier whose work substantially advanced Israel's security interests.[13]

Activism prior to B'Tselem

LGBT rights advocacy

Hagai El-Ad initiated his advocacy for LGBT rights during his university years, volunteering for gay rights initiatives at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, which he described as a meaningful but peripheral activity at the time.[14] After completing his studies in physics and astrophysics, El-Ad became executive director of the Jerusalem Open House, an organization established in 1999 to promote the rights and welfare of lesbians, gays, bisexuals, and transgender individuals in Jerusalem.[15][7] Under his leadership, the Open House operated as a community center offering support services, particularly for LGBT teenagers facing social isolation and discrimination in the conservative religious environment of the city.[16][17] El-Ad's tenure involved organizing public events such as the annual Jerusalem Gay Pride parade, which encountered violent opposition from ultra-Orthodox Jewish and Muslim groups; in 2004, the event saw 2,000 participants amid protests and arson attacks on the center.[15] In March 2005, religious clerics mobilized against a planned international LGBT film festival and pride events hosted by the Open House, prompting El-Ad to characterize the backlash as a broader assault on pluralism in Jerusalem.[18] He sought international funding and solidarity for the center, emphasizing its vulnerability in a politically volatile region where LGBT advocacy intersected with wider struggles for tolerance.[17] Publicly, El-Ad framed LGBT rights work in Jerusalem as emblematic of fighting intolerance during Israel's security challenges, arguing in an October 15, 2003, speech at Yale University that such advocacy in the city advanced democratic values amid the Second Intifada.[11] His efforts contributed to incremental visibility for the community, though they highlighted persistent risks, including physical attacks and institutional resistance from municipal authorities influenced by religious parties.[16] El-Ad stepped down from the Open House role prior to his appointment as executive director of the Association for Civil Rights in Israel in July 2008, where his focus expanded but retained elements of LGBT concerns within broader civil liberties.[9]

Transition to broader human rights work

In July 2008, El-Ad was appointed executive director of the Association for Civil Rights in Israel (ACRI), Israel's longest-standing civil liberties organization, marking his expansion from LGBT-specific advocacy to encompassing a wider array of human rights issues affecting residents within Israel's sovereign territory.[9] Under his leadership, ACRI pursued legal and advocacy efforts on matters including freedom of expression, equality for minority groups such as Arab Israelis, and protections against discriminatory policies, reflecting a deliberate broadening of focus beyond sexual orientation rights.[19] This role positioned him at the forefront of challenges to government actions perceived as infringing on civil liberties, including surveillance practices and restrictions on protest.[20] El-Ad's tenure at ACRI, spanning six years until June 2014, involved direct engagement with contentious issues linking domestic civil rights to broader societal tensions.[21] Notably, on January 15, 2010, he was arrested alongside 16 other activists during a demonstration in Sheikh Jarrah, East Jerusalem, protesting planned demolitions of Palestinian homes and evictions favoring Jewish settlersâ€”a case highlighting intersections between housing rights, urban development, and ethnic disparities.[22] Such incidents underscored ACRI's docket under El-Ad, which extended to critiquing policies exacerbating inequalities between Jewish and non-Jewish populations, thereby transitioning his activism toward systemic human rights advocacy preparatory to his subsequent specialization in occupied territories.[12]

Leadership at B'Tselem

Appointment and key developments during tenure

Hagai El-Ad was appointed executive director of B'Tselem in May 2014, succeeding Jessica Montell.[1] Prior to joining B'Tselem, El-Ad had served as director of the Association for Civil Rights in Israel for six years.[21] His appointment came amid ongoing documentation efforts by the organization on human rights issues in the occupied territories, with El-Ad emphasizing field visits on his first day, including to the South Hebron Hills and Hebron.[1] During El-Ad's tenure, which extended until June 2023, B'Tselem intensified its international advocacy.[2] In October 2016, El-Ad addressed a special session of the United Nations Security Council, describing the Israeli occupation as permanent after nearly half a century and calling for international intervention.[23] He returned to the Security Council in October 2018 to brief on the deteriorating situation in Gaza, stating that "Gaza is imploding" amid restrictions and violence.[24] A pivotal development occurred in January 2021, when B'Tselem under El-Ad's leadership published a report titled A regime of Jewish supremacy from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea: This is apartheid, expanding its analysis to encompass all territory controlled by Israel and accusing it of maintaining a single regime of supremacy over Palestinians.[25] The report argued that Israel's policies constituted apartheid under international law, marking a shift in the organization's framing from occupation-specific violations to a broader systemic critique.[5] This position built on prior work but represented an escalation in rhetorical and analytical scope during El-Ad's directorship.[25]

Major reports and positions

Under Hagai El-Ad's leadership as executive director of B'Tselem starting in June 2014, the organization issued reports documenting alleged human rights violations by Israeli authorities in the West Bank, East Jerusalem, and Gaza Strip, often framing these as part of a systemic regime favoring Jewish Israelis over Palestinians. A landmark publication was the January 12, 2021, position paper titled "A regime of Jewish supremacy from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea: This is apartheid," which contended that Israel maintains a unitary regime across the entire territory it controls, defined by intentional Jewish domination and Palestinian subjugation, meeting the international legal threshold for apartheid through inhumane acts and systematic oppression. The report rejected characterizations of Israel as a democracy alongside a temporary occupation, instead describing a "one-state reality" of institutionalized supremacy sustained by laws, policies, and violence.[25][26] El-Ad publicly defended this assessment in outlets like The Guardian, arguing that apartheid in this context avoids overt racial segregation but enforces supremacy via fragmented governance, land expropriation, and differential rights, necessitating an end to the regime rather than reform. B'Tselem's claims drew from data on residency laws, movement restrictions, and resource allocation disparities, though critics, including Israeli officials and organizations like NGO Monitor, have contested the apartheid label as a politicized distortion ignoring security contexts and Palestinian agency in the conflict.[27][28] Earlier reports under El-Ad's tenure included analyses of military accountability, such as a joint 2010s-era examination with the Palestinian Centre for Human Rights of Israeli investigations into Gaza operations, which B'Tselem deemed perfunctory and protective of perpetrators rather than truth-seeking. In 2016, El-Ad contributed to critiques of the Israeli military justice system in the occupied territories, portraying it as a mechanism to legitimize occupation through low conviction rates for Palestinian complainants and shielding of soldiers. B'Tselem's 2014 annual report, issued shortly after his appointment, detailed civilian impacts from that summer's Gaza conflict, including over 2,100 Palestinian deaths (mostly civilians per UN figures cited) and calls for independent probes into alleged violations.[29][30][31] El-Ad's positions extended to international advocacy, such as his October 18, 2018, UN Security Council address citing 294 Palestinian home demolitions, routine minor arrests, and settler attacks amid settlement expansion, positioning these as entrenching control rather than defensive measures. Similarly, in a November 2020 European Parliament speech, he highlighted a large-scale demolition in Khirbet Humsah as emblematic of displacement policies. These stances aligned with B'Tselem's broader rejection of the two-state framework in favor of confronting what El-Ad described as a 50-year "constant exception" enabling indefinite rule without rights for Palestinians.[32][33][34]

International advocacy efforts

Hagai El-Ad has conducted international advocacy on behalf of B'Tselem primarily through direct addresses to United Nations bodies, emphasizing the need for global intervention to address Israeli policies in the occupied Palestinian territories. On October 14, 2016, he testified before the UN Security Council during a special session convened by Egypt, Malaysia, Venezuela, and Angola to discuss settlement construction, where he implored member states to enforce international law and end the occupation, warning that "anything short of determined international intervention will preserve the current reality."[3] In the speech, El-Ad detailed the human rights implications of settlements, including land expropriation and restrictions on Palestinian movement, framing the issue as a violation of international obligations binding on Israel.[3] El-Ad returned to the UN Security Council on October 18, 2018, amid discussions on the Middle East peace process, where he described the occupation as a "regime of apartheid" that denies Palestinians basic rights and called for targeted international measures, such as sanctions, to dismantle it.[32][35] He argued that the "constant exception" of Israeli control over Palestinians required accountability from the international community, stating that "it is very difficult, if not impossible, to fully convey the indignity, the outrage and the pain of a people denied the benefit of human rights protections."[32] This testimony aligned with B'Tselem's broader strategy under El-Ad to elevate the conflict's visibility in global forums, though it drew criticism from Israeli officials who viewed it as an attempt to delegitimize the state internationally.[28] Beyond UN appearances, El-Ad has promoted B'Tselem's positions through op-eds and interviews in international media, including a January 12, 2021, Guardian article coinciding with the organization's apartheid report, where he asserted that "the systematic promotion of the supremacy of one group of people over another must end" via external pressure.[27] These efforts aimed to reframe the Israeli-Palestinian dynamic as a single regime of unequal rights from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea, urging bodies like the UN and European Union to adopt policies conditioning relations with Israel on policy changes.[25] El-Ad's advocacy has consistently prioritized empirical documentation of alleged violations, such as home demolitions and settler violence, to substantiate calls for boycotts or diplomatic isolation targeting settlement activities.[3][32]

Controversies and criticisms

Accusations of apartheid and regime of supremacy

In January 2021, under the leadership of executive director Hagai El-Ad, B'Tselem published a position paper titled A regime of Jewish supremacy from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea: This is apartheid, asserting that Israel maintains a unified regime across the entire territory between the Jordan River and the Mediterranean Sea, including Israel proper, the West Bank, East Jerusalem, and Gaza.[25] The report claims this system systematically privileges Jewish Israelis over Palestinians through discriminatory policies in key areas, defining it as apartheid under the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, which describes apartheid as an institutionalized regime of systematic oppression and domination by one racial group over another with the intent to maintain that domination.[25] B'Tselem argues the regime's intent is evident in the denial of equal rights based on ethnic belonging, with Jewish supremacy enforced via laws and practices that prioritize Jewish demographic control and land allocation.[25] The paper outlines four main mechanisms sustaining this alleged supremacy: (1) citizenship and nationality laws granting automatic citizenship to Jews worldwide while denying it to Palestinians; (2) residency and land policies that allocate state land preferentially to Jewish communities and restrict Palestinian access; (3) differential freedom of movement, such as checkpoints and barriers in the West Bank absent for Israeli Jews; and (4) exclusion of Palestinians from meaningful political participation, with over 7 million Palestinians under Israeli control lacking voting rights in the institutions governing them.[25] El-Ad described the regime's tenets as "supremacy of Jewish citizens over Palestinians, regardless of where they live," rejecting Israel's self-characterization as a democracy upholding a temporary occupation.[26] He emphasized that the analysis encompasses Gaza despite Israel's 2005 disengagement, citing ongoing control over borders, airspace, and resources as evidence of sustained domination.[26] El-Ad has reiterated these accusations in international forums, including a 2016 address to the UN Security Council where he compared Israeli policies to historical segregation, though the 2021 report marked B'Tselem's first formal apartheid designation. In public statements, he has framed the system as one of "Jewish supremacy in transparent disguise," linking it to broader policies like settlement expansion, which B'Tselem documented as involving over 700,000 Jewish settlers in the West Bank and East Jerusalem by 2021.[36][25] These claims align with similar assessments by organizations like Human Rights Watch, which in April 2021 described Israeli policies as amounting to apartheid and persecution crimes, though B'Tselem's report focuses explicitly on supremacy as the regime's purpose.[6]

Responses from Israeli government and officials

The Israeli government has consistently rejected Hagai El-Ad's characterizations of Israeli policies as akin to apartheid, viewing them as distortions aimed at delegitimizing the state. Following El-Ad's October 18, 2018, address to the UN Security Councilâ€”where he compared Israel's control over Palestinians to the apartheid regime in South Africaâ€”Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu described the speech as "full of lies" and announced intentions to prevent B'Tselem employees from participating in national civilian service programs, arguing that the organization promotes anti-Israel narratives abroad.[37] Israel's permanent representative to the UN, Danny Danon, condemned the Security Council's invitation to El-Ad as a misuse of the platform, stating it rewarded efforts to undermine Israel's right to self-defense.[38] Similarly, in response to B'Tselem's January 12, 2021, report declaring Israel a "regime of Jewish supremacy" enforcing apartheid from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea, government spokespersons labeled the claims libelous, asserting that B'Tselem had forsaken human rights documentation in favor of political advocacy intended to erode Israel's international standing.[39] Officials, including members of the Knesset, have accused El-Ad of prioritizing foreign audiences over domestic accountability; for instance, Likud MK Oren Hazan publicly called for investigations into B'Tselem's activities after El-Ad's UN testimony, prompting counter-complaints but underscoring the domestic backlash.[40] Israeli authorities maintain that policies in the territories stem from security imperatives amid ongoing conflict and terrorism threats, not racial domination, and contrast this with Israel's democratic institutions, which afford full citizenship and rights to its Arab population comprising about 20% of citizens.[41]

Critiques from pro-Israel organizations and analysts

NGO Monitor, a pro-Israel organization that scrutinizes NGOs for political bias and factual distortions, has argued that under Hagai El-Ad's leadership since May 2014, B'Tselem shifted from human rights monitoring to an explicitly political and ideological campaign against Israeli policies, prioritizing "dissent" over verifiable evidence.[42] In a 2014 analysis, NGO Monitor highlighted El-Ad's June blog post calling for "bolder strategies" to challenge the occupation and a July email urging continued criticism amid public support for government actions during the Gaza conflict, framing these as evidence of partisan bias rather than neutral advocacy.[21] A key example cited by NGO Monitor is B'Tselem's June 22, 2014, "Hitching a Ride" campaign, launched amid the kidnapping and murder of three Israeli teenagers in the West Bank, which featured images of Prime Minister Netanyahu and Defense Minister Yaalon to critique military responses; the group described this as "callous and tasteless," exploiting a security crisis to advance an anti-occupation agenda and undermine Israel's efforts without addressing Hamas involvement.[21] During the 2014 Gaza conflict, NGO Monitor faulted B'Tselem under El-Ad for relying on unverified Palestinian eyewitness accounts labeled as "initial findings," issuing unsubstantiated claims of international humanitarian law violationsâ€”such as barring Israel from targeting Hamas leaders using human shieldsâ€”without legal or military expertise, and admitting potential errors in testimonies while emphasizing emotive narratives over analysis.[42] Regarding B'Tselem's January 12, 2021, report accusing Israel of maintaining a "regime of Jewish supremacy" amounting to apartheid across territory from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea, NGO Monitor contended that El-Ad's promotion of the claims via media and UN appearances exemplified demonization, crossing into efforts to delegitimize Israel's existence rather than critiquing specific policies, and aligning with broader anti-Israel campaigns.[43] The organization further noted El-Ad's participation in events like a 2017 UN forum promoting BDS and featuring antisemitic rhetoric as indicative of a biased agenda that prioritizes international advocacy against Israel.[44] CAMERA, another pro-Israel media watchdog, has described B'Tselem's reports under El-Ad's tenure as rife with "factoids, fibs, and fraudulence," particularly in claims of Israeli efforts to "Judaize" areas, which it argued misrepresent demographic policies and historical context to inflame opinion rather than inform.[45] These critiques portray El-Ad's direction as eroding B'Tselem's credibility through selective sourcing from Palestinian accounts, omission of security contexts, and adoption of legally contested frameworks like apartheid without empirical rigor matching the term's historical application to South Africa's racial segregation system.[42][43]

Post-B'Tselem activities

Departure and succession

Hagai El-Ad announced his departure as executive director of B'Tselem in April 2023, after serving in the role for nine years since May 2014.[46][47] The transition concluded in June 2023, marking the end of his tenure amid ongoing political tensions in Israel, though El-Ad described the decision as a natural progression after a decade of leadership focused on documenting human rights violations in the occupied territories.[2][48] B'Tselem's board selected Yuli Novak as El-Ad's successor, with Novak assuming the position on June 26, 2023.[2][48] Novak, previously the executive director of Breaking the Silenceâ€”an organization of Israeli veterans documenting military conduct in the occupied territoriesâ€”brought experience in advocacy against the occupation, pledging to continue B'Tselem's mission of resistance amid Israel's judicial reforms and societal shifts.[48] Under Novak's leadership, B'Tselem has maintained its focus on international reporting, including statements on events in Gaza as of 2025.[49]

Recent writings and public statements

Following his departure from B'Tselem in June 2023, Hagai El-Ad has published a series of opinion pieces in Israeli and international outlets, maintaining his focus on critiquing Israeli government policies in the West Bank and Gaza, historical aspects of Zionism, and the perceived complicity of Israeli political factions in Palestinian displacement.[12] In a September 16, 2024, New York Times op-ed titled "One Issue Where Israeli Extremists Are Mainstream," El-Ad argued that mainstream Israeli support for West Bank annexation and settlement expansion reflects normalized extremism, contrasting it with fringe positions on other issues.[50] El-Ad's contributions to Haaretz in 2025 included an August 24 piece, "The Zionist Left Never Stood in the Way of Transfer," in which he contended that Israel's Zionist left has historically acquiesced to Palestinian expulsion policiesâ€”referred to as "transfer"â€”despite rhetorical opposition, citing unheeded warnings from figures 35 years prior and linking past displacements to current Gaza operations involving heavy bombardment.[51] On September 10, he published "Zionism: 77 Years of Expulsion," framing Zionism as a continuous project of Palestinian removal since Israel's founding, exemplified by a recent Israeli Defense Forces directive deeming the evacuation of Gaza City "inevitable."[52] El-Ad has also written frequently for The Irish Times, addressing public opinion and accountability. In an August 26, 2025, article, he claimed that Israeli media and majority views dismiss reports of slain Palestinian journalists, medics, and starving children in Gaza as unpersuasive, attributing this to incitement against Palestinian lives.[53] A June 29, 2025, piece criticized U.S. sanctions on individual settlers as insufficient, asserting that the Israeli state itself functions as the primary settler organization.[54] On October 15, 2025, in "We Can Only Hope Donald Trump Is Right and the World Wins," he expressed cautious optimism that international pressure, potentially amplified under a Trump administration, could counter Israeli policies he described as defiant of global norms.[55] Public statements by El-Ad post-B'Tselem have largely aligned with these writings, including social media posts reiterating calls for ending occupation and critiquing expulsion rhetoric, though he has conducted fewer formal interviews in 2024â€“2025 compared to his tenure at the organization.[56] His positions continue to draw from empirical claims of systemic discrimination but have elicited criticism for oversimplifying complex security dynamics and historical contexts.[52]
