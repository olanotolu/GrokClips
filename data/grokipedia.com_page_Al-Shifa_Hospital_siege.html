Al-Shifa Hospital siege

The Al-Shifa Hospital sieges consisted of two major Israel Defense Forces (IDF) operations at the Al-Shifa Medical Complex, Gaza City's largest hospital, during the Israel–Hamas war: an initial raid commencing on 15 November 2023 and a subsequent encirclement and assault from 18 March to 1 April 2024.[1][2] The operations were predicated on intelligence that Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad had established the hospital as a command and control node, including an extensive underground tunnel network for directing attacks and holding hostages, a assessment independently verified by U.S. intelligence agencies.[3][4] In the November raid, IDF ground forces, supported by engineering and intelligence units, entered the complex after a multi-day encirclement, discovering weapons caches, military equipment, and entrances to tunnels extending beneath the facility, with video evidence released showing a blast-proof door and electrical infrastructure indicative of prolonged militant use.[5][6] Efforts were made to evacuate patients and staff prior to and during the operation, though the hospital's functionality was severely impaired.[7] The March–April operation, described by IDF sources as one of the war's largest single-site actions, involved surrounding the site, engaging entrenched militants in close-quarters combat within wards and basements, and detaining over 300 suspected Hamas and Islamic Jihad operatives while seizing additional arms and documents.[2][7] Post-operation assessments revealed extensive structural devastation to the complex, with underground excavations confirming Hamas's tactical exploitation of medical infrastructure for military purposes, though debates persist over the scale of the command center relative to initial claims.[8][9] These events highlighted broader patterns of Hamas embedding operations in civilian sites, complicating Israeli efforts to target combatants while adhering to international law on protected medical facilities.[3]

Background

Hospital history and infrastructure

Al-Shifa Hospital, Gaza's largest medical facility, was established in 1946 during the British Mandate period as a general hospital serving the region's population.[10] Originally comprising a modest structure, it expanded significantly over subsequent decades, including renovations in the 1980s that enhanced its capabilities as the primary referral center for northern Gaza, handling complex cases such as trauma, oncology, and intensive care.[10] By the early 21st century, the complex spanned approximately 50 acres with over 20 buildings, functioning as a hub for specialized departments including surgery, pediatrics, and maternity services.[11] The hospital's infrastructure included a capacity of around 700-800 beds, more than 20 operating theaters, and advanced diagnostic equipment such as CT scanners and laboratories, positioning it as the enclave's main trauma center capable of managing high volumes of emergency cases.[12] [11] Multiple basement levels beneath key structures provided storage for medical supplies, pharmaceuticals, and equipment, as documented in pre-conflict engineering assessments of the facility's layout.[13] Its central location in Gaza City, near major population centers and access routes, underscored its strategic role in the territory's healthcare network, serving over 2 million residents.[13] Prior to 2023, Al-Shifa operated under chronic constraints, including underfunding from international donors and internal resource mismanagement, leading to frequent overcrowding where patient loads exceeded bed capacity by factors of 2-3 times during peaks.[14] Power shortages and equipment shortages were recurrent, exacerbated by reliance on generators and limited maintenance, yet it remained the backbone for tertiary care in Gaza despite these operational limitations.[13]

Hamas control and prior military use

Following Hamas's violent takeover of Gaza in June 2007 amid the Fatah-Hamas conflict, the group established de facto administrative control over key institutions, including Al-Shifa Hospital, Gaza's largest medical complex with over 500 beds and extensive surgical capabilities. The Hamas-controlled Gaza Health Ministry assumed oversight of hospital operations, diverting resources such as fuel, medical equipment, and construction materials intended for civilian care toward military purposes, including rocket production; for instance, in February 2018, Israeli forces intercepted a shipment of purportedly humanitarian medical supplies en route to Gaza that contained dual-use chemicals and components for Hamas's long-range weaponry.[15] This dominance extended to personnel decisions, with Hamas influencing or appointing directors and senior staff to ensure alignment with its governance, as evidenced by the later scrutiny of Al-Shifa director Mohammed Abu Salmiya's familial and operational ties to the group.[16] Historical patterns of military exploitation at Al-Shifa predated the 2023 conflict, with documented instances of weapons storage, detainee holding, and operational use. During the 2014 Gaza War, Amnesty International reported that Hamas forces occupied abandoned sections of the hospital complex as a makeshift command post, stockpiling arms and confining suspected collaborators there, actions corroborated by Palestinian witnesses and Fatah-aligned sources describing the X-ray department repurposed as an interrogation facility.[17] United Nations inquiries from the same period confirmed Palestinian armed groups, including Hamas, launching rockets from or near hospital premises, such as Al-Shifa, violating international humanitarian law by endangering protected civilian sites.[18] These practices aligned with broader Hamas tactics since 2007, embedding military assets in civilian infrastructure to exploit legal protections under the Geneva Conventions, which stipulate that hospitals forfeit immunity if used for "acts harmful to the enemy," thereby shifting partial responsibility for any resulting risks to the perpetrator.[17] Such dual-use persisted into the early 2020s, with Israeli intelligence and U.S. assessments noting ongoing Hamas storage of munitions and operational planning within or beneath Al-Shifa, informed by defector testimonies and signals intelligence rather than public satellite imagery, which showed structural anomalies like unexplained underground construction but lacked open-source confirmation of tunnels prior to November 2023.[19] Hamas consistently denied these allegations, attributing hospital militarization claims to Israeli propaganda, though independent verifications like Amnesty's 2014 findings underscored a pattern of instrumentalizing medical facilities for combat advantages.

Intelligence assessments pre-November 2023

In October 2023, following the Hamas-led attacks on October 7, Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) intelligence assessments identified Al-Shifa Hospital in Gaza City as the primary headquarters for Hamas's Northern Gaza battalion, responsible for coordinating terrorist operations in the region.[20] These evaluations, drawn from signals intercepts, geospatial analysis, and human intelligence, indicated that Hamas had constructed an extensive underground tunnel network beneath the hospital complex, connecting it to adjacent leadership bunkers and weapon storage sites to facilitate command and control functions shielded from aerial surveillance.[21] On October 27, 2023, an IDF spokesperson publicly stated that Hamas was directing portions of its ongoing military campaign from infrastructure underneath Al-Shifa, emphasizing the group's systematic embedding of military assets within civilian medical facilities to exploit international legal protections.[20] United States intelligence agencies independently corroborated Israel's assessments in early November 2023, confirming through their own collection—including electronic intercepts and analysis of militant communications—that Hamas operated a command node under Al-Shifa for directing attacks and logistics.[21] A U.S. official with access to this intelligence noted on November 13, 2023, that Hamas had diverted fuel supplies intended for the hospital's generators to power underground operations and that fighters used the facility for staging and resupply, with evidence of hostages from the October 7 abductions being held in sublevels.[21][22] The White House echoed these findings on November 14, 2023, stating that U.S. intelligence showed Hamas concealing military operations and holding captives within Gaza hospitals, including Al-Shifa, as part of a broader pattern of dual-use exploitation.[22] These pre-operation evaluations prioritized empirical indicators of threat—such as verified militant movements, resource diversions, and operational patterns—over Hamas's categorical public denials, which lacked supporting evidence and aligned with the group's historical playbook of embedding forces in protected sites to deter counteraction.[4] Interrogations of captured Hamas operatives and released hostages further substantiated the presence of an active command infrastructure, including server rooms and planning hubs, beneath the hospital, informing the IDF's determination of an imminent operational necessity.[21]

November 2023 IDF operation

Initiation and initial clashes (15-17 November)

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) commenced a targeted ground operation against Hamas militants at Al-Shifa Hospital on November 15, 2023, encircling the complex after issuing evacuation warnings to non-combatants, including directives to proceed southward along specified safe corridors.[23] [1] This action followed intelligence assessments confirming active Hamas presence, with militants utilizing the site for operational purposes.[24] IDF forces initially clashed with armed militants positioned outside the hospital perimeter, neutralizing four Hamas fighters in the exchange before penetrating the compound.[25] [26] Upon entry, troops encountered additional squads of combatants and explosive devices within the premises, leading to the elimination of five more militants during close-quarters engagements on November 15 and 16.[27] Amid these tactical confrontations, the IDF coordinated partial extractions of patients and medical personnel from vulnerable areas of the facility, prioritizing those in critical condition while securing routes against militant interference. These efforts occurred concurrently with Hamas-reported fuel shortages threatening generator-dependent equipment, claims the IDF rebutted by citing evidence of substantial fuel stocks held by militants on-site and the allowance of initial fuel convoys into Gaza that day.[28] [25] By November 17, ongoing skirmishes had subsided sufficiently to enable further limited departures from the hospital grounds under IDF oversight.[29]

On-site discoveries and extractions

During the raid commencing on November 15, 2023, Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) troops discovered weapons caches concealed within the Al-Shifa Hospital complex, including AK-47 rifles positioned behind an MRI machine, RPGs, grenades, sniper rifles, and other explosives stored in basements and rooms.[30][31] Laptops and electronic devices containing military data were seized from areas including server rooms, as documented in IDF-released footage and statements.[31] IDF forces recovered the body of abducted IDF soldier Corporal Noa Marciano, aged 19, on November 17 from a structure adjacent to the hospital, following intelligence indicating she had been held and killed there after sustaining minor injuries on October 7.[32] No live hostages were rescued during the operation, though Israeli officials cited evidence of prior hostage presence via CCTV footage showing individuals being transported into the facility post-October 7 attacks.[33] Amid ongoing exchanges of fire with Hamas gunmen embedded among civilians, IDF troops confronted and eliminated several militants attempting to use patients and staff as cover, while securing the premises to prevent further hostile activity.[34] To mitigate risks to non-combatants, Israeli forces facilitated the evacuation of hundreds of patients, medical staff, and displaced persons from the site between November 18 and 22, coordinating safe passage southward under protection; this included joint efforts that transferred critical cases, leaving a reduced number of immobile patients behind initially.[35][36]

Operational withdrawal and immediate aftermath

The Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) concluded the ground phase of their operation at Al-Shifa Hospital and withdrew from the immediate complex on November 24, 2023, approximately nine days after entering the site on November 15.[36] This pullback followed the extraction of remaining patients via a joint UN mission amid reported nearby combat, with the IDF asserting that all primary threats had been neutralized, including the elimination of Hamas operatives using the facility.[36] [37] During the withdrawal, the IDF handed operational control of the site to local Palestinian authorities, releasing video documentation showing the above-ground hospital structures largely undamaged and functional, in contrast to assertions of extensive devastation.[38] To counter humanitarian criticisms, Israeli forces had delivered limited fuel supplies earlier in the operation for medical generators, though distribution was reportedly obstructed by Hamas.[39] In the short-term aftermath, drone surveillance by the IDF detected initial militant activity consistent with Hamas efforts to reoccupy the area, contributing to the site's contested status under Gazan oversight.[40]

Evidence of Hamas exploitation

Tunnel networks and underground facilities

During the November 2023 Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) operation at Al-Shifa Hospital, troops uncovered a tunnel shaft located beneath a structure adjacent to the hospital's MRI building, leading to a fortified underground passage approximately 55 meters long and 10 meters deep.[41][42] The shaft featured concrete lining, electrical wiring for powering operations, and ventilation systems, culminating in a blast-resistant door that IDF engineers breached using specialized equipment.[43][44] Video footage released by the IDF, viewed by international journalists, demonstrated the tunnel's non-hospital infrastructure, including reinforced walls unsuitable for routine medical storage or access.[45][46] This tunnel connected to Hamas's extensive subterranean network across Gaza, known as the "Gaza Metro," which intelligence assessments estimate spans 500 to 700 kilometers in total length, facilitating command operations, weapon storage, and fighter movement.[47][45] At Al-Shifa, the passage extended beyond the initial shaft into broader corridors used for evasion and logistics, with entry points strategically placed under protected sites to exploit civilian infrastructure for military advantage.[8] Engineering features, such as the blast door and independent power lines, indicated deliberate fortification for prolonged combat use rather than medical emergencies, as hospital basements typically lack such defensive hardening.[43] Subsequent analysis by structural experts, including examinations of exposed sections, confirmed the tunnels' primary military orientation: ventilation pipes, often sourced from repurposed medical oxygen systems, supported human occupancy over extended periods without surface exposure, a configuration inconsistent with auxiliary hospital utilities.[8][48] The IDF's findings, corroborated by on-site visits from outlets like CNN and Reuters, refuted claims of mere storage voids, highlighting instead a tactical integration with the hospital's layout for operational cover.[42][45]

Weapons caches and command structures

Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) troops, during their raid on November 15, 2023, discovered caches of weapons and military equipment concealed within patient care areas and administrative offices of Al-Shifa Hospital's MRI building and adjacent departments. These included assault rifles such as AK-47s, hand grenades, RPG launchers, explosive charges, and tactical vests stored alongside medical supplies and furniture, illustrating Hamas's practice of embedding armaments in civilian medical infrastructure to exploit protected sites.[49][50][51] In the same building, forces located rooms repurposed as an operational command center for Hamas, equipped with computers, communication devices, and documents indicative of military coordination efforts conducted from within the facility.[50][34] IDF-released imagery further revealed disguised access points to subterranean structures integrated into hospital corridors, including reinforced doors hidden behind cabinets in hallways near treatment wards, which facilitated Hamas's dual use of the site while compromising its legal protections as a non-combatant medical complex.[52][53]

Independent and allied verifications

In January 2024, U.S. intelligence agencies assessed that Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad used Al-Shifa Hospital as a command center to direct forces, store weapons, and hold a small number of hostages, with underground tunnels facilitating these activities; the assessment noted that Hamas largely evacuated the site prior to the Israeli operation but left behind fighters and infrastructure.[54][55] This evaluation drew from multiple intelligence streams, including signals intelligence, and aligned with prior U.S. statements confirming Hamas's operational use of the facility.[3] The New York Times independently verified Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) videos depicting tunnel sections under Al-Shifa, confirming their location within the hospital grounds through geolocation and visual matching against known site imagery, distinguishing them from prior Hamas-released footage of shallower structures elsewhere.[56] Similarly, The Washington Post authenticated IDF-released videos of a tunnel network beneath the hospital by cross-referencing entrance points, structural features, and surrounding terrain with satellite imagery and open-source data, establishing the tunnels' connection to the complex despite questions about their full operational scale.[57] U.S. assessments incorporated evidence of hostages held at the site, corroborated by IDF footage from November 2023 showing Hamas transporting captives into Al-Shifa buildings shortly after their October 7 abduction.[58] Intercepted Hamas communications further indicated command-and-control activities from within the hospital, including coordination of fighters and logistics, as referenced in U.S. intelligence summaries supporting the site's military exploitation.[59]

Humanitarian and structural impacts of November operation

Casualties among patients and staff

The Gaza Health Ministry, operated by Hamas, reported that 40 patients died at Al-Shifa Hospital between November 11 and 17, 2023, attributing the deaths primarily to power outages, fuel shortages, and lack of medical supplies during the IDF's encirclement and initial raid.[60] These incidents occurred mostly before IDF forces entered the main hospital buildings on November 15, with specific claims including the deaths of three premature infants and 29 others amid generator failures.[61] The ministry provided no independent verification, and such figures from Hamas-controlled sources have been contested for conflating combat losses with civilian fatalities or exaggerating direct causation.[62] IDF operational accounts indicated minimal direct casualties among patients from Israeli fire, emphasizing precision targeting of confirmed militants embedded within the facility and surrounding crossfire initiated by Hamas fighters using hospital structures for cover.[63] The military reported facilitating evacuations of over 200 patients and staff during the operation, coordinating with international aid groups to transfer critically ill individuals, including 31 premature infants on November 19, without recording intentional strikes on non-combatants.[64] IDF spokespersons attributed patient deaths largely to pre-existing conditions aggravated by the hospital's collapse under Hamas control—such as restricted fuel access and failure to permit earlier evacuations—rather than operational engagements, noting that militants' presence endangered sheltered civilians by drawing fire into patient areas.[1] Among staff, verified combat-related deaths were limited and often linked to dual roles in militant activities. For instance, IDF forces eliminated several individuals identified as Hamas or Palestinian Islamic Jihad operatives masquerading as hospital personnel during raids on November 15–17, including those armed and operating from administrative and medical wards.[37] No corroborated reports exist of widespread IDF-inflicted injuries or fatalities specifically among unarmed patients or dedicated medical staff, with post-operation assessments highlighting that Hamas's fortification of the site—via weapon storage and command posts—prolonged exposure to hostilities, indirectly contributing to vulnerabilities for those remaining.[65]

Temporary displacements and aid disruptions

The Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) issued an evacuation order on November 18, 2023, directing the approximately 2,500 internally displaced persons sheltering on Al-Shifa Hospital grounds to relocate southward to areas outside the combat zone, facilitating safer passage during the precision operation targeting underground Hamas facilities.[66] This followed earlier voluntary displacements, with thousands of civilians having fled the complex prior to the raid amid intensifying clashes around the site.[67] The IDF coordinated with humanitarian actors, enabling the Palestine Red Crescent Society to evacuate around 200 individuals, including patients and staff, from the hospital on November 14.[67] To mitigate aid shortfalls, the IDF delivered medical supplies such as baby formula and equipment to Al-Shifa during the operation, sharing photographic evidence of the provisions positioned for hospital use.[68] On November 12, prior to the full incursion, the IDF placed 300 liters of fuel at the complex's entrance specifically for powering generators to support life-sustaining equipment, countering assertions of a total aid blockade; however, Hamas denied accepting the delivery and was reported to have obstructed its transfer to medical teams.[39][69] Operational necessities led to brief halts in routine aid inflows and patient mobility, but IDF statements attributed primary care disruptions to Hamas militants who fired from within hospital premises and impeded civilian departures, complicating medical access and generator maintenance.[70] These interferences, per military reports, prolonged vulnerabilities for remaining patients rather than stemming directly from IDF restrictions, with fuel offers aimed at preserving essential services like incubators despite rejection.[71]

Media narratives and information warfare

Initial mainstream media outlets, including the BBC and The New York Times, expressed significant skepticism toward Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) claims that Hamas maintained a command center and tunnel network beneath Al-Shifa Hospital during the November 2023 operation.[72][73] The BBC, for instance, highlighted the potential for heightened international pressure on Israel absent concrete proof, while relying on Hamas denials and unverified hospital communications controlled by the group.[72] This coverage often amplified Palestinian health ministry statements—operated by Hamas—without noting the inherent credibility issues stemming from the group's control over Gaza's information flow and history of embedding military assets in civilian sites.[72] On November 15, 2023, the IDF released footage and photographs documenting rifles, grenades, suicide vests, and other combat equipment discovered within hospital premises, directly contradicting denials from hospital staff interviewed by outlets like Al Jazeera.[49][74] Hamas officials and hospital spokespersons, such as those cited in BBC reports, insisted the military hardware was planted by Israeli forces, a claim unsupported by forensic or independent analysis at the time.[75] Such statements, disseminated through Hamas-affiliated channels, exemplified coordinated efforts to portray the hospital as a purely civilian facility despite empirical evidence of weapon caches in plain view during the raid.[49] Social media platforms rapidly propagated unverified accusations of IDF atrocities, including claims of summary executions of patients and staff, with viral posts garnering millions of views before substantiation efforts.[76] These narratives, often sourced from anonymous Gaza accounts or Hamas Telegram channels, lacked corroboration from neutral observers and were later contradicted by U.S. intelligence assessments confirming Hamas's operational use of the site.[77] The U.S. National Intelligence Council, in a January 2024 declassified summary, affirmed that Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad exploited Al-Shifa's underground infrastructure for command-and-control functions, including weapon storage and fighter sheltering, based on signals intelligence, interrogations, and seized documents—evidence that partially validated IDF findings despite persistent media hesitancy.[77] This discrepancy underscores how initial reliance on biased Gaza-based reporting delayed acknowledgment of verifiable military exploitation.

March 2024 IDF operation

Prelude and encirclement (18 March)

The Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) launched a targeted operation at Al-Shifa Hospital on March 18, 2024, based on fresh intelligence indicating that Hamas operatives, including senior figures who had evaded capture in the prior November raid, had regrouped within the compound to resume command activities and store weaponry.[78][79] This assessment followed observations of increased militant traffic and preparations in the area, distinct from the extensive tunnel networks uncovered previously, signaling a reconstitution of operational capacity under the guise of the partially restored medical site.[80] In the hours leading to ground maneuvers, the IDF disseminated explicit evacuation directives to non-combatants sheltering nearby, employing airdropped leaflets, automated phone alerts, and posts on platforms like X (formerly Twitter) to instruct movement westward via Al-Rashid Street toward designated humanitarian zones in Deir al-Balah.[81][82] These warnings emphasized immediate departure to avert exposure to ensuing combat, with the IDF asserting that Hamas's deliberate embedding amid civilians necessitated such precautions to differentiate threats from protected persons.[83] Troops from multiple brigades, supported by armored vehicles and engineering units, proceeded to encircle the 50-acre hospital perimeter, establishing a cordon to seal off ingress and egress points while conducting initial reconnaissance with drones and precision fire to neutralize outer sentries.[78][83] This phase aimed to contain the estimated 40-50 barricaded fighters, including members of Hamas's Al-Qassam Brigades and Palestinian Islamic Jihad, thereby isolating the threat and facilitating phased entries without broad-area bombardment.[78]

Raids, arrests, and engagements (19-28 March)

Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) troops advanced into the interior of Al-Shifa Hospital's buildings during raids from March 19 to 28, 2024, systematically searching departments including the MRI center and emergency rooms where Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) militants were found hiding among patients and medical staff.[84][85] Engagements involved exchanges of fire with armed terrorists positioned in hospital wards and defenses, resulting in the elimination of approximately 200 militants in the hospital vicinity over the operation's duration.[2][78] Raiding units arrested over 500 confirmed Hamas and PIJ operatives during this phase, including senior figures such as those involved in operational planning and promotion of terrorist activities abroad, with detainees captured in patient rooms and storage areas.[86][87] Earlier in the operation on March 18, but with follow-on actions extending into the period, IDF forces eliminated Faiq al-Mabhouh, head of operations for Hamas's internal security apparatus, during an attempted arrest amid gunfire from his position.[78][88] In specific engagements, troops neutralized gunmen in close-quarters combat within medical facilities, recovering weapons caches concealed in bathrooms adjacent to the MRI scanner and other strategic hiding spots used by militants.[84] By March 20, IDF reports indicated 90 terrorists killed in the preceding day's actions alone, with ongoing sweeps yielding additional arrests and seizures to dismantle command nodes embedded in the hospital structure.[89] These operations prioritized precision to limit collateral damage to civilians and infrastructure while targeting threats firing from protected positions.[90]

Final phases and withdrawal (29 March-1 April)

Israeli forces continued targeted operations against remaining Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad militants within and around Al-Shifa Hospital from 29 to 31 March, focusing on final sweeps to dismantle command nodes and secure the premises.[2] These actions included close-quarters engagements, with the IDF reporting the neutralization of additional operatives hiding in hospital wards used as firing positions.[9] As the operation concluded, the IDF facilitated the evacuation of civilians, medical staff, and patients present in the complex, asserting that non-combatants were given safe passage corridors during the raid.[2] Remaining patients, described as emaciated and in critical condition, were carried out on stretchers following the withdrawal and transferred to partially functioning hospitals elsewhere in Gaza, amid reports of limited international coordination through local health authorities.[11] On 1 April, after confirming the elimination of the immediate terrorist threat—including over 200 militants killed and hundreds detained—the IDF fully withdrew its ground forces from Al-Shifa, marking the end of the two-week incursion.[91] Palestinian health officials, controlled by Hamas, promptly claimed the site was left in total ruin with hundreds of bodies recovered inside and nearby, though IDF assessments emphasized precise strikes on militant-held structures rather than wholesale demolition.[92]

Evidence from March operation

Additional tunnel and weapons findings

During the March 2024 IDF operation at Al-Shifa Hospital, troops uncovered multiple weapons caches concealed within the facility, including rifles, grenades, explosive devices, and ammunition stored in areas such as the MRI center, bathrooms, and maternity ward.[84] [93] These discoveries followed engagements with Hamas operatives who had reportedly regrouped in the complex, using it to store arms and conduct activities.[94] IDF forces also accessed and exposed sections of the underground tunnel network beneath the hospital during the raid, including routes connected to prior infrastructure identified in November 2023.[95] A March 24, 2024, IDF report from a search and rescue soldier detailed the tunnels' fortified nature, intended for militant concealment and operations, though independent verification of expanded access or specific capacities remained limited.[96] Supply-related items, such as manifests indicating diversion of hospital resources like electricity and oxygen to sustain underground elements, reinforced claims of the site's role as a Hamas logistics point, consistent with intercepted materials from the operation.[97] No documents directly tying the findings to October 7 planning were publicly detailed from this phase, though the caches aligned with patterns of Hamas armament storage in civilian sites.[98]

Detainee interrogations and Hamas leadership

During the IDF's March 2024 operation at Al-Shifa Hospital, interrogations of captured Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) operatives revealed the facility's role as a command center and safe haven for militants following the October 7, 2023, attacks. A senior Hamas operative, responsible for commanding over 140 terrorists, confessed that the group exploited hospitals like Al-Shifa because the IDF avoids direct strikes on such sites to minimize civilian harm, allowing militants to store weapons, conduct operations, and regroup without fear of immediate retaliation.[99] Similarly, captured PIJ members admitted to being stationed at Al-Shifa for months, using it to coordinate terrorist activities amid the post-October 7 displacement of fighters into civilian infrastructure.[100] Further interrogations highlighted Al-Shifa's operational significance, with a senior Hamas military intelligence officer detailing extensive terror infrastructure under and around the hospital, including planning for rocket launches and other attacks launched from the premises.[101] These admissions, extracted from detainees during the raid's active phases, corroborated intelligence indicating 600 to 1,000 Hamas and affiliated operatives had sought refuge there, treating the hospital as a protected fallback after initial battlefield losses.[102] In tandem with these captures, IDF forces eliminated key Hamas leadership figures embedded at the site. Troops killed two senior Hamas commanders and two additional wanted terrorists during close-quarters engagements within the hospital complex, disrupting local command networks tied to ongoing militant activities.[103] These outcomes, based on real-time interrogations and targeted strikes, underscored Al-Shifa's evolution into a post-October 7 stronghold for Hamas reconstitution efforts.

Post-operation site assessments

A WHO-led multi-agency assessment team accessed Al-Shifa Hospital on April 5, 2024, four days after the IDF's withdrawal, documenting severe structural devastation across the complex. The main hospital building was described as an "empty shell," with the emergency department, surgical wards, maternity unit, and neonatal intensive care unit heavily compromised by explosive damage and fires; walls were torn down, and the oxygen production plant was obliterated. Unexploded ordnance littered the site, posing hazards, while no functional medical equipment or patient care capacity remained, rendering the facility inoperable without extensive external intervention.[13] The assessment focused on physical infrastructure and did not report observations of tunnels, weapons caches, or other military artifacts, consistent with IDF statements that such elements— including concealed arms in the maternity ward and intelligence materials—had been located, seized, or neutralized during the operation's active phases. Independent verifications of operational-era findings, such as U.S. intelligence assessments confirming Hamas's prior use of the site for command functions and hostage-holding, remained unchallenged by the post-withdrawal evaluation, though critics have questioned the scale of militarization in earlier raids without direct evidence of fabrication.[77] Satellite imagery from Maxar Technologies, reviewed post-withdrawal, illustrated the site's transformation into a debris field, with collapsed buildings and craters encircling the core complex, underscoring the operation's targeted engagements against reported Hamas positions while leaving no visible remnants of fortified structures in accessible areas.

Overall consequences and investigations

Hospital destruction and reconstruction challenges

The November 2023 and March 2024 Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) operations at Al-Shifa Hospital inflicted severe structural damage, rendering the facility largely inoperable. A World Health Organization (WHO)-led assessment on April 5, 2024, described the complex as an "empty shell," with most buildings incinerated, collapsed, or irreparably damaged, and essential infrastructure such as oxygen plants and water systems destroyed.[13] The IDF attributed much of the destruction to Hamas's military use of the site, including tunnels and weapon caches that necessitated targeted demolitions during raids.[104] Palestinian health officials claimed over 95% infrastructure loss by January 2025, though such estimates from Gaza authorities warrant scrutiny given Hamas oversight of reporting.[105] Pre-war conditions exacerbated vulnerabilities, as Al-Shifa operated under chronic strain from Hamas governance since 2007, marked by equipment shortages, power outages, and overcrowding due to diverted resources toward military priorities over civilian maintenance.[106] Hamas's embedding of command tunnels—extending hundreds of meters beneath the hospital—further compromised the site's integrity, with evidence indicating militant fortifications contributed to collateral ruin during clearance efforts.[8] Reconstruction faces formidable barriers amid persistent conflict and governance voids. As of October 2025, international emergency medical teams provide limited orthopedic and critical care via makeshift operating theaters at Al-Shifa, serving around 10,000 people, but with staff at roughly 47% of pre-war levels and severe supply shortages.[107] Hamas's history of intercepting aid—such as diverting humanitarian trucks for militant use—undermines delivery, while Israeli security restrictions on northern Gaza access prolong delays.[108] Broader Gaza rebuild plans, like the Palestinian Authority's $67 billion proposal, highlight the scale, yet Al-Shifa's full restoration remains stalled by unexploded ordnance, funding gaps, and lack of stable administration free from militant influence.[109]

Mass graves and forensic analyses

Palestinian civil defense teams, operating under Hamas administration, reported discovering multiple mass graves at Al-Shifa Hospital following the IDF's withdrawal on April 1, 2024, with initial exhumations beginning on April 9 yielding at least 50 bodies from sites including courtyards and parking areas.[110] [79] These graves were described as makeshift, with some bodies reportedly bound, stripped, or showing gunshot wounds to the head or chest, claims attributed by Gaza officials to Israeli executions during the March 18–April 1 operation.[111] The IDF rejected these assertions, stating that no mass graves were created by their forces and that the sites were pre-existing burials from the November 2023 siege or casualties from combat engagements, with any disturbances resulting from tunnel excavations or battles rather than deliberate burials.[112] Forensic examinations were conducted primarily by local Gaza teams without independent international verification, limiting reliability; bodies exhibited advanced decomposition, complicating precise cause-of-death determinations, and no peer-reviewed analyses have confirmed systematic IDF-perpetrated killings at the site.[113] UN preliminary assessments expressed concern over unverified reports of tied hands and executions but noted the absence of on-site forensic access to substantiate claims, while emphasizing the need for protected crime scenes to preserve evidence.[114] Red Cross statements similarly called for unimpeded investigations but provided no conclusive findings on attribution, highlighting instead the broader collapse of Gaza's forensic infrastructure.[115] Hamas's documented practice of interring combatants in hospital grounds—evident in prior conflicts to exploit protected status and fabricate narratives—raises doubts about the graves' origins, as some Al-Shifa burials predated the March operation and aligned with patterns of internal executions or infighting among Palestinian factions, evidenced by pre-war reports of summary killings without external involvement.[116] [117] Absent neutral autopsies linking deaths directly to IDF actions beyond combat, the graves reflect chaotic wartime burials tied to hospital-area fighting rather than verified atrocities.[79]

Long-term health system effects in Gaza

The March 2024 Israeli operation at Al-Shifa Hospital resulted in extensive structural damage, rendering the facility non-functional with collapsed buildings, destroyed equipment, and no remaining operational departments, as assessed by a WHO-led mission on April 5, 2024.[13] This contributed to Gaza's broader hospital bed capacity declining from about 3,500 pre-October 2023 to roughly 1,500 by mid-2024, severely constraining care for trauma cases, chronic illnesses, and infectious diseases amid ongoing conflict.[118] Only 16 of 36 hospitals remained partially functional by early 2025, with critical care units like Al-Shifa's pre-war 17 beds fully occupied and then eliminated, exacerbating mortality from untreated injuries estimated at over 100,000 wounded requiring specialized services.[119][120] Underlying these capacity shortfalls, Gaza's health infrastructure faced chronic underinvestment under Hamas rule, which allocated billions in international aid—potentially including over $1 billion skimmed from UNRWA supplies—toward an extensive tunnel network exceeding 500 kilometers and weapons stockpiles rather than hospital maintenance or expansion.[121] Pre-war audits highlighted that cement and materials designated for civilian projects, including health facilities, were systematically diverted for military purposes, leaving Gaza's system with only 36 hospitals serving 2 million people at limited capacity even before hostilities intensified.[122] Israeli assessments post-operation linked such militarization directly to the necessity of raids, arguing that Hamas's embedding of command centers in hospitals prioritized combat readiness over public health resilience.[123] Long-term recovery demands addressing these root misprioritizations, with projections indicating generational health impacts from unaddressed infrastructure decay and elevated disease burdens unless governance shifts enable dual-use civilian-military safeguards to deter future weaponization of medical sites.[124] Rebuilding efforts, hampered by ongoing aid looting—up to 88% of UN-tracked trucks since May 2024—underscore the need for transparent allocation mechanisms to restore functionality without recurrence of pre-war vulnerabilities.[125]

Key controversies

Claims of excessive force and civilian harm

Palestinian health officials and hospital staff reported instances of Israeli shelling near patient wards during the November 2023 siege, attributing civilian injuries and deaths to IDF actions, including a projectile impact documented by witnesses that wounded two individuals inside the complex.[126] The Gaza Health Ministry, controlled by Hamas, claimed broader civilian harm from the encirclement and fighting, though specific verified figures for non-combatant deaths directly tied to excessive force at Al-Shifa remain unconfirmed by independent forensic analysis.[127] The IDF maintained that all engagements involved precision-guided munitions and targeted raids against confirmed Hamas positions within the facility, based on intelligence of militant activity, with operations designed to limit collateral damage through ground incursions rather than indiscriminate bombardment.[62] In the March 2024 follow-up operation, Israeli officials reported eliminating over 200 militants and detaining hundreds more without any civilian casualties, emphasizing surgical strikes on verified threats embedded among patients and staff.[11] These claims of overreach must be contextualized against evidence of Hamas embedding military infrastructure, including a 55-meter tunnel network directly beneath patient areas, which effectively positioned civilians as inadvertent shields and complicated IDF efforts to isolate combatants.[128] Military assessments of the broader Gaza campaign indicate civilian-to-combatant casualty ratios below 2:1, lower than historical urban warfare averages of 9:1, attributable to Hamas's tactic of operating from densely populated sites like hospitals.[129] Such integration of fighters among non-combatants inherently elevates risks to civilians during necessary counter-terrorism actions, though IDF protocols prioritized evacuation warnings and targeted elimination over area suppression.[130]

Allegations of executions and mistreatment

Palestinian testimonies reported by Al Jazeera claimed that Israeli forces executed unarmed patients, staff, and civilians in execution-style killings during the November 2023 siege of Al-Shifa Hospital, with survivors describing individuals shot in beds or while attempting to flee.[131] These accounts emerged from individuals who evacuated the complex amid ongoing combat, where Israeli forces encountered armed resistance and uncovered Hamas weaponry, including rifles, grenades, and ammunition caches hidden in the facility.[49] Independent forensic analysis of such claims remains limited due to restricted access to the site, though the presence of combat gear suggests many fatalities resulted from engagements with militants rather than indiscriminate executions. Allegations of detainee mistreatment, including beatings, electric shocks, and denial of medical care, were voiced by released Palestinians, such as Al-Shifa's director Mohammad Abu Salmiya upon his July 2024 release after seven months in custody.[132] Reports from human rights organizations like Physicians for Human Rights Israel documented patterns of abuse among Gaza detainees, attributing them to interrogation practices.[133] However, claims of systematic sexual violence against female detainees at Al-Shifa were fabricated, as evidenced by Al Jazeera's March 2024 retraction of a video alleging IDF rapes; Hamas subsequently admitted the story lacked basis following its own investigation, highlighting instances of unsubstantiated propaganda disseminated through media aligned with Palestinian factions.[134] Such allegations often originate from sources under Hamas influence or outlets with documented histories of retracting unverified claims, complicating assessments amid the absence of neutral on-site verification during active military operations. Empirical scrutiny reveals inconsistencies, as combat dynamics—evidenced by recovered militant infrastructure—undermine narratives of purely civilian targeting, while proven fabrications erode credibility of broader mistreatment assertions without corroborating forensic or multi-sourced evidence.[135]

Hamas human shielding and legal violations

Hamas systematically integrated military infrastructure into Al-Shifa Hospital, Gaza's largest medical facility, by constructing tunnel networks beneath it, storing weapons within its premises, and using it as a command-and-control hub.[54][55] U.S. intelligence agencies assessed with high confidence that Hamas and allied Palestinian groups operated from the hospital to direct forces and detain hostages seized during the October 7, 2023, attacks, with underground sites facilitating these activities until militants evacuated prior to the Israeli operation in November 2023.[54][3] Physical evidence included multiple tunnel entrances linked to the hospital's substructure, caches of rifles, grenades, and ammunition hidden in wards and basements, and fortified rooms adapted for operational use.[8][136] Under international humanitarian law (IHL), as codified in the Fourth Geneva Convention and Additional Protocol I, hospitals retain protected status only insofar as they exclusively serve medical purposes; their use for "acts harmful to the enemy"—such as harboring combatants, storing armaments, or coordinating attacks—forfeits immunity and exposes them to lawful attack proportional to the military advantage gained.[137][17] Hamas's deliberate embedding of command centers and tunnels in Al-Shifa constituted such misuse, transforming the facility into a military objective while endangering patients, staff, and nearby civilians through proximity to hostilities.[138] This tactic exemplifies human shielding, prohibited under IHL Article 51(7) of Additional Protocol I, where a party exploits the presence of civilians or protected sites to deter enemy operations or gain propaganda advantages by anticipating international backlash to resulting damage.[137] Such practices by Hamas align with patterns observed in prior Gaza conflicts, including the 2008-2009 and 2014 operations, where militants similarly repurposed hospitals like Al-Shifa for weapon storage and fighter concealment, prompting targeted responses once protection lapsed.[17] In the 2014 war, investigations confirmed Hamas tunnel shafts and arms depots under or adjacent to medical sites, enabling operations that integrated civilian infrastructure into defensive networks for operational cover and media amplification of casualties.[139] These violations not only breach IHL's core principle of distinction between combatants and civilians but also strategically incentivize sieges by rendering protected sites hybrid military-civilian zones, thereby shifting the causal burden of escalation to the defending party while pursuing asymmetric gains.[140]

Reactions and analyses

Israeli and allied government positions

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) initiated a targeted raid on Al-Shifa Hospital on November 15, 2023, asserting that intelligence indicated the facility served as the primary headquarters for Hamas's northern Gaza battalion, including command-and-control operations, weapons storage, and hostage holding.[34][141] The operation was described by IDF spokespersons as precise and limited to specific areas within the complex to neutralize militants while minimizing risks to patients and staff, with forces reportedly delivering medical supplies such as incubators, baby formula, and fuel upon entry.[34][141] Israeli officials, including Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's office, emphasized that Hamas's deliberate militarization of civilian infrastructure like hospitals constituted a war crime under international law, justifying the action as a necessary response to ongoing threats from the group's underground network, which they claimed extended 250 meters beneath the hospital with electrical and ventilation connections to the facility above.[97] The government maintained that such embedding of military assets in protected sites shifted responsibility for any civilian endangerment to Hamas, calling for the group's leadership to face accountability for exploiting medical facilities to shield operations.[97] The United States provided intelligence support for the raid and affirmed its lawfulness, with National Security Council spokesman John Kirby stating on November 15, 2023, that U.S. assessments corroborated Hamas's use of Al-Shifa as a command node.[142] In a declassified assessment released on January 2, 2024, U.S. intelligence agencies reiterated high confidence that Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad operated from the hospital complex, including tunnels for command activities and hostage detention, while noting that militants had largely evacuated prior to the IDF's arrival.[77][54] American officials underscored that targeting such militarized sites aligned with principles of distinction and proportionality, provided civilian protections were prioritized.[143]

Palestinian and Hamas responses

Hamas officials, including spokesman Izzat al-Risheq, categorically denied using Al-Shifa Hospital for military operations during both the November 2023 and March-April 2024 sieges, asserting that the facility served solely civilian purposes and that no tunnels, command centers, or weaponry were present under or within the complex.[144][145] These denials persisted despite reports of captured Hamas documents and interrogations of detained militants indicating long-term military exploitation of Gaza hospitals, including Al-Shifa, for storage, command, and fighter presence.[146][79] In response to the raids, Hamas accused the IDF of deliberately targeting and destroying Al-Shifa to eliminate Gaza's healthcare infrastructure, labeling the operations as war crimes against patients, staff, and displaced civilians sheltering there, with claims of hundreds killed indiscriminately.[147][148] These narratives, disseminated via Hamas-affiliated media and the Gaza Health Ministry, framed the sieges as evidence of systematic extermination and genocide, emphasizing the hospital's near-total ruin post-withdrawal—leaving buildings bulldozed, equipment wrecked, and over 400 bodies reportedly recovered—while attributing all damage to Israeli aggression without acknowledging prior militant activity.[9][145] Following the IDF's April 1, 2024, withdrawal from the second siege, Hamas released videos depicting its al-Qassam Brigades fighters preparing and firing shells from within the Al-Shifa compound toward Israeli positions, signaling a rapid reassertion of presence at the site to sustain operational and propaganda capabilities amid the ruins.[149] This reoccupation mirrored patterns after the initial raid, where U.S. intelligence confirmed Hamas's return to the hospital area, enabling continued use despite the devastation.[92]

International organizations and media scrutiny

The World Health Organization (WHO) described Israel's November 2023 raid on Al-Shifa Hospital as "totally unacceptable," emphasizing the risks to patients and staff amid ongoing hostilities, while leading subsequent assessment missions that documented extensive damage to the facility, including the loss of critical infrastructure like oxygen supplies and surgical capabilities.[150] [66] Similarly, United Nations agencies, including through WHO-led efforts, reported in April 2024 that the hospital lay in ruins after repeated operations, with the UN calling for an end to the "systematic dismantling" of Gaza's healthcare system, though these statements focused primarily on the humanitarian toll without independently verifying Israeli claims of Hamas's military exploitation of the site.[151] [152] Human Rights Watch (HRW) and Amnesty International critiqued the raid's proportionality, with HRW stating on November 16, 2023, that publicly released Israeli evidence—such as images of weapons caches and a tunnel entrance—did "not appear sufficient" to justify revoking the hospital's protected status under international law, urging independent investigations into potential violations.[153] Amnesty echoed this on November 23, 2023, asserting it had "so far not seen any credible evidence" supporting Israel's assertion of a major Hamas command center beneath Al-Shifa, framing the operation as a "devastating attack on human rights" that exacerbated Gaza's medical crisis.[154] These organizations' assessments, however, relied heavily on Palestinian health ministry data controlled by Hamas and did not incorporate later U.S. intelligence evaluations confirming Hamas's use of the hospital for command operations and hostage holding.[54] Media coverage initially exhibited widespread skepticism toward Israeli assertions, with BBC Verify analyzing IDF-released footage in November 2023 and concluding it had "yet to produce evidence of the tunnels" claimed as a Hamas headquarters, while outlets like The Guardian reported that the evidence "falls well short" of proving a central command node.[72] [155] This phase often amplified hospital director Mohammad Abu Salmiya's denials—later contradicted by his own admissions of Hamas fighters' presence—and Hamas statements rejecting military use, reflecting a pattern where Western media deferred to Gaza-based sources without on-site verification amid restricted access.[156] Subsequent reporting showed partial shifts, including BBC acknowledgments of IDF-released CCTV from November 20, 2023, depicting Hamas operatives escorting hostages into the hospital premises, alongside U.S. assessments validating underground infrastructure for militant activities, though calls for probes persisted predominantly on Israeli conduct rather than Hamas's instrumentalization of protected sites.[157] [54] Such scrutiny highlighted tensions between immediate humanitarian reporting and empirical validation of dual-use claims, with NGOs and press often prioritizing damage documentation over pre-raid intelligence indicating Hamas's long-term embedding in Al-Shifa.[54]
