Quaranta is the fifth studio album by American rapper Danny Brown, released on November 17, 2023, by Warp Records. It is Brown's solo follow-up to 2019's U Know What I'm Sayin? , and his second album of 2023 following his collaboration with JPEGMafia, Scaring the Hoes. A deluxe edition was released on October 18, 2024, featuring additional tracks including instrumentals.[1] The album includes collaborations with Bruiser Wolf, Kassa Overall and Mike,[2] and was preceded by the lead single "Tantor".[3]

Quaranta (Danny Brown album)

Background

Brown first teased the album in October 2020 when he shared a clip of music with the caption "XXXX". He later clarified it was titled Quaranta and not 40.[4] The title means "40" in Italian, and has been described as a "spiritual bookend" to Brown's 2011 album XXX.[3] Brown worked with producers including the Alchemist, Quelle Chris, Skywlkr and Paul White on the album.[3] The album was largely recorded at the Bruiser Brigade house in Detroit during a period of personal hardship, including a breakup, isolation, and financial difficulties, while Brown was grappling with addiction.[5] In 2021, Brown relocated from Detroit to Austin, Texas. He entered rehab in late March 2023 after a severe episode of substance abuse and has since maintained sobriety, which influenced the album's themes.[5] Despite being completed around 2021, the album faced delays, with Brown publicly accusing his label Warp Records and management of shelving it in March 2023.[6] Brown has called it his "most personal" album, on which he "explor[es] his experiences with pain, isolation, and hitting rock bottom". He called it his "way of getting shit out" after spending time in rehab to recover from drug and alcohol addiction, and explained that "it was almost like, if I died, this is what I have to say".[7] A deluxe edition of the album was released on October 18, 2024, adding two new tracks, "Dark Sword Angel" and "Jenn's Terrific Vacation", along with four instrumentals.[1]

Quaranta received a score of 82 out of 100 on review aggregator Metacritic based on 16 critics' reviews, indicating "universal acclaim".[9] The album was included on several critics' year-end lists for 2023, such as The Line of Best Fit's best albums, Northern Transmissions' best albums, and Robert Christgau's Dean's List.[20] Editors at Stereogum chose this for Album of the Week, with critic Tom Breihan writing that "if you listen to the last five Danny Brown albums in sequence, it sounds like an absolute model of graceful aging within rap [...] so Quaranta feels like a time-capsule portrait of a great rapper teetering on the edge of oblivion" and he considered it "expansive enough to get lost in".[21] Exclaim! 's Nicholas Sokic wrote that "Brown brings a depth of introspectionâ€”and a healthy dose of frictionâ€”to the well-worn idea" of "music-as-lifeline", which Sokic felt allows Brown to "min an autobiographical clarity not present to the same degree in previous projects", remarking that he is "just a little less wild and a whole lot wiser" and "better for it".[12] Reviewing the album for Rolling Stone , Mosi Reeves stated that the "last five songs on Quaranta feel like a AAA (sic) session defined by confessions and naked vulnerability", concluding that it is "no matter if his fans embrace this newly restrained alt-rap hero or not," as "it's better to be alive and underrated than glorified and dead".[22] Mehan Jayasuriya of Pitchfork found that "on the more diaristic songs, the narratives aren't as vivid, the rapping isn't as nimble, and the songs lack momentum" and its "bright spots aren't enough to lift the album out of its dour funk. Growth can be awkward, and for better or worse, Quaranta feels like an apt reflection of this process".[18] Assessing the album for AllMusic, Fred Thomas concluded that, "It's not entirely the same rowdy, lascivious joyriding that made up some of his celebrated early work, but the album's fearless expression of a full emotional spectrum makes it remarkable and at times shatteringly beautiful."[10]
