List of current cardinals

The list of current cardinals enumerates the living members of the College of Cardinals, the assembly of senior clergy in the Roman Catholic Church appointed by the pope to serve as his primary council and to elect a successor upon the Holy See's vacancy. As of 20 October 2025, the college consists of 246 cardinals, divided into the orders of cardinal bishops, cardinal priests, and cardinal deacons, with 127 eligible as electors due to their age under 80 years.[1] These prelates, drawn from bishops and priests worldwide, reflect the Church's universal character, though their composition has shifted toward greater representation from Asia, Africa, and Latin America in recent decades under successive popes.[2] The cardinals convene in consistories for major decisions and rituals, such as creating new members, underscoring their role in governance amid ongoing debates over the college's size exceeding the traditional limit of 120 electors established by Pope Paul VI.

Role and Eligibility

Historical Role of Cardinals

The origins of the cardinalate trace to the principal clergy of the Diocese of Rome, where the term "cardinal" (from cardo, meaning "hinge") first applied to deacons overseeing the city's seven ecclesiastical regions by the early 6th century, as well as to priests serving the major basilicas and titular churches.[3] These figures, drawn from the Roman presbytery and diaconate, functioned as advisors and administrators under the bishop of Rome, handling liturgical, charitable, and jurisdictional duties amid the city's division into regiones for ecclesiastical governance.[4] By the 8th-9th centuries, cardinal-bishops emerged from suburban sees like Ostia and Albano, further structuring the group as a corps assisting in synodal decisions and papal succession.[4] The cardinalate's electoral role formalized in the 11th century amid reforms countering secular interference in papal selection. Pope Nicholas II's decree In Nomine Domini, promulgated at the Lateran Synod on April 13, 1059, vested the principal right to elect the pope in the cardinal-bishops, with input from lower clergy and laity restricted to acclamation, effectively excluding imperial vetoes that had plagued elections like that of 1046.[5] This shift, extended to the full college by subsequent practice, was bolstered under Pope Gregory VII (1073â€“1085), whose Dictatus Papae (1075) asserted papal supremacy and reinforced cardinals as key enforcers of ecclesiastical independence against lay investiture.[6] The 12th-century expansion under popes like Innocent II diversified membership beyond Roman natives, incorporating non-Italian bishops to broaden representation while centralizing governance in the curia.[4] By the late medieval and early modern periods, cardinals assumed broader advisory and administrative functions, acting as papal legates, judges in the Roman Rota, and overseers of dioceses in commendam. Numbers fluctuated with political exigenciesâ€”dipping to seven under Alexander IV (1254â€“1261) during crises, then rising amid the Western Schism (1378â€“1417) as rival claimants elevated supportersâ€”but stabilized under Pope Sixtus V's Postquam verus (December 3, 1586), which capped the college at 70: six cardinal-bishops, 50 cardinal-priests, and 14 cardinal-deacons, emulating biblical precedents like the 70 elders of Israel to curb proliferation and factionalism.[4][3] This limit, though often approached but not always reached due to mortality, underscored the college's evolution from local Roman aides to an international body pivotal in sustaining papal authority through election and counsel.[7]

Modern Functions and Eligibility Criteria

The primary function of the College of Cardinals in the modern Catholic Church is to elect a successor to the pope upon the vacancy of the Apostolic See, conducted in a conclave according to the norms established in the apostolic constitution Universi Dominici Gregis, promulgated by Pope John Paul II on 22 February 1996. This document specifies that the election requires a two-thirds supermajority of the cardinal electors present and voting, with provisions for secrecy, balloting procedures, and the burning of ballots to signal progress or completion. Eligibility to participate as an elector is restricted to cardinals who have not yet completed their 80th year of age at the time the Apostolic See becomes vacant, a criterion introduced by Pope Paul VI through the motu proprio Ingravescentem aetatem on 22 November 1970 and reaffirmed in subsequent legislation. Cardinals elevated to the College after reaching age 80 are ineligible to vote in any conclave, while those created beforehand retain voting rights until their 80th birthday.[8] Cardinals over 80, though barred from the conclave, continue to hold their titular rank, participate in advisory consistories, and may fulfill other ecclesiastical roles unless otherwise restricted.[8] In addition to papal elections, cardinals serve secondary advisory functions, assisting the pope collegially through ordinary and extraordinary consistories as outlined in Canon 353 of the Code of Canon Law.[8] Many cardinals head departments of the Roman Curia, govern major archdioceses, or contribute to doctrinal and pastoral governance, functioning as principal counselors to the supreme pontiff.[8] Pope Paul VI established a nominal limit of 120 cardinal electors in 1973 to maintain a manageable electoral body, but this has been routinely exceeded by subsequent popes; as of 20 October 2025, there are 127 electors among 246 living cardinals.[1] The age threshold aims to inject vitality and contemporary perspectives into the conclave but has drawn critique from some canonists and Church observers for diminishing the influence of seasoned wisdom accumulated over decades of service.[1]

Non-Electing Cardinals (Age 80 and Over)

As of 20 October 2025, 119 cardinals aged 80 or older form the non-electing segment of the College of Cardinals, out of 246 living members total.[1] These prelates lost voting eligibility upon reaching 80, per the apostolic constitution Ingravescentem aetatem issued by Pope Paul VI on 22 November 1970, which aimed to infuse conclaves with perspectives from active, recently appointed bishops while preserving the cardinals' lifetime dignity. Though barred from papal elections, non-electing cardinals retain precedence in the College's order, hold their diaconal or presbyteral titles in perpetuity, and contribute to non-conclave consistories, liturgical ceremonies, and informal counsel to the pontiff and Curia. Many occupy emeritus roles from prominent dioceses or Vatican offices, embodying institutional memory; however, their electoral exclusion concentrates conclave influence among under-80 members, whose appointments often align closely with the creating pope's priorities, thereby constraining the weight of prior generations' views in selecting successors.[12] Seniority among them underscores historical depth, with the oldest being Michael Michai Kitbunchu (born 25 January 1929, age 96), elevated to cardinal by Pope John Paul II on 2 February 1983 and Archbishop Emeritus of Bangkok following service from 1973 to 2009. Geographical Distribution

The geographical distribution of living cardinals has evolved significantly, with Europe maintaining numerical primacy but declining in relative share from over 70% of electors in pre-2000 conclaves to approximately 38% as of October 2025.[10] This shift underscores a deliberate emphasis on diversifying the College to include voices from Africa, Asia, and Latin America, aligning with post-Vatican II efforts to reflect the Church's global demographics where only about 24% of Catholics reside in Europe.[2]

Distribution by Religious Affiliation and Orders

Of the 246 living cardinals as of October 2025, 239 belong to the Latin Church, while 7 are from Eastern Catholic Churches sui iuris, comprising approximately 2.8% of the total.[19][12] These Eastern cardinals hail from diverse rites, including the Syro-Malabar (two cardinals), Ukrainian (one), Chaldean (one), Maronite (one), Syro-Malankara (one), and Ethiopic (one), reflecting limited but deliberate representation of the Catholic Church's 23 Eastern rites amid the Latin Rite's dominance.[19] The vast majority of cardinalsâ€”181, or about 73.6%â€”are diocesan clergy, typically serving as archbishops or bishops in territorial sees without vows of religious orders.[20] The remaining 65 cardinals (26.4%) are members of religious institutes of consecrated life, a proportion elevated under Pope Francis, whose consistories have increasingly drawn from orders, with nearly half of his December 2024 appointees from such backgrounds.[20][21] Prominent orders include the Salesians of Don Bosco and the Jesuits, which together account for 19 cardinals, underscoring their influence in global evangelization and curial roles, though critics note potential overemphasis on Latin American and missionary-oriented congregations relative to traditional European monastic traditions.[20]

Distribution by Age and Seniority

As of October 20, 2025, the 127 cardinal electors eligible to participate in a conclave range in age from 45 to 79 years, reflecting a balance between emerging voices and seasoned leaders within the College of Cardinals.[23] The youngest elector is Mykola Bychok, Archbishop of Melbourne, born in 1980, whose appointment underscores the inclusion of relatively junior clergy in recent consistories.[23] The average age among these electors stands at approximately 70 years, lower than in prior decades due to systematic elevations of bishops and priests in their 50s and 60s, which extends potential voting tenures beyond the 80-year threshold.[10] This younger profile, compared to the higher averages under Popes John Paul II and Benedict XVIâ€”where electors often exceeded 71 years on averageâ€”facilitates institutional renewal while preserving experiential depth from those created in the late 2000s and early 2010s.[10] Seniority among electors is heavily weighted toward post-2013 creations, with over 80% appointed during Pope Francis's pontificate across ten consistories from 2014 to 2024, clustering elevations in the 2010s and emphasizing peripheries over traditional curial seniority. Pre-2013 cardinals, numbering fewer than 20 electors, represent holdovers from earlier eras, providing continuity in canonical expertise but diminishing in influence as age-related ineligibility acceleratesâ€”typically 1-2 per month in recent years, with 14 turning 80 in 2025 alone.[24] This distribution highlights a trade-off: enhanced demographic vitality through lower average ages enables longer collective service (potentially 10-15 years for mid-career appointees), yet it reduces the proportion of electors with decades of Vatican governance experience, as evidenced by the scant representation from consistories before 2000.[25] Age cohorts further illustrate this dynamic, with roughly 15% under 60 (enabling up to two decades of future eligibility), 40% in their 60s, and the remainder approaching 80, per breakdowns from early 2025 adjusted for subsequent aging and losses.[24] Such stratification supports empirical stability in conclave sizes near the 120-vote limit set by Paul VI, as younger inflows offset outflows, though it amplifies the impact of any pontiff's appointing preferences on long-term composition.[10]

Recent Developments and Appointments

December 2024 Consistory

Pope Francis announced on October 6, 2024, during the Sunday Angelus, his intention to create 21 new cardinals at an ordinary public consistory, later held on December 7, 2024, in St. Peter's Basilica.[11][26] Of these, 20 were under age 80 and thus eligible as cardinal electors in a conclave, while Angelo Acerbi, a retired apostolic nuncio aged 88, was the sole non-voting appointee.[27] The selections drew from 17 nations across Latin America, Asia, Africa, Europe, and Oceania, reflecting the pope's stated aim to foster visible unity in a diverse global Church by elevating leaders from peripheral or historically underrepresented regions.[17][28] In his consistory homily, Francis emphasized humility and fidelity to Christ, instructing the new cardinals to "walk in the path of Jesus" amid worldly seductions of power and to prioritize service over clerical ambition.[29][26] The appointees included archbishops of major dioceses such as Carlos Gustavo Castillo Mattasoglio of Lima, Peru; Tarcisio Isao Kikuchi of Tokyo, Japan; Francis Leo of Toronto, Canada; and Domenico Battaglia of Naples, Italyâ€”the latter added to the list on November 5, 2024, and noted for his prior anti-organized crime advocacy as bishop of Cerreto Sannita-Telese-Sant'Agata de' Goti.[30][31] Other strategic choices encompassed figures from conflict-affected or minority contexts, including Joseph Mathieu of Tehran-Ispahan, Iran; Jean-Paul Vesco of Algiers, Algeria; Ladislav Nemet of Belgrade, Serbia; and Mykola Bychok of the Ukrainian Eparchy of Melbourne, Australia.[11] Curial and pastoral roles were also represented, such as Baldassare Reina, vicar general of Rome; Rolandas Makrickas, coadjutor archpriest of St. Mary Major Basilica; and under-secretaries Fabio Baggio of the Dicastery for Promoting Integral Human Development and George Jacob Koovakad of the Secretariat of State.[11] Non-episcopal elevations included Dominican theologian Timothy Peter Joseph Radcliffe, previously master of the order and a consultant on dialogue with LGBT communities.[11] These picks aligned with Francis' pattern of prioritizing synodality, missionary outreach to peripheries, and integration of Curial reformers, thereby elevating 110 of the 140 post-consistory electors personally appointed by him.[32]

Post-2025 Conclave Adjustments

The May 2025 papal conclave, held from May 7 to 8 following Pope Francis's death on April 21, 2025, involved 133 participating cardinal electors out of 135 eligible under the age of 80, resulting in the election of Cardinal Robert Francis Prevost as Pope Leo XIV.[33][34] Prevost, previously prefect of the Dicastery for Bishops, became the first U.S.-born pope and selected the name Leo XIV, signaling continuity in certain pastoral emphases while introducing an American perspective to the papacy.[35] No consistory for new cardinal creations was announced immediately by the new pontiff.[36] Since the conclave, the number of cardinal electors has declined to 127 as of October 2025, primarily due to eight cardinals reaching age 80 and thus becoming ineligible for future conclaves under norms established by Pope Paul VI in 1970 and reaffirmed in Universi Dominici Gregis.[37] Notable examples include Cardinal Carlos Osoro Sierra (May 16, 2025) and Cardinal Silvano Maria Tomasi (October 12, 2025), whose aging out reflects the natural attrition in the college absent new appointments.[38][39] This reduction, from 135 pre-conclave to the current figure, has not been offset by elevations, leaving the electorate at its lowest since early in Pope Francis's reign.[40] Pope Leo XIV's initial months have focused on administrative continuity and select liturgical permissions, such as expanded Latin Mass provisions in St. Peter's Basilica announced October 25, 2025, rather than rapid reconfiguration of the College of Cardinals.[41] The current composition thus continues to mirror Pope Francis's extensive appointmentsâ€”over 80% of electorsâ€”prioritizing geographical diversity from Asia, Africa, and Latin America, with potential for rebalancing toward curial experience or doctrinal emphases under the new pope remaining unrealized as of late October 2025.[42] No consistory has been scheduled, preserving the Francis-era imprint amid ongoing demographic shifts.

Controversies in Composition and Appointments

Geographical and Ideological Shifts

Under Pope Francis, the share of cardinal electors from Europe declined from about 50% at the time of his 2013 election to approximately 39% by the 2025 conclave, with the absolute number of European electors standing at 53 out of 135 eligible voters.[16][43] This reduction stemmed from Francis appointing 80% of the electors, prioritizing regions outside Europe, including a rise in Asia-Pacific representation to 18% from 10% in 2013 and Sub-Saharan Africa to 12%.[10][43] Such increases occurred despite Catholics comprising only about 11% of the global total in Asia and 17% in Africa, leading critics to argue that the overrepresentation of "peripheries" reflects a deliberate emphasis on social justice priorities over the numerical and historical weight of European dioceses facing rapid secularization.[44] Ideologically, these geographical changes have fueled debates over doctrinal orientation, with traditionalist commentators contending that Francis's selections diluted emphasis on orthodoxy in favor of synodalityâ€”a process promoting decentralized discernment and dialogue.[45] Figures like Cardinal Robert Sarah, a prominent African critic of liturgical reforms, have highlighted appointments that appear to marginalize voices prioritizing immutable teachings on marriage, liturgy, and moral theology, potentially engineering an electoral body more amenable to progressive shifts.[46] Proponents counter that enhanced non-European representation fosters genuine universality, aligning the college with the Church's demographic growth in the Global South, where evangelical challenges demand adaptive pastoral approaches rather than rigid Eurocentric doctrinal enforcement.[47] This tension underscores causal links between composition and future papal priorities: empirical patterns show non-European cardinals, often from mission territories, correlating with advocacy for issues like climate engagement and migrant rights, which some analyses tie to a post-conciliar evolution away from pre-Vatican II institutionalism.[48] Traditionalist outlets, however, attribute source biases in mainstream reportingâ€”such as underplaying synodality's risks to unityâ€”to institutional preferences for narrative alignment over unvarnished fidelity to conciliar texts, urging scrutiny of appointments as potential vectors for incremental doctrinal erosion.[49] Defenders invoke the Church's historical adaptability, noting that universality defenses predate Francis and stem from John XXIII's globalist impulses, though without empirical resolution to claims of engineered imbalance.

Criticisms of Appointment Criteria

Critics of Pope Francis's cardinal appointment criteria have argued that the process has prioritized demographic diversity, pastoral experience from peripheral regions, and younger candidates over longstanding traditions of elevating bishops from historic metropolitan sees and those with demonstrated fidelity to doctrinal orthodoxy. Since his election in 2013, Francis has held multiple consistories that frequently bypassed traditional cardinalatial sees such as Venice, Turin, Milan, Paris, and Los Angeles, instead selecting bishops from smaller dioceses or unexpected locales like Agrigento, Perugia, and marginalized areas.[50][51][52] This approach, while defended as breaking from historical precedents tied to political rather than ecclesiastical merit, has been faulted for undermining the College's role as a body of theological guardians, favoring "shepherds" perceived as more aligned with synodal and social emphases over rigorous defenders of the deposit of faith.[53][54] A key concern raised by traditionalist commentators is the resulting composition, where approximately 80% of cardinal electors as of early 2025 were appointed by Francis himself, potentially ensuring continuity of his pastoral priorities in future conclaves at the expense of broader ideological balance.[10][55] Figures like Cardinal Raymond Burke and other over-80 critics have highlighted this as a form of "stacking the deck," arguing that selections often reflect personal affinity or curial reforms rather than objective merit in upholding unchanging Church teaching, with anonymous cardinal letters decrying an "autocratic" style that promotes ambiguities in faith matters.[56][57] Empirical data supports observations of reduced representation from European sees, which historically provided doctrinal anchors, correlating with a shift toward non-European voices that may dilute emphasis on traditional curial expertise versus field-based pastoralism.[58] Proponents counter that such criteria better represent the global Church's demographic realities, fostering inclusivity from Asia, Africa, and Latin America over Eurocentric traditions.[59] However, skeptics contend this overlooks causal links between appointment patterns and potential erosion of fidelity to core tenets, as evidenced by the underrepresentation of bishops from sees with strong records of orthodoxy and the elevation of those associated with progressive synodal initiatives, raising questions about whether demographic metrics alone suffice for a body tasked with electing successors committed to unaltered doctrine.[50][60]
