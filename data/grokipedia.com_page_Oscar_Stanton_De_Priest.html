Oscar Stanton De Priest

Oscar Stanton De Priest (March 9, 1871 – May 12, 1951) was an American Republican politician who served three terms as U.S. Representative for Illinois's 1st congressional district from 1929 to 1935.[1] He holds the distinction of being the first African American elected to Congress in the twentieth century, ending a 28-year absence of Black representation in the House following the departure of George H. White, and the first such member from a northern district outside the South.[2][3] Born in Florence, Alabama, to parents who had been enslaved, De Priest moved with his family to Salina, Kansas, in 1878 and to Chicago, Illinois, in 1889, where he worked as a painter and decorator before establishing a successful real estate business that made him affluent.[2][1] Entering politics as a Republican, he won election to the Cook County Board of Commissioners in 1904 and became Chicago's first Black alderman in 1915, though he resigned amid a gambling indictment from which he was later acquitted.[2] After a period focused on business, he revived his career, securing a House seat in 1928 following the death of incumbent Martin Madden, amid special election dynamics that highlighted his organizational prowess in Chicago's Black community.[2][3] In Congress, De Priest stood as the sole Black member during his tenure, advocating for civil rights through measures such as an anti-lynching bill, resolutions on the Scottsboro Boys case, and an antidiscrimination rider to the Civilian Conservation Corps legislation, while also securing federal funding for Howard University's library and challenging segregation in House facilities.[2][3] He faced indictments for alleged election fraud in 1928 but was cleared before taking office, underscoring the political opposition he encountered from entrenched interests.[2] Defeated in the 1934 Democratic primary by Arthur Mitchell amid the New Deal's shift in Black voter allegiance, De Priest later returned to the Chicago City Council until 1947.[2][1] He died in Chicago following injuries from a bus accident.[3]

Early life

Birth and family background

Oscar Stanton De Priest was born on March 9, 1871, in a cabin in Florence, Lauderdale County, Alabama, to Alexander De Priest and Mary Karsner De Priest, both of whom had been enslaved prior to emancipation following the Civil War.[1][4][5] His parents, as freedmen in the post-Reconstruction South, sought improved prospects amid persistent racial challenges in Alabama.[4][6] In 1878, when De Priest was seven years old, his family relocated to Salina, Kansas, as part of the broader migration of Southern Black families westward during that era to escape Jim Crow oppression and access better economic and educational environments.[1][5][7] This move reflected the aspirations of many formerly enslaved individuals to build stability in less restrictive regions, though Kansas still presented barriers for Black settlers.[5]

Education and relocation to Chicago

De Priest received initial schooling in Florence, Alabama, at a Congregational church school established for freedmen.[4] Following his family's relocation to Salina, Kansas, in 1878, he attended local public grade schools.[2][5] He subsequently enrolled at Salina Normal School, where he pursued a two-year business course emphasizing bookkeeping.[2][7] This vocational training equipped him with practical skills in accounting and commerce, though he did not pursue advanced academic degrees.[2] In 1889, at age 18, De Priest moved to Chicago, Illinois, joining an early wave of African American migrants seeking economic opportunities in the industrial North prior to the larger Great Migration.[8][5] Upon arrival, he secured employment as a house painter and decorator, leveraging manual labor to build initial financial independence in the city's burgeoning construction sector.[5][9] This relocation marked a pivotal shift from rural Southern and Midwestern roots to urban political and business engagement.[6]

Business career

Real estate and contracting ventures

Upon arriving in Chicago around 1889, De Priest initially labored as an apprentice plasterer, house painter, and decorator in the construction trades.[2] He subsequently founded his own contracting firm, capitalizing on the city's expanding urban infrastructure and housing demands during the late 19th and early 20th centuries.[10] This venture positioned him as a key figure in home construction and renovation within Chicago's growing Black communities, particularly in the South Side's Black Belt area.[8] De Priest expanded into real estate brokerage and property management, amassing substantial wealth through investments in residential developments amid the pre-Great Depression boom.[2] One notable holding was an eight-unit apartment building constructed in 1920 at 3263 South Calumet Avenue in Bronzeville, which served as both an investment property and a residence associated with his family.[11] His operations facilitated property transactions and developments that supported community growth, though they operated within the era's restrictive racial covenants and segregationist housing practices prevalent in Chicago.[10] These enterprises provided financial independence that underpinned his later political endeavors, with De Priest leveraging profits from contracting and real estate—alongside stock market activities—to build a fortune before the 1929 crash.[8] Following his congressional defeat in 1934, he resumed full-time involvement in real estate, maintaining brokerage and management activities until his death in 1951.[5]

Financial strategies and community influence

De Priest began his business career in Chicago as an apprentice plasterer, house painter, and decorator following his arrival in 1889, eventually founding a contracting firm focused on painting and decorating services.[2] [12] This venture provided initial capital accumulation through skilled trades catering to the expanding African American population in the city's South Side.[13] He diversified into real estate by establishing a management firm and working as a broker, with a particular emphasis on property transactions in the South Side's Black Belt district.[2] During a seven-year political interlude from 1908 to 1915, De Priest prioritized real estate operations, leveraging the Great Migration's influx of black migrants to invest in and develop properties, which propelled him to affluent status.[2] [13] Additional wealth derived from stock market speculation before the 1929 crash, alongside his roles as a furniture dealer and landlord.[2] A core financial tactic involved brokering the movement of African American families into all-white neighborhoods, systematically challenging racial restrictive covenants and facilitating integration despite white backlash and violence.[12] This approach, akin to early blockbusting practices, generated profits from property turnover and rentals as demand surged in segregated housing markets, though it prioritized economic opportunity over uncontroversial methods.[12] De Priest's self-made prosperity as a contractor, developer, and broker elevated his stature in Chicago's African American community, particularly in Bronzeville and the South Side, where he cultivated influence by employing black workers, extending credit networks, and using business revenues to fund Republican political organizing.[2] [13] His economic independence enabled patronage systems that mobilized voters in the 2nd and 3rd wards, forging a durable political machine independent of white-dominated party structures and emphasizing fiscal self-reliance over dependency.[2] This model of entrepreneurial agency reinforced community resilience amid Jim Crow barriers, positioning De Priest as a patron of black uplift through market-driven means rather than government aid.[13]

Local political career

Cook County Board of Commissioners

De Priest was elected to the Cook County Board of Commissioners in 1904 as a Republican, securing his first position in elective public office.[2] The board, responsible for overseeing county finances, public works, and welfare services in the Chicago area, included commissioners elected from districts; De Priest represented a ward with significant Black population growth amid early 20th-century migration.[1] He served two terms, from 1904 through 1908.[3] Throughout his tenure, De Priest continued operating his real estate brokerage and contracting firms, using political influence to secure contracts and property deals that bolstered his financial standing.[5] This period aligned with his efforts to build a Republican political machine in Chicago's Black neighborhoods, though specific legislative initiatives from his board service remain sparsely documented in primary records.[10] De Priest sought re-election in 1908 but lost due to factional strife within the local Republican organization, which sidelined his faction and forced a temporary withdrawal from politics until 1915.[14] This setback reflected broader intraparty power struggles in Chicago's machine politics rather than voter rejection of his performance.[15]

Chicago City Council tenure

De Priest was elected alderman for Chicago's 2nd Ward in February 1915, becoming the first African American to serve on the City Council.[2] His victory marked a milestone in local representation for Black residents on the South Side, where he leveraged his real estate success and community ties to mobilize voters through the newly formed People's Movement political organization.[4] Serving from 1915 to 1917, De Priest prioritized civil rights advocacy, including opposition to discriminatory practices in public accommodations and employment, and pushed for progressive labor reforms benefiting Black workers.[5] He also focused on patronage, distributing municipal jobs to supporters to strengthen his political machine and consolidate influence within the Republican Party's Black faction.[10] These efforts helped expand Black political participation amid Chicago's growing urban Black population, though his tenure faced resistance from white aldermen wary of integrating council deliberations. In November 1917, De Priest resigned amid an indictment for allegedly accepting payments from a gambling operation, tied to broader graft scandals under Police Chief Charles C. Healey.[2] Republican leaders urged his departure to mitigate party damage, but he retained defense counsel Clarence Darrow and was acquitted following trial, clearing him of charges while his endorsed successor won the ensuing special election.[16] This episode highlighted the precarious position of early Black officeholders in machine-dominated politics, yet De Priest's acquittal preserved his reputation for later campaigns.

Graft allegations, resignation, and trial

In 1917, while serving as alderman for Chicago's Second Ward, Oscar De Priest faced indictment on bribery charges stemming from allegations that he accepted payments to shield illegal gambling operations in the South Side's Black Belt district.[5] [9] The accusations arose amid broader scrutiny of vice protection rackets in Chicago politics, where enforcement efforts targeted Black Belt activities perceived as unfairly singled out compared to white-dominated wards.[17] De Priest resigned from the City Council immediately following the indictment, effectively halting his local political career at that time.[5] He engaged Clarence Darrow, the renowned attorney known for high-profile defenses, to represent him in the proceedings.[18] At trial, De Priest was acquitted of all charges, with no conviction recorded despite the prosecution's case alleging direct involvement in graft.[5] [18] The outcome highlighted challenges in securing convictions against Black politicians in Chicago's era of machine politics and segregated juries, though it did not erase public associations with the scandal during his later campaigns.[17]

Congressional service

1928 election and entry to Congress

Following the death of incumbent Republican Representative Martin B. Madden on April 13, 1928, an open seat emerged in Illinois's 1st congressional district, encompassing Chicago's South Side with its growing Black population. De Priest, leveraging his prominence as a Chicago alderman and his appeal to Black voters, entered the Republican primary and consolidated support from the district's African American community, securing the nomination despite intra-party competition.[2] In the general election on November 6, 1928, De Priest faced Democratic nominee Harry E. Baker and independent William Harrison, among others, in a multi-candidate race. He prevailed with a plurality of approximately 48 percent of the vote, attributing his victory primarily to the unified Black electorate, which provided a margin of about 4,000 votes, while receiving negligible white support.[2][19] This triumph marked De Priest as the first African American elected to Congress from a northern state and the first Black member in the twentieth century, ending a 28-year absence since George H. White's departure in 1901.[2][3] Post-election challenges arose, including a contest by Cook County Democrats alleging voting irregularities, and subsequent Illinois legislative redistricting attempts that sought to dilute Black voting power by merging the 1st district with predominantly white areas. These efforts failed, allowing De Priest's credentials to be validated. He was sworn into the 71st Congress (1929–1931) on April 15, 1929, becoming the sole African American representative and immediately confronting segregationist opposition, such as proposals to assign him to a separate office.[2][3] De Priest's entry symbolized a resurgence of Black political agency in urban centers amid the Great Migration's demographic shifts.[20]

Legislative priorities and positions

De Priest's legislative priorities reflected his Republican affiliation and focus on fiscal conservatism, coupled with advocacy for racial equality in federal operations and enforcement of constitutional protections. He served on committees including Enrolled Bills, Indian Affairs, Invalid Pensions, and Post Office and Post Roads, through which he influenced appropriations and policy oversight.[2] His efforts prioritized anti-discrimination measures, such as securing funding for Howard University's library in the 1930 Interior Department appropriation bill.[2] A key achievement was attaching an antidiscrimination rider to the 1933 Civilian Conservation Corps bill, prohibiting racial bias in hiring for the New Deal public works program despite his broader opposition to such expansive federal initiatives.[2] [21] De Priest introduced H.J. Res. 171 in 1933 to permit federal courts to relocate trials amid racial prejudice, targeting cases like Scottsboro Boys, and H.R. 6157 in 1934, an anti-lynching bill imposing fines and imprisonment on complicit local officials.[2] He also proposed H.R. 10098 in 1932 for pensions to formerly enslaved individuals over age 75.[2] These bills underscored his commitment to civil rights legislation, though most failed to pass amid limited support.[5] De Priest actively challenged segregation within Congress, introducing a resolution on January 24, 1934, to investigate and end Jim Crow practices in the House Restaurant after it refused service to his African-American staff; he bypassed the Rules Committee via discharge petition with 145 signatures, leading to a House vote, though the subsequent panel upheld existing policies along party lines.[22] He advocated enforcing the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments during public addresses, emphasizing federal protection against racial disenfranchisement.[21] Criticizing New Deal programs for risking inflation, excessive spending, and exacerbating Black unemployment—particularly among Southern farmworkers—De Priest opposed liberal federal relief efforts, favoring state and local solutions over centralized intervention.[21] He introduced H. Res. 194 in 1933 to probe subversive propaganda in schools, aligning with concerns over ideological influences in education.[2] These positions highlighted his blend of civil rights advocacy with resistance to welfare expansion, prioritizing individual opportunity over government dependency.[21]

Re-elections, opposition to New Deal, and 1934 defeat

De Priest secured re-election to the U.S. House in November 1930, defeating Democratic challenger Harry Baker with 58.4 percent of the vote to Baker's 41.2 percent.[14] He won a second term in November 1932 with 55 percent of the vote against Democratic opponent Thomas L. Bauer, reflecting continued Republican strength in Chicago's First District amid the early Great Depression.[3] These victories solidified his position as the sole African American in Congress, though they occurred against a backdrop of national economic distress that would soon favor Democratic policies.[2] As a fiscal conservative aligned with Republican orthodoxy, De Priest opposed President Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal initiatives, criticizing them as excessive federal expansion that undermined local governance and individual initiative.[23] He joined GOP colleagues in condemning relief programs, higher taxes, and interventions like the Agricultural Adjustment Act, which he labeled socialist for distorting markets through government coercion rather than voluntary economic recovery.[2][13] De Priest advocated state and local solutions to the Depression, arguing that federal overreach risked dependency and inefficiency, a stance rooted in his experience with Chicago's municipal politics where he prioritized self-reliance over handouts.[9][24] His resistance extended to broader New Deal architecture, favoring amendments to programs like the Civilian Conservation Corps to curb discrimination without endorsing the underlying welfare framework.[7] This opposition contributed to De Priest's narrow defeat in the November 6, 1934, general election by Democrat Arthur W. Mitchell, a former Republican who campaigned aggressively on New Deal support and portrayed De Priest as out of touch with constituents' immediate economic needs.[25] Mitchell's victory, the first by an African American Democrat in Congress, reflected shifting Black voter allegiance toward Roosevelt's relief efforts amid persistent unemployment, with De Priest securing only a plurality as independent and splinter candidacies diluted Republican turnout.[26][27] The loss ended De Priest's congressional tenure after three terms, highlighting the electoral risks of principled conservatism during a period when federal aid programs gained traction despite their fiscal burdens.[2]

Political ideology

Republican loyalty and fiscal conservatism

De Priest maintained unwavering loyalty to the Republican Party, refusing to defect despite the political pressures of the Great Depression era, when many African American voters began shifting toward the Democrats due to the appeal of Franklin D. Roosevelt's relief programs.[3] His steadfast partisanship drew criticism from some contemporaries who viewed it as out of step with changing voter priorities, yet he prioritized ideological consistency over electoral expediency, emerging as the GOP nominee in key races through disciplined organization within Chicago's Republican machine.[28] This loyalty extended to defending the party platform against Democratic encroachments, even as it contributed to his 1934 reelection defeat by emphasizing Republican critiques of federal overreach.[29] As a self-described fiscal conservative, De Priest advocated for restrained government spending and opposed the expansion of federal authority into economic relief, arguing that solutions should originate from state and local levels rather than centralized Washington interventions.[4] He consistently resisted higher taxes and the proliferation of New Deal programs, viewing them as fiscally irresponsible and likely to foster dependency rather than self-reliance, positions that aligned with traditional Republican orthodoxy but clashed with the era's demands for expansive public works and welfare initiatives.[9] In Congress, he joined fellow Republicans in condemning President Roosevelt's policies, including the Agricultural Adjustment Act, which he criticized as an overreach that distorted markets through coercive federal mandates.[13] This conservatism reflected his broader belief in limited government as essential to preserving individual initiative, particularly for marginalized communities reliant on entrepreneurial effort amid economic hardship.[5]

Stances on civil rights, race relations, and government role


De Priest actively pursued civil rights legislation during his congressional tenure, introducing an anti-lynching bill in 1934 that sought to impose fines and imprisonment on local authorities failing to prevent such acts.[2] He also proposed a 1933 joint resolution to amend the U.S. Constitution, enabling federal courts to relocate trials threatened by racial bias, as exemplified by the Scottsboro Boys case.[2] Additionally, De Priest successfully amended the Civilian Conservation Corps legislation in 1933 to prohibit racial discrimination in its programs.[5] In confronting institutional segregation, he challenged the House of Representatives' restaurant policy in 1934 by dispatching his African American secretary, Morris Lewis, to test service denial, followed by House Resolution 236 to investigate discriminatory practices, which garnered 145 signatures on a discharge petition to force a floor vote.[30] [2] On race relations, De Priest emphasized black political agency and community solidarity as pathways to advancement, viewing his election as reliant on unified African American voter support rather than broader interracial coalitions. He defended equal rights for all citizens on the House floor while nominating qualified African Americans, such as Benjamin O. Davis Jr., to U.S. military academies to counter discriminatory barriers in federal institutions.[2] De Priest's approach integrated advocacy for legal equality with promotion of personal initiative, as demonstrated by his pre-political real estate efforts to foster black economic independence through property ownership and neighborhood integration.[5] Regarding the role of government, De Priest advocated limited federal intervention, opposing expansive New Deal programs under President Roosevelt for their potential to induce inflation and invite Communist influences, positions that contributed to his 1934 electoral defeat against a New Deal proponent.[2] He favored constitutional bounds on government action, prioritizing self-reliance and black community empowerment over dependency on federal aid during the Great Depression. This fiscal conservatism aligned with his Republican ideology, rejecting welfare expansions in favor of individual and communal responsibility for socioeconomic progress.[31]

Personal life

Marriage and immediate family

Oscar De Priest married Jessie L. Williams, a music teacher originally from Rockford, Illinois, on February 23, 1898.[15][2][32] The couple resided primarily in Chicago, where Jessie supported her husband's political career, including accompanying him to Washington, D.C., after his election to Congress.[4] They had two sons: Laurence W. De Priest, born around 1900, and Oscar Stanton De Priest Jr.[2][4] Laurence died at age 16 in a drowning accident on July 28, 1916, prompting numerous condolence letters to the family.[33][4] Oscar Stanton Jr. survived into adulthood and later had descendants, including a son born in 1928.[34]

Health issues and daily life in politics

De Priest's tenure in Congress from March 4, 1929, to January 3, 1935, as the sole African American member involved navigating routine institutional segregation and marginalization. On his first day, proceedings drew intense press coverage, with African American observers restricted to the segregated visitors' gallery, underscoring the racial barriers embedded in congressional operations.[2] His wife, Jessie De Priest, encountered exclusion from a White House tea hosted by First Lady Lou Henry Hoover on June 12, 1929, intended for congressional wives; De Priest publicly defended her attendance, asserting equal representation regardless of race.[2] [4] Daily legislative work reflected his isolation, with assignments to less influential committees such as Indian Affairs, Invalid Pensions, and Enrolled Bills across the 71st through 73rd Congresses, later including Post Office and Post Roads in the 73rd. These placements limited his influence on major policy while he focused on anti-discrimination efforts, including bills against lynching and for equal employment in federal agencies.[2] A notable incident occurred on January 23, 1934, when his Black secretary was denied service in the House restaurant, prompting De Priest to introduce a resolution for desegregation, which highlighted ongoing Capitol discrimination but failed to pass amid Southern opposition.[30][2] No major health impediments are recorded as affecting De Priest's political duties during this period, allowing him to maintain active advocacy amid these challenges. His experiences exemplified the personal and professional resilience required to operate as a minority voice in a Jim Crow-era Congress.[2]

Later years and legacy

Post-Congress activities and death

Following his defeat in the 1934 congressional election, De Priest returned to Chicago and resumed his pre-Congress career in real estate development and investment, focusing on properties in the city's Black South Side neighborhoods.[4][13] He maintained involvement in Republican Party affairs, including service as a delegate to the 1940 Republican National Convention in Philadelphia, where he supported Wendell Willkie's nomination as the presidential candidate.[13] De Priest made an unsuccessful bid to reclaim his House seat in the 1936 election, receiving limited support amid the Democratic dominance under President Franklin D. Roosevelt.[14] Beyond these efforts, he advised emerging Black political figures and participated in local civic organizations, though he held no further elected office.[5] On January 17, 1951, at age 79, De Priest was struck by a bus while crossing a street on Chicago's South Side, sustaining severe injuries including fractures and internal trauma.[2] He died four months later on May 12, 1951, at Provident Hospital in Chicago, with complications from the accident cited as the primary cause; he was 80 years old.[2][5] De Priest was buried at Graceland Cemetery in Chicago.[35]

Historical assessments, achievements, and criticisms

Oscar Stanton De Priest is historically assessed as a pioneering figure in American politics, becoming the first African American elected to the U.S. Congress in the 20th century and ending a 28-year absence of Black representatives since George H. White left office in 1901.[6][3] His election from Illinois's 1st congressional district in 1928 symbolized hope and progress for African Americans amid widespread disenfranchisement in the South, positioning him as a de facto national spokesman for Black interests during his tenure from March 4, 1929, to January 3, 1935.[2] Scholars and contemporaries viewed De Priest as a trailblazer who demonstrated Black electoral viability in northern urban centers, paving the way for subsequent African American politicians, including future congressmen from Chicago.[36] Key achievements include his advocacy for civil rights legislation, such as efforts to prohibit racial discrimination in federal hiring practices under New Deal programs like the Civilian Conservation Corps, ensuring non-discrimination clauses in relief efforts despite his broader opposition to expansive federal intervention.[37] As the sole Black member of Congress during the Great Depression, De Priest championed anti-lynching measures and labor protections aligned with progressive reforms, while maintaining fiscal restraint and emphasizing self-reliance over dependency on government aid.[7] His success in Chicago's machine politics elevated Black participation in local governance, where he previously served as alderman, fostering community economic development through Republican patronage networks.[5] Criticisms of De Priest centered on his staunch Republican loyalty and fiscal conservatism, particularly his opposition to President Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal initiatives, which he viewed as fiscally irresponsible expansions of federal power that risked entrenching poverty through handouts rather than promoting individual initiative.[2] Opponents, including Democratic challenger Arthur W. Mitchell, attacked De Priest for voting against emergency federal relief bills during the Depression's depths, arguing it neglected constituents' immediate needs and contributed to his 1934 electoral defeat by a 1,407-vote margin amid shifting Black voter allegiance toward the Democrats.[25] Some historical accounts note a reputation for corruption tied to Chicago's patronage-driven political machine, where influence peddling and graft were commonplace, though De Priest defended such practices as essential for minority empowerment in a hostile system.[5] Despite these critiques, his resistance to welfare paternalism has been later praised by free-market advocates as prescient caution against long-term government dependency.[13]

Electoral history

Local elections

De Priest entered elective office in 1904 when he won election to the Cook County Board of Commissioners, defeating incumbent John Schradsky in the Republican primary and securing the general election seat for a four-year term.[7] He was reelected in 1908, serving until 1912.[9] In the spring of 1915, De Priest was elected as alderman for Chicago's 2nd Ward, becoming the first African American to hold that position in the city's history.[2] Representing a predominantly Black district on the South Side, he focused on civil rights issues and patronage appointments during his tenure from 1915 to 1917.[10] His service ended prematurely in 1917 when he resigned amid allegations of assisting his son in evading the military draft, marking him as the first Black alderman to leave office under such circumstances.[37] De Priest staged a political resurgence in 1943, winning election as alderman for the 3rd Ward and returning to the Chicago City Council.[2] He served until 1947, when he was defeated in his bid for reelection, concluding his local electoral career.[5]

U.S. House campaigns

Oscar De Priest entered the 1928 U.S. House election for Illinois's 1st congressional district following the death of incumbent Republican Martin B. Madden on April 27, 1928. De Priest secured the Republican nomination to succeed Madden and won the general election on November 6, 1928, defeating Democratic nominee William L. Daley and Progressive Joel Golub with a plurality of 48 percent of the vote. His victory relied heavily on consolidated black voter support, securing approximately four thousand more votes from black precincts while receiving negligible white support. This made De Priest the first African American elected to Congress in the 20th century, ending a 28-year absence of black representation in the House since George H. White's departure in 1901.[2][19] De Priest was reelected in the 1930 midterm elections, capturing nearly 60 percent of the vote against Democratic challenger Harry Baker and three independent candidates. The district's black voters provided strong solidarity, enabling his retention of the seat amid national Republican losses. He successfully defended his incumbency again in the 1932 presidential election year, overcoming the Democratic landslide that ousted President Herbert Hoover, due to sustained support from the district's African American community despite broader economic distress from the Great Depression.[14][8] In the 1934 elections, De Priest faced Republican-turned-Democrat Arthur W. Mitchell, who capitalized on the popularity of President Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal policies among black voters shifting toward the Democratic Party. Mitchell narrowly defeated De Priest on November 6, 1934, becoming the first African American Democrat elected to Congress; the race highlighted emerging partisan realignments in Chicago's black electorate, with Mitchell eking out victory through targeted appeals to economic relief programs. De Priest's loss ended his congressional tenure after three terms.[29][38][14]
