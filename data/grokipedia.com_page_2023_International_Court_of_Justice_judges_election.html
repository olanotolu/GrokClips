2023 International Court of Justice judges election

The 2023 International Court of Justice judges election was the triennial ballot held simultaneously by the United Nations General Assembly and Security Council on 9 November 2023 to fill five vacancies on the principal judicial organ of the United Nations, with elected judges commencing nine-year terms on 6 February 2024.[1][2] The successful candidates were incumbent Hilary Charlesworth of Australia, who was re-elected, and newcomers Bogdan-Lucian Aurescu of Romania, Sarah Hull Cleveland of the United States, Juan Manuel GÃ³mez Robledo Verduzco of Mexico, and Dire Tladi of South Africa.[1][2] The election involved nine candidates vying for the seats, requiring an absolute majority in both bodiesâ€”97 votes in the General Assembly (out of 193 members) and eight in the 15-member Security Councilâ€”via secret ballot without vetoes or discussions in the latter.[1][2] In the General Assembly, GÃ³mez Robledo Verduzco topped the poll with 143 votes, followed by Cleveland (135), Aurescu and Charlesworth (117 each), and Tladi (113), while lower tallies eliminated others including Russia's Kirill Gevorgian (77 votes), who sought re-election but failed amid the Court's ongoing advisory proceedings on Ukraine-related obligations.[1][2] The Security Council process extended to five rounds of voting to secure majorities for all five, reflecting competitive regional and bloc dynamics in judge selection, which aims for equitable geographical representation, diversity of legal systems, and the Court's overall composition under the UN Charter and ICJ Statute.[2] Notable outcomes included the United States regaining a seat after an absence since 2010, with Cleveland's election as a constitutional law expert, and Tladi's selection bolstering African representation; the results underscored the influence of state nominations and informal co-nominations to balance the bench's 15 members, though critics have noted persistent challenges in achieving full geographical equity and independence from national interests.[1][2] No formal controversies disrupted proceedings, but the non-re-election of Gevorgian highlighted geopolitical tensions affecting judicial continuity.[1][2]

Legal Framework and Background

ICJ Statute Provisions on Elections

The Statute of the International Court of Justice establishes the Court as comprising fifteen independent judges elected regardless of nationality from candidates of high moral character who possess qualifications for the highest judicial offices in their countries or are jurisconsults of recognized competence in international law.[3] No two judges may share the same nationality, with dual nationals deemed to hold the nationality of the state where they primarily exercise civil and political rights.[3] Judges serve nine-year terms and are eligible for re-election, though no judge may hold more than two consecutive terms; the initial election staggered terms such that five expire every three years and five every six years, establishing a triennial cycle for electing five judges to maintain continuity.[3] This structure ensures the Court's ongoing operation without full replacement at once, with vacancies from death or resignation filled by the same process.[3] To preserve judicial independence, judges are prohibited from exercising political or administrative functions or engaging in any professional occupation; any doubt regarding compatibility is resolved by the Court itself.[3] The Statute mandates that electors consider not only individual qualifications but also the need for the Court as a whole to represent the main forms of civilization and principal legal systems of the world, promoting broad representation without imposing formal quotas or geographical allocations, thus depending on state nominations and voting discretion for balance.[3]

Triennial Election Cycle and 2023 Vacancies

The International Court of Justice (ICJ) holds triennial elections to replace one-third of its 15 judges, electing five members every three years for non-renewable nine-year terms to ensure continuity and institutional memory.[4] These elections occur concurrently in the UN General Assembly and Security Council, with terms commencing on 6 February of the year following the election, regardless of the precise election date.[1] The cycle aligns with the ICJ Statute's provisions for staggered terms, preventing full turnover and maintaining expertise across cases.[3] In 2023, the election addressed five vacancies resulting from term expirations on 5 February 2024, held by President Joan Donoghue (United States), Vice-President Kirill Gevorgian (Russian Federation), Giorgio Gaja (Italy), Patrick Lipton Robinson (Jamaica), and Hilary Charlesworth (Australia).[1] These seats aligned with the regional distribution requirements under Article 9 of the ICJ Statute, specifically one from Eastern European States (Gevorgian), one from Latin American and Caribbean States (Robinson), and three from Western European and Other States (Donoghue, Gaja, Charlesworth).[3] The UN Secretary-General notified member states of these impending vacancies on 7 July 2023, initiating the nomination process under established deadlines ahead of the 9 November 2023 election.

Regional and Ideological Balance Prior to Election

Prior to the 2023 election, the International Court of Justice consisted of 15 judges whose nationalities reflected the customary practice of equitable geographical representation across United Nations regional groups, as guided by Article 9 of the ICJ Statute emphasizing broad coverage of civilizations and legal systems. This distribution typically allocated three seats to African states, three to Asian states, two to Eastern European states, two to Latin American and Caribbean (GRULAC) states, and five to Western European and Other States (WEOG) group, including major powers like permanent Security Council members.[3][1] Specific examples included judges from Uganda and Somalia (Africa), China, Japan, and India (Asia), Russia and Slovakia (Eastern Europe), Mexico and Brazil (GRULAC), and the United States, France, Germany, Australia, and Lebanon (WEOG).[1] This structure aimed to prevent dominance by any single region while accommodating the Court's role in global disputes.

Nomination Process and Candidates

Required Qualifications and Nomination Rules

The qualifications for judges of the International Court of Justice (ICJ) are outlined in Article 2 of the ICJ Statute, which requires the Court to consist of independent judges elected regardless of nationality from persons of high moral character who possess the qualifications in their respective countries for appointment to the highest judicial offices, or who are jurisconsults of recognized competence in international law.[3] This criterion emphasizes judicial expertise or equivalent scholarly eminence in international law, with an explicit focus on independence to ensure impartiality in adjudication.[11] Nominations for ICJ judges are governed by Articles 4 and 5 of the Statute, restricting submissions exclusively to the national groups constituting the Permanent Court of Arbitration (PCA), with each group comprising four members: typically two from the highest national judicial courts and two qualified in international law, as designated by governments.[3] Prior to nominating up to four candidatesâ€”who must meet Article 2 qualificationsâ€”each national group is required to consult its highest court of justice, legal faculties, schools of law, and national academies or similar bodies to identify suitable individuals.[3] Only UN member states or PCA signatories participate in this process, ensuring government-mediated but ostensibly consultative input, though the national groups operate with autonomy in final selections. For the 2023 election, the UN Secretary-General circulated invitations for nominations in early 2023, with submissions due by early July to allow compilation of the candidate list ahead of the November balloting. The resulting report, issued on 7 July 2023, documented 28 nominees from various national groups, reflecting adherence to the statutory timeline without centralized pre-screening. The nomination framework lacks formal vetting mechanisms beyond the national groups' internal consultations, permitting the advancement of candidates influenced by domestic political priorities rather than independent empirical assessment of qualifications or independence.[12] While the Statute prohibits overt campaigns, empirical accounts indicate prevalent informal lobbying by governments and candidates' supporters to secure endorsements from additional national groups, potentially prioritizing geopolitical alliances over merit-based selection.[13] This structure, unchanged for the 2023 cycle, underscores reliance on nominators' discretion without mandatory transparency or cross-verification, which has drawn critiques for enabling politically aligned appointments despite the Statute's independence mandate.[12]

The eight nominees for the 2023 International Court of Justice judges election possessed varied expertise in international law, diplomacy, and human rights, drawn primarily from governmental advisory roles, academic positions, and prior judicial service.[2] Bogdan-Lucian Aurescu (Romania) served as Romania's Minister of Foreign Affairs from 2019 to 2024, during which he represented Romania in cases before the ICJ, including acting as judge ad hoc in the Allegations of Genocide under the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (Ukraine v. Russian Federation). Previously, he held positions as Romania's agent before the ICJ and European Court of Human Rights, and as director of legal affairs in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs since 1996, contributing to Romania's accession to the EU and NATO. His career emphasized treaty negotiation and international dispute resolution.[1] Hilary Charlesworth (Australia) is a Laureate Professor at the University of Melbourne and Melbourne Law School, specializing in international human rights law and feminist approaches to international law. She served as a member of the UN Human Rights Committee from 2015 to 2022 and as co-director of the Australian Research Council Centre of Excellence for the Elimination of Violence Against Women and Children. Charlesworth has authored influential works on the structure of international law and its gaps in addressing gender-based issues, and she contributed to Australia's foreign policy on international justice.[1] Sarah Hull Cleveland (United States) holds the Louis Henkin Chair at Columbia Law School, with expertise in U.S. constitutional law, national security, and international human rights. She advised the U.S. State Department as Counselor on International Law from 2011 to 2014, focusing on treaty interpretation and multilateral negotiations, and previously served as a legal adviser in the Office of the Legal Adviser. Cleveland has litigated before U.S. courts on behalf of human rights organizations and co-authored reports critiquing U.S. compliance with international obligations. The U.S. government highlighted her scholarly independence and advisory experience as qualifications.[14] Juan Manuel GÃ³mez Robledo Verduzco (Mexico) is a career diplomat who served as Mexico's Legal Adviser in the Secretariat of Foreign Affairs and as Permanent Representative to the Organization of American States. He represented Mexico in ICJ proceedings, including advisory opinions on nuclear weapons, and held ambassadorships in several countries. His background includes negotiation of multilateral treaties on arms control and human rights, with prior roles in Mexico's foreign ministry emphasizing Latin American regional law.[1] Dire Tladi (South Africa) is a professor of international law at the University of Pretoria, where he teaches public international law and has advised the South African government on international legal matters. He served as Special Rapporteur for the International Law Commission on the protection of the environment in relation to armed conflicts and as a member of the UN International Law Commission from 2010 to 2021. Tladi's scholarship focuses on state responsibility, immunities of state officials, and third-world approaches to international law, including critiques of Western dominance in global norms.[1] Kirill Gevorgian (Russian Federation), the incumbent judge seeking re-election, had been a member of the ICJ since 2015, following a career as Deputy Minister of Foreign Affairs and Legal Adviser to the Russian Ministry of Foreign Affairs since 2004. He represented Russia in numerous international disputes, including before the ICJ in cases involving sanctions and territorial claims, and contributed to Russia's positions on UN Security Council resolutions. Gevorgian's governmental roles raised questions about potential alignment with state interests, particularly amid Russia's ongoing military actions in Ukraine, though proponents cited his extensive diplomatic experience in complex interstate litigation.[2] Chaloka Beyani (Zambia) is a professor of international law at the London School of Economics and former UN Special Rapporteur on the human rights of internally displaced persons from 2010 to 2016, during which he reported on displacement crises in over 20 countries. He has advised the African Union and Southern African Development Community on peace and security law, and served as Zambia's ambassador to the UN in Geneva. Beyani's work emphasizes refugee law and regional African integration, with publications on the responsibility to protect and post-conflict reconstruction.[2] Ahmed Amin Fathalla (Egypt) is a professor of international law at Cairo University and former judge at the African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights. He has served as Egypt's agent in ICJ cases and as a member of the UN Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination. Fathalla's expertise includes Arab regional law, Islamic perspectives on international norms, and dispute settlement in the Nile Basin, with prior roles in Egypt's Ministry of Foreign Affairs focusing on multilateral diplomacy.[2]

Election Mechanics

Voting Procedures in General Assembly and Security Council

In the United Nations General Assembly, each of the 193 member states holds one vote in the election of International Court of Justice judges, conducted by secret ballot.[3] A candidate must secure an absolute majority, equivalent to more than half of the votes cast by members present and voting, for election; in practice, this threshold adjusts based on attendance, which reached near-full participation in relevant sessions.[1] Voting proceeds in rounds until five candidates (for a triennial election filling five seats) achieve the requisite support across simultaneous ballots with the Security Council, without public disclosure of individual state ballots but with aggregated vote counts per candidate released post-election.[3] In the Security Council, the 15 membersâ€”five permanent and ten electedâ€”each cast one equal vote by secret ballot, requiring an absolute majority of eight for a candidate's election.[3] Unlike resolutions on substantive matters under Article 27(3) of the UN Charter, ICJ elections function as a non-substantive electoral process, exempting permanent members' veto and ensuring no distinction in voting weight among members. Aggregated vote tallies per candidate are similarly announced after balloting, maintaining ballot secrecy while enabling verification of majorities.[2] These procedures, rooted in Articles 8 and 10 of the ICJ Statute, emphasize concurrent majorities to balance broad state representation in the Assembly against the Council's concentrated geopolitical composition, though the absence of veto in the latter underscores an intent to prioritize electoral consensus over blocking power.[3]

Simultaneous Balloting and Quorum Requirements

The balloting for ICJ judges occurs simultaneously in the United Nations General Assembly (GA) and Security Council (SC), with separate sessions held at the same time to ensure concurrency, as stipulated in Article 10 of the ICJ Statute.[11] However, the votes cast in each organ remain independent, requiring candidates to secure an absolute majorityâ€”defined as more than half the total membershipâ€”in both bodies for election.[11] [15] This dual-threshold mechanism prevents unilateral decisions by either organ and can necessitate multiple rounds of voting if initial ballots fail to produce joint majorities, highlighting the structural potential for procedural stalemates driven by divergent state interests.[16] Quorum requirements further constrain the process: the GA maintains a standard plenary quorum of one-third of its 193 members (approximately 65 states), though achieving the absolute majority of 97 affirmative votes demands far higher attendance in practice. In the SC, a quorum of nine members (a majority of its 15) must be present to validate proceedings, with elections requiring eight affirmative votes irrespective of permanent member vetoes, as ICJ balloting treats all members equally. [2] These thresholds ensure broad representation but amplify deadlock risks if attendance falters or preferences misalign, as seen in historical cases like the 2010 election, where over 25 ballots were needed for the final seat due to persistent splits among members.[16] [17]

Non-Elected Candidates and Their Performance

The non-elected candidates in the 2023 International Court of Justice judges election included Ahmed Amin Fathalla from Egypt, Chaloka Beyani from Zambia, Kirill Gevorgian from the Russian Federation, and Antoine Kesia-Mbe Mindua from the Democratic Republic of the Congo.[2][1] Each required an absolute majority in both the UN General Assembly (97 votes out of 192 valid ballots cast) and the Security Council (8 votes out of 15 members) to secure election, with balloting continuing until five candidates achieved this simultaneously across both bodies.[1][2] These candidates received substantial support in the General Assembly but fell short of the threshold, while their performances in the Security Council were notably weaker, particularly in the final (fifth) round of voting on 9 November 2023, where none exceeded 7 votes.[1][2] Fathalla and Beyani each garnered 81 votes in the General Assembly, reflecting competitive regional backingâ€”Fathalla from Arab and African states, and Beyani from sub-Saharan African groupsâ€”but only 7 and 1 votes, respectively, in the Security Council, indicating limited consensus among permanent members.[1][2] Gevorgian, the incumbent Russian judge seeking re-election, obtained 77 General Assembly votes but just 5 in the Security Council, marking the first failure of a permanent Security Council member's nominee since the Court's establishment.[1][2] Mindua received the lowest totals, with 65 in the General Assembly and 2 in the Security Council, amid multiple African candidacies vying for one regional seat ultimately awarded to Dire Tladi of South Africa.[1][2]

Analysis and Controversies

Changes in Court Composition

The 2023 election altered the International Court of Justice's composition effective 6 February 2024, with five judges assuming nine-year terms: Hilary Charlesworth (Australia) re-elected, Sarah Hull Cleveland (United States) newly elected, Bogdan-Lucian Aurescu (Romania) newly elected, Juan Manuel GÃ³mez Robledo Verduzco (Mexico) newly elected, and Dire Tladi (South Africa) newly elected.[1][2] These replacements addressed vacancies from terms expiring 5 February 2024, held previously by Charlesworth (completing a partial term), Joan Donoghue (United States), Kirill Gevorgian (Russia), Giorgio Gaja (Italy), and Patrick Lipton Robinson (Jamaica).[1][19] Regionally, the shifts included continuity in Eastern Europe via replacement of Russia's Gevorgian with Romania's Aurescu, the first such change since 1946 and ending continuous Soviet/Russian representation; Latin American and Caribbean Group continuity by substituting Jamaica's Robinson with Mexico's GÃ³mez Robledo; and Western European and Others Group (WEOG) reduction from five to four seats through the unelected Gaja (Italy) and Donoghue, offset by re-election of Australia's Charlesworth and election of the United States' Cleveland.[20][2] African representation increased from three to four judges with Tladi's election, effectively reallocating a WEOG seat.[1] Asian and other regional shares remained unchanged.[5] In terms of gender, two womenâ€”Charlesworth and Clevelandâ€”were elected, replacing the outgoing Donoghue (female), thereby sustaining four women on the 15-judge bench (27% female), up from historical levels below 20% but short of parity goals emphasized in recent UN discussions.[21][22] Ideologically, the United States maintained its presence with Cleveland succeeding Donoghue, potentially balancing prior perceptions of bench tilts in cases involving U.S. interests; African continuity persisted via Tladi alongside incumbents like Uganda's Julia Sebutinde; and Slavic representation endured through Romania despite Russia's exclusion.[2][20] The resulting bench includes judges from states with opposing stakes in conflicts, such as NATO-aligned Romania amid the Russia-Ukraine war, without a Russian national for the first time.[20]

Evidence of Politicization and Bloc Voting

The 2023 International Court of Justice election demonstrated patterns of bloc voting aligned with geopolitical affiliations, particularly in the Security Council, where voting required five rounds due to initial overachievement of the absolute majority threshold by more than the five available seats. Incumbent Russian judge Kirill Gevorgian received consistently low support, garnering only 5-6 votes across roundsâ€”likely from Russia, China, and a handful of non-aligned membersâ€”while candidates from Western-aligned states, such as Sarah Hull Cleveland of the United States (12-14 votes) and Hilary Charlesworth of Australia (9-11 votes), secured strong backing from the P3 permanent members (United States, United Kingdom, France) and their allies. This disparity reflects coordinated opposition to Gevorgian amid Russia's ongoing invasion of Ukraine, which began in 2022, prioritizing state power dynamics over judicial incumbency and experience.[2][20]

Implications for Judicial Independence

The 2023 election of International Court of Justice (ICJ) judges underscored the tension between the Court's statutory mandate for independent jurists qualified in international law and the political dynamics of United Nations voting, where absolute majorities in both the General Assembly and Security Council often reflect regional blocs and geopolitical alliances rather than merit alone.[1][2] The failure of Russia's incumbent judge, Kirill Gevorgian, to secure re-electionâ€”receiving only 77 votes in the General Assembly and peaking at 6 in the Security Council across five ballotsâ€”illustrated how ongoing conflicts, such as Russia's invasion of Ukraine, can mobilize opposition from permanent Security Council members like the United States, United Kingdom, and France, prioritizing strategic interests over continuity in representation from major powers.[20] This outcome deviated from the historical norm of near-automatic re-election for judges from permanent members, signaling a erosion of informal norms that had preserved balanced geopolitical input on the bench.[20] While the successful election of Sarah Hull Cleveland from the United Statesâ€”marking the first American judge since Joan Donoghue's term ended in 2021â€”may introduce perspectives attuned to rule-of-law traditions in contentious cases involving territorial disputes or sanctions, it also perpetuates a system vulnerable to quid pro quo negotiations in multi-round Security Council balloting.[2] Empirical analyses of ICJ voting patterns reveal that judges frequently favor the states that elected them or those with comparable economic profiles, suggesting latent national biases that compromise impartial adjudication in advisory opinions on human rights or state responsibility.[23] The addition of judges like Dire Tladi from South Africa, representing Global South viewpoints, sustains a bench majority from developing regions, which has correlated with rulings emphasizing decolonization narratives but potentially underweighting empirical accountability in cases of internal governance failures or asymmetric conflicts.[24] Such composition risks normalizing outcomes that align with bloc preferences, as seen in post-2023 proceedings on polarized issues like the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, where individual declarations reflect prior national stances.[25] To mitigate these risks to truth-seeking adjudication, reforms short of statutory amendmentâ€”such as mandatory independent vetting of candidates' qualifications by a specialized panelâ€”could prioritize legal expertise over diplomatic horse-trading, fostering greater causal detachment from electing states' agendas. Without such measures, the 2023 results exemplify how electoral politicization entrenches representational imperatives under Article 9 of the ICJ Statute, potentially biasing long-term decisions toward collective state interests rather than rigorous, evidence-based application of international law.[13][23]
