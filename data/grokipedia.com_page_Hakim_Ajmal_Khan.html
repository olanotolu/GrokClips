Hakim Ajmal Khan

Hakim Ajmal Khan (11 February 1868 – 29 December 1927) was an Indian Unani physician, independence activist, and educational reformer renowned for reviving indigenous medicine systems and contributing to the anti-colonial movement.[1][2] Born into a lineage of hakims tracing back to Mughal times in Delhi, Khan trained in Unani medicine from an early age and established practices that integrated traditional Greco-Arabic healing with local adaptations, founding the Hindustani Dawakhana in 1906 as a center for affordable treatment and research.[1][3] He spearheaded the defense of Unani and Ayurvedic systems against British colonial policies favoring Western allopathy, organizing protests and establishing the Tibbi College in 1906 to train practitioners and conduct scientific validation of herbal remedies.[4][5] In the independence struggle, Khan aligned with Mahatma Gandhi's non-cooperation movement, serving as acting president of the Indian National Congress in 1921 and president of the All India Khilafat Conference in 1920 to foster Hindu-Muslim unity against British rule.[4][2] As a founder and first chancellor of Jamia Millia Islamia in 1920, he relocated the institution from Aligarh to Delhi and personally funded its growth by mortgaging family properties, emphasizing nationalist education free from colonial influence.[2][4]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Hakim Ajmal Khan was born on February 11, 1868, at Sharif Manzil in Delhi to the Sharifi family, a renowned lineage of Unani hakims originating from the Delhi school of traditional medicine.[1] [6] His ancestors arrived in India accompanying Mughal emperor Babur in the early 16th century and formally entered the medical profession during Akbar's reign in the late 16th century.[7] The family held the title of Rais of Delhi and maintained a tradition of serving as court physicians across generations.[1] Ajmal Khan's grandfather, Hakim Sharif Khan, served as personal physician to Mughal emperor Shah Alam II and established Sharif Manzil as an early hub for Unani treatment and instruction.[8] [1] He was the youngest of three sons born to Hakim Mahmud Khan, one of Sharif Khan's sons; his elder brother, Hakim Abdul Majid, died in 1901, followed by the second brother in 1904.[8] Raised in this hereditary milieu of physicians, Ajmal Khan received a conventional early education emphasizing Quranic memorization, followed by instruction in Arabic, Persian, and foundational Islamic sciences, as was customary for Muslim families of scholarly standing in 19th-century Delhi.[7] [1] This groundwork immersed him from childhood in the intellectual and practical demands of Unani practice, apprenticing under elder relatives before formal qualification.[1]

Training in Unani Medicine

Hakim Ajmal Khan was born into the Sharifi family of Delhi, renowned for generations as practitioners of Unani medicine, tracing its lineage to prominent hakims including his grandfather Hakim Sharif Khan and great-grandfather Hakim Muhammad Sharif Khan.[1][2] Following the family's traditional apprenticeship model, he began his medical education after completing early religious and linguistic studies, memorizing the Quran by heart and mastering Arabic and Persian languages essential for accessing classical Unani texts.[1][9] His primary training occurred under the guidance of his father, Hakim Mahmud Khan, a distinguished Unani physician whose untimely death in Ajmal's youth profoundly influenced his path.[10][5] Ajmal supplemented this familial instruction by studying with other experts, such as Hakim Ghulam Raza Khan, noted for his proficiency in pulse diagnosis (nabz), and by delving into key Unani texts like those on pharmacology and clinical practice from family elders.[6] This hands-on, mentor-based approach, typical of Unani pedagogy in 19th-century India, emphasized empirical observation, humoral theory, and holistic treatment over formal institutional degrees, equipping him with skills in compounding medicines and patient management by his early twenties.[11][2]

Medical Contributions

Clinical Practice and Innovations

Hakim Ajmal Khan established a prominent clinical practice in Unani medicine at the family-run Sharifi Dawakhana in Ballimaran, Delhi, following his qualification as a hakim in 1892. He served as chief physician to the Nawab of Rampur from that year onward and treated patients irrespective of religion, caste, or socioeconomic status, providing free consultations to the local poor while charging 1,000 rupees for out-of-town visits. Renowned for his diagnostic acumen, including assessments via facial observation, Khan earned acclaim for effective treatments across diverse ailments, contributing to titles such as Massiha-e-Hind (Messiah of India) for purportedly miraculous cures.[1][12][4] In his innovations, Khan emphasized integrating select Western scientific advances, particularly chemical analysis, into Unani pharmacology to modernize drug compounding (davāsāzī) without compromising foundational principles like humoral theory. He developed and patented 84 herbal formulas, enhancing efficacy through refined preparations at the Hindustani Dawakhana, which he established in the early 1900s to support research and production. A key collaboration involved assigning chemist Dr. Salimuzzaman Siddiqui in 1925 to scientifically validate indigenous drugs; this yielded the isolation of ajmaline, an antiarrhythmic alkaloid from Rauwolfia serpentina, named in Khan's honor, alongside related compounds like reserpine for hypertension management, bridging empirical Unani uses with modern verification.[1][12][4] Khan further institutionalized these advancements by founding the Majlis-i Tahqiqat ‘Ilmī in 1926 at Tibbia College for systematic Unani research, promoting evidence-based refinements amid colonial skepticism toward indigenous systems. His approach countered proposals like the 1910 Medical Practitioners Registration Bill, which threatened Unani's autonomy, by advocating hybrid methodologies that preserved clinical traditions while incorporating verifiable scientific tools.[12][4]

Revival and Institutionalization of Unani Medicine

During the early 20th century, Unani medicine faced significant decline under British colonial rule, which prioritized Western medical practices and marginalized indigenous systems through legislation like the proposed Medical Practitioners Registration Bill. Hakim Ajmal Khan responded by launching a nationwide campaign in 1910 against this bill, advocating for the scientific validation of Unani to secure its legitimacy and prevent erasure.[4] His efforts emphasized integrating empirical research and modern methods while preserving core Unani principles, countering colonial critiques that dismissed traditional systems as unscientific.[12] Khan institutionalized revival through foundational organizations, founding the All India Vedic and Unani Tibbi Conference in 1910 to foster collaboration between Unani hakims and Ayurvedic vaids, mobilizing over 300 practitioners at its inaugural Delhi session to oppose restrictive provincial acts.[12] [13] In 1911, he delivered a key speech in Lucknow urging unity among indigenous practitioners to strengthen their collective advocacy, drawing from his 1911 European visit to study institutional models for reform.[13] [4] He further opposed the Medical Registration Act of 1914, lobbying for official recognition of Unani practitioners.[12] To advance scientific institutionalization, Khan established the Majlis-i Tahqiqat ‘Ilmī in 1926 for systematic Unani research, commissioning studies that validated traditional herbs like Rauvolfia serpentina through collaborations with chemists such as Dr. Salimuzzaman Siddiqui starting in 1925.[12] These initiatives promoted research-based practice and critical inquiry among hakims, aligning Unani with emerging scientific standards.[12] By 1919, his persistent advocacy yielded government concessions, including inquiry committees on indigenous drugs and support for traditional medical institutions, marking a turning point in Unani's formal acceptance and revival.[13]

Educational Initiatives

Establishment of Tibbia College

Hakim Ajmal Khan assumed leadership of the precursor institution, Madrasa e Tibbia, in 1911 following the deaths of his elder brothers, including founder Hakim Abdul Majeed Khan, who had established it in 1883 in Gali Qasim Jan, Delhi, as a center for Unani medical education incorporating elements of modern science.[14][4] Under Ajmal Khan's direction, the madrasa was reorganized as a trust linked to his family-run Hindustani Dawakhana and relocated to a 33.33-acre plot in Karol Bagh to accommodate expanded facilities, with its curriculum broadened to encompass Ayurveda alongside Unani medicine, leading to its renaming as Ayurvedic and Unani Tibbia College.[14] The foundation stone for the new campus was laid on March 29, 1916, by Viceroy Lord Harding, overcoming resistance from British officials, including Malcolm Hailey, who viewed indigenous medical systems as unscientific and sought their marginalization under colonial policy.[14][4] Ajmal Khan's initiative responded to these pressures by aiming to institutionalize and scientifically validate traditional Unani practices, preserving Muslim cultural heritage while integrating Western subjects like anatomy and surgery to address critiques of lacking empirical rigor.[4] The college was formally inaugurated on February 13, 1921, by Mahatma Gandhi, marking a convergence of medical reform and emerging nationalist sentiments.[14] The established college featured clinical infrastructure, including the Dar-ul-Marda hospital for patient care and Dar-ul-Tahqiqat for pharmacological research and herbal medicine development, positioning it as a hub for advancing Unani through evidence-based innovation rather than rote traditionalism.[4] Ajmal Khan directed the institution until his death in 1927, during which it gained recognition for elevating indigenous medicine's status amid colonial dominance.[14]

Founding of Jamia Millia Islamia


Jamia Millia Islamia was established on 29 October 1920 in Aligarh as a response to the Non-Cooperation Movement launched by Mahatma Gandhi earlier that year, aiming to create a nationalist educational institution amid boycotts of British-affiliated colleges.[15] Discontent with Aligarh Muslim University's perceived loyalty to colonial authorities prompted nationalist Muslim leaders and students to form an 18-member foundation committee, which announced the university's creation to foster anti-colonial education aligned with Indian independence aspirations.[16] The foundation stone was laid by Maulana Mehmud Hasan, reflecting the institution's roots in Islamic activism intertwined with the Khilafat Movement.[15] Hakim Ajmal Khan served as the major inspiration behind Jamia's foundation, leveraging his stature as a physician, educationist, and emerging political figure to rally support.[17] On 22 November 1920, he was elected as the first Chancellor, or Amir-e-Jamia, a position he held until his death in 1927, providing moral and administrative guidance during the institution's nascent phase.[15] Khan personally addressed financial challenges by mobilizing funds through his networks and, according to accounts, even selling personal assets like a diamond ring to cover teachers' salaries, underscoring his commitment amid resource scarcity.[16] Alongside Khan, key co-founders included Maulana Mohamed Ali Jauhar, who became the first Vice-Chancellor, Dr. Mukhtar Ahmad Ansari, and Abdul Majeed Khwaja, who collectively embodied the shift toward swadeshi education rejecting colonial influence.[18] This founding effort positioned Jamia as a symbol of Hindu-Muslim unity in the freedom struggle, with Khan's involvement bridging medical, educational, and political domains to sustain the university's early operations in Aligarh before its relocation to Delhi in 1925.[15]

Political Involvement

Initial Engagement and All-India Muslim League Ties

Hakim Ajmal Khan's political engagement began in the early 20th century amid efforts to secure representation for Muslims in British India's legislative councils. On October 1, 1906, he participated in the Simla Deputation, a delegation of 35 prominent Muslim leaders led by the Aga Khan that presented a memorandum to Viceroy Lord Minto, demanding separate electorates, weightage in representation, and protections against Hindu-majority dominance in elections—demands that influenced the Morley-Minto Reforms enacted in the Indian Councils Act 1909.[7][19] This advocacy extended to the formation of the All-India Muslim League (AIML) later that year. During the All-India Muhammadan Educational Conference in Dhaka on December 30, 1906, Khan seconded Nawab Khwaja Salimullah's proposal to establish the AIML as a political organization dedicated to advancing Muslim interests, promoting loyalty to the British government, and preventing hostility toward other communities.[20][21] The league's founding resolution, supported by Khan and others including Maulana Muhammad Ali Jauhar, marked the first formalized Muslim political party in India, initially focused on elite constitutional petitions rather than broad mobilization.[4] Khan maintained active ties with the AIML in its formative phase, serving as President of the Reception Committee at the league's third annual session in Delhi on January 29–30, 1910, presided over by the Aga Khan.[22] In his address, he underscored the need for Muslim educational and political unity to counter perceived marginalization, reflecting the organization's early emphasis on reforms within the imperial framework.[23] These roles positioned Khan among the AIML's Delhi-based influencers, though his engagements remained aligned with the league's loyalist orientation until broader nationalist currents drew him toward inter-communal cooperation.[7]

Shift to Indian National Congress

Hakim Ajmal Khan's political orientation shifted decisively towards the Indian National Congress in the aftermath of the Jallianwala Bagh massacre on April 13, 1919, which eroded his prior loyalty to British rule and prompted alignment with nationalist forces.[4] Previously engaged with the All-India Muslim League since its inception in 1906, where he participated in the Simla Deputation and supported its formation at the Dacca meeting, Khan had advocated for Muslim interests including separate electorates.[7] By 1917, he had begun approaching Mahatma Gandhi and Congress leaders, signaling an early drift from loyalist politics amid broader discontent with colonial policies such as the proposed withdrawal of professional recognition for indigenous medical practitioners in 1910.[7] The Khilafat Movement, aimed at preserving the Ottoman Caliphate, facilitated this transition by forging a temporary Hindu-Muslim alliance against British imperialism. On March 19, 1920—designated Khilafat Day—Khan renounced his titles in protest, a act that underscored his growing nationalist commitment.[4] He actively supported Gandhi's Non-Cooperation Movement launched later that year, resigning from his position at Aligarh Muslim University when its authorities refused participation, thereby prioritizing swaraj over institutional ties.[7] This stance reflected his emphasis on Hindu-Muslim unity, as evidenced by his earlier presidency of the Muslim League's Amritsar session in December 1919, where he urged Muslims to avoid cow slaughter during Bakrid to foster communal harmony.[4] Khan's alignment culminated in his election as acting president of the Indian National Congress at the Ahmedabad session in December 1921, stepping in after Chittaranjan Das's arrest.[7] In this role, he advocated non-violent resistance and reinforced the Congress-Khilafat partnership, positioning himself as a bridge between Muslim and broader Indian aspirations for independence. His dual presidencies—of the Muslim League in 1919 and Congress in 1921—highlighted efforts to reconcile communal interests with anti-colonial struggle, though underlying tensions persisted.[4]

Leadership in Non-Cooperation and Khilafat Movements

Hakim Ajmal Khan played a pivotal role in the Khilafat Movement, which sought to pressure the British government to preserve the Ottoman Caliphate following World War I. In 1919, he helped organize the All-India Khilafat Committee alongside leaders like the Ali brothers and Maulana Abul Kalam Azad.[4] On 19 March 1920, designated as Khilafat Day, Khan publicly renounced his British honors, including the Kaiser-i-Hind Gold Medal, two coronation silver medals, and the title Haziq-ul-Mulk, in protest against British policies toward the Caliphate.[4] He was elected president of the All-India Khilafat Committee in 1921, providing leadership during a period of heightened Muslim agitation.[4] Khan's commitment extended to the Non-Cooperation Movement launched by Mahatma Gandhi in August 1920, which allied with Khilafat efforts to promote Hindu-Muslim unity against British rule. To advance the boycott of government institutions, he resigned from the trusteeship of Aligarh Muslim University in 1920 after its management refused to endorse the movement.[7] On 19 October 1920, Khan co-founded Jamia Millia Islamia in Aligarh as a nationalist alternative to British-affiliated education, serving as its first chancellor until 1927 and relocating it to Delhi in 1925.[4] In December 1921, at the Ahmedabad session of the Indian National Congress, Khan acted as president in place of C. R. Das, who had been arrested prior to the event.[7] In his presidential address, he underscored the imperative of non-cooperation and personal sacrifice to achieve Swaraj, reinforcing the movement's momentum amid widespread participation.[7] His leadership bridged communal divides, positioning him as a key figure in sustaining the alliance between Khilafat activists and Congress nationalists until the movements' suspension in February 1922 following the Chauri Chaura incident.[4]

Imprisonments and Personal Challenges

Arrests During Freedom Struggle

Hakim Ajmal Khan actively supported the Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–1922) and the concurrent Khilafat Movement, both of which prompted widespread British crackdowns, including the arrests of key figures such as the Ali brothers and, later, Mahatma Gandhi in March 1922.[7] Despite his prominent involvement—such as presiding over the 1921 Indian National Congress session as acting president in place of the imprisoned C. R. Das—Ajmal Khan evaded personal arrest, enabling sustained organizational leadership.[7] Following Gandhi's six-year sentence for sedition, Ajmal Khan assumed the chairmanship of the Central Non-Cooperation Committee, coordinating efforts amid escalating government repression that jailed thousands of participants.[19] His unarrested status, unusual among top Khilafat and Congress leaders, allowed him to bridge Hindu-Muslim alliances and advocate for boycotts of British institutions, though the movements' suspension after the February 1922 Chauri Chaura violence curtailed mass actions.[7] No records indicate Ajmal Khan faced formal detention or trial during these campaigns, contrasting with contemporaries like Abul Kalam Azad, who endured imprisonment; this may reflect British leniency toward select Muslim elites with prior loyalty during World War I, as Ajmal Khan had aided war efforts before shifting to nationalism amid Khilafat leader arrests.[7] His freedom facilitated post-1922 reconstruction of Congress activities, including support for swarajist factions.

Health Decline and Final Years

In the aftermath of his arrest in December 1921 amid the Non-Cooperation Movement, Hakim Ajmal Khan's health began to decline, compounded by the rigors of imprisonment and his earlier bout with tuberculosis contracted during medical studies.[24] He had been detained in Calcutta for his role as acting president of the Indian National Congress, a position thrust upon him following C.R. Das's arrest, but the physical strain of incarceration exacerbated underlying vulnerabilities.[7] Released after several months, he persisted in public duties despite persistent ailments, which he publicly acknowledged as limiting his activities by the mid-1920s.[24] By 1925, worsening health prompted his withdrawal from frontline political engagement, though he maintained oversight of institutions like Jamia Millia Islamia, where he served as chancellor until his death.[25] In early 1927, undeterred by frailty, he toured Bihar to rally support for nationalist causes, arriving in Patna on February 10 and addressing gatherings on communal harmony and independence.[16] This exertion reflected his commitment, even as contemporaries noted his two-year struggle with debilitating illness.[24] Khan renounced his British-awarded titles, including Masih-ul-Mulk, in solidarity with the freedom struggle shortly before succumbing to a massive heart attack on the night of December 27–28, 1927, while visiting Rampur; his death was confirmed the following day, December 29.[16][14] His body was transported to Delhi for burial at Mutawaliyan graveyard, marking the end of a life marked by unyielding service amid physical toll.[16]

Legacy and Assessments

Impact on Medicine and Education

Hakim Ajmal Khan advanced Unani medicine by establishing the All India Unani Tibbi Conference in 1906, which united practitioners across India and advocated for the system's recognition against colonial prioritization of Western biomedicine.[5] In 1908, he formed the Anjuman Tibbiyya to oversee the Madrasa Tibbiyya, evolving it into the Ayurvedic and Unani Tibbia College in Delhi, the first institution to offer structured education in both Unani and Ayurvedic traditions, despite opposition from traditionalists wary of formalization.[12] He promoted synergy between the systems, likening them to complementary eyes, and co-founded the Indian Medicines Company to fund the college through commercial production of herbal remedies.[1] [4] These initiatives institutionalized Unani training in northern India, countering marginalization under British rule and laying groundwork for its integration into modern regulatory frameworks post-1947.[2] Khan's medical legacy emphasized empirical validation of herbal treatments, drawing from his proficiency in Unani pharmacology to treat ailments like diabetes and respiratory disorders, while pushing for scientific standardization without diluting Greco-Arabic principles.[3] His work influenced subsequent policy, including the establishment of dedicated Unani departments in universities and the 1970 Indian Medicine Central Council Act, which formalized indigenous systems.[26] In education, Khan co-founded Jamia Millia Islamia on October 29, 1920, in Aligarh as a swadeshi alternative to government colleges boycotted during the Non-Cooperation Movement, serving as its first Chancellor (Amir-e-Jamia) from November 22, 1920, until his death.[17] Under his guidance, the institution prioritized nationalist curricula blending Islamic ethics, modern sciences, and vocational skills, relocating to Delhi in 1925 to evade colonial suppression.[27] He pioneered women's admission, fostering inclusive access in an era of gender segregation, and directed resources toward self-reliance, including technical programs that produced early graduates in engineering and teaching.[28] Jamia's enduring status as a central university reflects his vision of education as a tool for cultural preservation and anti-colonial resistance, impacting over 30,000 students annually today.[29]

Evaluations of Political Role and Hindu-Muslim Unity Efforts

Hakim Ajmal Khan's political role is assessed by historians as that of a pivotal nationalist Muslim figure who bridged early separatist tendencies in the All-India Muslim League with inclusive Congress leadership, notably as its president at the Ahmedabad session on December 28, 1921, the only such instance for a Muslim before independence.[30] This shift underscored his advocacy for swaraj within a united India, prioritizing anti-colonial mobilization over communal exclusivity, though some analyses note it reflected a minority position among Muslims increasingly drawn to separate electorates post-1909 Morley-Minto reforms.[30] In the Khilafat and Non-Cooperation movements from 1919 to 1922, Khan mobilized Muslims via religious symbolism while aligning with Hindu leaders, renouncing titles like Haziq-ul-Millat on January 19, 1921, to exemplify sacrifice.[30] Evaluations credit him with fostering temporary alliances, as in his role convening the All India Khilafat Committee, but causal analyses highlight the movement's fragility: its collapse after the Turkish Caliphate's abolition on March 3, 1924, exposed reliance on external pan-Islamic ties rather than enduring intra-Indian reconciliation, precipitating riots like those in Kohat (1924) that eroded joint fronts.[31] Efforts at Hindu-Muslim unity, including Khan's speeches urging cooperation—such as at the 1919 Muslim League session emphasizing shared anti-imperial goals—were sincere but empirically limited by unaddressed divergences in political aspirations, with unity pacts fracturing amid post-1922 communal violence that claimed thousands of lives.[30][32] Historiographical assessments, often from nationalist perspectives, portray Khan as a model of composite patriotism, yet partition-era outcomes reveal these initiatives as insufficient against rising separatism, where Muslim League demands for safeguards evolved into demands for division by 1940, rendering unity efforts a prelude to failure rather than prevention.[30] Mainstream academic sources, potentially influenced by post-independence secular narratives, emphasize his bridging role without fully reckoning with the causal primacy of unresolved identity conflicts over optimistic alliances.[30]

Modern Commemorations and Critiques

The Government of India issued a commemorative postage stamp honoring Hakim Ajmal Khan on February 13, 1987, recognizing his contributions to Unani medicine, education, and the independence movement.[33] His birth anniversary on February 11 is annually observed as World Unani Day, with events held across India and Pakistan to celebrate his pioneering efforts in reviving and modernizing Unani medicine through institutions like the Tibbi College in Delhi and the Central Council for Research in Unani Medicine.[34][35] Modern commemorative activities include seminars, health awareness programs, and tributes at institutions such as Jamia Millia Islamia, which he co-founded, and the Hakim Ajmal Khan Institute, often tied to national initiatives like Rashtriya Poshan Maah.[36] These events emphasize his role in bridging traditional healing with scientific validation, including his establishment of the Majlis-i Tahqiqat 'Ilmi in 1926 for systematic Unani research.[12] Critiques of Ajmal Khan's legacy are sparse in contemporary scholarship, which predominantly affirms his integrative approach to medicine and politics without significant contention.[4] Some observers have noted a relative obscurity of his broader political and educational impacts beyond Unani circles, attributing this to the overshadowing of pre-partition unity advocates in post-independence historiographies focused on partition-era figures.[37] His opposition to separate electorates and emphasis on composite nationalism, while praised for promoting Hindu-Muslim cooperation, has not drawn substantial modern reevaluation or criticism, even amid debates on communal politics.[38]
