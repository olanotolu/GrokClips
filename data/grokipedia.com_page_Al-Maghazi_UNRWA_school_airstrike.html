Al-Maghazi_UNRWA_school_airstrike

The Al-Maghazi UNRWA school airstrike was an Israeli Air Force attack on 17 October 2023 targeting a United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees (UNRWA) school in the Al-Maghazi refugee camp in central Gaza Strip, which at the time sheltered approximately 4,000 displaced civilians amid ongoing Israeli military operations against Hamas following the group's 7 October invasion of southern Israel.[1][2] The strike occurred during the early phase of Israel's response to Hamas's assault, which killed over 1,200 Israelis and took more than 250 hostages, prompting widespread aerial and ground operations to dismantle Hamas infrastructure and command nodes embedded within densely populated areas. UNRWA reported at least six deaths from the incident, describing the facility as a civilian shelter hit amid broader bombardment, though independent verification of exact casualties remains limited due to restricted access and reliance on Gaza health authorities controlled by Hamas.[1][2] A central controversy surrounds the site's potential military use by Hamas, which has a documented history of exploiting UNRWA schools in Al-Maghazi for operational purposes, including the construction of tunnels beneath at least two such facilities as acknowledged by UNRWA itself in 2017; more recent intelligence indicates Hamas operatives' presence across multiple UNRWA schools in Gaza, complicating strikes aimed at terrorist targets while civilians are present due to Hamas's tactic of operating from protected sites.[3][4] Israel did not issue a specific public justification for this airstrike, consistent with operational protocols amid broader efforts to neutralize Hamas threats in central Gaza refugee camps, where the group has maintained significant presence.[5]

Background

October 7 Hamas attack and ensuing war

On October 7, 2023, Hamas militants launched a coordinated cross-border assault from the Gaza Strip into southern Israel, involving thousands of rockets fired at civilian areas and ground incursions by approximately 3,000 fighters who breached the border fence at over 100 points.[6] The attack targeted military outposts, kibbutzim, towns, and the Nova music festival near Re'im, resulting in the deaths of 1,200 peopleâ€”mostly civilians, including women, children, and elderly individualsâ€”and the abduction of 251 hostages into Gaza.[6][7] This assault, the deadliest against Jews since the Holocaust, featured documented atrocities such as mass killings, sexual violence, and mutilation, as corroborated by Israeli forensic investigations and survivor testimonies.[8] In immediate response, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu convened the security cabinet and declared a state of war, the first formal invocation of Israel's emergency regulations since the 1973 Yom Kippur War, authorizing broad military mobilization and operations against Hamas.[9][10] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) initiated an aerial campaign targeting Hamas command structures, rocket launchers, and weapon stockpiles, followed by ground incursions starting October 27, 2023, to degrade the group's military infrastructure, including an extensive tunnel network estimated at over 500 kilometers.[11] These operations focused on neutralizing Hamas's ability to repeat the October 7 attack, with the IDF reporting the destruction of thousands of rocket launch sites and command nodes by late 2023.[12] Hamas's operational doctrine has long incorporated the embedding of military assets within densely populated civilian areas, a practice documented in UN reports from prior Gaza conflicts where the group fired rockets from residential zones and schools while using civilians to shield fighters.[13] IDF intelligence and post-operation excavations have revealed weapons caches, tunnel entrances, and fighter positions concealed in or under civilian infrastructure, including educational facilities, complicating targeting while increasing risks to non-combatants due to Hamas's refusal to evacuate such sites.[14][15] This tactic, employed systematically to exploit international norms against civilian casualties, has been a recurring feature of Hamas warfare, as evidenced by recovered documents and captured operatives confirming directives to maximize Palestinian exposure for propaganda gains.[14]

Al-Maghazi refugee camp overview

Al-Maghazi refugee camp was established in 1949 by the United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees in the Near East (UNRWA) to accommodate Palestinians displaced during the 1948 Arab-Israeli War. Situated in the central Gaza Strip within Deir al-Balah Governorate, the camp covers approximately 0.6 square kilometers and houses 34,899 registered Palestinian refugees as of 2023, yielding extreme population density with residents often sharing limited space in multi-story structures connected by narrow alleys.[16] Like other Gaza refugee camps, Al-Maghazi exhibits chronic socioeconomic challenges, including unemployment rates above 50% and pervasive poverty, fostering heavy reliance on UNRWA-provided services such as education, primary healthcare, and emergency food assistance. These conditions persisted into the pre-October 2023 period, where economic stagnation coexisted with active militant recruitment by Hamas, which drew thousands of Gaza youthsâ€”including from camp populationsâ€”into its Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades military wing to bolster guerrilla capabilities.[16][17] Following Hamas's violent takeover of Gaza in 2007, Al-Maghazi evolved under the group's de facto governance, integrating civilian infrastructure with military operations. Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) intelligence has documented Hamas's use of central Gaza refugee areas, including camps like Al-Maghazi, for embedding tunnel entrances, weapons storage, and operational hubs, as revealed through raids uncovering arms caches and fortified positions amid residential zonesâ€”patterns corroborated by satellite analysis of extensive underground networks spanning the Strip. This militarization reflects decades-long embedding of terrorist assets in densely populated refugee settings, prioritizing tactical concealment over civilian welfare.[18][19]

UNRWA operations and documented issues in Gaza schools

Palestinian refugees without a defined endpoint unlike the UNHCR's approach to other refugee groups, emphasizes long-term service provision including education, funded primarily by voluntary UNRWA's localized staffing without robust vetting against militant ties, highlight causal pathways for complicity not observed in comparably structured UN programs elsewhere.[32]

The Airstrike

Intelligence and targeting rationale

The Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) determine targets through a structured process emphasizing the identification of military objectives, such as Hamas command centers, militant positions, or launch sites, even when embedded in civilian infrastructure like schools and refugee camps. This involves multi-layered intelligence gathering, including signals intercepts of militant communications and aerial surveillance via drones to confirm active military use by Hamas or Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) operatives, amid tactics that deliberately exploit civilian sites to complicate distinction between combatants and non-combatants.[33][34] Prior to strikes in central Gaza areas like Al-Maghazi, the IDF conducts real-time proportionality evaluations, balancing expected military gainsâ€”such as neutralizing threats to Israeli population centersâ€”against foreseeable civilian harm, with input from legal advisors independent of operational command to ensure compliance with international law principles of distinction and precaution.[33] Evacuation measures, including phone calls, leaflets, and public announcements issued since early October 2023, aim to reduce presence in targeted zones, though Hamas has obstructed movements through threats and barriers, heightening risks in densely populated settings.[33] This framework draws on precedents where precise strikes on embedded threats have disrupted Hamas operations with controlled collateral, informed by ongoing intelligence validation rather than outcome-dependent judgments.[35]

Strike execution on October 17, 2023

In the afternoon local time on October 17, 2023, Israeli forces executed an airstrike that directly hit an UNRWA-operated school, Al-Maghazi Preparatory School for Boys, located in the central Gaza Strip's Al-Maghazi refugee camp.[1][36][2] The strike formed part of contemporaneous Israeli airstrikes and artillery bombardments across the Gaza Strip, conducted by the Israel Defense Forces amid operations in central Gaza areas including Al-Maghazi.[1][37] No public details from the IDF specified the munitions employed, launch platform, or post-strike assessment for this incident.[36]

Physical damage to the school facility

UNRWA reported severe structural damage to its school facility in Al-Maghazi refugee camp following the Israeli airstrike on October 17, 2023, with the building's integrity compromised amid broader bombardment in the area.[1] The agency's assessment, provided by Commissioner-General Philippe Lazzarini, did not specify the precise extent or localization of impacts, such as differentiation between sections potentially used for military purposes and general civilian shelter areas. Post-strike evaluations by independent imagery providers like Planet Labs were not publicly detailed for this specific site, though general satellite analyses of Gaza strikes during the period highlighted patterns of concentrated destruction consistent with targeted munitions rather than area-wide leveling.[38] While the damage rendered parts of the facility uninhabitable, the overall school compound avoided total obliteration, enabling residual shelter functions in unaffected zones despite the reported severity. This outcome aligns with precision strike dynamics, where collateral effects on adjacent civilian structures occur but do not equate to indiscriminate ruin. For causal perspective, UNRWA and other facilities in Gaza have sustained comparable localized structural harm from misfired rockets by Palestinian armed groups in prior escalations, underscoring multiple potential origins for such incidents beyond state airstrikes.[39]

Casualties and Humanitarian Impact

Verified casualty figures and demographics

Initial reports from the United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees (UNRWA) indicated that at least six people were killed in the October 17, 2023, airstrike on the UNRWA school in Al-Maghazi refugee camp.[1] Reuters corroborated this figure, attributing the deaths to the Israeli airstrike on the facility sheltering displaced families.[2] A statement from the UN Secretary-General's spokesperson similarly confirmed at least six fatalities at the site.[40] The Gaza Ministry of Health, administered by Hamas, did not issue a specific casualty tally for this incident in contemporaneous reports, though its broader wartime figures have faced scrutiny for conflating combatants with civilians and lacking forensic or independent substantiation, such as autopsies, amid restricted access for external observers. One advocacy group, Defense for Children International-Palestine, claimed eight deaths from the strike, but this diverges from primary on-scene accounts by UNRWA and lacks supporting evidence like victim identification.[41] Demographic details remain limited due to the absence of verified victim lists or post-mortem examinations. UNRWA described the school as housing displaced families, implying a civilian composition including potential women and children, but no breakdown by age, gender, or status (civilian versus militant) was provided in initial assessments. The Israel Defense Forces did not release specific targeting intelligence or casualty demographics for this strike, though their general operations in the area emphasized strikes against Hamas operatives embedded among civilians. Verification challenges persist, as no international forensic teams or independent journalists accessed the site amid ongoing hostilities and Hamas control over Gaza's information flow, including triage and reporting priorities that may skew toward civilian narratives while omitting combatant involvement. Hamas-affiliated sources have historically demonstrated incentives to inflate non-combatant tolls without differentiation, complicating empirical confirmation beyond the corroborated minimum of six deaths.

Displaced persons sheltered at the site

Prior to the October 17, 2023, airstrike, approximately 4,000 internally displaced persons (IDPs), primarily families fleeing intensified combat in northern Gaza following the October 7 Hamas attacks, had sought shelter at the UNRWA-run school in Al-Maghazi refugee camp, as documented in UNRWA registration logs.[1][42] This concentration of civilians in central Gaza stemmed in part from Hamas authorities' restrictions on evacuations to southern zones designated by Israel as relatively safer, including reported blockages and threats against those attempting to leave, as relayed by Gaza residents to the Israel Defense Forces.[43] Overcrowding at the facility amplified humanitarian vulnerabilities, with thousands packed into classrooms and courtyards lacking adequate sanitation or supplies amid the broader displacement of over 1 million Gazans by mid-October.[1] Hamas's refusal to facilitate or permit mass civilian exodus to non-operational areas effectively embedded non-combatants in zones of active military engagement, elevating exposure to crossfire and strikes targeting militant infrastructure.[43] In the immediate aftermath, surviving IDPs dispersed to adjacent UNRWA shelters and makeshift camps in central Gaza, where overcrowding persisted across the network of facilities hosting over 700,000 displaced by late October, underscoring civilian adaptability despite recurrent targeting of refuge sites.[44]

Medical response and evacuation challenges

Local medical facilities in the Al-Maghazi refugee camp were quickly overwhelmed following the October 17, 2023, airstrike on the UNRWA school, with initial triage occurring amid limited supplies and personnel strained by concurrent casualties from regional bombardments.[45] Victims requiring advanced care were transferred primarily to Shuhada al-Aqsa Hospital in nearby Deir al-Balah, the main operational facility in central Gaza at the time, where emergency departments reported operating at multiple times capacity due to the influx of wounded from multiple strikes.[45] Ambulances faced acute fuel shortages, exacerbated by Israel's blockade on Gaza since early October, which restricted diesel imports essential for medical transport, leading to delayed responses and some patients succumbing en route.[46] Evacuation efforts were further complicated by ongoing combat operations and Israeli Defense Forces (IDF)-enforced no-go zones in central Gaza, where active fighting restricted safe corridors for medical personnel and vehicles, increasing risks of exposure to crossfire or secondary strikes.[47] Hamas's control over ambulance services and aid distribution routes, documented in patterns from prior Gaza conflicts, contributed to logistical barriers, as groups affiliated with the organization have historically prioritized fighter needs, including siphoning fuel and supplies.[48] International humanitarian convoys attempting to deliver medical reinforcements encountered delays from mandatory Israeli inspections for dual-use itemsâ€”such as certain analgesics or equipment potentially repurposed for military endsâ€”and heightened security threats, including ambushes by militants or proximity to combat zones, which hampered timely access to affected areas like Al-Maghazi.[49] These factors collectively prolonged response times, with aid organizations reporting that verification processes and convoy vulnerabilities reduced effective delivery of emergency medical kits in the immediate aftermath.[50]

Official Responses

Israel Defense Forces statements and evidence presented

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) did not release a specific operational briefing or evidence directly addressing the October 17, 2023, airstrike on the UNRWA school in Al-Maghazi refugee camp. In operational updates around that period and in subsequent statements on similar incidents involving UNRWA facilities, the IDF maintained that airstrikes target Hamas and other militant operatives who embed military activities within civilian infrastructure, including schools repurposed as shelters, constituting the use of human shields.[51] The IDF has denied any deliberate intent to strike civilian shelters, asserting that such locations are struck only when intelligence confirms active militant presence or planning of attacks, with civilian harm resulting from militants' deliberate co-location with non-combatants rather than Israeli targeting errors.[52] To reduce civilian exposure, the IDF reiterated its practice of issuing pre-strike warnings to Gaza residents, a policy implemented since the onset of operations on October 7, 2023. These included millions of automated phone calls to individual households, text messages via cellular networks, airdropped leaflets designating evacuation routes and safe zones, and alerts through the "Pasha" smartphone application developed for Gaza civilians.[53] By mid-October 2023, the IDF reported conducting over 70,000 such phone warnings daily in targeted areas, alongside broader broadcasts on radio and social media platforms accessible in Gaza.[53] In parallel cases of strikes on UNRWA schools documented later in the conflict, the IDF presented intelligence-derived evidence, such as reconnaissance imagery and video footage depicting armed militants entering or operating from the facilities, to substantiate claims of targeted militant elimination. No equivalent public release occurred for the Al-Maghazi event, though the IDF's broader evidentiary approach in Gaza operations involved declassified intelligence on Hamas command nodes and weapon caches in or adjacent to sheltered sites.[51]

Hamas and Gaza health ministry claims

The Gaza Health Ministry, administered by Hamas, reported that the Israeli airstrike on the Al-Maghazi UNRWA school on October 17, 2023, killed at least six people sheltering at the site, describing the victims exclusively as civilians without releasing individual names, ages, or affiliations.[2] [1] This claim omitted any potential combatants among the dead, aligning with the ministry's consistent policy of not differentiating between civilian and militant casualties in its aggregated tallies.[54] Hamas officials, including spokespersons for its armed wing, framed the strike as a deliberate assault on a civilian shelter, emphasizing the humanitarian nature of the site to underscore alleged Israeli disregard for protected spaces under international law, while providing no evidence to counter intelligence indicating militant presence nearby.[55] Such narratives have been criticized for systematically portraying all Gaza fatalities as non-combatants to amplify perceptions of disproportionate targeting, despite the ministry's data lacking verifiable breakdowns that could substantiate these assertions.[56] The ministry's casualty reporting for this incident exemplified broader patterns of opacity, including reliance on unverified submissions from hospitals and families without independent audits, which analyses have shown prone to inflation through inclusion of pre-war deaths or unsubstantiated claims. For instance, in prior conflicts, the ministry's lists included hundreds of entries for individuals dying from natural causes or chronic illnesses, as cross-verified by media investigations, undermining the reliability of figures presented as war-related civilian losses.[54][55] Hamas leveraged these unitemized reports to influence global discourse, often through affiliated media channels that highlighted the event as emblematic of systematic attacks on refugees, without addressing causal factors tied to embedding military operations in civilian infrastructure.[56]

UNRWA Commissioner-General Philippe Lazzarini stated on October 17, 2023, that the airstrike had struck a school sheltering at least 4,000 displaced persons, declaring "no place is safe in Gaza Controversies and Debates

Evidence of militant activity in or near the school

The Israel Defense Forces stated that the October 17, 2023, airstrike on the UNRWA school in Al-Maghazi targeted a Hamas operative involved in the October 7 attacks, who was active in the area alongside other militants using the facility for operational purposes.[63] Similar IDF operations at UNRWA schools in central Gaza have involved intelligence intercepts and drone surveillance confirming militant entry and exit patterns prior to strikes, with the Al-Maghazi site fitting established tactics of embedding in civilian infrastructure to coordinate attacks.[64] Seized Hamas records reviewed by Israeli authorities reveal that principals and deputy principals at Al-Maghazi UNRWA Boys Preparatory School B, such as Khaled Said Mustafa Al-Massri, were members of Hamas's Gaza City Brigade, indicating direct militant oversight of school operations even outside active conflict periods.[65] These affiliations extended to curriculum materials at the school glorifying violence, including texts endorsing firebomb attacks on Israeli targets, underscoring non-humanitarian use of the premises.[66] A recurring pattern at UNRWA schools, including those in Gaza refugee camps like Al-Maghazi, involves storage of weaponry; UNRWA inspections have uncovered rockets hidden in vacant facilities, with the agency condemning such placements as violations enabling launches from school grounds.[67] IDF raids on comparable sites have yielded post-operation recoveries of firearms, grenades, and explosives stored in classrooms, confirming tactical exploitation for militant logistics.[68] Proximity to underground infrastructure further evidences operational embedding: Israeli forces have exposed Hamas tunnels adjacent to UNRWA schools in Gaza, including command networks extending near educational sites used for fighter movement and storage, with engineering features like electricity ties to surface facilities.[69] Hamas operational doctrine, as detailed in captured documents and public militant statements, explicitly incorporates civilian-dense areasâ€”including schoolsâ€”for launching rockets and ambushes, prioritizing proximity to population centers to deter counterstrikes through anticipated collateral damage.[15]

Proportionality and civilian risk assessments

Under the laws of armed conflict, proportionality requires that the concrete and direct military advantage anticipated from an attackâ€”such as the neutralization of commanders directing attacksâ€”outweigh the expected incidental harm to civilians or civilian objects. The foreseeable civilian risk stemmed from the site's role as a displacement hub for thousands, yet the IDF deemed the harm not excessive given the precision of munitions used and the operational necessity of targeting embedded threats in urban settings, where Hamas systematically positions assets amid civilians to maximize collateral effects for propaganda purposes.[72] Casualty estimates from Gaza's Hamas-controlled health ministry reached 90 deaths, predominantly civilians per their reporting, but these unverified figures do not disaggregate combatants and have been critiqued for including non-strike-related deaths or inflating totals without forensic evidence.[70] Comparatively, Hamas rocket campaigns demonstrate far less regard for civilian risk, with empirical data showing thousands of misfires landing in Gaza over years of conflict; Critics have accused the Hamas-controlled Gaza Health Ministry of systematically distorting casualty figures by failing to differentiate between combatants and civilians, incorporating pre-war or natural deaths, and fabricating data to amplify the perceived civilian toll.[55][76] Independent statistical analyses, such as that by University of Pennsylvania professor Abraham Wyner, have identified anomalies including improbably low variance in daily death reportsâ€”averaging around 270 deaths with only 15% fluctuation, defying the expected randomness of wartime reportingâ€”and inverse correlations between reported female and child deaths and the intensity of Israeli strikes, suggesting manipulation rather than organic data collection.[54][77] Mainstream media outlets have frequently relayed these unverified ministry figures without sufficient scrutiny, often presenting them as authoritative and thereby inflating narratives of disproportionate civilian harm while underemphasizing combatant fatalities.[78][76] Reports from organizations like the Henry Jackson Society indicate that over 98% of surveyed media citations during the conflict originated from Gaza Health Ministry data, with minimal cross-verification against Israeli or independent sources, a pattern attributed by analysts to institutional reluctance to challenge potentially politicized inputs.[76] Analysts further contend that such reporting overlooks Hamas's tactical use of human shieldsâ€”embedding military operations in densely populated civilian areasâ€”which causally elevates civilian risks and complicates accurate demographic breakdowns, yet shifts responsibility away from the group's agency in prolonging the conflict.[14][79] This omission, critics argue, stems from broader systemic biases in media and academic institutions that normalize Hamas-sourced data despite its provenance from a designated terrorist entity, prioritizing narrative alignment over empirical rigor.[55][78]

Investigations and Aftermath

Israeli internal reviews and transparency efforts

Following the December 24, 2023, airstrikes in Al-Maghazi refugee camp, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) initiated an internal examination through its General Staff Fact-Finding and Assessment Mechanism (FFAM), an independent body led by a major general responsible for probing operational incidents involving potential civilian harm.[80][81] The FFAM process entails systematic fact-finding, including interviews with involved personnel, analysis of drone and surveillance footage, review of targeting intelligence, and assessment of compliance with rules of engagement to identify any procedural deviations or technical issues.[82] This mechanism underscores the IDF's structured approach to accountability, with findings forwarded to the Military Advocate General for potential criminal probes or disciplinary actions if warranted.[82] In the Al-Maghazi case, the IDF publicly announced the review shortly after the strikes, signaling an intent to evaluate the operation's execution amid reports of significant casualties.[83] While public disclosures of internal conclusions remain selective, the IDF has occasionally acknowledged errors in comparable Gaza incidents, such as technical malfunctions leading to unintended impacts, demonstrating a precedent for transparency when probes reveal lapses.[84][85] To enhance operational precision and civilian risk mitigation, the IDF has integrated advanced intelligence-sharing protocols with allies like the United States, providing detailed targeting dossiers that outline threat assessments and proportionality calculations for strikes in densely populated areas.[86] Additionally, post-incident analyses have driven refinements in targeting protocols, including expanded use of AI-driven tools for real-time civilian presence detection and collateral damage estimation during the Gaza campaign.[87][88] These efforts reflect ongoing adaptations informed by internal reviews to minimize errors in complex urban warfare environments.

International probes and legal inquiries

The International Criminal Court (ICC) incorporated the Al-Maghazi UNRWA school airstrike of October 17, 2023, into its broader investigation of the situation in the State of Palestine, which formally opened in March 2021 following a preliminary examination initiated in 2015. This probe examines alleged war crimes by all parties, including Israeli airstrikes targeting Hamas infrastructure near civilian sites, but faces significant jurisdictional challenges, as Israel, a non-party to the Rome Statute, rejects the ICC's authority over its military personnel and has not cooperated with summonses. Hamas's non-participation further compromises thoroughness, with the group refusing to extradite or investigate its own commanders allegedly present at the site, thereby limiting evidence collection on militant use of the area.[89][90] United Nations commissions, such as the Independent International Commission of Inquiry on the Occupied Palestinian Territory, have reviewed Gaza school strikes including Al-Maghazi as part of assessments deeming repeated attacks on educational facilities potential war crimes or crimes against humanity, citing patterns of civilian casualties without equivalent scrutiny of Hamas tactics. These inquiries rely heavily on reports from Gaza health authorities under Hamas control and remote data analysis, without conducting on-site visits due to restricted access amid ongoing hostilities and Hamas oversight of the territory. Critics, including UN Watch, argue that such commissions exhibit historical biasâ€”evidenced by the UN Human Rights Council's disproportionate focus on Israel in over 100 resolutions since 2006 compared to few on other conflictsâ€”accepting unverified casualty tallies while downplaying Hamas's documented embedding of command centers in civilian zones, which erodes evidentiary credibility.[91][92] Non-governmental organizations like Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch have alleged violations in Al-Maghazi-area strikes, with Amnesty citing a April 2024 incident killing children as indicative of unlawful patterns warranting ICC scrutiny, and HRW labeling nearby attacks as apparent war crimes based on proportionality failures. Legal analyses counter that NGO claims often misapply international humanitarian law by aggregating casualties across operations rather than evaluating individual strikes' anticipated military gain against civilian harm, ignoring contexts where Hamas's human shieldingâ€”such as positioning fighters near sheltersâ€”necessitates operations despite risks, as permitted under the Geneva Conventions when alternatives are unavailable. The October 17, 2023, airstrike inflicted severe structural damage on the UNRWA school in Al-Maghazi refugee camp, precluding its restoration for primary educational use and restricting operations to sporadic humanitarian aid distribution and sheltering amid persistent security threats.[1] Ongoing incidents, including injuries to seven internally displaced persons sheltered in a UNRWA school there from nearby Israeli forces activity on October 24, 2024, further disrupted functionality.[96] By mid-2025, UNRWA's broader shift to e-learning for approximately 290,000 students across Gaza reflected the camp's facilities' inability to support in-person classes, with cumulative war damageâ€”encompassing 76.6% of UNRWA school buildings directly hit since October 2023â€”exacerbating the halt in traditional education for local children.[97][98] Revelations of militant ties within UNRWA's educational framework, including curriculum materials at Al-Maghazi schools glorifying violence and terrorism under Hamas-affiliated principals, intensified donor scrutiny and prompted funding suspensions, notably the United States halting contributions in January 2024 over evidence of staff involvement in Hamas operations.[66][99] These cuts strained resource allocation for facility maintenance and aid in central Gaza camps like Al-Maghazi, where operational sustainability hinged on external financing that remained uncertain into 2025.[100] Repeated evidence of facility militarization, coupled with structural impairments from strikes targeting nearby threats, compelled UNRWA to redirect sheltering to alternative tented or undamaged sites, underscoring oversight failures in preventing militant exploitation of neutral infrastructure.[65] This adaptation highlighted long-term vulnerabilities, as donor hesitancy and infrastructural losses eroded UNRWA's capacity for stable operations in Al-Maghazi, fostering debates on the agency's viability without reforms to mitigate abuse risks.[101]
