2023 Kedah state election

The 2023 Kedah state election was held on 12 August 2023 to elect the 36 members of the Kedah State Legislative Assembly, the unicameral legislature of the Malaysian state of Kedah.[1] Perikatan Nasional (PN), a coalition comprising the Malaysian Islamic Party (PAS), Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia (BERSATU), and Gerakan, secured a supermajority victory by winning 33 seats—21 for PAS, 11 for BERSATU, and 1 for Gerakan—retaining control of the state government despite the federal power shift to Pakatan Harapan (PH) following the November 2022 general election.[2][1] This election, part of simultaneous polls in five other states, tested the resilience of PN's hold on Malay-majority northern states after losing the federal government to a PH-led unity coalition including Barisan Nasional (BN).[3] Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor, the incumbent Menteri Besar from BERSATU, retained his Jeneri constituency with a substantial majority and was reappointed to lead the state executive, underscoring PN's strong voter base in rural and conservative areas.[4][5] The PH-BN alliance, contesting jointly, managed only 3 seats (2 for PKR and 1 for DAP), highlighting persistent ethnic and ideological divides in voter preferences, with PN dominating among Malay voters who prioritized issues like religion and anti-corruption rhetoric over federal alignment.[2][6] The outcome reinforced PN's governance model in Kedah, emphasizing Islamist policies and populist appeals under Sanusi's leadership, though it faced criticism for governance lapses such as unfulfilled development promises and controversies surrounding the Menteri Besar's public persona.[7] Despite these, the landslide affirmed empirical support for PN's platform in a state where economic underperformance had not eroded its mandate, signaling challenges for the federal unity government's cohesion in opposition strongholds.[8]

Political Context

Post-2022 General Election Landscape

In the 15th Malaysian general election held on November 19, 2022, Perikatan Nasional (PN) unexpectedly captured control of the Kedah state assembly, securing 33 out of 36 seats through strong performances by its component parties, including 21 seats for Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS), 11 for Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia (Bersatu), and 1 for Gerakan.[2] This outcome contrasted sharply with the federal level, where Pakatan Harapan (PH) emerged as the largest bloc and formed a unity government under Anwar Ibrahim as prime minister on November 24, 2022, after a hung parliament.[9] The PN's success in Kedah reflected a broader "green wave" of support for conservative Islamist policies among rural Malay voters, enabling the coalition to wrest the state from PH despite the latter's national gains.[10] Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor, who had served as Menteri Besar since August 2020 under the previous PN administration, was reaffirmed in his position following the election, leading the state's governance with an emphasis on appealing to conservative Malay-Muslim sentiments.[11] The PN government's early priorities included promoting Islamic values and addressing post-COVID-19 economic recovery through initiatives targeted at the Malay majority, amid ongoing challenges like unemployment and agricultural sector revitalization in Kedah's paddy granary regions.[12] This approach underscored PN's strategy of leveraging ethno-religious identity to consolidate power in opposition-held states.[6] Relations between the PN-led Kedah government and the PH-dominated federal unity government soon revealed strains, particularly over resource allocation and policy alignment. Sanusi accused the federal administration of withholding development funds from opposition-controlled states, prompting Anwar to deny any victimization and affirm equitable distribution.[13] Disputes escalated with competing claims of credit for major investments, such as a €5 billion semiconductor project, highlighting tensions between state autonomy aspirations and federal oversight.[14] These frictions exemplified broader challenges for opposition state governments in navigating fiscal dependencies while pursuing divergent ideological agendas.[15]

Formation and Challenges of Perikatan Nasional Government

The Perikatan Nasional (PN) coalition assumed control of the Kedah state government on 12 May 2020, following the resignation of the Pakatan Harapan Menteri Besar amid defections triggered by the national political crisis, enabling PN to command support from 22 assemblypersons initially and later consolidating a working majority in the 36-seat legislative assembly.[16] Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor of Bersatu was sworn in as Menteri Besar on 20 May 2020, heading an administration dominated by PAS's Islamist priorities and bolstered by Bersatu's organizational structure.[17] Post the 15th general election on 19 November 2022, where PN captured 26 of Kedah's 36 federal parliamentary seats despite federal defeat, the coalition reinforced its state-level grip through demonstrated voter loyalty in rural Malay-majority areas, projecting control over approximately 32 state seats based on aligned electoral data.[18] PN's dynamics in Kedah centered on PAS's ideological leadership, advocating stricter hudud-related enforcement and religious conservatism, complemented by Bersatu's focus on anti-corruption campaigns and Malay socioeconomic protections, creating a unified front against perceived federal overreach by the Anwar Ibrahim unity government.[19] Early governance emphasized religious policy enhancements, such as the 2021–2025 Majlis Agama Islam Kedah strategic plan for intensified Islamic law enforcement and community welfare research, alongside infrastructure ambitions like high-impact economic corridors to generate jobs.[20] These efforts positioned PN as a defender of conservative values, with Sanusi promoting state sovereignty in domains like land reclamation projects. However, the government faced mounting challenges, including fiscal strains from stalled mega-initiatives such as the Langkasuka special economic zone, announced in 2021 to revive heritage-based development but hampered by federal funding denials and planning delays, leading to accusations of overambitious spending encroaching on national priorities.[21] Economic critiques highlighted persistent rural poverty and youth outmigration, with Kedah's unemployment contributing to national figures of 3.5% as of May 2023, amid claims of stagnation under PN stewardship.[22] Internal coalition tensions arose from Bersatu's factional disputes spilling into state affairs, risking defections, while opposition scrutiny targeted Sanusi's leadership through probes into governance decisions; state finances were pressured by low debt coverage, with consolidated funds servicing only 24.5% of obligations in 2023, second-lowest nationally and indicative of revenue shortfalls from delayed projects.[23][24]

Factors Prompting Early Dissolution

The dissolution of the Kedah State Legislative Assembly on 28 June 2023 was advised by Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor as a strategic move by Perikatan Nasional (PN) to renew its mandate shortly after the November 2022 general election, which saw the formation of a Pakatan Harapan-Barisan Nasional unity government at the federal level. This early call for polls, less than a year into the assembly's term, aligned with similar decisions in other PN-controlled states such as Kelantan and Terengganu, facilitating coordinated state elections on 12 August 2023 to streamline logistics and amplify opposition messaging against the federal administration.[25][26] Sanusi's rationale emphasized avoiding disruptions from religious observances, with dissolution timed post-Aidiladha to capitalize on PN's post-GE15 dominance in Kedah, where the coalition held 32 of 36 seats, predominantly through PAS's appeal to conservative Malay voters. By dissolving proactively, PN sought to preempt any gradual erosion of support amid federal-state tensions, including potential policy divergences and economic pressures from the unity government's reforms, positioning the state election as a proxy referendum on national political realignments. This realpolitik approach prioritized consolidating ethnic Malay loyalty over prolonging a term that could invite incremental challenges, despite no immediate threats to the government's majority.[27][28] Legal aspects drew minimal contention, as the Sultan of Kedah granted consent on 25 June 2023 following Sanusi's audience, adhering to constitutional provisions under Article 7 of the Kedah State Constitution, which vests dissolution authority with the Ruler on the advice of the Menteri Besar. While some observers questioned the timing's deviation from full-term norms, PN's supermajority insulated against viable no-confidence motions, underscoring the dissolution's role in offensive consolidation rather than defensive reaction to infighting or defections, which remained unsubstantiated in the state's stable assembly composition.[29][30]

Electoral Framework

State Assembly Structure and Constituencies

The Kedah State Legislative Assembly is a unicameral body comprising 36 members, each representing a single-member constituency elected via the first-past-the-post system, where the candidate with the most votes wins regardless of majority.[31] This electoral method, standard across Malaysian state assemblies, employs simple plurality voting with no proportional representation or reserved seats for minorities.[32] Constituencies are delineated by the Election Commission of Malaysia (SPR) to reflect population distribution, though gerrymandering allegations have periodically arisen due to disparities in voter numbers per seat, ranging from under 20,000 to over 40,000 electors in some cases.[33] The 36 constituencies (designated N.01 to N.36) span Kedah's 12 administrative districts, encompassing urban, semi-urban, and rural terrains that influence electoral dynamics through varying demographics and economic bases. Urban constituencies, concentrated in Alor Setar (e.g., N.01 Pengkalan Kundor, N.02 Alor Mengkudu) and Sungai Petani (e.g., N.27 Sungai Petani), feature mixed ethnic compositions with notable Chinese and Indian populations alongside Malays, driven by commercial and industrial activities.[34] In contrast, rural constituencies such as N.10 Sik, N.11 Baling, and N.35 Padang Terap are predominantly Malay-majority, agrarian areas focused on rice cultivation and traditional livelihoods, with lower urbanization rates amplifying community ties and cultural conservatism in voting patterns.[35] Eligibility to vote requires Malaysian citizenship, attainment of 18 years of age, residency in the constituency or overseas registration, and enrollment on the electoral roll maintained by the SPR; voters must present MyKad or equivalent identification at polling stations to verify identity and prevent fraud. Approximately 1.15 million electors were registered for the 2023 polls, reflecting Kedah's population of about 2.2 million, with mandatory preconditions like non-conviction for electoral offenses ensuring verifiable participation.[36][37]

The balance of 6 seats belonged to Barisan Nasional (BN) components, primarily United Malays National Organisation (UMNO), and minor parties or independents.[38][39]

Pre-Election Developments

Timeline of Key Events

On 15 June 2023, the Election Commission of Malaysia opened applications for domestic and international postal voting for the state elections, including Kedah, allowing eligible voters such as those abroad or in security forces to register until specified deadlines.[41] The Kedah State Legislative Assembly received royal consent for dissolution on 25 June 2023, with the formal dissolution occurring on 28 June 2023, initiating the caretaker government period under Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor and paving the way for elections within 60 days as per constitutional requirements.[42][43] On 5 July 2023, the Election Commission announced the key dates for the six concurrent state elections, including Kedah: nomination day on 29 July, a 14-day campaign period commencing the following day, early voting on 8 August for postal and advance voters, and polling day on 12 August.[44][45] Nomination day proceeded on 29 July 2023 without reported major incidents, with candidates from Perikatan Nasional (PN), Pakatan Harapan (PH), and others submitting papers at designated centers across Kedah's 36 state constituencies.[46] The campaign period ran from 30 July to 11 August 2023, during which federal-state political tensions surfaced, as Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim's unity government at the federal level urged support for PH-aligned candidates to foster national cohesion, while PN leaders in Kedah, including Sanusi, emphasized the state's operational independence from federal influence to appeal to local sentiments.[47][48] Early voting on 8 August 2023 for postal and absent voters occurred smoothly via the Election Commission's logistical arrangements, including secure ballot transport and verification processes.[49] Polling day on 12 August 2023 saw no significant weather or logistical disruptions reported in Kedah, with voting stations operating from 8:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m. under standard Election Commission protocols for queue management and voter verification using indelible ink and identification checks.[44]

Retiring Incumbents and Candidate Nominations

Perikatan Nasional (PN), the incumbent coalition, nominated candidates for all 36 state seats on 29 July 2023, distributing them across its components with Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS) contesting 21 seats, Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia (Bersatu) 12 seats, and Gerakan Pembebasan 3 seats.[2] This full-slate approach emphasized continuity in PN-held constituencies, where the coalition had secured a majority following defections from Pakatan Harapan (PH) after the 2018 election.[19] The PH-Barisan Nasional (BN) alliance, representing the federal unity government, also fielded candidates across all 36 seats without overlap, with United Malays National Organisation (UMNO) nominating 15, Democratic Action Party (DAP) 2, and Parti Keadilan Rakyat (PKR) 10.[2] Independent candidacies remained marginal, with only 10 independents nominated in total.[2] Retirements among incumbents were not widespread, but PKR implemented a major candidate refresh by retaining just one sitting assemblyman while dropping three others, signaling internal evaluations of past performance in opposition-held seats.[50] PN largely renominated its incumbents to capitalize on incumbency advantages, particularly in PAS strongholds, amid motivations tied to maintaining coalition discipline and avoiding disruptions from scandals or age-related withdrawals.[6] Nomination patterns highlighted persistent gender imbalances, with women forming only about 12.5% of candidates across the six states' contests (71 out of approximately 570), and similarly low representation in Kedah's predominantly Malay constituencies.[51] This reflected the absence of enforced gender quotas and a preference for ethnic Malay male candidates aligned with local demographics and party loyalist networks, such as PAS's focus on ideologically committed figures.[52] Smaller parties like Malaysian United Democratic Alliance (MUDA) attempted higher female quotas, but their impact was limited in Kedah.[53]

Campaign and Public Opinion

Major Parties, Coalitions, and Strategies

Perikatan Nasional (PN), comprising the Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party (PAS) and Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia (Bersatu), campaigned on platforms emphasizing Islamic moral governance under the principle of "amar makruf, nahi mungkar" (enjoining good and forbidding evil) and opposition to federal overreach following the state's early dissolution amid tensions with the federal unity government.[6] Their strategy centered on mobilizing rural and mono-ethnic Malay-majority voters in northern and eastern Kedah through established PAS religious networks, including mosque-based grassroots efforts, to consolidate the "green wave" momentum from the 2022 general election where PN secured 14 of 15 parliamentary seats.[6] PN leveraged populist appeals via Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor's community engagement programs, such as the 'Sembang Santai' sessions held on August 1, 2023, in Kuala Kedah, and fielded popular local candidates like former footballers Baddrol Bakhtiar in Gurun and Radhi Mat Din in Alor Mengkudu to broaden voter resonance.[6] The opposing Pakatan Harapan (PH)-Barisan Nasional (BN) alliance, formed to support the federal unity government, prioritized themes of national unity, economic development, and social welfare, highlighting initiatives like the RM25 billion Infineon semiconductor investment to appeal to pragmatic voters.[6] However, the coalition faced criticism for potentially diluting Malay identity and interests due to the inclusion of the ethnic Chinese-majority Democratic Action Party (DAP) alongside Malay-centric parties like UMNO, PKR, and Amanah, exacerbating ethnic polarization in a Malay heartland state.[54] Tactically, PH-BN targeted urbanized industrial areas and mixed-ethnic constituencies in southern Kedah, where they held competitive edges from prior elections, through high-profile rallies featuring Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim and structured seat allocations—UMNO contesting 15 seats, PKR 10, Amanah 9, and DAP 2—to unify disparate voter bases and counter PN's rural dominance.[6] Minor parties and independents played a limited role, with multi-cornered contests occurring in only 8 of the 36 state seats, reflecting the binary nature of the competition between the two major coalitions and the absence of significant third-party interventions, such as from Sabah-based Warisan.[6]

Prominent Campaign Issues and Debates

A central economic debate centered on state resource management and development initiatives, with Pakatan Harapan-Barisan Nasional (PH-BN) criticizing Perikatan Nasional (PN) for alleged mismanagement in rare earth elements (REE) mining approvals. The Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (MACC) probed claims of corruption exceeding RM13 million in bribes and abuse of power linked to illegal REE extraction permits issued under PN's administration, summoning Kedah Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor and 10 state executive council members in July 2023.[55] PN countered by highlighting its manifesto commitments to foreign investment attraction, clean water supply improvements, and welfare enhancements, positioning these as pragmatic responses to Kedah's role as a major rice producer contributing 43% of national output.[6] Federal-state fiscal tensions emerged as a flashpoint, with Sanusi accusing the Anwar Ibrahim-led unity government of withholding development funds, including allocations for Kulim International Airport expansion, to undermine opposition-controlled states like Kedah.[56] He framed PN as a bulwark against federal overreach, arguing such actions prioritized political loyalty over state needs despite Kedah's heavy reliance on federal transfers. PH-BN responded by pledging smoother fund flows and project approvals upon alignment with the federal coalition, emphasizing collaborative governance to address infrastructure deficits.[56] Identity and religious dimensions intensified the discourse, as PN leveraged Malay-first appeals to consolidate support among Malay-Muslim voters, portraying PH-BN alliances as concessions to non-Malay influences like the Democratic Action Party (DAP). PAS, PN's dominant partner in Kedah, advocated enforcing Islamic principles such as "amar makruf, nahi mungkar" (enjoining good and forbidding evil) to uphold moral governance, building on its post-GE15 "green wave" gains in 14 of 15 parliamentary seats.[6] Opponents raised secular concerns over potential hudud implementation, though PN balanced this with bread-and-butter pragmatism to mitigate fears of extremism while capitalizing on observed Malay voter consolidation toward Islamist-nationalist platforms.[6][19]

Opinion Polling Trends

A Merdeka Center survey conducted from July 3 to August 11, 2023, among 501 Malay voters in Kedah indicated dominant support for Perikatan Nasional (PN), with 66% expressing preference for the coalition compared to 17% for the Pakatan Harapan-Barisan Nasional (PH-BN) alliance.[57] This reflected consistent trends from earlier post-general election analyses, where PN held strong Malay backing amid dissatisfaction with the federal unity government's performance, though pre-dissolution polls in June showed slightly narrower leads due to uncertainty over state assembly dissolution.[57] The survey predicted PN would retain Kedah's state government while gaining further in Malay-majority seats at Barisan Nasional's expense, aligning with PN's emphasis on local governance critiques and Islamist appeals via its PAS component.[57] Methodologically, the focus on Malay respondents captured the election's ethnic dynamics, as Malays constitute over 70% of Kedah's electorate, but the sample's unspecified urban-rural distribution may have introduced biases favoring PH-BN in mixed areas, potentially understating PAS's rural mobilization strength.[57] In retrospect, the polls accurately foresaw PN's retention of power but underestimated the scale of victory, as PN captured 33 of 36 seats on August 12, 2023, exceeding projections derived from 2022 federal results that anticipated around 32 seats.[18] This discrepancy highlights limitations in pre-election surveys' ability to fully account for late-campaign turnout surges in conservative rural constituencies, where PN's ground operations proved decisive.[58]

Election Results

Overall Vote Shares and Seat Outcomes

Perikatan Nasional (PN) won 33 of the 36 seats in the Kedah State Legislative Assembly in the election held on 12 August 2023, securing a supermajority.[59][60] Within PN, Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS) captured 21 seats, Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia (Bersatu) 11 seats, and Malaysian Chinese Association ally Gerakan 1 seat.[2] Pakatan Harapan (PH) obtained the remaining 3 seats, with Parti Keadilan Rakyat (PKR) winning 2 and Democratic Action Party (DAP) 1.[2]

Breakdown by Parliamentary Constituency

Perikatan Nasional (PN) achieved comprehensive dominance in the state seats comprising each of Kedah's 15 federal parliamentary constituencies during the 12 August 2023 election, capturing 33 of the 36 available seats overall. This control spanned rural, semi-urban, and urban-influenced blocs, with PN securing all state seats in predominantly rural constituencies such as Padang Terap (P.001), Pendang (P.013), Sik (P.014), and Baling (P.015).[1] In these areas, PN's vote shares frequently exceeded 70%, reflecting strong consolidation among rural voters.[6] Mixed constituencies like Jerlun (P.003) exhibited notable swings toward PN, with the coalition gaining over 15 percentage points in aggregate vote share compared to prior state polls in seats such as Sanglang (N.04) and Jerlun (N.05), where PN candidates prevailed by margins exceeding 5,000 votes.[63] Similar patterns emerged in other semi-rural areas, including Kuala Kedah (P.002) and Kubang Rotan (P.004), where PN maintained majorities despite localized challenges from Pakatan Harapan-Barisan Nasional (PH-BN). Urban-rural gradients were observable, with PN's leads narrowing slightly in more developed blocs like Sungai Petani (P.009) and Alor Star (P.016), yet sufficient to secure all but isolated seats.[64] The three PH victories—Jitra (N.06) in Kubang Rotan, Kota Darul Aman (N.13) in Alor Star, and Bakar Arang (N.28) in Sungai Petani—represented exceptions in constituencies with higher non-Malay populations and urban influences, where PH garnered vote percentages around 40-50% in those specific seats.[65] [2] Across blocs, PN's Bersatu and PAS components divided contests effectively, achieving combined vote shares averaging 62% statewide, with rural dominance amplifying effective control.[1]

Seats That Changed Hands and Margins

Perikatan Nasional (PN) secured all of its incumbent seats in the 2023 Kedah state election while gaining two seats from Pakatan Harapan (PH), resulting in a total of 33 seats for PN and three retained by PH in Bakar Arang, Sidam, and Kota Darul Aman.[66][1] These gains occurred in constituencies where PH incumbents had held out against prior defections to PN, reflecting consolidated PN support amid limited overall shifts from the pre-election composition dominated by PN through defections since 2020.[67] Winning margins in PN-retained seats averaged approximately 20-30%, underscoring non-competitive dynamics in most contests, with no races decided by margins under 5%.[68] The flipped seats saw PN candidates prevail by similar comfortable margins, attributable to stronger local organization and candidate familiarity compared to PH challengers in those areas. Vote swings toward PN ranged from 5-10% relative to GE15 parliamentary baselines in overlapping constituencies, amplifying PN's hold without widespread volatility.[69][18]

Analysis of Outcomes

Voter Turnout and Demographic Patterns

The voter turnout in the 2023 Kedah state election stood at 68 percent, lower than the 74.4 percent national average recorded in the preceding 15th general election (GE15).[70][71] This decline aligned with broader trends in the concurrent state polls across six states, where overall participation dipped amid factors such as midday heat and voter fatigue following GE15, though Kedah's rate exceeded the national state election average of around 60 percent.[70] Early polling data indicated stronger engagement in rural constituencies, particularly those with high concentrations of registered voters in Perikatan Nasional (PN) strongholds.[72] Demographic patterns underscored ethnic divides in participation, with pre- and post-election analyses highlighting lower turnout among non-Malay minorities, including Chinese and Indian voters concentrated in urban and semi-urban areas like Alor Setar.[73] Malays, comprising approximately 77 percent of Kedah's population and a similar share of the electorate, demonstrated robust engagement, correlating with PN's capture of 32 out of 36 state seats, predominantly in Malay-majority rural and semi-rural districts.[2] Pakatan Harapan (PH) secured only three seats, largely in constituencies with higher minority populations, reflecting splits or abstention patterns among these groups that favored PN's consolidated Malay base.[74] These trends mirrored socio-economic data, with higher rural voter density—Kedah's agriculture-dependent interior—and limited urban minority mobilization contributing to the observed disparities.[73]

Causal Factors in Perikatan Nasional's Victory

Perikatan Nasional's (PN) decisive retention of Kedah on August 12, 2023, securing 31 of 36 state seats—a supermajority—stemmed primarily from the consolidation of Malay voters behind PAS's unyielding commitment to Islamic governance and Malay special rights, which contrasted sharply with the perceived dilutions under the federal unity government's multi-ethnic framework.[1][58] This shift reflected not mere "polarization," but a rational preference among conservative rural Malays for PN's moralistic policies, such as enforcing "amar makruf nahi mungkar" (enjoining good and forbidding wrong), which aligned with local cultural norms in a state where Malays comprise over 80% of the population.[6] Pre-election surveys indicated PN's dominance in 32 of 36 seats based on 2022 general election patterns, underscoring sustained ethnic loyalty over reformist appeals from Pakatan Harapan-Barisan Nasional (PH-BN).[18] Effective grassroots mobilization through PAS's extensive religious networks, including mosques and surau, amplified PN's reach in rural strongholds, where agricultural communities prioritized identity preservation over federal economic incentives.[6] Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor's populist rhetoric, often defiant against federal critics, further galvanized support by framing PN as a bulwark against Anwar Ibrahim's administration, perceived as encroaching on state autonomy post-2022.[58] Sanusi's controversies, including sedition charges, paradoxically boosted his appeal by portraying him as a victim of elite persecution, resonating with voters wary of central overreach.[17] This anti-federal sentiment was causal, as Kedah's assembly dissolution in May 2023 followed PN's resistance to unity government influence, turning the poll into a proxy referendum on national coalitions.[75] Kedah's governance under PN since August 2021 demonstrated relative stability, with no acute economic downturns despite national challenges, reinforcing voter trust in local administration over abstract federal promises.[6] The state's role as Malaysia's rice bowl, producing 43% of national padi, underscored PN's focus on rural welfare initiatives, which PH-BN failed to credibly challenge amid their alliance's internal tensions and weaker Malay machinery.[6] Empirical vote shares showed PN capturing nearly all Malay-majority constituencies, with turnout at 76.4% indicating high engagement driven by these identity and autonomy factors rather than ideological polarization critiques often amplified in urban-centric media.[1][76]

Ethnic and Religious Dimensions of Support

The 2023 Kedah state election underscored persistent ethnic cleavages, with Perikatan Nasional (PN), dominated by the Islamist Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS), securing its victory through near-unanimous backing from the Malay Muslim majority, who form over 75% of the electorate in most constituencies.[19] PN's platform, emphasizing stricter sharia enforcement and Islamic moral governance, resonated deeply with conservative rural Malay voters prioritizing religious identity over economic incentives.[6] Post-election analyses confirmed that Malay support for PN in northern states like Kedah aligned closely with patterns from the 2022 general election, where PAS captured substantial majorities among Malays through appeals to piety and cultural preservation, often exceeding 60-70% in surveys of similar demographics.[74][77] Non-Malay communities, including Chinese and Indian voters concentrated in urban pockets such as Alor Setar, provided negligible support to PN, with turnout and preferences favoring the Pakatan Harapan-Barisan Nasional (PH-BN) coalition in the few seats it retained.[78] This reflected longstanding ethnic polarization, where PN's race-and-religion (2R) rhetoric alienated minorities while consolidating Malay loyalty, as evidenced by PN's sweep of 33 out of 36 seats, all but three in Malay-dominant areas.[79] PH-BN's inability to erode PN's Malay base, despite Barisan Nasional's historical ties to Malay nationalism, demonstrated that religious conservatism and identity-based mobilization outweighed appeals to stability or heritage.[74] Religious dimensions amplified these patterns, with PAS's advocacy for expanded hudud laws and anti-vice campaigns drawing fervent support from observant Muslims, particularly in rural heartlands where sharia courts handle family and moral issues.[6] Voter behavior affirmed traditional bases, showing limited defection among urban or younger Malays, contrary to narratives of generational economic pragmatism; instead, piety-driven voting persisted, as younger conservatives echoed elders' preferences for Islamist governance.[80] This outcome highlighted causal primacy of ethno-religious solidarity in Kedah's demographics, where economic critiques of the incumbent PN government failed to override identity loyalties.[54]

Controversies

Dissolution and No-Confidence Maneuver Disputes

The Kedah State Legislative Assembly, elected in November 2022 as part of the 15th Malaysian general election, was dissolved on 28 June 2023 after Sultan Sallehuddin Sultan Badlishah granted consent on 25 June at the request of Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor.[30][81] This action paved the way for snap state elections on 12 August 2023, less than a year after the previous polls.[1] Pakatan Harapan (PH) leaders criticized the dissolution as an unconstitutional haste to evade emerging threats of no-confidence motions, citing internal Perikatan Nasional (PN) frictions and governance controversies such as proposed rare earth mining projects. However, no formal no-confidence motion had been tabled in the assembly prior to dissolution, and PH claims lacked substantiation of imminent loss of majority support. PN countered that the move was a legitimate strategic renewal of mandate, reflecting voter sentiment for early validation amid national political shifts following the 2022 hung parliament.[82] The tactic aligned with precedents in Malaysian state politics, where governments have dissolved assemblies to preempt instability, as seen in Sabah's 2020 snap polls amid defection threats, without proven irregularities or selective legal scrutiny. No significant court challenges overturned the dissolution, with the Election Commission proceeding unhindered, underscoring the constitutional prerogative of the Menteri Besar with royal assent under Article 36 of the Kedah Constitution.[29]

Allegations of Identity Politics and Polarization

Perikatan Nasional (PN) campaigned in the 2023 Kedah election on a platform emphasizing Malay and Islamic priorities, including pledges to strengthen religious education and protect Islamic institutions, which contrasted with Pakatan Harapan's (PH) emphasis on multi-ethnic unity under the federal Unity Government framework.[83] Critics, including PH leaders, alleged that PN's rhetoric exacerbated ethnic divisions by prioritizing Malay-Muslim interests over broader inclusivity, portraying PH's approach as a more secular or diluted alternative that failed to resonate in Kedah's predominantly Malay demographics.[79] However, empirical results indicate voter preference for explicit identity-based appeals: PN secured 26 of 36 state seats with 52.9% of the vote, retaining a supermajority in a state where Malays comprise over 80% of the population, suggesting demand driven by cultural and religious affinity rather than coerced polarization.[1] [6] Accusations of PAS-led "extremism" within PN, often leveled by federal government-aligned voices, claimed the party's Islamist stance alienated non-Malays and risked societal fragmentation, yet these were undermined by PN's consistent electoral advances reflecting organic support among conservative Malay voters.[84] PAS, as PN's dominant partner in Kedah, expanded its vote share from 41.5% in the 2022 general election to higher margins in 2023 state polls, capturing seats with majorities exceeding 70% in rural Malay-majority areas, which analysts attribute to genuine grassroots mobilization on issues like moral governance rather than manipulative tactics.[58] [85] Such outcomes challenge narratives of fringe appeal, as PAS's platform aligned with long-standing voter priorities in northern states, where similar patterns yielded PN victories in 32 projected seats pre-election.[18] Media coverage amplified perceptions of polarization, with some outlets framing PN's gains as a "green wave" of religious radicalism favoring the federal narrative of unity against division, while underreporting PN's policy substance on economic and religious preservation.[54] Reports from government-leaning sources highlighted alleged ethnic tensions post-election, yet turnout data—71.98% in Kedah—showed sustained participation without evidence of suppressed minority votes, indicating divisions were more reflective of pre-existing demographic preferences than election-induced strife.[86] This selective emphasis, evident in analyses decrying deepened racial lines without quantifying shifts beyond PN's Malay base consolidation, underscores a bias toward critiquing identity politics when it disadvantages multi-ethnic coalitions.[87]

Criticisms of Campaign Tactics and Media Coverage

Perikatan Nasional (PN) was accused by opponents of fear-mongering through emphasis on hudud implementation and threats to Islamic values, with critics claiming PAS portrayed PH-BN as undermining religious governance to mobilize conservative Malay voters.[88] PN's campaign manifesto included commitments to enforce "amar makruf, nahi mungkar" (enjoining good and forbidding evil), which analysts described as pietistic appeals prioritizing moral enforcement over economic pragmatism.[6] Caretaker Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor employed populist rhetoric, including controversial statements on expanding Kedah's borders as "Greater Kedah," dismissed by rivals as inflammatory posturing rather than feasible policy.[89] In response, PH-BN faced allegations of vote inducement via federal incentives, particularly after Deputy Prime Minister Ahmad Zahid Hamidi linked civil servant salary hikes and unity government stability to electoral support, prompting rivals to label it as leveraging national resources for state-level gains.[90] Despite these claims, the Election Commission documented only minimal breaches of electoral rules, with no substantiated widespread irregularities during the campaign period from July 29 to August 11, 2023.[8] Media coverage drew scrutiny for perceived imbalances, with federal-aligned outlets accused of downplaying PN's entrenched rural support by focusing on urban economic critiques, while social media platforms like TikTok amplified unverified narratives and Sanusi's viral content to bolster PN's outreach.[6] Post-election reviews by observers confirmed the process's overall integrity, with audits revealing no systemic fraud and voter turnout at 76.9% reflecting orderly participation absent major disruptions.[8]

Aftermath and Long-Term Implications

Government Formation and Stability

Following Perikatan Nasional's victory in the 12 August 2023 election, securing 33 of 36 seats in the Kedah State Legislative Assembly, Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor was reappointed as Menteri Besar on 14 August 2023. He took the oath of office before the Sultan of Kedah, Sultan Sallehuddin Ayub, at Istana Anak Bukit, affirming PN's continued leadership without opposition claims to form the government.[91][92] The State Executive Council was reconstituted with substantial continuity from the prior PN administration, featuring minor reshuffles to consolidate coalition loyalty. On 21 August 2023, ten assemblymen were sworn in as exco members before the Sultan, including seven newcomers alongside retained incumbents; Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia received three portfolios, reflecting intra-coalition balancing.[93][94][95] PN's supermajority faced no substantive challenges, enabling the assembly's prompt reconvening and focus on legislative priorities such as budget approvals. The government has maintained stability, with no assemblyman defections recorded from August 2023 through October 2025, precluding any no-confidence motions or leadership contests.[96]

Impacts on National Coalition Dynamics

The Perikatan Nasional (PN) coalition's decisive retention of Kedah on August 12, 2023, with 26 of 36 state seats, reinforced its credentials as a formidable opposition force capable of sustaining governance in Malay-majority states, thereby intensifying competitive pressures on Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim's unity government comprising Pakatan Harapan (PH) and Barisan Nasional (BN).[97] This outcome underscored PN's strategic emphasis on Malay-centric issues like religious identity and economic grievances, positioning the coalition to challenge federal policies more assertively and demand concessions on matters such as affirmative action and development funding.[79] The PH-BN alliance's failure to penetrate Kedah exposed underlying tensions, particularly between the Democratic Action Party's (DAP) predominantly non-Malay urban base and BN's efforts to reclaim Malay support through UMNO's traditional patronage networks, as PN capitalized on perceptions of federal neglect toward rural Malay heartlands.[54] Analysts noted that this ethnic divergence hampered coordinated campaigning, with BN candidates struggling against PN's narrative of cultural preservation, leading to heightened intra-coalition debates over seat allocations and messaging in subsequent contests.[6] Kedah's status as a "Malay fortress"—with over 80% ethnic Malay population—amplified PN's momentum in opposition strategies, influencing federal by-elections like the June 2023 Pulai and Simpang Jeram polls where PN gained ground in similar demographics, signaling sustained voter preference for Islamist-leaning alternatives amid federal-state frictions over resource allocation.[19] These dynamics prompted Anwar's administration to navigate adversarial relations with PN-led states, including disputes with Kedah Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor over projects like the €5 billion data center investment, where both levels of government vied for credit.[14]

Policy Shifts and Governance Achievements Versus Shortcomings

Following the 2023 election victory, the Perikatan Nasional (PN)-led Kedah state government under Menteri Besar Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor pursued policies emphasizing fiscal expansion and investment attraction, alongside continuity in conservative Islamic governance priorities. Revenue collection reached a record RM700 million in 2024, surpassing prior years and aligning with targets for RM1 billion annually by enhancing untapped income sources.[98][99] Approved investments totaled RM11.6 billion in 2023, generating over 3,400 jobs through collaborations with agencies like the Northern Corridor Implementation Authority.[100] This momentum continued into 2025, with RM4.2 billion in investments recorded in the first quarter alone.[101] Economic performance under PN governance outperformed national benchmarks, with Kedah's GDP growing by 6.6% in 2024 compared to Malaysia's 5.1%.[102][103] These gains stemmed from sustained promotion of tourism and manufacturing, evidenced by 1.5 million tourist arrivals in early 2025.[101] On social policy fronts, the administration maintained emphasis on religious education, benefiting from federal allocations such as RM8.9 million for Islamic institutions in 2023, which supported enhancements in pondok and tahfiz schools.[104] Such conservative continuities, rooted in PAS's platform, bolstered core voter support by prioritizing moral and faith-based initiatives over rapid secular reforms. However, shortcomings persisted in addressing entrenched rural challenges and advancing long-promised legislative agendas. Hardcore poverty in Kedah remained at 0.3% as of recent assessments, higher than some states and indicative of ongoing rural disparities despite national declines from 12.0% to lower rates between 2022 and 2024.[105] Federal-state tensions delayed infrastructure projects, with land acquisition issues halting developments requiring RM195 million in allocations as of early 2025.[106] Implementation of hudud laws, a PAS staple, faced indefinite postponement due to constitutional barriers requiring federal amendments and sultanic consent, with no progress by 2025 amid PN's state-level advocacy.[107] These lags highlighted causal constraints from national dynamics, limiting PN's ability to fully enact ideological shifts while economic advances provided tangible governance metrics.
