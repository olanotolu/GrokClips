Citizen Kane trailer

The Citizen Kane trailer is a four-minute promotional short film directed, produced, and narrated by Orson Welles for RKO Pictures, released in theaters in 1940 to publicize his debut feature Citizen Kane.[1][2] Featuring Welles as Charles Foster Kane alongside key cast members from the Mercury Theatre troupe, the trailer eschews conventional plot teasers in favor of a self-contained "making-of" narrative that tours the opulent Xanadu set, intersperses behind-the-scenes glimpses with dramatized character vignettes, and employs innovative cinematographic techniques like deep-focus shots and montages to evoke the feature's stylistic ambition.[2][3] This approach, blending documentary realism with fictional reenactments, mirrors the film's narrative fragmentation and visual experimentation, establishing the trailer itself as a landmark in advertising history for its artistic merit over mere hype.[2] Released amid preemptive controversy from media magnate William Randolph Hearstâ€”whose life inspired elements of the storyâ€”the trailer's sophisticated execution helped generate buzz despite suppression efforts against the full film, underscoring Welles's audacious fusion of theatrical innovation and Hollywood promotion.[3]

Production Details

Development and Filming

The trailer for Citizen Kane was developed by Orson Welles as a distinctive promotional vehicle to build anticipation for his directorial debut, emphasizing the film's stylistic innovations without revealing actual footage from the feature. Produced by Welles' Mercury Theatre company in collaboration with RKO Pictures, it adopted a pseudo-documentary newsreel format mirroring the movie's opening sequence, featuring narrated vignettes of key characters reflecting on Charles Foster Kane's life and snippets of on-set activity to evoke mystery and grandeur. This approach stemmed from Welles' desire to differentiate it from standard Hollywood previews, leveraging his radio-honed storytelling to intrigue audiences amid the project's high expectations and budget of approximately $839,000 for the full film production.[2][1] Filming occurred concurrently with principal photography for Citizen Kane, which began on June 29, 1940, and extended through late summer and fall, enabling the inclusion of genuine behind-the-scenes elements such as microphone booms, set rigging, and actor rehearsals on the RKO lots in Hollywood. Directed by Welles, the four-minute short utilized the same core creative team, including cinematographer Gregg Toland, whose deep-focus techniques and low-angle shots informed the trailer's visual dynamism, and editor Robert Wise, who handled the assembly to maintain narrative tease. Cast members reprised roles in scripted reenactmentsâ€”Welles as Kane, Joseph Cotten as Jedediah Leland, and othersâ€”shot on constructed sets like Xanadu interiors, with the production wrapping in time for theatrical previews in late 1940 to precede the film's May 1941 premiere. This integrated shooting schedule, totaling under a day for the trailer amid the feature's 58-day principal shoot, yielded the only surviving contemporaneous footage of Citizen Kane's making, highlighting Welles' hands-on oversight despite his novice status in feature filmmaking.[1][8][2]

Crew Contributions

Orson Welles directed the Citizen Kane trailer, conceiving it as a self-contained four-minute promotional featurette that functioned as a "making of" documentary to generate anticipation without disclosing plot details or using footage from the feature film itself.[1] This approach allowed Welles to showcase sets, introduce key characters through actor vignettes, and maintain narrative secrecy amid sensitivities surrounding the film's inspiration in William Randolph Hearst's life.[9] Welles also provided the unseen narration, guiding viewers through Xanadu's interiors and emphasizing the production's technical ambitions, such as deep-focus shots and elaborate miniatures, thereby extending his multifaceted role from the Mercury Theatre production.[7] Harry J. Wild served as cinematographer for the trailer, departing from Gregg Toland's work on the main film to capture behind-the-scenes footage at RKO's Pathe Studios.[10] Wild began principal photography for the trailer on November 20, 1940, handling sequences like actor interviews and set tours that highlighted the film's innovative visual style without Toland's signature deep-focus techniques.[9] His contributions ensured the trailer's polished, documentary-like aesthetic, which complemented Welles' vision by focusing on production elements to intrigue audiences.[11] The trailer was produced under Mercury Productions, with Welles overseeing operations in collaboration with RKO Radio Pictures, enabling rapid assembly concurrent with the feature's filming to meet promotional deadlines ahead of the 1941 release.[7] This streamlined process reflected the crew's efficiency in adapting theatrical techniques from Welles' Mercury Theatre to cinematic promotion, though specific editing and sound credits for the trailer remain unitemized separately from the film's Robert Wise and Bernard Herrmann.[9]

Reception and Analysis

Initial Critical Response

The Citizen Kane trailer, a four-minute promotional short released in theaters in late 1940, garnered scant formal critical reviews, as was typical for advertising materials of the era which were not customarily analyzed independently of the parent film.[10] Its structure diverged markedly from prevailing trailer conventions by omitting any footage from the feature itself, instead functioning as a self-contained "making of" documentary narrated and directed by Orson Welles, who toured viewers through sets, explained deep-focus cinematography, and demonstrated makeup and lighting effects.[3] This innovative format, suggested by cinematographer Gregg Toland, emphasized the production's technical ambitions and Welles' auteur status, fostering intrigue amid his transition from radio notoriety to Hollywood.[3] Contemporary trade publications like Variety noted the trailer's role in preview screenings but prioritized its promotional efficacy over aesthetic evaluation, reporting it as a tool to heighten expectations for Welles' directorial debut amid RKO's marketing push.[15] The piece's rhetorical style, blending showmanship with behind-the-scenes revelation, avoided plot spoilers while teasing thematic enigmas like the meaning of "Rosebud," thereby amplifying buzz without provoking early controversy akin to that later faced by the film due to William Randolph Hearst's opposition.[16] Screenings of the trailer preceded private previews of the full film in early 1941, which elicited enthusiastic responses from select critics, suggesting the short's success in priming audiences for the feature's stylistic boldness.[17] Overall, while not a focal point for 1940s reviewers, the trailer's execution underscored Welles' command of multimedia persuasion, honed from his Mercury Theatre background, and presaged the feature's reception as a technical milestone tempered by commercial and political headwinds.[3]

Legacy and Availability

Cultural Impact

The Citizen Kane trailer, a four-minute promotional short released in 1940, pioneered a documentary-style format that deviated from the era's standard clip compilations and hyperbolic montages, instead featuring Orson Welles narrating behind-the-scenes footage, set models like Xanadu, and brief character vignettes teasing the central mystery without revealing plot details.[2][19] This self-contained "making-of" approach, leveraging Welles's radio-honed voice-over for subtle dramatic tension rather than overt bombast, marked a shift toward introspective promotion that emphasized thematic intrigue over spectacle.[3] Its innovations influenced modern trailer design by introducing narrative teasers and meta-elements, such as director-led exposition and mystery-driven hooks that prioritize emotional or intellectual engagement over exhaustive previews, a technique echoed in subsequent promotional shorts that blend exposition with enigma to build anticipation.[20][21] Film historians note its role in prefiguring trailers as mini-films in their own right, contributing to the evolution of advertising from mere hype to storytelling devices that mirror a project's artistic ambitions.[22] In broader cultural terms, the trailer's audacious style amplified Citizen Kane's reputation for technical boundary-pushing even before the 1941 premiere, sustaining scholarly interest in Welles's Mercury Theatre methods and underscoring how promotional materials could embody cinematic experimentation amid Hollywood's studio constraints.[23] Despite initial box-office challenges tied to controversy, its enduring study in film courses highlights a legacy of elevating trailers from disposable ads to artifacts of auteur vision.[24]

The Citizen Kane trailer, a four-minute promotional featurette directed by Orson Welles, was originally distributed theatrically by RKO Radio Pictures in 1940 as a "making-of" preview to generate anticipation for the feature film's release the following year.[1][3] This innovative trailer, featuring Welles narrating behind-the-scenes elements and introducing the cast, aired in cinemas prior to the May 1, 1941, premiere of Citizen Kane in New York City.[25] Home video distribution of the trailer began in 1987 with its inclusion on the Voyager Company's laserdisc edition of Citizen Kane, marking the first time it reached non-theatrical audiences.[26] Subsequent releases expanded accessibility: it appeared on VHS tapes from Warner Home Video and Turner Classic Movies, as well as early Criterion Collection laserdiscs featuring supplementary materials like commentaries.[3] By the DVD era, the trailer was standard in special editions, such as those from Warner Bros. in the early 2000s, often paired with restored footage and production notes.
