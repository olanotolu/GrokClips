Hoyt Sherman


Hoyt Sherman (1827–1904) was an American banker, insurance pioneer, and politician who played a foundational role in the development of Des Moines, Iowa, after arriving there in 1848 as one of its earliest permanent settlers.[1][2] The youngest of eleven children born to Ohio Supreme Court judge Charles Robert Sherman, he was the brother of U.S. Senator John Sherman, author of the Sherman Antitrust Act, and General William Tecumseh Sherman, commander of Union forces in the Civil War.[2][3] Upon reaching Fort Des Moines, then a frontier outpost with a population of around 127, Sherman was appointed its first postmaster by the federal government, constructing the area's inaugural post office and bank while also serving on the town council.[2][1] During the Civil War, President Abraham Lincoln commissioned him as a major in the Union Army to serve as a paymaster, a position that drew on his financial acumen amid the conflict's logistical demands.[2][3] Postwar, he co-founded the Equitable Life Insurance Company of Iowa in 1867, rising to its presidency from 1874 to 1888, and represented Polk County in the Iowa House of Representatives in 1866, influencing local governance and economic expansion.[1] Sherman's enduring contributions extended to philanthropy and civic infrastructure, including advocacy for public schools, a local college, waterworks, and the establishment of Mercy Hospital in 1893 with the Sisters of Mercy; his 1877 estate, now preserved as Hoyt Sherman Place, hosted prominent visitors like Generals Ulysses S. Grant and Philip Sheridan and continues as a cultural landmark.[2][3] Married to Sara Moulton from 1855, with whom he had five children, Sherman amassed an estate valued at $600,000 by his death on January 25, 1904, reflecting his success in real estate, hardware, and asset management during economic panics like those of 1857 and 1873.[1][2]

Early Life

Family Background and Upbringing

Hoyt Sherman was born in 1827 in Lancaster, Ohio, as the youngest son among eleven children born to Charles Robert Sherman, a justice of the Ohio Supreme Court, and his wife Mary Elizabeth Hoyt.[3] His siblings included notable figures such as William Tecumseh Sherman, who rose to prominence as a Union general during the American Civil War, and John Sherman, a longtime U.S. Senator from Ohio who authored the Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890.[3][4] Sherman's early upbringing in Lancaster emphasized formal education, where he attended local schools until approximately age eighteen.[5] Following his father's death in 1829, the family maintained stability through connections in Ohio's legal and political circles, though Hoyt pursued practical skills by apprenticing in the printing trade with his brothers Charles and John Sherman in Mansfield, Ohio, while continuing his studies. This period laid the groundwork for his later ventures, blending scholarly preparation with hands-on work in publishing before he ventured westward in 1848.

Education and Initial Employment

Hoyt Sherman attended local schools in Lancaster, Ohio, during his early years, dividing his time between formal education and practical work in a printing office.[1] By his late teens, he had gained experience as a newspaper printer in Cincinnati, Ohio, which supplemented his schooling until approximately age eighteen in 1845.[1] This period laid the groundwork for his later professional pursuits, reflecting the self-reliant ethos common among frontier-era families like the Shermans, who emphasized practical skills alongside basic literacy and arithmetic. After completing his early schooling, Sherman briefly studied law in Mansfield, Ohio, under informal apprenticeship arrangements typical of the era before standardized legal education.[1] His initial formal employment thus transitioned to legal preparation, culminating in admission to the bar in 1849 shortly after his relocation to Iowa.[4] This early focus on printing and law positioned him for subsequent roles in public administration and finance, though his printing work represented his first wage-earning occupation.[1]

Settlement in Iowa

Arrival in Des Moines

Hoyt Sherman, born in Lancaster, Ohio, in 1827 as the youngest of eleven children—including future Civil War general William Tecumseh Sherman—relocated westward seeking opportunities amid the expanding American frontier. Prior to his move, he had apprenticed as a newspaper printer in Cincinnati, Ohio, and undertaken brief legal studies in Mansfield, Ohio, gaining practical skills in communication and administration that would later prove valuable.[1][6] In May 1848, at age 21, Sherman arrived in what was then the nascent settlement of Fort Des Moines, Iowa—a remote military outpost established in 1843 to secure the frontier against Native American tribes and opened to civilian entry after Iowa's statehood in 1846. The area, situated along the Des Moines River, had a sparse population of several hundred, primarily traders, soldiers, and early speculators drawn by fertile lands and prospects for trade following the removal of the Sac and Fox tribes via treaty. Sherman's journey from Ohio reflected the broader migration patterns of young Midwesterners to Iowa's capital region during the late 1840s, driven by economic promise in a territory transitioning from military fort to county seat.[1][3][7]

Early Public Roles

Shortly after arriving in Des Moines in May 1848, Hoyt Sherman entered public service by working in the office of Thomas McMullen, the School Fund Commissioner.[8] He later served as assistant school fund commissioner, contributing to the management of educational land revenues in the territory.[9] In May 1849, Sherman was appointed Postmaster of Des Moines by President Zachary Taylor following the resignation of R. L. Tidrick, with the office initially designated as "Raccoon River" by the Postal Department.[8] [10] He held the position until 1853 and oversaw the construction of the city's first dedicated post office building on Second Street near Vine.[8] In 1852, Sherman was elected Clerk of the District Court for Polk County.[8] [10] That same year, he also administered the estate of Judge Burbridge, further showcasing his administrative reliability.[8] These early appointments established Sherman's reputation for integrity and capability in local governance.[11]

Professional Career

Banking and Financial Innovations

Hoyt Sherman began his banking activities shortly after arriving in Des Moines in May 1848, engaging in financial transactions alongside land dealings that supported the area's early economic growth.[1] By 1854, he established and led the banking house of Hoyt Sherman & Co. as its senior partner, constructing what became the first dedicated bank building in the city and laying foundational infrastructure for local finance.[3] [4] In 1855, Sherman played a key role in organizing the State Bank of Iowa, a significant step toward centralized state-supervised banking amid the era's decentralized "free banking" system, which often featured unstable private notes and wildcat operations.[10] He was appointed cashier of the Des Moines branch, overseeing its operations and contributing to the broader supervision of the bank's statewide network, which aimed to provide more reliable currency and credit in Iowa's frontier economy.[4] His institution survived the Panic of 1857—one of only two Des Moines banks to do so—through strategic partnerships, including with Benjamin F. Allen, and financial support from his brother, William Tecumseh Sherman, then a banker in New York City.[1] Sherman's experiences informed his later writings on Iowa's banking history, where he detailed early practices such as bankers facilitating "land on time" sales—purchasing federal land and reselling it to settlers on installment credit at rates up to 40 percent interest—to bridge capital shortages in a cash-poor agrarian region.[12] These methods, while reflective of the period's high-risk credit extension, underscored the multifunctional role of pioneers like Sherman, who combined banking with real estate agency to foster settlement and development, though they also amplified vulnerabilities during economic downturns.[1] His efforts helped transition Iowa from speculative private banking toward more structured institutions, stabilizing finance in the post-Civil War era before he shifted focus to insurance.[12]

Insurance Industry Foundations

In January 1867, Hoyt Sherman co-founded the Equitable Life Insurance Company of Iowa, joining a group of Des Moines businessmen including Frederick Hubbell to establish one of the region's earliest mutual life insurance providers.[1] This venture marked a pivotal development in Iowa's financial sector, as Equitable became the first life insurance company organized west of the Mississippi River, addressing a prior lack of local options for policyholders in the expanding frontier territories.[7] Sherman's prior experience in banking and law provided the organizational expertise needed to navigate the regulatory and operational challenges of the nascent industry.[6] As general manager for many years following its incorporation, Sherman oversaw Equitable's growth into a leading western insurer, implementing policies that emphasized mutual benefits for policyholders and sound financial management to build long-term stability.[10] Under his leadership, the company expanded its asset base and policy issuance, contributing to Des Moines' emergence as an insurance hub by demonstrating the viability of localized, community-driven risk pooling amid post-Civil War economic recovery.[1] Sherman's hands-on role in underwriting and governance helped mitigate early risks such as mortality fluctuations and investment volatility, fostering trust that attracted subsequent firms to the area.[4] Equitable's success under Sherman laid foundational precedents for Iowa's insurance sector, including the adoption of actuarial standards and reserve requirements that influenced state regulations and encouraged competition.[13] By 1904, at the time of his death, the company's stability—rooted in his strategic oversight—had solidified Des Moines' reputation for financial innovation, with Equitable remaining family-influenced into the 20th century.[14]

Real Estate and Other Business Activities

In 1850, Sherman acquired a five-acre parcel from the public auction of the Hugh Pursley estate for $105, comprising $100 from his personal funds and $5 earned as auction clerk; this property formed the basis of the Sherman Hill neighborhood in Des Moines and served as the site for his residence until his death.[8] [15] He engaged in land speculation as a leading pioneer land agent in central Iowa, acting as a major western agent for the Cincinnati-based firm Easley & Willingham, through which he facilitated transactions including time-entry contracts that yielded profits at rates up to 40 percent interest during the 1850s amid settler and speculator activities.[16] Correspondence with the firm, documented from 1855 to 1865, underscores his role in coordinating speculation efforts in Iowa.[16] Sherman expanded his real estate holdings by constructing the Sherman Block in 1853 at the corner of Third Street and Court Avenue, a three-story structure featuring commercial space on the ground floor, offices above, and an amusement hall on the top level, which later accommodated county and city offices.[8] Beyond real estate, Sherman co-founded the Des Moines Water Company in 1871, which developed the city's initial public water supply infrastructure.[8] In early 1876, he organized and presided over the Iowa Industrial Exposition Company, directing the erection of a 132-foot-square, three-story brick exhibition hall; the enterprise was sold to Mills & Company in 1881 and subsequently repurposed as the Iliad Block.[8]

Political Career

Local Government Service

Sherman arrived in Des Moines (then Fort Des Moines) in 1848 and was appointed the city's first postmaster in June 1849 by President Zachary Taylor, serving until his resignation in 1853.[10][6] In this role, he established the initial post office facilities, which also housed early banking operations, contributing to the rudimentary civic infrastructure of the frontier settlement.[2] Following his postmaster tenure, Sherman was elected clerk of the Polk County District Court in 1853, a position he held while continuing his legal practice.[17][13] This county-level judicial office involved managing court records and administrative duties during Des Moines' formative years as Iowa's capital. Sherman additionally served on the town council of early Des Moines, participating in local governance amid the community's transition from military outpost to incorporated city in 1851.[2][3] His involvement reflected broader civic leadership in establishing municipal order, though specific council actions attributed to him remain sparsely documented in primary records.

State Legislative Involvement

Sherman served as a Republican representative for District 30 in the Iowa House of Representatives during the Eleventh General Assembly, which convened from 1865 to 1866.[10] His election followed his resignation from the U.S. Army paymaster position in April 1864, marking his transition to postwar civic roles in Des Moines.[18] As a member of the House, Sherman chaired the Committee on Railroads, reflecting Iowa's postwar emphasis on infrastructure expansion to connect rural areas to markets.[4] He also served on additional committees, including those addressing congressional districts and other legislative priorities of the session.[19] [20] These roles aligned with his business interests in banking and real estate, which benefited from improved transportation networks, though no specific legislation sponsored by Sherman is documented in primary records from the assembly. His legislative tenure was brief, limited to this single term, after which he focused on private enterprises such as founding the Equitable Life Insurance Company of Iowa in 1867.[10] Contemporary accounts praised his contributions as a "valuable and honored citizen," underscoring his influence in early state governance amid Iowa's rapid settlement and economic growth.[10]

Personal Life

Marriage and Immediate Family

Hoyt Sherman married Sarah Elvira Moulton of Ohio on December 25, 1855. The couple settled in Des Moines, where they raised five children: Frank Allen Sherman, Adeline Moulton Sherman, Charles Moulton Sherman, Arthur Hoyt Sherman (born 1869), and Helen Hoyt Sherman (born 1873).[2][1] Sarah Sherman managed the household at their family residence, Hoyt Sherman Place, built in 1877, while Hoyt pursued his business and public roles.[2] She outlived her husband, who died in 1904, and remained involved in family matters until her own death.[2] The children grew up in Des Moines amid their father's prominence in banking and politics. Frank Allen Sherman entered business pursuits, Adeline focused on family life, Charles engaged in professional endeavors, Arthur Hoyt Sherman (1869–1945) married Corsa Linnie Kintzley in 1904 and continued family traditions in Iowa, and Helen Sherman (1873–1961) wed William O. Nelson.[2][21] No records indicate additional immediate family members beyond this nuclear unit, though Hoyt's extended kin included notable brothers like General William Tecumseh Sherman and Senator John Sherman.[1]

Descendants and Family Legacy

Hoyt Sherman and his wife, Sara Elvira Moulton, whom he married on December 25, 1855, had five children: Frank Allen Sherman, Adeline Moulton Sherman, Charles Hoyt Sherman, Arthur Moulton Sherman, and Helen Hoyt Sherman.[5][22] The family resided in the Hoyt Sherman Place mansion in Des Moines, Iowa, completed in 1877, where the children were raised amid Sherman's growing prominence in banking and civic affairs.[22] Among the children, daughter Adeline (1859–1917) married industrialist Frank Bestow Wiborg in 1882 and bore three daughters: Sara Sherman Wiborg (1883–1975), Mary Hoyt Wiborg (1888–1964), and Olga Wiborg Fish. Sara Sherman Wiborg Murphy, Adeline's eldest, became a prominent expatriate socialite on the French Riviera in the 1920s, associating with figures like Pablo Picasso and Ernest Hemingway; she and her husband Gerald Murphy inspired characters in F. Scott Fitzgerald's Tender Is the Night and appeared in Picasso's artworks.[23] Another daughter, Helen Sherman Griffith (1873–1961), authored the popular Letty Grey series of children's books, including titles like Letty's New Home and Letty and the Twins, published by Penn Publishing Company in the early 20th century. The Sherman grandchildren totaled nine, including duplicates of names Sara, Hoyt, and John, as well as Hanna, Florence, and Helen; grandson John Ramsbotham Griffith, a landscape architect, donated family archives containing Hoyt Sherman's letters to preserve historical records.[23] The family's legacy extends through the preservation of Hoyt Sherman Place, which passed to surviving family members after Sherman's death in 1904 and Sara's in 1887.[22] Daughter Helen Sherman Griffith contributed to restoration efforts by retrieving original dining room furniture, while descendants leased the property to the Des Moines Women's Club in 1941, facilitating its transition into a cultural venue.[23] In 1977, the Hoyt Sherman Place Foundation was established by family heirs and community stakeholders to maintain the site as a performing arts center, art gallery, and educational hub, ensuring its role in Iowa's cultural heritage.[2] This stewardship reflects the Sherman descendants' commitment to honoring Hoyt's foundational contributions to Des Moines amid broader familial ties to national figures like General William Tecumseh Sherman.[3]

Residences and Later Years

Hoyt Sherman Place

Hoyt Sherman Place, located at 1501 Woodland Avenue in Des Moines, Iowa's Sherman Hill neighborhood, served as the primary residence for Hoyt Sherman and his family from its completion in 1877 until his death in 1904.[24] [7] The mansion, designed by local architect William Foster, exemplified Victorian-era architecture with features such as a grand staircase, ornate woodwork, and spacious rooms suited for entertaining Des Moines' emerging elite.[22] Constructed on a portion of Sherman's expansive estate, the home reflected his status as a prominent banker and civic leader, immediately earning recognition as a social hub for gatherings and community events.[22] During Sherman's occupancy, the property hosted significant civic activities, including the establishment of Des Moines' first hospital in 1893. Sherman recruited the Sisters of Mercy from Davenport, Iowa, to operate the facility within the mansion's walls, providing early medical care to the city's residents before a dedicated hospital was built elsewhere.[2] This initiative underscored Sherman's commitment to public welfare, leveraging his personal residence for communal benefit amid Des Moines' rapid growth from a population of 127 in 1848 to over 60,000 by the 1890s.[7] In his later years, Sherman continued to use the home as a center for family life and professional entertaining, aligning with his roles in banking, politics, and philanthropy. Following his death on January 2, 1904, the property remained vacant for several years, preserving its original furnishings and layout until its transition to public use.[25]

Death and Final Contributions

Hoyt Sherman died on January 25, 1904, at his home in Des Moines, Iowa, at the age of 76.[18] He was interred at Woodland Cemetery in Des Moines.[26] In the years leading up to his death, Sherman continued his philanthropic efforts, notably by recruiting the Sisters of Mercy from Davenport, Iowa, in 1893 to establish the city's first hospital within his Woodland Avenue mansion.[2] The facility, known as Mercy Hospital, accommodated 52 beds and operated for nearly two years while the Sisters prepared a permanent site.[27] This initiative marked a significant contribution to Des Moines' early healthcare infrastructure.[4] Sherman's estate, valued at $400,000, was bequeathed to his five children upon his passing, reflecting his accumulated wealth from banking, insurance, and real estate ventures.[7] His final years underscored his role as a community leader, providing ongoing support for local institutions such as schools and utilities.[6]

Overall Legacy

Economic Impact on Des Moines

Hoyt Sherman contributed to Des Moines' early economic development by establishing one of the city's first banks shortly after arriving in 1848, providing essential financial services during Iowa's frontier expansion phase.[3] As a banker and lawyer, he facilitated capital access for local businesses and infrastructure, aligning with the settlement's transition from Fort Des Moines to a burgeoning commercial hub amid railroad arrivals and land speculation in the 1850s.[1] His financial acumen supported population growth from fewer than 2,000 residents in 1850 to over 22,000 by 1880, driven by agricultural and mercantile expansion.[28] In 1867, Sherman co-founded the Equitable Life Insurance Company of Iowa alongside 14 other Des Moines business leaders, marking a pivotal step in institutionalizing the insurance sector that later positioned the city as a regional financial center.[6] This venture capitalized on post-Civil War demand for life insurance among farmers and entrepreneurs, channeling premiums into local investments and underwriting economic stability amid Iowa's agrarian boom.[29] By his death in 1904, with an estate valued at $400,000, Sherman's influence had helped embed finance and insurance as core industries, fostering long-term capital formation that sustained Des Moines' growth into the 20th century.[7]

Historical Recognition

Hoyt Sherman Place, Sherman's 1877 residence in Des Moines, Iowa, was listed on the National Register of Historic Places on September 19, 1977, recognizing its architectural and historical significance as a preserved example of late 19th-century Italianate design and its role in the city's early cultural development.[30] This designation underscores the site's value in American history, highlighting Sherman's status as a foundational figure in Des Moines through his land acquisitions and civic leadership. The Sherman Hill neighborhood, one of Des Moines' oldest dating to 1850, derives its name from Sherman's purchase of five acres there, commemorating his contributions to the area's growth as the city's first postmaster, banker, and politician.[31] This naming reflects enduring local acknowledgment of his role in establishing key institutions, including the first post office and bank.[3] Hoyt Sherman Place continues as a venue for art exhibitions, performances, and community events, functioning as Des Moines' inaugural public art gallery since 1907 and perpetuating Sherman's philanthropic influence.[27] Historical lectures, such as the West Des Moines Historical Society's 2025 "Iowa Files" series titled "Hoyt Sherman's Enduring Legacy," further honor his multifaceted career as a Union Army paymaster, insurance pioneer, and civic leader.[32]
