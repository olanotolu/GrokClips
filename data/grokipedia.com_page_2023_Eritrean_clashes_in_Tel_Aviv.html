2023 Eritrean clashes in Tel Aviv

The 2023 Eritrean clashes in Tel Aviv were violent street confrontations on 2 September 2023 in southern Tel Aviv between Eritrean asylum seekers divided along pro- and anti-government lines, which escalated into widespread disorder involving Israeli police intervention.[1][2] The incident stemmed from an attempt by anti-regime protesters to disrupt a pro-government Eritrean event, leading to brawls with thrown rocks, wooden planks, and other improvised weapons, prompting police to deploy rubber bullets, stun grenades, and warning shots.[1][3] In total, the clashes injured over 170 people, including more than 50 police officers and civilians with serious wounds such as fractures and gunshot injuries from crowd-control measures; at least 39 individuals were arrested on the day, with subsequent administrative detentions imposed on 53 suspects to prevent further unrest.[3][4][5] These events exposed entrenched political fissures within Israel's community of roughly 20,000 Eritrean migrants, many of whom fled Eritrea's indefinite national service and authoritarian rule under President Isaias Afwerki, yet included a minority of regime loyalists who clashed with opponents importing homeland antagonisms.[6][2] The violence prompted Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu to advocate deporting pro-regime participants, arguing they lacked legitimate refugee claims, while Eritrean authorities baselessly accused Israel's Mossad of instigating the disorder; in response, police temporarily shuttered several Eritrean community centers in Tel Aviv to curb recurrence.[5][7][8] The clashes underscored challenges in managing diaspora conflicts among asylum seekers in Israel, where prior warnings of potential violence had been issued but not fully heeded, contributing to the rapid escalation.[2][9]

Historical and Political Context

Eritrean Regime and Diaspora Divisions

Eritrea has been ruled by an authoritarian regime under President Isaias Afwerki since its independence in 1993, with no national elections held and power consolidated through the People's Front for Democracy and Justice (PFDJ), the sole legal political party.[10] [11] The regime enforces indefinite national service, requiring citizensâ€”typically starting at age 18â€”to serve for undefined periods often extending decades, under conditions of low pay, harsh labor, and military-like control that human rights organizations equate to forced labor or enslavement.[12] [13] This policy, intended post-independence to build national infrastructure and defense but perpetuated amid a "no-war, no-peace" stalemate with Ethiopia until 2018, has driven mass defections and emigration, as conscripts face shoot-to-kill orders at borders and perpetrators of evasion are punished severely.[14] To sustain control and funding, the regime extends its reach into the diaspora through mechanisms like a mandatory 2% income tax on expatriates' earnings, collected via consulates and regime-affiliated networks using coercion, threats to family members back home, and denial of services such as passport renewals or inheritance rights.[15] [16] Non-payment often invites harassment or intimidation by pro-regime diaspora enforcers, transforming expatriate communities into extensions of domestic repression despite host countries' laws.[17] This extraction, justified by the regime as rehabilitation tax for independence costs, funds PFDJ operations and suppresses dissent abroad.[18] These dynamics fracture the diaspora into pro-regime loyalists, who promote narratives of national unity and participate in state-sanctioned cultural events, and anti-regime opponentsâ€”many former conscripts or persecution victimsâ€”who organize protests viewing such gatherings as propaganda platforms that glorify the dictatorship.[17] [19] Clashes arise when opposition groups disrupt pro-regime festivals, such as independence celebrations, leading to physical confrontations imported from Eritrea's internal authoritarianism, where dissent is criminalized and opposition fragmented by regime infiltration.[20] [21] The regime's transnational repression, including mobilizing loyalists to counter protests, sustains these divisions, turning diaspora spaces into battlegrounds reflective of unmet demands for democratic reform.[16][22]

Infiltration and Legal Status of Eritreans in Israel

Between 2006 and 2012, tens of thousands of Eritreans entered Israel without authorization via the Sinai Peninsula, with annual crossings peaking at over 10,000 in 2011 amid a surge driven by Eritrea's indefinite military conscription and regional instability.[23][24] This influx totaled more than 60,000 African migrants by late 2012, predominantly from Eritrea and Sudan, often facilitated by Bedouin traffickers who subjected many to extortion, torture, and ransom demands during the perilous desert journey.[25] To curb these unauthorized entries, Israel completed a 240-kilometer border fence with Egypt in 2013, equipped with advanced surveillance, which reduced successful crossings to near zero by 2014, reflecting a policy prioritizing national security and border sovereignty over open reception of mass migrant flows.[26][24] By 2023, approximately 30,000 African asylum seekers remained in Israel, with Eritreans comprising the majorityâ€”estimated at 17,000 to 18,000 individualsâ€”who had evaded deportation due to non-refoulement obligations under international law, prohibiting return to Eritrea where returnees face risks of persecution, torture, or reprisals for desertion.[27][28][29] Israel adheres to this principle for Eritreans and Sudanese but grants formal refugee status in under 1% of cases, rejecting over 98% of Eritrean applications since 2019, as authorities determine many claims stem from economic motivations rather than individualized persecution, compounded by the absence of systematic vetting for mixed migrant flows that include labor seekers exploiting Eritrea's dire conditions without meeting refugee criteria.[30][31][32] Eritreans in Israel hold "conditional release" status, a provisional residency that prevents deportation but offers no pathway to citizenship, permanent settlement, or full integration, as Israel's immigration framework reserves such benefits primarily for Jewish immigrants under the Law of Return and limits non-Jewish migrants to deter demographic shifts and preserve sovereignty.[33] While some received limited work permits starting around 2016â€”initially for sectors like cleaning and caregivingâ€”these are conditional, employer-tied, and insufficient for the population, leaving most in legal limbo and reliant on informal, unregulated labor markets prone to exploitation and without access to social services, health insurance, or family reunification.[34][35] This status quo sustains a de facto tolerated presence without endorsement of asylum claims en masse, balancing humanitarian non-return with policies aimed at preventing Israel from becoming a magnet for unchecked economic migration from Africa.[36][37]

Socioeconomic Conditions in South Tel Aviv

South Tel Aviv, encompassing neighborhoods such as Neve Sha'anan, Hatikva, and Shapira, has long been characterized by socioeconomic degradation, including high poverty rates, infrastructural neglect, and elevated levels of petty crime, prostitution, and drug-related activities, conditions that intensified following the influx of African migrants, predominantly from Eritrea and Sudan. By 2018, approximately 90% of Israel's African asylum seekers resided in this area, transforming low-income districts into densely populated migrant enclaves where local services strained under the pressure of overcrowding.[38] [39] Residents and police reports have documented correlations between these concentrations and rises in visible social ills, such as open-air prostitution and drug dealing, with Eritrean women increasingly resorting to sex work amid economic desperation and limited legal employment options.[40] [41] Police data from migrant-heavy precincts indicate disproportionate involvement of African nationals in offenses like theft, drug sales, and assaults, contributing to a perceived crime wave that predates 2023 and erodes neighborhood safety.[42] For instance, areas around the old central bus station evolved into hubs for drug trafficking and prostitution, with unchecked migrant gatherings exacerbating sanitation problems like littering and unauthorized encampments that overburden municipal resources.[43] Local testimonies highlight cultural frictions, including noise from large migrant assemblies, disregard for public order, and competition for affordable housing, framing these as direct consequences of rapid, unregulated demographic shifts rather than solely governmental neglect of pre-existing slums.[44] [45] These dynamics fostered pre-2023 unrest, including sporadic brawls among Eritrean groups over political loyalties and resource access, alongside resident-led protests decrying the erosion of communal security and identity.[46] Such tensions underscored causal links between migrant agency in criminal activities and localized decay, prompting calls for enforcement against infiltrations that locals viewed as sovereignty challenges, independent of broader policy debates.[47]

Prelude to the Clashes

Eritrea Day Event Planning

The Eritrea Day event, commemorating the September 1, 1961, start of Eritrea's armed struggle for independenceâ€”known domestically as Revolution Dayâ€”was organized by the Eritrean Embassy in Tel Aviv and scheduled for September 2, 2023.[48][49] Presented as a cultural festival, it targeted supporters of President Isaias Afwerki's government, serving as a platform for diaspora mobilization and reinforcement of regime loyalty through organized gatherings.[50][51] The embassy coordinated the event to include cultural displays, speeches, and activities affirming allegiance to Eritrea's ruling People's Front for Democracy and Justice, the sole legal party since 1974.[50] Hundreds of government loyalists attended, drawn from the local Eritrean community, with the gathering emphasizing national unity under the regime's narrative.[48][49] Regime orchestration was evident in the embassy's direct sponsorship and mobilization efforts, mirroring longstanding practices where such overseas festivals function as tools for engaging expatriates, often without prior major incidents in host countries.[51][50] These events typically proceed as controlled pro-government affairs, distinct from opposition activities, and reflect Eritrea's strategy of extending political influence abroad through embassy-led initiatives.[50]

Opposition Mobilization

Anti-regime Eritrean asylum seekers in Israel, estimated in the hundreds and comparable in number to pro-regime participants, organized protests against the Eritrean embassy's "Eritrea Day" event scheduled for September 2, 2023, viewing it as a platform endorsing President Isaias Afwerki's authoritarian rule.[48][1] These opponents, primarily refugees fleeing indefinite national service and repression, mobilized through community networks, Eritrean churches, and social media to coordinate gatherings and amplify calls for the event's cancellation, citing anticipated intimidation by regime loyalists known for enforcing diaspora taxation and silencing dissent.[9][52] In the lead-up, anti-regime groups issued direct warnings to Israeli police and authorities about the risk of violence, urging prevention measures based on prior experiences with pro-regime mobilizations that involved physical threats against critics.[53][9] These efforts echoed a broader pattern among Eritrean diaspora communities, where opponents have routinely disrupted regime-sponsored cultural festivals in host countries like Sweden, Canada, and the United States, framing them as propaganda tools that glorify the dictatorship and facilitate harassment.[54][55] Such tactics stem from verifiable grievances over the regime's extraterritorial control, including forced remittances and attacks on exiles, though they have often escalated into confrontations despite organizers' stated aim of non-violent protest.[50]

Prior Tensions Among Eritrean Groups

Tensions between Eritrean diaspora factions supporting and opposing the Isaias Afwerki regime have produced recurrent violent confrontations abroad, often centered on regime-organized events like national holidays. In Sweden, mass fighting erupted on September 17â€“18, 2022, in the Husby district of Stockholm between pro-regime participants at a People's Front for Democracy and Justice (PFDJ) gathering and anti-regime opponents, leading to 105 detentions by police.[56] In the United Kingdom, anti-regime activists stormed a pro-government theater event in London's Camberwell in December, injuring officers and resulting in 44 arrests.[17] These episodes illustrate a pattern of factional violence exported from Eritrea's domestic political repression, frequently involving weapons such as sticks, stones, and knives during cultural festivals.[17] Within Israel's Eritrean community, inter-group frictions included assaults aimed at enforcing loyalty to the regime. In 2020, a brawl between regime supporters and dissidents culminated in the arrest of a pro-regime participant, who was later bailed out by the Eritrean embassy in Tel Aviv.[57] Defector testimonies describe targeted attacks on opponents, such as a case involving two knife assaults on a dissident in south Tel Aviv, accompanied by text threats and intimidation of family members in Eritrea to compel compliance or silence.[57] Regime-affiliated networks, including the "Fourth Front" (4G) youth militia extension, have facilitated such harassment through diaspora surveillance and coercion, with documented links to attacks on opposition figures in Israel and elsewhere.[16] Agents purportedly maintain operations from a south Tel Aviv base, leveraging threats of property confiscation, relative imprisonment, or denied services to monitor and punish critics, particularly around regime commemorations.[57]

Sequence of Events

Initial Confrontations

The clashes began on September 2, 2023, in south Tel Aviv near the site of a pro-government Eritrean event organized by the Eritrean embassy to mark Revolution Day. Anti-regime Eritrean opponents mobilized to disrupt the gathering, breaking through barriers and initiating violence by throwing stones and engaging in physical assaults against pro-regime participants.[58][1] Pro-regime supporters responded with counterattacks, using clubs to beat their opponents, as captured in circulating social media footage.[48] The confrontations quickly escalated into widespread street brawls involving hundreds from both factions, who wielded improvised weapons including construction lumber, metal pieces, rocks, and at least one axe.[58][1] Video evidence depicted mutual aggression as the violence spilled from the event vicinity into adjacent streets and a nearby neighborhood.[1]

Escalation to Widespread Violence

The clashes escalated from initial confrontations between pro-regime Eritrean supporters attending the Eritrea Day event and anti-regime opponents into widespread disorder across south Tel Aviv, with participants from both factions engaging in mutual violence that damaged public and private property.[1][48] Rioters broke through police barriers, smashing windows of shops, vehicles, and police cars, while footage captured chaotic scenes of brawls spilling into streets lined with commercial storefronts and nearby residential zones.[59][60] This destruction, including shattered glass and dented car bodies visible on sidewalks, contradicted claims of one-sided aggression by emphasizing inter-factional melee where weapons like sticks and stones were wielded by members of opposing groups.[1][61] The violence persisted for several hours, beginning in the afternoon around the event site near the Eritrean embassy and extending into the evening, disrupting traffic and pedestrian areas in the mixed-use neighborhoods of south Tel Aviv.[1][49] No fatalities occurred during this phase, though the melee involved hundreds of participants hurling objects and grappling in the streets, leading to bloodstains on pavements amid the debris.[58] Verifiable video evidence from the scene documented acts of vandalism by individuals identifiable as part of the feuding Eritrean contingents, underscoring the bilateral nature of the property damage prior to intensified law enforcement intervention.[61][48]

Police Deployment and Dispersal

As violence escalated between Eritrean regime supporters and opponents in southern Tel Aviv on September 2, 2023, Israel Police deployed riot control units equipped with protective gear to the area around the Levi Eshkol Cultural Center, where the clashes had spread from an initial Eritrea Day event. Officers faced crowds hurling rocks, wooden planks, and other objects, prompting the use of mounted police and non-lethal dispersal tactics to restore order and safeguard nearby infrastructure and civilians.[1][62] Initial de-escalation efforts transitioned to forceful measures after assailants targeted police directly; forces employed stun grenades and rubber bullets to break up the stone-throwing groups, with reports indicating these were calibrated to the immediate threats posed by aggressive rioters. In instances where officers perceived lethal danger, such as during close-range assaults, live ammunition was discharged, injuring several participants amid the chaos that left approximately 49 police officers hurt. These actions were framed by authorities as necessary to counter unprovoked attacks on law enforcement, contrasting narratives from some Eritrean community members alleging disproportionate force without acknowledging the preceding assaults on personnel.[49][63][48] Dispersal operations intensified as the confrontations persisted for several hours, resulting in 39 immediate arrests of suspects for assaulting officers and pelting them with stones, with police maintaining presence to prevent further outbreaks in adjacent streets. By the operation's conclusion, the total number of Eritrean detainees reached 68, many held on suspicions of involvement in the rioting that disrupted public safety in the neighborhood. Clearance efforts extended into the evening, prioritizing the evacuation of injured parties and securing the site against renewed violence from dispersed groups.[64][59][4]

Casualties, Arrests, and Damages

Injuries and Medical Response

More than 170 people were injured in the clashes, including approximately 50 police officers, according to Israeli police and medical reports.[3] [7] The majority of the wounded were Eritrean migrants, with injuries including cuts, fractures, and gunshot wounds sustained during the violence.[65] [1] Magen David Adom, Israel's national emergency medical service, treated over 130 casualties on site, categorizing eight as severe, 14 as moderate, and the remainder as minor; dozens remained hospitalized the following day, with 12 in serious condition.[66] [3] Among police injuries, one officer suffered a serious head trauma requiring surgical intervention to remove a foreign object.[67] Treatment was provided at Tel Aviv facilities including Ichilov Medical Center, which admitted 38 patients, among them 11 with gunshot wounds and 14 in serious condition overall.[65] [1] No fatalities were recorded, and medical aid was extended impartially to police, Eritrean government supporters, and regime opponents alike, with no reported prioritization disputes.[48]

Arrests and Detentions

During the clashes on September 2, 2023, Israeli police arrested 39 Eritrean suspects accused of assaulting officers, throwing stones, and possessing weapons including tear gas canisters and an axe.[59][68] These initial detentions included individuals from both pro-Eritrean government and opposition factions, with reports specifying 46 pro-democracy protesters among those held.[69] By September 5, authorities had detained a total of 68 Eritreans suspected of involvement, placing 53 in administrative detention without trial to facilitate rapid processing due to their irregular migrant status as asylum seekers lacking permanent residency.[4] Officials prioritized administrative measures over criminal prosecutions, citing the challenges of pursuing charges against non-citizens and the intent to assess deportability.[70] Additional arrests included one pro-regime Eritrean for carrying an unlicensed firearm during the events and eight others in a related street brawl.[70][4] Some detainees were released pending further hearings; on September 18, an Israeli court ordered the release of 23 Eritreans due to insufficient evidence linking them to the violence.[64] The arrests reflected enforcement actions against participants from opposing Eritrean groups, underscoring impartial targeting of rioters regardless of factional allegiance.[4]

Property Damage

Rioters smashed windows on multiple police vehicles during the September 2, 2023, clashes in south Tel Aviv.[59] [1] Protesters also broke through barriers to vandalize nearby storefronts, including those along Levinsky Market street.[71] [72] One affected business, the Bechor & Shoshi restaurant, sustained shattered glass on its faÃ§ade, prompting owners in the migrant-dense neighborhood to subsequently guard their premises against further incidents.[73] Private vehicles in the vicinity faced similar window damage from thrown objects.[5] These losses compounded economic pressures on local merchants operating in an area marked by ongoing tensions over migrant concentrations.[73] [74] Public infrastructure, including trash bins set ablaze, required municipal intervention for removal and site restoration following the dispersal of crowds.[48] The vandalism imposed repair burdens on affected parties, though no aggregated fiscal estimates were publicly detailed.[60]

Immediate Government Response

Law Enforcement Actions

Israeli police deployed rapidly to south Tel Aviv on September 2, 2023, following reports of escalating violence between rival Eritrean groups near the Eritrean embassy, where protesters hurled stones, set fire to trash bins, and wielded makeshift weapons including metal bars. Officers initially attempted dispersal using stun grenades and non-lethal measures to separate the factions, amid a chaotic scene involving hundreds of participants.[48][1][60] As the confrontations intensified and posed direct threats to officers, police fired warning shots into the air and, in targeted instances, discharged live ammunition when personnel reported feeling endangered by advancing rioters. This response contributed to injuries among approximately 30 officers and over 100 Eritreans, including gunshot wounds to a dozen protesters, though the clashes persisted for several hours before full dispersal.[65][63][1] During the incident, authorities arrested between 39 and 52 suspects primarily for assaulting police, throwing projectiles, and public disturbances, with detentions facilitating identity verification and separation of agitators from bystanders.[75][65][63] Immediately afterward, police maintained heightened presence in the area to deter reprisals, coordinating with the Population and Immigration Authority for further identity checks and administrative detentions of 53 additional Eritreans linked to the unrest, enabling swift processing without immediate trials.[76][77]

Political Statements from Israeli Leadership

Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu stated on September 3, 2023, that Eritrean migrants involved in the Tel Aviv clashes would face immediate deportation, emphasizing that the government sought "strong steps against the rioters" while distinguishing violent actors from law-abiding residents.[78][79] His office described the participants as "illegal infiltrators," and he directed officials to formulate a plan for expediting the removal of such individuals nationwide.[80][81] Netanyahu convened a ministerial committee to oversee these measures, underscoring the need to address imported violence through enforcement of immigration laws.[82] The Interior Ministry, under Netanyahu's directive, initiated accelerated procedures for deporting Eritreans identified in the riots, aligning with prior policies classifying most Eritrean arrivals as ineligible for asylum due to improved conditions in their home country.[83] This response prioritized restoring public order in southern Tel Aviv, where migrant populations had concentrated, by targeting those who engaged in the disorder rather than broader community expulsions.[84]

Broader Reactions

Israeli Public and Political Spectrum

The clashes intensified long-standing frustrations among many Israelis regarding the presence of African migrants, particularly in south Tel Aviv, where approximately 17,000 Eritreans reside amid reports of elevated local crime rates.[1][85] Data from 2018 indicated 543 violent offense cases involving Eritrean and Sudanese migrants nationwide, contributing to perceptions of neighborhood deterioration, including property crimes and assaults concentrated in migrant-heavy areas.[85] Polls from prior years, such as a 2018 survey showing 66% of Israeli Jews approving deportation policies for African asylum seekers, reflect broader public fatigue with unchecked migration, though no equivalent post-clash polling emerged immediately.[86][87] Right-wing figures and south Tel Aviv residents vocally endorsed deportations following the violence, framing it as a culmination of years of resident protests against migrant influxes that have strained local infrastructure and safety. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu demanded immediate expulsion of participants, labeling African migration a state threat and ordering renewed efforts to remove illegal entrants.[80][53] Local activists highlighted pre-existing demonstrations in south Tel Aviv, where communities have decried the transformation of residential areas into de facto migrant enclaves marked by open prostitution, drug trade, and violence.[88] Left-leaning groups and some opposition voices expressed concerns over police tactics and potential mass deportations, portraying calls for expulsion as xenophobic while minimizing the inter-migrant nature of the unrest. Organizations like HIAS Israel condemned the violence but emphasized humanitarian recovery without endorsing deportations, aligning with critiques that downplayed migrant-linked crime in favor of integration arguments.[89] Such perspectives often attribute local tensions to policy failures rather than causal links to migrant demographics, though empirical crime data from police records contradicts claims of negligible impact.[90][91]

Eritrean Community Viewpoints

Within the Eritrean diaspora in Israel, the September 2, 2023, clashes in Tel Aviv exposed profound internal divisions between supporters of President Isaias Afwerki's government and its opponents, primarily asylum seekers who fled indefinite national service and repression. Pro-regime factions organized the event as a cultural festival to demonstrate loyalty and raise funds for state programs, framing opposition protesters as disruptive elements undermining national unity and pride.[92] Anti-regime Eritreans, including groups like Brigade Nehamedu, portrayed the gathering as an extension of Asmara's authoritarian control abroad, involving extortion through mandatory "2% income tax" payments and intimidation against non-participants, with fears that compliance signals acceptance of reprisals against relatives back home.[92][9] One anti-regime asylum seeker stated, "I have no choice but to speak out and protest," prioritizing confrontation with pro-regime aggressors over deportation risks.[93] These factional tensions, rooted in Eritrea's domestic politics, manifested as self-perpetuated violence, with both sides arming themselvesâ€”pro-regime with weapons to defend the venue and opponents seeking to halt itâ€”resulting in mutual recriminations rather than unified community reflection.[9] Broader diaspora members expressed apprehension over escalating intra-community hostility, fearing it invites external repercussions like heightened scrutiny or fragmentation, with some anonymously condemning the use of knives and guns by all parties as exacerbating their precarious status in Israel.[93]

International and NGO Perspectives

The United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) expressed deep concern over the clashes on September 2, 2023, in southern Tel Aviv, which injured over 100 Eritreans and dozens of police officers, urging Israeli authorities to exercise restraint and avoid measures risking refoulement of asylum seekers to Eritrea, where conditions include indefinite national service often likened to forced labor.[94][95] UNHCR's stance emphasized compliance with international refugee law principles, including non-refoulement, amid discussions of deportations following the violence.[96] The Eritrean government disavowed responsibility for the pro-regime participants in the clashes, attributing the unrest to orchestration by foreign intelligence agencies, including Israel's Mossad, aimed at inciting divisions among expatriates.[8][97] This narrative from Asmara framed the violence as externally manipulated rather than reflective of internal regime loyalties imported via migration, consistent with Eritrea's pattern of deflecting criticism of its authoritarian governance. Western media coverage, including reports from Reuters and PBS, focused on the immediate human tollâ€”with over 100 injuries from stones, clubs, and gunfireâ€”and the involvement of asylum seekers protesting a pro-government event, often highlighting migrant vulnerabilities and police interventions while providing limited context on Eritrea's role in sustaining factional animosities through its diaspora mobilization tactics.[48][58] Such framing aligns with observed institutional tendencies in international outlets and NGOs to prioritize narratives of migrant persecution over the causal import of source-country conflicts into host societies, potentially understating sovereignty concerns for nations like Israel absorbing large unintegrated populations.[59]

Aftermath and Consequences

Deportation Initiatives

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu instructed authorities on September 3, 2023, to pursue the immediate deportation of Eritrean migrants directly involved in the Tel Aviv clashes, citing their participation in violent riots as grounds for expedited removal. He specified that approximately 1,000 individuals implicated in the disturbances would face expulsion, framing the action as a necessary response to threats against public safety.[98][99] In parallel, Netanyahu ordered the development of a comprehensive plan to deport all unauthorized African migrants residing in Israel, describing them collectively as a "real threat" to the country's Jewish character. This directive aimed at the estimated 30,000 remaining African asylum seekers and migrants, primarily from Eritrea and Sudan, many of whom had entered illegally via Egypt.[83][78][100] To facilitate removals, the Israeli cabinet allocated 20 million NIS (about $5 million) on September 10, 2023, for incentives encouraging voluntary departures among African migrants, building on prior mechanisms that had prompted over 30,000 exits through payments or third-country relocations. Deportations of clash participants were to incorporate individual security screenings to address non-refoulement obligations under international law, prioritizing cases where involvement in violence justified revocation of temporary protections.[101][78] The United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) contested mass expulsions of Eritreans, arguing on September 5, 2023, that such actions would violate prohibitions against return to persecution, given Eritrea's documented human rights abuses; however, targeted removals of convicted rioters proceeded on national security rationales, with courts permitting expedited processes for criminal violators.[102]

Policy Shifts on African Migrants

In response to the September 2, 2023, clashes, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu instructed officials to develop a comprehensive plan for the removal of all African asylum seekers, framing the violence as evidence of the broader security and demographic threats posed by their sustained presence.[83] [80] This initiative built on the efficacy of prior deterrence measures, particularly the Egypt-Israel border barrier completed in 2013, which reduced illegal infiltrations from approximately 16,000 annually in 2012 to effectively zero by year's end, achieving over a 99% decline in crossings.[103] [104] The government's approach combined incentives for voluntary departure with expedited forced removals for those involved in unrest. On September 10, 2023, the cabinet allocated 20 million NIS (about $5.3 million) to fund programs encouraging migrants to leave, echoing earlier mechanisms that provided cash stipendsâ€”typically $3,500 per personâ€”upon relocation to third countries such as Rwanda or Uganda.[101] [105] In contrast, Netanyahu explicitly called for the immediate deportation of Eritreans implicated in the Tel Aviv violence, without awaiting judicial review, signaling a shift toward prioritizing enforcement over prolonged legal stays.[106] [107] These measures reflected a strategic pivot toward total migrant reduction, informed by the border fence's success in halting new arrivals and aimed at addressing the estimated 30,000-40,000 remaining African migrants, predominantly Eritreans and Sudanese, whose asylum claims Israel largely rejects as economic rather than persecution-based.[101] The post-clash policy emphasis underscored the need for rigorous vetting to prevent indefinite residence, as prior leniencyâ€”rooted in non-refoulement obligationsâ€”had allowed low approval rates (under 1% for Eritreans) to coexist with de facto permanence amid limited repatriation options.[83]

Long-Term Community Impacts

The persistence of intra-community violence among Eritreans in Tel Aviv, including a May 2024 brawl that left one dead and five injured, has contributed to heightened fears and self-segregation within the migrant population.[108] This ongoing conflict between regime opponents fleeing persecution and supporters of Eritrea's government, which escalated following the September 2023 clashes, has driven hundreds of asylum seekers to voluntarily relocate from south Tel Aviv to nearby areas such as Bat Yam, Givatayim, Yavneh, and Herzliya in recent months.[109] Segregation in educational settings has compounded these divisions, with most Eritrean children attending under-resourced, predominantly migrant schools that lag behind mainstream Israeli ones by two to three years in curriculum.[108] A 2023-2024 pilot program integrated approximately 80 first-grade migrant children into 11 Tel Aviv schools alongside Israeli peers, yielding reported successes in language acquisition and social acceptance among participants; however, the initiative covers only a fraction of the affected children, leaving broader tensions unresolved and prompting further family departures amid safety concerns.[108] These dynamics have fostered deeper residential and social segregation between Eritrean migrants and south Tel Aviv locals, with the latter expressing sustained resentment over perceived disruptions from migrant concentrations, though quantifiable revitalization metrics remain limited as of late 2024.[109] Eritrean regime loyalists continue attempts to organize cultural and political events in Israel, but recurrent violence has strained community cohesion without evidence of formalized reductions in event permits by authorities.[108]
