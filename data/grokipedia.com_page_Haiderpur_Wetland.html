Haiderpur Wetland

Haiderpur wetland is a Ramsar site located near the Bijnor Ganga Barrage within the Hastinapur Wildlife Sanctuary in Uttar Pradesh, India.[1]

Formation and Geography

Haiderpur wetland is a human-made wetland that was formed in 1984 after the construction of Madhya Ganga Barrage.[1] The region is fed by the Ganges and its tributary Solani river, constituting an area of 6,908 hectares within the Hastinapur Wildlife Sanctuary in Muzaffarnagar and Bijnor districts.[1] The wetland lies in the strategic Central Asian Flyway which is as an important stop over site for the winter migratory birds.[2]

Biodiversity

Bird species

The wetland is home to over 300 species of birds, including many globally threatened species.[1] Notable threatened species include the vulnerable Sarus crane (Grus antigone), the endangered Black-bellied tern (Sterna acuticauda), and the vulnerable Steppe eagle (Aquila nipalensis).[1] Commonly observed avian species include partridge, quail, peafowl, pigeon, falcon, hawk, spot-billed duck, crane, eagle, owl, white-rumped vulture, cuckoo, and nightingale. Kingfisher, myna, red-vented bulbul, sparrow, and baya weaver, among others, are also found in abundance in the wetland.

Other fauna

Among the mammals, leopard, wildcats, monkeys, fox, wolf, nilgai, jackal, mongoose, honey badger, barasingha (swamp deer), wild boars, rabbits, and bats inhabit the wetland and surrounding sanctuary region. Additional species include the endangered hog deer (Axis porcinus) and smooth-coated otter (Lutrogale perspicillata).[1][3] Reptiles such as monitor lizard, python, Indian cobra, krait, and viper are found in large numbers.[3] A significant population of IUCN Red List critically endangered gharial (Gavialis gangeticus) and many vulnerable amphibian species are observed in the wetland and the adjoining Ganga river basin.[1]

Conservation Status

Following the conservation efforts of wildlife activists and involvement of local community,[4] Haiderpur was designated as the 47th Ramsar site, a wetland of international importance, on 13 April 2021.[1] The World Wide Fund for Nature and the government have initiated several schemes for the conservation and management of Haiderpur wetland, including a bird festival and conservation planning workshop in 2020.[5][6][7] In 2023, the Uttar Pradesh government intervened to halt the dewatering of the wetland for agricultural purposes, addressing a major threat to its ecological integrity. [8] Ongoing efforts by The Art of Living and local conservationists, such as annual bird censuses, have supported biodiversity, with a 2025 census recording 74 rare Indian skimmers, indicating successful community-driven protection. [9]
