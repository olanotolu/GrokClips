2023 Kano State gubernatorial election

The 2023 Kano State gubernatorial election was held on 18 March 2023 to elect the governor of Kano State, Nigeria's most populous state, amid a competitive contest between major parties including the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP), All Progressives Congress (APC), and Peoples Democratic Party (PDP).[1][2] The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) declared NNPP candidate Abba Kabir Yusuf the winner with 1,019,846 votes, defeating APC's Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna who received 890,695 votes, a margin of 129,151.[1][3] This outcome reflected strong voter turnout in Kano's urban and rural areas, though marred by reports of logistical delays, violence, and disputes over electronic result transmission.[4] Yusuf's victory faced immediate challenges from Gawuna, leading the Kano State Governorship Election Petition Tribunal to nullify it in September 2023, primarily on grounds of electoral non-compliance, including the deduction of approximately 165,000 votes for NNPP due to missing official stamps or markings on ballots, which recalculated results in Gawuna's favor.[5][6] The Court of Appeal upheld the tribunal's ruling in November 2023, citing invalid nomination documents and overreach in vote certification.[7][8] The Supreme Court of Nigeria reversed these decisions on 12 January 2024, affirming Yusuf's election by holding that lower courts erred in applying strict technicalities over substantial compliance in ballot validation and lacked jurisdiction on pre-election nomination issues, emphasizing empirical evidence of voter intent through other ballot features like serial numbers and party symbols.[7][8][9] This resolution highlighted ongoing tensions in Nigeria's electoral system regarding judicial intervention in vote counting versus administrative certification, with Yusuf assuming office as governor.[7]

Electoral framework

Constitutional and legal basis

The 1999 Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria (as amended) establishes the office of Governor for each state, including Kano State, under Chapter VI, Part II, stipulating that the Governor shall be the chief executive and is elected for a four-year term renewable once by direct popular vote through universal adult suffrage.[10] Section 177 specifies eligibility criteria, requiring candidates to be Nigerian citizens by birth, at least 35 years old, possess a school certificate or equivalent qualification, be members of a political party, and be sponsored by that party; additionally, candidates must not have been convicted of certain offenses or declared bankrupt within specified periods.[10] Section 179 mandates a simple majority victory in a direct election, with provisions for run-offs if no candidate secures over 50% of valid votes in multi-candidate contests.[10] The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC), established by Section 153(1)(f) of the Constitution and detailed in the Third Schedule Part 1, holds exclusive authority to organize, conduct, and supervise gubernatorial elections nationwide, including in Kano State, ensuring compliance with democratic principles and timelines not later than 30 days before the expiration of the incumbent's term.[10] The Electoral Act 2022, which operationalizes these constitutional provisions, governs procedural aspects such as voter registration, candidate nomination, ballot design, polling procedures, and result collation, introducing innovations like electronic transmission of results (though later subject to judicial scrutiny) and mandatory party primaries via direct or indirect modes.[11] Sections 28â€“38 of the Act detail nomination processes, requiring political parties to submit candidate lists to INEC at least 30 days before the election, while Sections 65â€“76 outline voting, counting, and dispute resolution mechanisms at polling units.[11] Post-election challenges, including petitions against gubernatorial results, are adjudicated under Section 188 of the Constitution and Sections 130â€“141 of the Electoral Act, allowing the Governor's election to be nullified by the Election Petition Tribunal or appellate courts for reasons such as non-compliance with electoral laws, corrupt practices, or violence substantially affecting the outcome, with decisions appealable to the Court of Appeal and Supreme Court within strict 180-day timelines from declaration of winner.[11] This framework ensures uniformity across states, with INEC's guidelines for the 2023 general elections aligning Kano's pollâ€”held on March 18, 2023, following a postponement from March 11 due to logistical issuesâ€”with these mandates, though implementation faced criticisms for inconsistencies in electronic systems as noted in official reports.[4]

INEC procedures and requirements

The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) administered the 2023 Kano State gubernatorial election in accordance with the Electoral Act 2022, its Regulations and Guidelines for the Conduct of Elections 2022, and the commission's published timetable for the general elections.[12][13] These frameworks mandated political parties to adhere to strict timelines for candidate selection and nomination, ensuring internal democracy through primaries while allowing INEC to verify compliance. INEC required parties to upload nomination documents via its online portal, including Forms EC9 (expression of interest) and EC9B (nomination), along with candidates' personal particulars such as oaths of allegiance, educational credentials, and affidavits confirming eligibility and non-conviction for specified offenses.[13][14] Key pre-election requirements included the collection of expression of interest and nomination forms from INEC offices between April 4 and June 3, 2022, followed by online submission of completed forms from July 1 to July 15, 2022.[13] Parties conducted primaries starting from March 1, 2022, with most gubernatorial selections occurring via direct or indirect methods by early July, after which INEC published candidates' personal particulars on July 22, 2022, for public scrutiny and potential challenges.[13] The final list of validated candidates was released on October 4, 2022, after INEC's review for compliance, including verification that candidates met constitutional eligibility under Section 177 of the 1999 Constitution (as amended), such as being Nigerian citizens by birth, at least 35 years old, and sponsored by a registered party.[13][15] Withdrawals or replacements were permitted until August 12, 2022, but INEC rejected invalid nominations, as seen in disputes resolved through courts for parties like the Labour Party in Kano.[13] Campaigning commenced on October 12, 2022, and concluded on March 9, 2023, with parties required to submit lists of polling and collation agents by January 6, 2023.[13] On election day, March 18, 2023 (postponed from March 11 due to logistical issues nationwide), polling units operated from 8:30 a.m. to 2:30 p.m., allowing all queued voters to cast ballots thereafter.[16] Accreditation used the Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS), mandating verification via Permanent Voter's Card (PVC), biometric fingerprints, or facial capture to prevent multiple voting, followed by marking with indelible ink.[16] Voters marked ballots secretly in screened cubicles and deposited them in transparent boxes, with no photography permitted. Results were counted manually at polling units, recorded, and uploaded in real-time to INEC's Result Viewing Portal (IReV) via BVAS for public access, before collation at ward, local government, and state levels.[16] INEC officials, trained per the 2023 Election Manual, oversaw processes to ensure transparency, with parties appointing one agent per polling unit.[17]

Political context

Historical rivalries in Kano politics

Kano State politics has long been characterized by intense personal and factional rivalries, often revolving around influential figures and their networks rather than strictly ideological divides. In the post-1999 democratic era, the most prominent rivalry emerged between Rabiu Kwankwaso and Abdullahi Ganduje, who began as political allies. Kwankwaso served as governor from 1999 to 2003 under the All People's Party (APP), with Ganduje as his chief of staff, and again from 2011 to 2015 under the People's Democratic Party (PDP), where Ganduje acted as deputy governor.[18] [19] Their partnership facilitated electoral successes, including Kwankwaso's endorsement of Ganduje's 2015 gubernatorial bid under the All Progressives Congress (APC), which Ganduje won, marking a shift from PDP dominance in the state.[20] The alliance fractured after Ganduje's assumption of office in 2015, primarily over control of party structures and patronage networks. Kwankwaso accused Ganduje of disloyalty, particularly in denying him the APC's 2019 senatorial ticket in Kano Central, leading Kwankwaso to defect and eventually form the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) in 2022.[21] [22] This schism deepened into a proxy battle, with Ganduje consolidating APC control and restricting Kwankwaso's access to Kano for over three years, while Kwankwaso built a grassroots base among youth and talakawa (masses), drawing from his earlier emphasis on education and infrastructure.[19] The rivalry extended beyond elections to traditional institutions, exemplified by Ganduje's 2019 dethronement of Emir Muhammadu Sanusi II, a Kwankwaso ally, amid accusations of insubordination, which polarized public opinion along factional lines.[23] These tensions trace roots to earlier patterns in Kano's political history, where regional parties like the Northern People's Congress (NPC) clashed with radical groups such as the Northern Elements Progressive Union (NEPU) in the 1950s, pitting traditional elites against populist reformers influenced by Aminu Kano's ideology. In the Fourth Republic, similar dynamics played out between establishment figures and challengers, with violence often erupting during elections, as seen in post-2007 and 2011 polls where intra- and inter-party conflicts in Kano claimed lives amid disputes over primaries and results.[24] By 2023, the Kwankwaso-Ganduje feud had evolved into a bipolar contest between NNPP (Kwankwaso-backed) and APC (Ganduje-aligned), influencing voter mobilization and contributing to the NNPP's upset victory, though legal challenges from the APC highlighted persistent factional instability.[25][19]

Key parties, candidates, and factions

The 2023 Kano State gubernatorial election was dominated by two major parties, the All Progressives Congress (APC) and the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP), with the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) playing a lesser role amid internal divisions. Fourteen political parties fielded candidates in total, but the contest effectively hinged on the APC's Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna and the NNPP's Abba Kabir Yusuf, who secured the vast majority of votes.[26][27] Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna, the APC candidate, served as deputy governor from 2015 to 2023 under Abdullahi Ganduje and represented the incumbent administration's continuity, drawing support from the Ganduje political faction that controlled state structures.[28] Abba Kabir Yusuf, the NNPP flagbearer, was a close associate of Rabiu Kwankwaso, the party's presidential candidate and former Kano governor (1999â€“2003, 2011â€“2015), mobilizing the Kwankwasiyya movementâ€”a grassroots network emphasizing education, empowerment, and opposition to perceived Ganduje-era corruption.[29] The PDP nominated Sadiya Umar Farouk, a former federal minister, but the party suffered from factional disputes and defections, limiting its competitiveness.[30] Kano's political landscape featured entrenched factions rooted in the long-standing rivalry between Ganduje and Kwankwaso, both former governors whose falling out dated to Kwankwaso's PDP tenure and Ganduje's APC alignment. The APC embodied Ganduje's machinery, leveraging state resources and federal APC ties under President Bola Tinubu, while the NNPP served as Kwankwaso's platform after his PDP exit, capitalizing on his enduring popularity among urban youth and middle-class voters disillusioned with APC governance. Minor parties, such as the Labour Party and others among the 14 contenders, fielded candidates like those from the Zenith Labour Party or African Democratic Congress but garnered negligible support, functioning more as spoilers or protest vehicles without significant factional backing.[31][32]

Primary elections

All Progressives Congress (APC)

The All Progressives Congress (APC) conducted its indirect primary election for the Kano State gubernatorial nomination on May 26, 2022, involving votes from party delegates across the state.[33] Incumbent Deputy Governor Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna emerged as the flagbearer, polling 2,289 votes.[34] [35] Gawuna, who had served as deputy to Governor Abdullahi Umar Ganduje since 2015, received strong backing from the governor and APC stakeholders, positioning him as the consensus candidate amid internal party alignments.[36][37] The primary proceeded without major reported disruptions, reflecting the party's organizational control in the state at the time.[38] Following his victory, Gawuna was officially recognized by the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) as the APC candidate for the March 18, 2023, general election.[2]

New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP)

The New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) adopted Abba Kabir Yusuf, a close associate of party leader Rabiu Kwankwaso and former commissioner in Kano State, as its consensus gubernatorial candidate for the 2023 election on May 18, 2022.[39] This decision reflected the party's alignment with Kwankwaso's political influence in Kano, where NNPP positioned itself as an alternative to established parties amid local rivalries.[39] Yusuf faced no other aspirants in the formal primary, held on June 6, 2022, which consisted of an affirmation process rather than competitive voting.[40] A total of 1,452 delegates, comprising three representatives from each of Kano's 484 wards, unanimously endorsed his candidacy through a yes/no affirmation conducted in accordance with party guidelines.[40] The screening committee, chaired by Senator Musa Bako Aujara and including members such as Dr. Sani Ado and Bashir Yusuf, oversaw the unopposed ratification, confirming Yusuf's emergence as the nominee without reported disputes.[40]

People's Democratic Party (PDP)

The People's Democratic Party (PDP) held its Kano State gubernatorial primary on May 25, 2022, adopting direct primaries in line with the Electoral Act 2022 and party constitution requirements for delegate participation.[41] Muhammad Sani Abacha, son of late military head of state General Sani Abacha, secured the nomination with 111 votes, defeating Sadiq Aminu Wali who received 100 votes; other aspirants, including minor contenders, polled negligible support in the tally reported by party officials.[41] The exercise occurred at the PDP state secretariat in Kano amid claims of procedural adherence, though turnout details for the estimated thousands of accredited delegates from the state's 44 local government areas were not publicly itemized by the party.[41] Legal disputes immediately followed, with Wali challenging Abacha's candidacy on grounds of alleged non-participation in screening and irregularities in delegate lists submitted to the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC).[30] A Federal High Court in Kano initially upheld Abacha's victory on December 23, 2022, citing insufficient evidence of exclusion from primaries.[42] However, the Court of Appeal in Kano overturned this on February 10, 2023, reinstating Wali based on findings that Abacha's name was absent from critical INEC-uploaded forms and primary participation records, rendering the process flawed.[43] The Supreme Court affirmed this ruling on March 1, 2023, dismissing Abacha's final appeal and solidifying Wali as the PDP flag-bearer ahead of the March 18 general election.[44] These court interventions highlighted PDP's internal factionalism in Kano, exacerbated by historical power struggles between party elders and newer entrants, but did not alter the initial primary's vote outcome as recorded.[45] No widespread violence or ballot stuffing was reported during the primary itself, though the protracted litigation delayed campaign momentum and underscored vulnerabilities in PDP's delegate verification processes.[43]

Minor parties and independents

Several minor parties held primary elections or selection processes under the provisions of the Electoral Act 2022 to nominate candidates for the Kano State governorship. These processes generally received limited media coverage compared to those of the APC, NNPP, and PDP, with many involving consensus agreements or minimal contests due to the parties' smaller structures and resources.[26][46] The African Democratic Party (ADP) nominated Ibrahim Shaâ€™aban Sharada, aged 38, following its primary. The All Progressives Grand Alliance (APGA) selected Sulenkuka Umar Yakasai, aged 62, as its candidate. Other minor parties, including the African Action Congress (AAC) with Sani Mahmud (aged 65), African Democratic Congress (ADC) with Khalic Ibrahim Idris (aged 69), Allied Peoples Movement (APM) with Sani Ibrahim (aged 59), and Action Peoples Party (APP) with Muhammad Ibrahim (aged 45), completed their nominations without reported disputes.[46] Additional selections included the Boot Party (BP) nominating Yakubu Furera Ahmad (aged 40, female), Labour Party (LP) with Abdullahi Mohammed Raji (aged 61), New Reward Movement (NRM) with Mahmud Aishatu (aged 53, female), Peoples Redemption Party (PRP) with Yakasai Salihu Tanko (aged 45), Social Democratic Party (SDP) with Mohammed G Bala (aged 60), Young Progressives Party (YPP) with Muhammad Ahmed Isa (aged 36), and Zenith Labour Party (ZLP) with Nuhu Isa (aged 56). These outcomes were ratified by INEC, confirming 11 minor party candidates alongside the three major ones.[46][47] No independent candidates participated, as the Nigerian constitution requires affiliation with a registered political party for gubernatorial contests.[46]

Campaign dynamics

Candidates' platforms and strategies

Dr. Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna, the APC candidate and incumbent deputy governor, campaigned on continuity of the Ganduje administration's policies, emphasizing inclusive development and economic growth. His platform included pledges to enhance the education sector through improved infrastructure and access, boost industrialization by attracting investors, and grant financial autonomy to local governments to foster grassroots development.[48][49][50] Gawuna's strategy relied on his endorsement by Governor Abdullahi Ganduje and outreach to diaspora stakeholders in cities like Lagos, presenting a manifesto focused on equitable resource distribution to counter perceptions of elite favoritism under the ruling party.[51][52] Abba Kabir Yusuf, the NNPP candidate and ally of Senator Rabiu Kwankwaso, positioned his campaign as a break from entrenched corruption and inefficiency, leveraging the Kwankwasiyya movement's grassroots appeal in Kano's urban and rural areas. Key promises centered on pro-people governance, including reinvigorating education with modern facilities and conducive learning environments, alongside a broader blueprint for sectoral revival in health, agriculture, and infrastructure.[53][54] The NNPP strategy emphasized anti-incumbency sentiment against the APC, portraying itself as an outsider alternative to traditional parties, with Kwankwaso's personal popularity driving mobilization in a state historically dominated by PDP-APC rivalries.[55][56] Muhammad Sadiq Aminu Wali, the PDP candidate following Supreme Court affirmation amid internal primaries disputes, focused on restoring Kano's "lost glory" through targeted development in water resources, education, and security.[57][44] His platform highlighted addressing out-of-school children, healthcare deficiencies, and urban planning, drawing from his prior role as water resources commissioner.[58] However, PDP's strategy was undermined by factionalism and candidate litigation, limiting effective voter outreach compared to the APC-NNPP duel, with efforts centered on reuniting party stalwarts like Ibrahim Shekarau to challenge the incumbents.[59][60] Minor parties, such as the ADC's Ibrahim Khalil, adopted niche strategies like community-focused reforms but lacked the organizational reach to significantly influence the contest.[61] Overall, the election dynamics pitted APC's continuity against NNPP's change narrative, with platforms converging on education and economic revitalization amid Kano's youth bulge and urban poverty challenges.[62]

Key events and rallies

The campaign period for the 2023 Kano State gubernatorial election was characterized by heightened tensions between supporters of the All Progressives Congress (APC) and the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP), leading to several incidents of violence and restrictions on public gatherings. On February 23, 2023, the Kano State Police Command banned all political rallies across the state amid escalating clashes between APC and NNPP loyalists, citing risks to public safety.[63] This followed reports of planned grand rallies by both parties, with NNPP accusing APC Governor Abdullahi Ganduje of orchestrating disruptions to their events.[64] [65] NNPP leader Rabiu Kwankwaso, whose influence bolstered candidate Abba Kabir Yusuf's campaign, proceeded with a homecoming rally in Kano on February 24, 2023, drawing large crowds but resulting in attacks on participants. Kwankwaso confirmed the assaults on his supporters during the event, which security forces struggled to contain.[66] Earlier, on February 6, 2023, Kwankwaso attended an NNPP rally in Tauruni Local Government Area, emphasizing grassroots mobilization for Yusuf. These gatherings highlighted NNPP's reliance on Kwankwaso's "Kwankwasiyya" movement to counter APC dominance. APC candidate Nasir Yusuf Gawuna focused on final mobilization efforts closer to the March 18 election date. On March 12, 2023, Gawuna addressed supporters at a rally, calling for massive turnout to secure victory and promising equitable governance.[67] The event underscored APC's strategy of leveraging incumbent structures amid NNPP's momentum from presidential polls. Overall, rally restrictions and sporadic violence reflected deep-seated rivalries, with no major large-scale events reported after mid-February due to security concerns.

Polling and pre-election projections

An October 2022 opinion poll commissioned by the ANAP Foundation and conducted by NOIPolls Limited surveyed voter preferences for the Kano State gubernatorial election.[68] Abba Kabir Yusuf of the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) led with 29% support, followed by Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna of the All Progressives Congress (APC) at 21%, and Mohammed Sadiq Wali of the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) at 3%.[68] The survey highlighted significant uncertainty, with 30% of respondents undecided and 11% preferring not to disclose, suggesting these voters could determine the outcome; undecided rates were higher among women (35%) than men (22%).[68] Limited formal polling data was available for the election, with the ANAP survey representing one of the few pre-election assessments reported publicly.[68] No additional statewide polls from independent firms or academic institutions were widely documented, reflecting constraints in Nigeria's polling landscape, including potential methodological challenges in a politically polarized environment like Kano. Pre-election projections from analysts often emphasized the influence of undecided voters and historical voting patterns favoring incumbency-linked factions, but lacked quantitative models specific to Kano.[68]

Election conduct

Timetable and logistics

The timetable for the 2023 Kano State gubernatorial election followed the Independent National Electoral Commission's (INEC) schedule for Nigeria's general elections, with political parties required to conclude primaries by June 3, 2022.[13] Campaigns officially began on October 12, 2022, and were to conclude 24 hours prior to polling, originally set for March 11, 2023.[13] However, INEC postponed the governorship and state assembly elections nationwide to March 18, 2023, citing delays in deploying non-sensitive materials, cash shortages affecting logistics, and incomplete distribution of bimodular voter accreditation systems (BVAS).[4] INEC designated 11,222 polling units across Kano's 44 local government areas to facilitate voting, an increase aimed at decongesting overcrowded sites from prior elections.[69] Of the state's approximately 5.6 million registered voters, 5,594,193 had collected permanent voter cards (PVCs) by election time, enabling participation upon presentation at assigned units.[1] Logistics involved transporting ballot papers, BVAS devices, and result sheets to units, with INEC training thousands of ad hoc staff for accreditation, voting, and collation duties.[4] Polling stations opened at 8:30 AM on March 18, with voting scheduled to close at 2:30 PM, though INEC guidelines permitted continuation for voters already queued.[70] The process required voters to verify names against the register, undergo BVAS accreditation via fingerprint or facial recognition, receive marked ballots, and deposit them in boxes; INEC mandated BVAS deployment to all units for real-time voter verification and result transmission via the INEC Result Viewing Portal (IReV).[70][4] Collation began at ward levels post-4:00 PM, progressing to local governments, senatorial districts, and the state headquarters in Kano.[70]

Security arrangements and violence incidents

The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) coordinated with security agencies for the March 18, 2023, gubernatorial election in Kano State, deploying personnel to polling units and collation centers. Security forces, including police, were present at over 85% of observed polling units by 6:30 to 8:00 a.m., with armed patrols maintaining visibility on major roads while adhering to guidelines limiting proximity to polling sites.[71] Despite these measures, violence disrupted voting in several local government areas (LGAs). In Dala LGAâ€”specifically Layin Maiunguwa, Filin Chiranchi, Kabuga, Kankarofi, and Gawunaâ€”youth gangs known as yan daba chased away voters, attacked participants, and destroyed ballot boxes and papers to suppress turnout. Similar incidents occurred in Tudun Wada, Takai, and Nassarawa LGAs, where attacks targeted collation centers and aimed to influence results through intimidation.[72] Casualties included three deaths in Tudun Wada LGA from an APC-aligned yan daba assault on an NNPP office, alongside at least 10 injuries in the same area from voter attacks. Broader reports documented electoral killings, voter intimidation, and thuggery across Kano, contributing to suppressed participation amid partisan clashes between APC and NNPP supporters.[72][71][73] Post-election violence followed result announcements, with political thugs invading and setting fire to the residence of APC chieftain and singer Dauda Rarara, destroying property in apparent retaliation.[74]

Allegations of irregularities and malpractice

The All Progressives Congress (APC), whose candidate Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna trailed in preliminary counts, accused the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) of disseminating fabricated election results on March 18, 2023, during ongoing collation to incite unrest and mask an anticipated defeat.[75] The APC claimed these parallel announcements portrayed undue advantages for the NNPP's Abba Kabir Yusuf, exacerbating tensions in a state with a history of electoral friction, though the NNPP countered that their figures derived from the Independent National Electoral Commission's (INEC) official IReV portal.[75] A coalition of INEC-accredited domestic observers, led by Friday Maduka, petitioned INEC to scrutinize the poll's integrity, citing premature declaration of results amid incomplete collation, in breach of procedural guidelines.[76] They highlighted that Yusuf's declared 130,000-vote margin fell short of the over 270,000 cancelled votes across affected polling units, invoking Section 65 of the Electoral Act 2022, which mandates supplementary elections when margins are eclipsed by disruptions.[76] Broader infractions included documented violence, thuggery, and non-adherence to INEC protocols on voter accreditation and ballot handling, rendering the process deficient against benchmarks for transparency and credibility.[76] The Peoples Democratic Party (PDP), with candidate Sadiya Umar Farouk placing third, echoed concerns over procedural lapses but lodged fewer contemporaneous public claims specific to Kano's gubernatorial race compared to the APC; their petition later alleged pervasive non-compliance, though contemporaneous reports emphasized general disenfranchisement via logistical delays and security gaps rather than isolated fraud.[76] INEC dismissed many real-time complaints as unsubstantiated, attributing disruptions to technical glitches in the Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) and insisting results reflected uploaded polling unit data, yet observers noted persistent mismatches between BVAS records and manual tallies in select units.[76]

General election results

Official vote tallies

The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) declared the results of the 2023 Kano State gubernatorial election on March 20, 2023, following collation at the state level. Abba Kabir Yusuf, the candidate of the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP), was announced as the winner with 1,019,602 votes, securing more than the required majority and at least 25% of votes in over two-thirds of the state's 44 local government areas. His closest rival, Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna of the All Progressives Congress (APC), received 890,705 votes.[77] Of the 17 candidates who contested, the remaining votes were distributed among representatives of minor parties and independents, totaling 67,565 valid votes. INEC reported 2,032,955 accredited voters out of 5,921,370 registered, with 1,977,872 valid votes cast and 27,092 rejected ballots, yielding a total of 2,004,964 votes cast.[77]

Breakdown by senatorial districts, constituencies, and LGAs

The vote distribution in the 2023 Kano State gubernatorial election reflected regional differences across the state's three senatorial districtsâ€”Kano Central, Kano North, and Kano Southâ€”each comprising multiple local government areas (LGAs) and state assembly constituencies. Kano Central, encompassing urban LGAs such as Kano Municipal, Dala, Fagge, and Tarauni, saw strong performance by the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) candidate Abba Kabir Yusuf, driven by voter turnout in densely populated areas loyal to NNPP leadership influences.[3] In contrast, All Progressives Congress (APC) candidate Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna secured relatively higher shares in select rural LGAs within Kano North (e.g., areas like Gwarzo and Madobi) and Kano South (e.g., Kumbotso and Ungogo), where traditional party structures held sway, though insufficient to offset statewide totals.[78] Collation from the 44 LGAs revealed NNPP leading in a majority after announcements from over 30 areas, with early reports indicating victories in 8 of the first 12 LGAs announced, primarily urban and semi-urban ones.[79] Yusuf met the constitutional threshold of 25% of votes in at least two-thirds (29) of LGAs, contributing to his overall margin of 1,019,846 votes against Gawuna's 890,695.[77] State assembly constituencies mirrored these patterns, with NNPP dominating in Central district seats tied to metropolitan Kano, while APC held ground in northern and southern rural constituencies. Detailed per-LGA tallies, aggregated via INEC's local collation centers and uploaded to the IReV portal, confirmed these trends but highlighted close contests in border LGAs like Dawakin Tofa and Bichi.[80]

Post-election disputes

Governorship election tribunal

Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna, the All Progressives Congress (APC) candidate in the 2023 Kano State gubernatorial election, filed a petition at the Kano State Governorship Election Petition Tribunal on April 14, 2023, challenging the Independent National Electoral Commission's (INEC) declaration of Abba Kabir Yusuf of the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) as the winner. The petition cited substantial non-compliance with the Electoral Act 2022, including over-voting in multiple polling units, invalid ballots lacking INEC's official stamp or signature, and allegations of electoral malpractices such as ballot stuffing and voter intimidation.[5] Gawuna sought the nullification of Yusuf's victory, deduction of invalid votes, and his own declaration as governor based on adjusted tallies.[81] The tribunal, chaired by Justice R.M. Aikawa with members Justice Olusegun Oyebel and Justice Ibrahim Karaye, commenced pre-hearing sessions in May 2023 and admitted evidence including witness testimonies, electoral forms, and ballot papers from disputed units across Kano's 44 local government areas.[82] Proceedings focused on forensic analysis of ballot authenticity, with petitioners presenting data showing over 200,000 invalid votes due to missing INEC markings, which they argued violated Section 71 of the Electoral Act requiring official authentication.[5] Respondents, including Yusuf and INEC, countered that the unstamped ballots were valid administrative oversights, not grounds for wholesale invalidation, and challenged the petitioners' evidence as insufficient to prove widespread malpractice impacting the overall result.[81] On September 20, 2023, the tribunal delivered a unanimous 400-page judgment sacking Yusuf as governor, holding that the election was marred by non-compliance and irregularities, particularly the invalidation of 165,292 ballots lacking INEC stamps, which, when deducted from Yusuf's margin, swung the victory to Gawuna with 1,019,845 votes against Yusuf's 1,019,103.[81][82] The panel ordered INEC to withdraw Yusuf's certificate of return and issue one to Gawuna, emphasizing that the petitioners proved their case beyond reasonable doubt on the substantial effect of the malpractices.[28] The ruling drew mixed reactions, with APC supporters hailing it as vindication of electoral integrity, while critics questioned the tribunal's strict interpretation of ballot validity amid broader INEC lapses nationwide.[83]

Court of Appeal proceedings

Following the Kano State Governorship Election Petition Tribunal's dismissal of Nasir Yusuf Gawuna's petition on September 20, 2023, which upheld Abba Kabir Yusuf's victory as lacking merit despite claims of irregularities, Gawuna, the All Progressives Congress (APC) candidate, filed an appeal at the Court of Appeal in Abuja.[84][85] The appeal challenged the tribunal's findings on issues including Yusuf's nomination validity, over-voting, and non-compliance with electoral procedures under the Electoral Act 2022. On November 17, 2023, a three-member panel of the Court of Appeal, presided over by Justice Muâ€™azu Abubakar, unanimously allowed Gawuna's appeal in a lead judgment delivered by Justice Obiora Egbo. The court set aside the tribunal's decision, nullified Yusuf's election, and declared Gawuna the valid winner, ordering the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) to withdraw Yusuf's certificate of return and issue a new one to Gawuna. The primary ground was that Yusuf was not validly sponsored by the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP), as the party failed to conduct congresses and primaries across all 44 local government areas and 484 wards as required by Sections 33 and 84 of the Electoral Act; evidence, including affidavits and INEC Form EC9 submitted by NNPP, indicated primaries occurred only in Kano Municipal local government area, rendering the process non-compliant and the nomination preclusive to Yusuf's eligibility.[85][86][87] The panel also upheld claims of electoral malpractices, including over-voting and failure to electronically transmit results from polling units in 22 local government areas, deducting 165,663 invalid votes from Yusuf's recorded 1,185,454 tally, reducing it to 1,019,791â€”below Gawuna's 1,038,081 votes. This deduction was based on discrepancies between accredited voters, votes cast, and ballot papers used, violating Sections 64 and 68 of the Electoral Act.[85][88] A subsequent controversy emerged over the certified true copy (CTC) of the judgment. Initial CTCs distributed to parties reflected the vote deductions as integral to the ruling, supporting Gawuna's victory on vote counts alone. However, on November 22, 2023, the court's chief registrar, Umar Bangari, issued a statement attributing the inclusion of deductions to a "clerical error" in transcription, insisting the judgment hinged solely on the invalid nomination issue without altering vote tallies. Yusuf's legal team contested this, arguing the lead judgment explicitly addressed deductions and that altering CTC content post-delivery undermined judicial integrity; Gawuna's counsel maintained the nomination defect was dispositive regardless of votes. The clarification did not resolve the dispute, with observers noting it fueled perceptions of inconsistency in the appellate process.[89][90][91]

Supreme Court decision and implications

On January 12, 2024, Nigeria's Supreme Court, in the case of Yusuf Abba Kabir v. All Progressives Congress (APC) & Ors (Suit No. SC.CV/1179/2023), unanimously upheld Abba Kabir Yusuf's victory in the March 18, 2023, Kano State gubernatorial election, reversing the Court of Appeal's November 17, 2023, judgment that had declared Nasiru Yusuf Gawuna of the APC as the winner.[92][7] The apex court held that the lower courts erred in invalidating over 165,000 ballots for lacking the Independent National Electoral Commission's (INEC) official stamp or serial numbers, ruling that the Electoral Act 2022 does not mandate such markings for ballot validity and that substantial compliance with electoral laws suffices unless forgery is proven.[8][93] It further affirmed Yusuf's membership in the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) as valid under the party's constitution and dismissed claims of non-qualification, describing the appellate court's findings as perverse and unsupported by evidence.[7][93] The decision reinforced the principle of substantial compliance in Nigerian electoral jurisprudence, prioritizing voter intent over procedural technicalities attributable to INEC's lapses, such as inconsistent stamping during polling.[8][93] Justices, led by a seven-member panel headed by Justice John Okoronkwo, emphasized that invalidating votes en masse without direct proof of manipulation undermines democratic outcomes, a stance that contrasted with the lower courts' stricter interpretation of ballot integrity requirements.[92][7] Politically, the ruling solidified NNPP control in Kano, Nigeria's most populous state with over 10 million registered voters, enabling Yusuf to continue initiatives like free education and health reforms without further legal interruptions.[8] It represented a setback for the ruling APC, which had leveraged lower court wins to challenge opposition victories nationwide, potentially weakening its northern stronghold and prompting internal recriminations over electoral strategy.[94][95] Broader implications included heightened scrutiny of INEC's operational flaws, calls for Electoral Act amendments to clarify ballot authentication, and criticism from APC stakeholders who argued the verdict could incentivize irregularities by downplaying verifiable defects like unstamped ballots, though supporters hailed it as safeguarding electoral finality.[93][9] The judgment contributed to the Supreme Court's resolution of most 2023 governorship disputes, restoring stability but fueling debates on judicial consistency in interpreting technical versus substantive electoral violations.[9][7]
