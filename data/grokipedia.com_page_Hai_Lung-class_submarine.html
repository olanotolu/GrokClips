Hai Lung -class submarine

The Hai Lung-class submarines comprise two diesel-electric attack submarines, Hai Lung (SS-793) and Hai Hu (SS-794), serving in the Republic of China Navy since their commissioning in 1987 and 1988, respectively.[1][2] These vessels, constructed in the Netherlands as variants of the Dutch Zwaardvis-class design—which itself derives from the American Barbel class—represent the core of Taiwan's undersea warfare capability for decades, equipped for anti-surface, anti-submarine, and intelligence-gathering missions.[1][3] Ordered in September 1981 amid efforts to bolster naval defenses against regional threats, the submarines were laid down in December 1982 and launched in 1986 before undergoing sea trials and transport to Taiwan.[1][4] Featuring a teardrop hull for improved hydrodynamics, each displaces approximately 2,400 tons submerged and achieves speeds up to 20 knots underwater via three diesel engines producing around 5,100 shaft horsepower.[1][5] Armed with torpedoes and Harpoon anti-ship missiles, they have undergone periodic upgrades to extend service life, though plans for a larger indigenous submarine program signal their eventual replacement.[3][2]

Development and Procurement

Origins and Acquisition Process

In the late 1970s, the Republic of China Navy (ROCN) operated a limited submarine force centered on two refurbished World War II-era Hai Shih-class vessels, which lacked the endurance, sensors, and weaponry to effectively counter the People's Liberation Army Navy's (PLAN) modernization, including its growing fleet of diesel-electric submarines derived from Soviet Romeo-class designs. This capability gap heightened vulnerabilities in the Taiwan Strait, where Taiwan depended on secure sea lines of communication (SLOCs) for over 90% of its energy imports, amid escalating cross-strait tensions during the Cold War.[2][6] To rectify this without pursuing nuclear submarines that risked broader escalation, Taiwan prioritized conventional diesel-electric platforms for asymmetric sea denial roles. In September 1981, the ROCN contracted the Dutch firm Wilton-Fijenoord for two submarines derived from the Zwaardvis-class design, valued at approximately $500 million, with payments partly offset by Taiwanese industrial investments in the Netherlands.[1][7][8] The transaction proceeded despite Dutch governmental hesitations triggered by intense diplomatic pressure from the PRC, which protested the sale as a violation of emerging understandings on arms transfers to Taiwan and accused the Netherlands and United States of collusion. Beijing's objections underscored its strategic sensitivity to Taiwan's naval enhancements, yet the Netherlands approved the export, prioritizing alliance dynamics and commercial benefits over immediate PRC relations.[9][8]

Construction Timeline

The keels for Hai Lung (SS-793) and Hai Hu (SS-794) were laid down in December 1982 at the Wilton-Fijenoord shipyard in Schiedam, Netherlands, as part of an order placed in September 1981 for two diesel-electric attack submarines based on an enhanced Zwaardvis-class hull form.[1] Construction incorporated specific adaptations for Republic of China Navy requirements, including improved air-conditioning for tropical operational environments and expanded fuel storage to achieve greater endurance and range compared to standard Dutch variants.[2] Both submarines were launched in 1986, with Hai Lung entering the water on October 6 and Hai Hu on December 20, marking the completion of hull fabrication after approximately four years of build time.[1] Fitting-out and initial systems integration followed, during which international diplomatic pressures from the People's Republic of China—opposing arms sales to Taiwan—intensified scrutiny but did not halt progress on the contracted pair, though plans for four additional boats were abandoned.[1] Sea trials for Hai Lung began in March 1987, encompassing propulsion, sensor, and weapon system evaluations in the North Sea, with similar testing for Hai Hu shortly thereafter.[1] Final acceptance and delivery proceeded despite export-related controversies, enabling the submarines to transit under their own power to Taiwan for commissioning: Hai Lung on October 9, 1987, and Hai Hu in early 1988.[4]

Design and Technical Specifications

Hull and Structural Features


The Hai Lung-class submarines employ a teardrop hull form derived from the U.S. Navy's Barbel-class design, prioritizing hydrodynamic efficiency and reduced drag for enhanced underwater performance in diesel-electric configuration.[10] This shape contributes to improved maneuverability and stealth, essential for operations in contested littoral environments. The hull measures 66.9 meters in length and 8.4 meters in beam, with a displacement of 2,376 tons surfaced and 2,660 tons submerged.[11] Structurally, the class utilizes a hybrid single-double hull arrangement, featuring a robust pressure hull enveloped by lighter outer sections in forward and aft areas for buoyancy control and damage resistance.[12] This configuration enhances survivability against underwater threats by allowing compartmentalization of flooding or damage, while the single-hull pressure vessel maintains structural integrity and volume efficiency typical of Western non-nuclear designs. The pressure hull, constructed from high-tensile steel, is rated for operational depths up to 300 meters, balancing depth capability with cost constraints inherent to conventional submarine builds.[4] These dimensions and features are tailored for effectiveness in the Taiwan Strait's variable bathymetry, where average depths rarely exceed 60 meters, enabling agile navigation and ambush tactics without compromising the platform's blue-water potential.[13] The emphasis on economical materials and fabrication—eschewing nuclear propulsion complexities—facilitated procurement from Dutch shipyards, yielding reliable vessels suited to Taiwan's defensive posture against regional naval imbalances.[1]

Propulsion and Performance

The Hai Lung-class submarines utilize a diesel-electric propulsion system featuring three Stork-Werkspoor diesel engines generating approximately 4,200 horsepower for battery charging and surface operations, paired with Holec electric motors providing up to 5,100 shaft horsepower for submerged propulsion via a single shaft and propeller.[14][10] This configuration lacks air-independent propulsion, necessitating snorkeling to run diesels and recharge batteries, which limits fully submerged operations to battery capacity alone.[2] Maximum speeds attain 12 knots surfaced and 20 knots submerged, enabling tactical maneuvering in littoral waters but constrained by the diesel-electric limitations compared to nuclear-powered counterparts.[14][4] Operational range extends to 10,000 nautical miles at 9 knots while snorting, supporting extended patrols in the Taiwan Strait vicinity without blue-water transoceanic capability.[14] Submerged endurance on batteries supports approximately 48 hours at low speeds, after which snorkeling is required for recharging to maintain stealth and mobility in regional defense roles.[2]

Armament and Weaponry

The Hai Lung-class submarines feature six 533 mm forward torpedo tubes designed for launching heavyweight torpedoes in both anti-surface and anti-submarine roles.[1] These tubes support wire-guided torpedoes, enabling precise targeting through fiber-optic or electrical wire guidance systems that allow real-time adjustments during transit.[1] In their original configuration, the primary armament consisted of AEG SUT 264 heavyweight torpedoes, acquired as part of the late-1980s procurement package from the Netherlands, which provide dual-purpose capability with active/passive acoustic homing for engaging surface ships and submarines.[15] The submarines have a total weapons capacity of 20 torpedoes or equivalent ordnance, stored in pressure hull racks for reload during operations.[4] Compatibility extends to the U.S.-supplied Honeywell Mk 48 heavyweight torpedo, a wire-guided design with advanced sonar countermeasures resistance, though integration was not part of the initial loadout and requires specific adaptations for the class's fire control systems.[16] No vertical launch systems are present in the original design, limiting launches to horizontal tube ejections.[1] Defensive and area-denial options include the ability to deploy naval mines from the torpedo tubes, supporting minelaying missions to create barriers against enemy naval forces, though specific mine types remain classified or tied to standard 533 mm encapsulation.[1] This armament suite emphasizes stealthy, close-range engagements, leveraging the submarines' diesel-electric propulsion for quiet approaches prior to weapon release.[4]

Sensors, Electronics, and Stealth Capabilities

The Hai Lung-class submarines are fitted with a Thomson Sintra TSM 2272 Eledone Octopus bow-mounted sonar array for active and passive target detection, complemented by the DUUX-5 Fenelon system for passive acoustic rangefinding and search.[17] This suite is integrated with the Signaal SIASS-Z hull-mounted sonar, enabling passive and active modes for underwater situational awareness.[14][18] The submarines also incorporate conventional search periscopes for optical observation and electronic support measures (ESM) antennas to detect and analyze enemy radar and communications emissions during periscope depth operations.[17] Central to sensor data fusion and target acquisition is the Signaal SINBADS-M combat management system, which handles inputs from sonar, periscopes, and ESM to support decision-making in submerged environments.[18] This 1980s-era architecture processes multiple contacts but lacks the networked data links and automation of modern systems, constraining its effectiveness against advanced threats reliant on integrated sensor grids. Stealth features emphasize acoustic quieting through a teardrop hull derived from the U.S. Barbel-class design and a single seven-bladed propeller, reducing cavitation and machinery noise during battery operation.[17] While electronic warfare capabilities include ESM for threat warning, the absence of contemporary decoys or advanced countermeasures—limited by the era's technology—renders the class vulnerable to modern active sonar and multi-static detection networks prevalent in contested waters as of the 2020s.[14]

Operational History and Service

Commissioning and Initial Deployments

The Hai Lung (SS-793) was commissioned into Republic of China Navy (ROCN) service on 9 October 1987, marking the first delivery of the class, while the Hai Hu (SS-794) followed on 9 April 1988.[4] Both vessels had completed builder's sea trials in the Netherlands—Hai Lung starting in March 1987 and Hai Hu in January 1988—before being transported to Taiwan on heavy-lift ships for final handover and integration.[1] Upon arrival, the submarines were based at the Tsoying Naval Base in Kaohsiung, the ROCN's primary submarine facility.[2] Initial post-commissioning activities included localized shakedown trials to validate performance in the Western Pacific environment, alongside crew familiarization to build proficiency in diesel-electric propulsion and submerged operations, which differed from the ROCN's prior aging fleet.[1] These efforts emphasized rapid operational readiness amid heightened secrecy protocols, driven by persistent intelligence surveillance from the People's Republic of China across the Taiwan Strait. Early deployments established routine patrol patterns centered on the Taiwan Strait, prioritizing coastal defense and deterrence roles over extended blue-water missions.[1] The submarines' integration bolstered the ROCN's undersea capabilities, with initial operations focusing on verifying sensor integration, battery endurance, and quiet running in littoral waters to counter potential amphibious threats.[2]

Key Exercises and Missions

The Hai Lung-class submarines have taken part in Taiwan's annual Han Kuang exercises, which simulate invasion repulsion scenarios and incorporate anti-submarine warfare (ASW) training to enhance defensive readiness against potential amphibious threats. During the 2022 Han Kuang exercise, held from July 26 to 29, these submarines operated alongside approximately 20 surface vessels—including Kee Lung-class destroyers—and various aircraft types such as F-16Vs and Mirage 2000s, focusing on integrated naval maneuvers in coastal and open-water environments.[19] In surveillance operations, a Hai Lung-class boat conducted patrols near Dongsha (Pratas) Island and joined defense drills in the Spratly Islands chain during November 2021, tracking movements of People's Republic of China naval assets amid heightened tensions in the South China Sea. These deployments emphasized stealthy intelligence gathering and deterrence signaling rather than direct confrontation, with no verified combat incidents involving the class.[20][21] Interoperability efforts have included procedural alignments with U.S. naval forces through shared ASW protocols and communication standards, derived from the submarines' Dutch-designed systems compatible with Western allies, though specific joint drills remain classified or unpublicized.[22] Such activities reinforce Taiwan's undersea deterrence posture without escalating to open engagements.

Maintenance Challenges and Reliability

The Hai Lung-class submarines, commissioned in 1987 and 1988, have required ongoing maintenance to address wear from extended service in Taiwan's humid subtropical climate, which exacerbates corrosion on hull structures, batteries, and diesel propulsion systems common to aging diesel-electric designs.[2] Frequent overhauls of these components have been necessary to sustain operational readiness, with the Republic of China Navy (ROCN) relying on a combination of imported spares and local expertise to mitigate degradation.[23] Parts sourcing has presented difficulties due to post-delivery political pressures on the Netherlands, including a 1984 informal agreement restricting further strategic exports to Taiwan amid Chinese objections, though annual exports of submarine spare parts valued at 15-20 million euros continued as of 2012 to support upkeep.[24] [25] In response, the ROCN pursued Dutch approval for modernization in 2018, enabling local execution of repairs to extend hull life and reliability without full reliance on foreign yards.[23] A 2016 life-extension program, initiated by Taiwan's Ministry of National Defense, successfully prolonged the class's service by fifteen years through comprehensive refits addressing structural and mechanical fatigue, demonstrating effective indigenous repair capabilities that have preserved the vessels' deterrence value despite narratives of impending obsolescence.[2] These efforts have maintained baseline reliability, with both submarines remaining seaworthy after nearly three decades of high operational tempo, though public data on precise downtime or availability rates—such as periods with only one boat active in the 1990s-2000s—remains limited in open ROCN disclosures.[26]

Modernization and Upgrades

Life-Extension Program Details

The life-extension program for the Hai Lung-class submarines commenced in 2016 under the Republic of China Ministry of National Defense, aiming to prolong the operational lifespan of both vessels by approximately 15 years into the 2030s through targeted refits addressing hull integrity, mechanical systems, electrical components, and combat capabilities.[2] Valued at US$12.35 million, the initiative focuses on enhancing propulsion reliability via mechanical upgrades, integrating advanced weaponry such as US Mark 48 heavy torpedoes, and modernizing electronic warfare and combat management systems to counter obsolescence.[26][27] The design phase spanned roughly two years from 2016 to 2018, led by the National Chung-Shan Institute of Science and Technology (NCSIST) with the Shipbuilding and Offshore Industries Corporation serving as subcontractor, culminating in feasibility assessments for structural and systems overhauls.[26] In 2018, Dutch firm RH Marine granted permission for the upgrades after verifying technical viability, subsequently partnering on systems integration to ensure compatibility with upgraded propulsion, sensors, and armaments.[27][23] Execution proceeded in phases, with software development, equipment acquisition, and initial integration starting in 2019, followed by physical refits from 2020 to 2022, enabling the first submarine's return to service by 2024.[27] Key refit elements include hull reinforcements for sustained structural integrity, electrical overhauls potentially encompassing battery replacements, new periscope integrations tied to combat systems like Lockheed Martin's SUBICS, and enhancements to the TIMNEX 4CH(V2) electronic support measures for improved stealth and detection.[26] These measures prioritize causal enhancements in submerged endurance, weapon delivery precision, and sensor fusion without altering the submarines' core diesel-electric architecture.[23]

Recent Enhancements and Integration with New Technologies

The Hai Lung-class submarines received Harpoon Block II missile retrofits, enabling precision strikes against land targets in addition to anti-ship missions, with integration efforts announced in 2013 and contributing to operational enhancements through the 2020s.[28] These upgrades expand tactical options for coastal engagements, such as disrupting harbor infrastructure, while maintaining compatibility with Taiwan's evolving missile ecosystem.[29] Taiwan's Republic of China Navy has incorporated Hai Lung-class vessels into multi-layered anti-submarine warfare networks, leveraging sonar arrays and electronic warfare systems to counter People's Republic of China unmanned underwater vehicle incursions, alongside coordination with unmanned surface vessels for reconnaissance and decoying.[30] This integration aligns older platforms with newer technologies, including potential UUV deployments for mine detection and surveillance, enhancing overall fleet responsiveness without requiring full hardware overhauls.[30] As indigenous Hai Kun-class submarines face delivery delays—pushing prototype sea acceptance trials and handover beyond November 2025 due to propulsion and subsystem calibration issues—the Hai Lung-class provides interim fleet continuity, operating in tandem with emerging platforms to sustain undersea patrol capabilities.[31][32] These adaptations ensure interoperability with Taiwan's broader defense architecture amid production setbacks in domestic builds.[33]

Strategic Role and Assessments

Contribution to Taiwan's Asymmetric Defense

The Hai Lung-class submarines form a cornerstone of the Republic of China Navy's (ROCN) asymmetric defense posture, which prioritizes sea denial operations to offset the People's Liberation Army Navy's (PLAN) numerical and technological superiority in blue-water capabilities.[34][35] By leveraging their diesel-electric design for extended submerged patrols, these submarines enable the ROCN to conduct persistent surveillance and disruption in littoral zones, focusing on interdicting amphibious and logistical threats rather than engaging in symmetrical fleet battles.[36] This approach aligns with Taiwan's doctrinal shift toward "porcupine" strategies, where submarines act as force multipliers in denying adversaries uncontested access to the Taiwan Strait and adjacent chokepoints.[37] In operational terms, the class supports ambush tactics by exploiting acoustic stealth advantages inherent to conventional submarines in shallow, noisy coastal waters, where surface fleets are more vulnerable to detection and counterfire.[38] Their ability to loiter undetected near critical maritime bottlenecks—such as the strait itself or approaches from the east—facilitates targeted strikes on invasion convoys or resupply lines, enhancing Taiwan's capacity for attrition warfare without requiring parity in tonnage or firepower.[39] Empirical data from submarine acoustics studies underscore this edge, as diesel-electric platforms like the Hai Lung achieve lower radiated noise levels during battery operation compared to gas-turbine surface combatants, allowing for closer-range engagements in confined spaces.[36] During the submarines' primary service era from commissioning in 1987–1988 through the 2000s, their integration into ROCN patrols coincided with a track record of no successful PRC naval blockades or amphibious landings, despite escalatory episodes like the 1995–1996 missile tests and live-fire exercises.[40] This deterrence value stemmed from the submarines' role in maintaining credible sea-lane denial, compelling PLAN forces to operate cautiously amid uncertainty over hidden underwater threats, thereby preserving Taiwan's economic lifelines and complicating coercive maneuvers.[35]

Effectiveness in Deterring PRC Threats

The Hai Lung-class submarines enhance deterrence against People's Republic of China (PRC) threats by introducing uncertainty into amphibious invasion planning, where their stealthy operations in the Taiwan Strait's confined waters could target concentrated landing forces traversing predictable routes. Diesel-electric submarines like the Hai Lung excel in shallow, noisy littoral environments, enabling ambush tactics against vulnerable troop transports and amphibious assault ships that lack the maneuverability of blue-water fleets. Military analyses emphasize that such subsurface threats elevate the logistical and temporal risks of a cross-strait operation, potentially sinking dozens of vessels in the initial phases and disrupting the rapid buildup required for PLA success.[41][42] Post-commissioning in 1987–1988, the Hai Lung-class prompted observable shifts in PLA Navy (PLAN) preparations, with amphibious exercises incorporating dedicated anti-submarine warfare (ASW) countermeasures to simulate countering Taiwan's underwater assets amid strait-crossing maneuvers. This adaptation reflects the submarines' role in compelling the PRC to diversify invasion tactics, such as emphasizing air-mobile insertions or mine-laying to mitigate submersible interdiction, thereby extending operational timelines and exposing forces to attrition. Empirical assessments of PLAN drills highlight increased ASW asset deployment— including helicopters and corvettes—in Taiwan contingency scenarios, attributing this evolution to the persistent deterrent posed by Taiwan's diesel-electric fleet.[43][44] In direct comparison, the Hai Lung-class maintained a qualitative edge over PRC Ming-class (Type 035) submarines through superior acoustic stealth and torpedo systems until the mid-2010s, when China's Yuan-class (Type 039A) introduced air-independent propulsion for extended submerged endurance. The Dutch-designed Hai Lung achieved lower radiated noise levels via advanced hull mounting and propeller optimization, outperforming the noisier Soviet-derived Ming, which suffered from higher detectability and reliance on less precise acoustic-homing torpedoes like the Yu-4. Armed with up to 20 SUT Mod 1 wire-guided torpedoes capable of 12 km range at 35 knots, the Hai Lung provided reliable anti-surface strike capability against PLAN amphibious groups, sustaining deterrence during the era when Ming boats formed the bulk of China's conventional submarine force.[45][46] By 2025, ongoing ROC Navy integrations of enhanced sonar and electronic warfare suites have reinforced the Hai Lung-class's utility against evolving PLAN tactics, including unmanned underwater vehicle swarms intended to screen invasion fleets, per official assessments of life-extension programs. These upgrades enable better discrimination of low-signature threats in cluttered strait acoustics, preserving the submarines' role in asymmetric denial strategies despite numerical inferiority to PRC surface assets.[47]

Limitations, Criticisms, and Comparative Analysis

The limited fleet size of two Hai Lung-class submarines constrains the Republic of China Navy's capacity to maintain high sortie rates, particularly in scenarios requiring persistent underwater presence amid competing demands for training and patrols.[2] This scarcity amplifies the impact of individual vessel downtime, as evidenced by broader Taiwanese submarine maintenance overruns totaling 6,296 days of unavailability over a recent multi-year period.[43] High maintenance demands relative to operational output have drawn criticism, with the aging diesel-electric design—lacking air-independent propulsion (AIP)—necessitating frequent surfacing for battery recharging, which increases detectability and shortens effective mission durations compared to AIP-equipped contemporaries.[48] Analysts in the 2010s highlighted opportunity costs, arguing that resources devoted to sustaining these 1980s-era vessels could have accelerated indigenous submarine programs, potentially yielding a larger, more sustainable fleet sooner rather than extending reliance on foreign-sourced spares for obsolete systems.[49] The class faces heightened vulnerability to evolving People's Liberation Army Navy (PLAN) anti-submarine warfare assets, such as the Y-8Q maritime patrol aircraft, which integrates dipping sonar, sonobuoys, and torpedoes to detect and engage surface-dependent diesel submarines in contested waters like the Taiwan Strait.[50] In comparative terms, the Hai Lung-class trails advanced nuclear attack submarines like the U.S. Virginia-class in submerged endurance, speed (exceeding 25 knots sustained), and integrated combat systems, rendering it less suited for blue-water operations against peer adversaries.[51] Against early People's Liberation Army Navy Type 039 Song-class boats, the Hai Lung offers marginally better acoustic performance from its Dutch Zwaardvis-derived hull design but remains outmatched by subsequent Song variants incorporating AIP for extended submerged patrols without compromise.[52] Overall, the absence of AIP curtails the class's tactical flexibility relative to modern conventional submarines, limiting its role to coastal defense rather than prolonged offensive missions.[48]

Fleet Composition

Hai Lung (SS-793)

Hai Lung (SS-793), the lead submarine of her class, was constructed by Wilton-Fijenoord in the Netherlands and launched on 6 October 1986.[1] Sea trials commenced in March 1987, after which the vessel was transported to Taiwan aboard a heavy-lift ship for final outfitting and commissioning into the Republic of China Navy on 9 October 1987.[4] Throughout her service, Hai Lung has primarily conducted patrol missions in the waters surrounding Taiwan, contributing to the navy's subsurface surveillance efforts.[1] On 26 March 2009, during a training exercise, Hai Lung's commanding officer was swept overboard by a 30-foot wave, marking a notable safety incident for the vessel; the captain was recovered but the event highlighted operational risks in rough seas.[53] As the initial boat selected for the life-extension program approved in 2016, Hai Lung entered refit to address aging systems and extend operational life by approximately 15 years, with upgrades including enhancements to propulsion, sensors, and weaponry integration supervised by the Chungshan Institute of Science and Technology.[2][1] The refit, prioritized due to her status as lead ship, was projected for completion between 2023 and 2024, enabling continued service into the post-2030 period contingent on successful implementation.[54][55]

Hai Hu (SS-794)

Hai Hu (SS-794), the second vessel of the Hai Lung class, was launched on 10 December 1986 at the Wilton-Fijenoord shipyard in the Netherlands.[56] Following sea trials and transport to Taiwan, she was commissioned into the Republic of China Navy on 16 December 1988, entering service shortly after her sister ship Hai Lung.[4] Throughout her operational history, Hai Hu has participated in routine patrols and naval exercises in the Taiwan Strait and surrounding waters, occasionally serving as the lead vessel in training scenarios to simulate asymmetric warfare tactics. In one documented exercise, she contributed to the sinking of the decommissioned USS Lyman K. Swenson as a target off Pingtung County, demonstrating torpedo proficiency. Unlike Hai Lung's earlier 2009 incident involving the loss of its captain to a large wave, Hai Hu experienced a distinct mishap on 21 December 2023, when three crew members were swept overboard by an unexpected wave during a submerged retrieval operation near the Bashi Channel; they were presumed drowned after extensive searches, with no disciplinary actions imposed on the command.[53][57][58] Hai Hu underwent a life-extension upgrade program initiated in 2016, which extended her service life by approximately 15 years through hull, mechanical, electrical, and sensor enhancements, with completion targeted for 2024; these modifications trailed those on Hai Lung and included integration trials for advanced sonar and electronic systems.[2][54] As of 2025, she remains fully operational within the ROC Navy's submarine flotilla, contributing to deterrence postures amid heightened regional tensions.[1]
