Montecristo (cigar)


Montecristo is a brand of premium handmade cigars produced in Cuba by Habanos S.A., the state-owned tobacco company responsible for exporting Cuban cigars.[1][2] Launched in 1935 in Havana by Alonso Menéndez and Pepe García after acquiring the Particulares factory, the brand was named after Alexandre Dumas' novel The Count of Monte Cristo, a story popular among cigar rollers who were read to during work.[3][4] Known for its balanced flavor profile featuring notes of cream, cedar, and earth from tobacco grown in the Vuelta Abajo region, Montecristo offers a range of vitolas including the iconic No. 2—a 6 1/8-inch by 52-ring gauge pirámide that has become a global standard for quality and is one of the most sought-after cigars.[3][5] The brand gained prominence post-World War II through European distribution and endures as a benchmark for Habanos products, though counterfeits remain a persistent issue due to its popularity.[1][3] Following the 1959 Cuban Revolution, the original factory was nationalized, leading Menéndez to establish non-Cuban versions elsewhere, but the authentic Cuban Montecristo continues under Habanos control.[3][5]

Origins and Early History

Founding and Naming

The Montecristo cigar brand was established in Havana, Cuba, in 1935 by Alonso Menéndez and José A. García, who formed the firm Menéndez, García y Cía.[6][7] Menéndez acquired the Particulares Factory, previously producing the Particulares and lesser-known Byron brands, with the intent to develop a new premium cigar line.[8][5] The partners capitalized on Cuba's established tobacco expertise and export markets, initially blending tobaccos from the Vuelta Abajo region to craft cigars aimed at international connoisseurs.[9] The brand's name derives from Alexandre Dumas's 1844 novel The Count of Monte Cristo, reflecting the reading habits of the factory's torcedores (cigar rollers), who were provided literature to occupy them during the meticulous hand-rolling process.[7] This naming convention followed a precedent set by the Romeo y Julieta brand, where cigars were titled after literary works favored by workers or managers, evoking themes of adventure and refinement to appeal to affluent smokers.[7] The choice aligned with early marketing strategies that positioned Montecristo as a sophisticated product, distinct from the factory's prior utilitarian output.[3]

Pre-Revolution Development

In 1935, Cuban cigar manufacturer Alonso Menéndez acquired the Particulares factory in Havana, partnering with José "Pepe" García to establish the Montecristo brand.[10][11] The name derived from Alexandre Dumas's novel The Count of Monte Cristo, which the partners reportedly read during factory breaks, reflecting a deliberate choice to evoke sophistication and adventure for the premium cigar market.[8] Initially produced at the rebranded facility, Montecristo cigars utilized high-quality Vuelta Abajo tobacco, emphasizing meticulous hand-rolling techniques honed in Cuba's established cigar tradition.[3] The early lineup consisted of five vitolas, designated No. 1 through No. 5, each varying in size and ring gauge to cater to diverse preferences while maintaining a consistent full-bodied profile from aged filler and binder leaves wrapped in Cameroon or Connecticut shade.[2] Production remained modest in scale, secondary to the Menéndez family's primary H. Upmann operations, yet the brand gained traction among European exporters and affluent smokers for its balanced strength, smooth draw, and aromatic complexity.[12] By the 1940s, innovations included the introduction of aluminum-tubed cigars to preserve freshness during transatlantic shipping, enhancing market appeal without altering core blends.[2] Pre-revolution expansion focused on refining consistency through selected torcedores (rollers) at the Havana facility, with output increasing gradually to meet demand from diplomatic circles and luxury retailers, though annual production stayed below major marques like Romeo y Julieta.[11] No significant mechanization occurred, preserving artisanal methods amid Cuba's tobacco economy, which relied on state-regulated leaf auctions and family-owned estates.[13] This period solidified Montecristo's reputation for reliability, setting the stage for postwar growth until nationalization in 1960.[14]

Nationalization and Brand Division

Cuban Government Seizure

Following the Cuban Revolution of 1959, the administration of Fidel Castro pursued the nationalization of private industries, including the tobacco sector, as part of broader economic reforms aimed at consolidating state control. The Montecristo brand, originally established in 1935 by Alonso Menéndez and Pepe García at a facility in Havana that later incorporated the H. Upmann factory in 1937, became subject to these policies. By early 1960, amid escalating tensions, Menéndez and other key figures associated with the brand had fled Cuba to escape the revolutionary government's expropriations.[8][5] On September 15, 1960, Cuban military forces, acting under direct orders from Castro, seized control of 16 major cigar factories in a single operation, including the H. Upmann facility where Montecristo production was centered. At approximately 5:30 p.m., soldiers entered the premises, confiscating the Montecristo brand name, inventory, machinery, and all related assets without compensation to the original owners, who had already departed. This action marked the effective end of private ownership for Montecristo in Cuba, transferring it to state oversight as part of the nationalization of the entire premium cigar industry.[14][15][16] The seizure prompted the permanent exile of Cuban cigar entrepreneurs, including Menéndez, who resettled operations abroad using the Montecristo trademark where legally permissible outside Cuba. In Cuba, production of the brand persisted under government management, initially through Cubatabaco and later via Habanos S.A., a state entity formed in 1994 as a joint venture with international partners, though the original trademarks remained contested internationally due to the lack of compensation or legal transfer. This division solidified the bifurcation of Montecristo lines, with Cuban variants controlled by the regime and non-Cuban versions emerging from expatriate efforts.[17][12][18]

Establishment of Non-Cuban Lines

Following the nationalization of Cuban tobacco assets in 1960, original Montecristo proprietors Alonso Menéndez and José García fled the island and established Compañía Insular Tabacalera S.A. in Las Palmas, Canary Islands, in 1961. There, they resumed premium cigar production using Spanish and imported tobaccos to replicate pre-revolution Cuban profiles, but trademark assertions by the Cuban state exporter Cubatabaco compelled initial marketing under the variant name Montecruz rather than Montecristo.[19][3] To circumvent U.S. import restrictions imposed by the 1962 trade embargo—which barred Cuban-made goods—the Menéndez interests shifted operations to the Dominican Republic in the mid-1970s. Production of explicitly branded Montecristo cigars commenced at the Tabacalera de García factory in La Romana, employing Dominican filler and binder tobaccos often derived from Cuban-seed varietals, paired with Cameroon or Connecticut wrappers to evoke the marque's heritage smoothness and complexity. This facility, operational since the early 1970s, enabled scalable output for embargo-affected markets, with annual volumes reaching millions of units by the 1980s.[20][21] Corporate evolution solidified the line's independence: Consolidated Cigar Corporation acquired Canary Islands assets in 1972 and integrated Dominican manufacturing, later merging into Altadis (now under Imperial Brands) by 1999. Altadis expanded Montecristo variants, incorporating Nicaraguan tobaccos from the 2000s onward at facilities like Tabacalera A.J. Fernández, while retaining core Dominican blends; by 2025, the non-Cuban portfolio includes over 20 vitolas, emphasizing aged fillers for notes of cedar, cream, and spice.[22][23] This bifurcation from Cuban counterparts stemmed from jurisdictional trademark separations—Cuban claims unenforceable in the U.S. due to embargo non-recognition—allowing parallel brands without Cuban tobacco or oversight.[24]

Production Processes

Cuban Manufacturing

Montecristo cigars are manufactured exclusively in Cuba by Habanos S.A., utilizing tobacco cultivated in the Vuelta Abajo region of Pinar del Río province, renowned for producing the world's finest cigar tobacco due to its unique microclimate of red clay soils, high humidity, and moderate rainfall.[25][1] The production adheres to traditional methods, employing tripa larga, totalmente a mano construction, where long filler leaves are hand-rolled without short scraps, ensuring consistent burn and flavor draw.[1] Tobacco farming follows a single annual crop cycle spanning approximately nine months, with seeds planted in nurseries before transplanting to fields; wrapper leaves are shade-grown under cheesecloth tents to promote elasticity and vein minimization, while sun-grown leaves for filler and binder develop greater strength and robustness.[26] Post-harvest, leaves undergo curing in ventilated barns for 25 to 50 days depending on type, followed by bulk fermentation in pilones (piles) to metabolize nicotine and reduce ammonia content, a process lasting up to two months and repeated if necessary for optimal flavor development.[27] Selected leaves are then moistened, stripped from veins, and meticulously sorted by color, texture, strength, and aroma by expert clasificadores; Montecristo blends prioritize Vuelta Abajo tobaccos for all components—corojo or criollo hybrids for wrappers, robust fillers from viso and seco leaves, and binders for structural integrity—aged for periods ranging from months to years to enhance complexity.[26][6] Due to Montecristo's high production volume as one of Cuba's top-selling premium brands, cigars are rolled across multiple state-owned factories, primarily in Havana, including the H. Upmann facility designated as the "mother factory" for the brand, alongside others like Partagás, La Corona, and El Laguito for specific vitolas.[26] Skilled torcedores bunch filler leaves into a core, apply the binder, wrap with the capa (wrapper), and shape using wooden molds pressed under hydraulic weights for several hours, followed by hand-trimming and cap application with a vegetable glue derived from taro root.[27] Each cigar passes through rigorous quality control, including draw tests via specialized machines, visual inspections for blemishes, and categorization into first (export), second, and third qualities, with only first-quality Montecristos receiving the brand's triple-cap band and cedar aging in controlled humidity warehouses for up to several years before release.[27][6] This labor-intensive process, reliant on artisanal expertise amid Cuba's centralized production model, contributes to output fluctuations influenced by harvest yields and embargo-related supply constraints, though quality has reportedly stabilized in recent years through refined blending techniques.[6]

Non-Cuban Manufacturing

Following the nationalization of Cuban cigar factories in 1960, exiled members of the founding Menéndez family initiated non-Cuban production under variant branding before securing rights to the Montecristo name. In 1961, Benjamin Menéndez established Compañía Insular Tabacalera S.A. in Las Palmas, Canary Islands, producing Montecruz cigars as a close facsimile to Montecristo, utilizing similar blends and packaging to serve markets restricted from Cuban imports.[21] A 1972 U.S. court ruling in Menéndez v. Faber, Coe & Gregg, Inc. affirmed trademark rights for pre-revolution Cuban brands held by exiles, enabling relocation and expansion of production to the Dominican Republic by the 1970s, where Montecruz became a bestseller with Dominican-grown tobaccos and Cameroon wrappers.[21] Licensed Montecristo production for the U.S. market commenced in 1990 under Consolidated Cigar Corporation (later acquired by Altadis in 2000), which obtained rights from Cuban Cigar Brands N.V., a entity formed by the Menéndez interests.[21] Initial manufacturing occurred at factories in the Dominican Republic, focusing on blends with Dominican filler tobaccos derived from Cuban seeds, Dominican binders, and wrappers such as Connecticut shade or Ecuadorian Habano for milder profiles suited to American preferences.[3] Altadis USA, a subsidiary of Imperial Brands, now oversees primary non-Cuban Montecristo output at the Tabacalera de García factory in La Romana, Dominican Republic, where hand-rolling persists using aged Dominican long-filler tobaccos selected for consistency and fermentation processes akin to pre-revolution standards.[3] This facility produces core lines like the Montecristo Classic and Original series, emphasizing triple-cap construction and controlled humidity aging to achieve smooth draws and flavors of nuts, cream, and subtle spice.[28] Certain premium extensions, including the Montecristo Nicaragua Series (introduced around 2012) and the 2024 Montecristo M with its 20-year-aged wrapper, are crafted in Estelí, Nicaragua, by A.J. Fernández Cigar Company, incorporating Nicaraguan Jalapa Valley fillers and binders for fuller-bodied expressions with notes of earth and pepper.[29][30] These operations prioritize volume for embargo-affected markets, yielding millions of units annually with quality controls like electronic draw resistance testing, though critics note occasional inconsistencies in wrapper uniformity compared to Cuban counterparts due to differing terroir and seed adaptations.[21] Non-Cuban Montecristos comprise over a dozen vitolas, from the mild No. 2 equivalent to robustos, all boxed in yellow packaging to evoke heritage while complying with international licensing stipulations that prohibit direct Cuban tobacco use.[3]

Varieties and Product Lines

Core Cuban Vitolas

The core Cuban vitolas of Montecristo cigars consist of the original numbered series, Nos. 1 through 5, which form the foundational lineup established after the brand's inception in 1935 and remain in continuous production by Habanos S.A. using tobacco from the Vuelta Abajo region. These vitolas embody the brand's signature medium-to-full-bodied profile, characterized by a balanced blend of complexity, refinement, and aroma, with cedar notes, creaminess, and subtle spice predominating across sizes.[25][31] The series sets the benchmark for the brand, with No. 4 recognized as the world's best-selling individual handmade Cuban cigar due to its accessible size and flavor depth.[32]

Non-Cuban and Special Releases

Following the nationalization of Cuban cigar factories after the 1959 revolution, the original Montecristo proprietors, including members of the Menéndez family, relocated production outside Cuba, initially producing similar cigars under the Montecruz name in the Canary Islands starting in 1961.[10] In 1990, Consolidated Cigar Corporation—later acquired by Altadis U.S.A.—secured rights to manufacture Montecristo cigars for the U.S. market, establishing primary production in the Dominican Republic at factories such as Tabacalera de García.[10] [28] These non-Cuban Montecristos utilize Dominican and Nicaraguan tobaccos, differing in blend composition and flavor profile from their Cuban counterparts while aiming to evoke the brand's legacy heritage.[45] Altadis produces several core non-Cuban series, including the Original Series (often labeled "Yellow"), which features aged Dominican fillers and binder with a Connecticut Shade wrapper for a mild-to-medium body and notes of cream, nuts, and cedar.[46] The White Series employs a Nicaraguan binder and a mix of Dominican and Nicaraguan long-filler tobaccos wrapped in Ecuadorian Connecticut, delivering a lighter, creamy smoke with subtle spice.[47] Additional lines like the Epic, crafted by the Grupo de Maestros at Tabacalera de García, incorporate Dominican and Nicaraguan tobaccos for fuller-bodied expressions with earth and leather tones.[48] The Nicaragua Series, a puro using exclusively Nicaraguan tobacco, was introduced as a medium-full option ranked among top cigars in 2018 for its balanced wood, pepper, and chocolate profile.[10] Special non-Cuban releases include the Montecristo 1935 Anniversary series, launched to mark the brand's 1935 founding, with production at Tabacalera A.J. Fernández in Nicaragua using 100% Nicaraguan tobacco for a medium-to-full-bodied puro featuring flavors of wood, coffee, cinnamon, earth, and spice.[49] [50] Variants such as the Edición Diamante and Edición Doble Diamante offer box-pressed formats with maduro wrappers, emphasizing creaminess, cinnamon, and earthiness, and earning ratings up to 95 points from industry reviewers.[51] [52] These limited editions highlight Altadis's experimentation with Nicaraguan estates for bolder, region-specific expressions distinct from standard lines.[53]

Market Dynamics and Reception

Global Popularity and Sales

The Montecristo brand maintains substantial global popularity as one of the premier premium cigar lines, with its Cuban variants produced by Habanos S.A. driving significant demand in markets across Europe, Asia, and Latin America. The Montecristo No. 4 vitola, a petit corona measuring 5 1/8 inches by 42 ring gauge, has been recognized as the best-selling Cuban cigar size for decades, owing to its balanced medium-bodied profile and consistent construction.[1] This vitola's prominence underscores the brand's appeal to both novice and experienced smokers seeking a refined, creamy smoke with notes of cedar, leather, and spice. Habanos S.A., which handles Cuban Montecristo production and distribution, reported record annual revenues of $827 million in 2024, reflecting a 14.7% increase from $721 million in 2023, fueled by rising demand in key markets like China—the company's largest outlet—and Spain.[54] [55] While exact brand-specific figures are not publicly broken out, Montecristo consistently ranks among Habanos' top performers, with retailers and industry observers estimating it accounts for approximately 25% of the company's total cigar sales volume annually.[32] The brand's No. 2 piramide and Edmundo robusto formats also feature prominently in global sales, contributing to Montecristo's status as a volume leader outside the U.S. embargo-restricted market. Non-Cuban Montecristo lines, manufactured primarily in the Dominican Republic by Altadis U.S.A., have carved out a strong presence in North America and select international outlets, offering comparable vitolas adapted to non-Cuban tobaccos for broader accessibility. These variants benefit from the brand's name recognition but operate in a fragmented premium cigar sector valued at over $24 billion worldwide in 2025 projections, where Cuban originals retain prestige-driven dominance in legal export markets.[56] Overall, Montecristo's sales resilience amid global cigar volume fluctuations—such as a 7% decline in traditional sales outside China in 2024—highlights its entrenched consumer loyalty and marketing through exclusive launches like the Línea Open series.[57]

Critical Evaluations and Comparisons

Montecristo cigars, particularly the Cuban-produced variants, have received high acclaim in blind tastings for their balance of flavor complexity, construction, and burn quality. The Montecristo No. 2, a piramide vitola measuring 6 1/8 inches by 52 ring gauge, earned a record 96-point score from Cigar Aficionado in a 2013 blind taste test, marking it as Cigar of the Year and the highest rating ever for that publication's evaluation of the line; tasters noted notes of earth, coffee, and cream with a refined strength progression.[58] Other vitolas, such as the No. 4 petit corona, have been praised for floral, herbal, and black tea undertones despite a rustic appearance, scoring consistently in the high 80s to low 90s range in professional reviews.[59] The No. 3 corona similarly delivers almond, butter, and creamy finishes, contributing to the brand's reputation for approachable yet nuanced profiles suitable for both novices and experts.[60] In comparisons among Cuban brands, Montecristo is often evaluated as more versatile and smoother than Cohiba, which tends toward greater intensity and spice; for instance, in lancero formats, Montecristo offerings provide comparable elegance at a lower price point, with experts favoring the former for everyday smoking over Cohiba's premium positioning.[61] Against Partagás or H. Upmann, Montecristo's darker wrapper leaves yield earthier, fuller-bodied expressions, though Diplomaticos No. 2 may edge it in wrapper uniformity due to selective leaf allocation in Cuban factories.[62] Recent production has shown quality stabilization, with Cigar Aficionado observers noting improvements in consistency across Habanos lines, including Montecristo, countering earlier critiques of variability in handmade construction.[63] Non-Cuban Montecristo lines, produced primarily in the Dominican Republic by Altadis U.S.A., differ markedly in tobacco sourcing and profile, emphasizing milder, creamier notes from Connecticut-seed wrappers rather than Cuban Vuelta Abajo leaf; these are not direct replicas but adaptations, with lines like Montecristo White scoring for tangy-to-creamy transitions but lacking the terroir-driven depth attributed to originals.[64][65] Critics assess them as reliable alternatives under U.S. trade restrictions, though empirical tastings reveal Cuban versions superior in perceived authenticity and layered earthiness due to unique soil and fermentation processes, without inherent quality deficits in non-Cuban builds.[66] Overall, Montecristo's enduring appeal stems from empirical strengths in blind-rated balance, positioning it as a benchmark for Cuban premium cigars amid debates over production fidelity.[67]

Controversies and Challenges

Counterfeiting Prevalence

Counterfeiting of Montecristo cigars is a persistent and widespread issue, driven by the brand's status as one of the most iconic and demanded Cuban premium cigars, which creates strong incentives for illicit replication. Industry analyses indicate that fake Montecristos are produced in large volumes, often using substandard tobacco and mimicking packaging to infiltrate both tourist markets and online sales channels. Phony production occurs extensively within Cuba itself, complicating enforcement efforts by authorities like Habanos S.A., the official exporter, despite their implementation of serial number verification systems since the early 2000s.[68][69] Prevalence is particularly acute in regions with high cigar tourism or restricted official access, such as the Caribbean and North America. For example, in Jamaica, up to 95% of cigars available for sale, including those labeled as Montecristo, have been estimated as counterfeit, with many originating from unauthorized Cuban sources. In the United States, where U.S. trade embargoes prohibit legal imports of Cuban cigars, retailers and experts assert that the vast majority—potentially over 90%—of purported Cuban Montecristos circulating in the market are fakes, often sourced from Dominican Republic or Chinese manufacturing hubs using inferior blends. These figures underscore a broader pattern where counterfeits account for millions of dollars in annual economic losses to legitimate producers, eroding consumer trust and brand integrity.[70][71][72] The problem extends to Europe and Asia, where even authorized distributors report seizures of fake shipments mimicking Montecristo vitolas like the No. 2, often sold at 50-70% below market prices to lure unsuspecting buyers. Habanos has documented ongoing raids and collaborations with Interpol, yet the decentralized nature of small-scale forgery—frequently in rural Cuban workshops or overseas factories—sustains high circulation rates, with no comprehensive global quantification available but anecdotal evidence from seizures suggesting counterfeits comprise 20-50% of total Montecristo-labeled products in informal markets. This prevalence not only dilutes the premium experience associated with authentic Montecristos but also poses health risks from unregulated tobacco and additives in fakes.[73][68]

Quality Fluctuations and Debates

The quality of Cuban Montecristo cigars has fluctuated notably since the brand's post-revolutionary nationalization, with empirical tastings indicating a peak in the early 1990s followed by a decline through the early 2000s. Blind evaluations by Cigar Aficionado magazine documented average score drops for top Cuban vitolas, including Montecristo Robustos, which exhibited poor construction such as tight draws and harsh flavors in samples from the late 1990s. This downturn, spanning approximately 1992 to 2001, contrasted with higher ratings in the magazine's initial years of systematic testing.[74] Causal factors included a mid-1990s global cigar boom that spurred overproduction under Habanos S.A., leading to the use of immature, unaged tobacco, rushed curing and fermentation processes, and deployment of inexperienced rollers to meet quotas. Tobacco shortages, particularly of flavorful ligero filler and high-quality wrappers, compounded these issues, exacerbated by adverse weather, experimental hybrid seeds prone to disease, and insufficient materia prima reserves. Economic pressures after the Soviet Union's collapse in 1991 intensified output demands without proportional improvements in agricultural or manufacturing rigor.[74][75] Specific to Montecristo lines, such as the No. 2 and No. 5, aficionados have reported diminished complexity and body in post-2000 releases compared to earlier vintages, with Montecristo No. 5s showing a shift to more uniform, less nuanced medium profiles lacking the richness of aged counterparts. Construction inconsistencies, including tight draws and wrapper defects, persist as common critiques, attributed in part to production across multiple factories like Partagás and Romeo y Julieta, which introduces variability.[75][67] Debates among experts and smokers center on whether quality has stabilized or recovered since Habanos' post-2000 reforms, including production cuts and partnerships like Altadis' oversight, with some tastings in 2012 suggesting improvement over the prior decade. However, ongoing reports of flavor shifts—such as noted changes around 2018 for Montecristo No. 4—and persistent quality control lapses fuel skepticism, prompting comparisons to more consistent non-Cuban alternatives amid Cuba's structural agricultural challenges.[74][76][77]

Effects of Trade Restrictions

The United States embargo against Cuba, enacted in 1962 under the Trading with the Enemy Act and codified in subsequent legislation like the Cuban Democracy Act of 1992, prohibits the commercial importation of Cuban cigars, including Montecristo, into the US market.[78][79] This restriction has denied Habanos S.A., the state-owned entity producing Montecristo cigars, access to what was historically a major consumer base, resulting in estimated annual revenue losses for the Cuban tobacco industry in the tens of millions of dollars prior to market redirection.[80] Pre-embargo, the US accounted for a substantial portion of Cuban cigar exports; post-1962, sales pivoted toward Western Europe, Canada, and Asia, where Montecristo remains a flagship brand driving Habanos' global revenue, which exceeded $500 million in some years despite the exclusion.[78][81] Temporary policy shifts have modulated personal importation allowances but not commercial trade. Under the Obama administration's 2014-2016 normalization efforts, US travelers could import up to $100 worth of Cuban tobacco products for personal use, briefly increasing gray-market inflows of Montecristo cigars via tourism.[82] However, the Trump administration reversed this in 2017, and further restrictions effective September 24, 2020, barred authorized US travelers from returning with any Cuban tobacco or alcohol, reinstating full prohibition and curtailing even limited personal access as of 2025.[83][84] These fluctuations have fostered a persistent US black market for Cuban Montecristo, where smuggled or pre-embargo stock commands premiums of 2-5 times retail prices in legal markets, exacerbating counterfeiting risks but sustaining underground demand estimated at millions of units annually.[85] The embargo has indirectly bolstered non-Cuban Montecristo production, licensed under the original trademark acquired by US firms before 1960, primarily in the Dominican Republic by manufacturers like Altadis U.S.A. With Cuban originals barred, these alternatives—using non-Cuban tobacco—dominate the US premium cigar segment, capturing market share that authentic Montecristos might otherwise claim, potentially up to 70-90 million additional units yearly for Habanos if restrictions lifted.[86][87][81] Cuban industry officials argue the policy stifles economic diversification and investment in tobacco infrastructure, contributing to broader GDP drags from sanctions, though Habanos has adapted by emphasizing exclusivity in permitted markets, where Montecristo vitolas like the No. 2 remain top sellers.[88] Conversely, US producers benefit from shielded competition, avoiding direct rivalry with Cuba's Vuelta Abajo tobacco, though they face separate challenges like tobacco import tariffs unrelated to the embargo.[89][90]
