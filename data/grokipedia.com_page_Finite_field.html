Finite field

In mathematics, a finite field or Galois field (so-named in honor of Évariste Galois) is a field that has a finite number of elements. As with any field, a finite field is a set on which the operations of multiplication, addition, subtraction and division are defined and satisfy certain basic rules. The most common examples of finite fields are the integers mod ppp when ppp is a prime number. The order of a finite field is its number of elements, which is either a prime number or a prime power. For every prime number ppp and every positive integer kkk there are fields of order pkp^kpk. All finite fields of a given order are isomorphic. Finite fields are fundamental in a number of areas of mathematics and computer science, including number theory, algebraic geometry, Galois theory, finite geometry, cryptography and coding theory.

Properties

A finite field is a field that is a finite set; this means that it has a finite number of elements on which multiplication, addition, subtraction and division (excluding division by zero) are defined and satisfy the field axioms. The number of elements of a finite field is called its order or, sometimes, its size. A finite field of order qqq exists if and only if qqq is a prime power pkp^kpk (where ppp is a prime number and kkk is a positive integer). In a field of order pkp^kpk, adding ppp copies of any element always results in zero; that is, the characteristic of the field is ppp. For q=pkq = p^kq=pk, all fields of order qqq are isomorphic (see § Existence and uniqueness below). Moreover, a field cannot contain two different finite subfields with the same order. One may therefore identify all finite fields with the same order, and they are unambiguously denoted Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​, Fq\mathbf{F}_qFq​ or GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q), where the letters GF stand for "Galois field". In a finite field of order qqq, the polynomial Xq−XX^q - XXq−X has all qqq elements of the finite field as roots. The non-zero elements of a finite field form a multiplicative group. This group is cyclic, so all non-zero elements can be expressed as powers of a single element called a primitive element of the field. (In general there will be several primitive elements for a given field.) The simplest examples of finite fields are the fields of prime order: for each prime number ppp, the prime field of order ppp may be constructed as the integers modulo ppp, Z/pZ\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}Z/pZ. The elements of the prime field of order ppp may be represented by integers in the range 0,…,p−10, \ldots, p-10,…,p−1. The sum, the difference and the product are the remainder of the division by ppp of the result of the corresponding integer operation. The multiplicative inverse of an element may be computed by using the extended Euclidean algorithm (see Extended Euclidean algorithm § Modular integers). Let FFF be a finite field. For any element xxx in FFF and any integer nnn, denote by n⋅xn \cdot xn⋅x the sum of nnn copies of xxx. The least positive nnn such that n⋅1=0n \cdot 1 = 0n⋅1=0 is the characteristic ppp of the field. This allows defining a multiplication (k,x)↦k⋅x(k, x) \mapsto k \cdot x(k,x)↦k⋅x of an element kkk of GF(p)\mathrm{GF}(p)GF(p) by an element xxx of FFF by choosing an integer representative for kkk. The identity (x+y)p=xp+yp(x + y)^p = x^p + y^p(x+y)p=xp+yp (sometimes called the freshman's dream) is true in a field of characteristic ppp. This follows from the binomial theorem, as each binomial coefficient of the expansion of (x+y)p(x + y)^p(x+y)p, except the first and the last, is a multiple of ppp.: 548 By Fermat's little theorem, if ppp is a prime number and xxx is in the field GF(p)\mathrm{GF}(p)GF(p) then xp=xx^p = xxp=x. To use a piece of jargon, finite fields are perfect. A more general algebraic structure that satisfies all the other axioms of a field, but whose multiplication is not required to be commutative, is called a division ring (or sometimes skew field). By Wedderburn's little theorem, any finite division ring is commutative, and hence is a finite field.

Existence and uniqueness

Let q=pnq = p^nq=pn be a prime power, and FFF be the splitting field of the polynomial P=Xq−XP = X^q - XP=Xq−X over the prime field GF(p)\mathrm{GF}(p)GF(p). This means that FFF is a finite field of lowest order, in which PPP has qqq distinct roots (the formal derivative of PPP is P′=−1P' = -1P′=−1, implying that gcd⁡(P,P′)=1\gcd(P, P') = 1gcd(P,P′)=1, which in general implies that the splitting field is a separable extension of the original). The above identity shows that the sum and the product of two roots of PPP are roots of PPP, as well as the multiplicative inverse of a root of PPP. In other words, the roots of PPP form a field of order qqq, which is equal to FFF by the minimality of the splitting field. The uniqueness up to isomorphism of splitting fields implies thus that all fields of order qqq are isomorphic. Also, if a field FFF has a field of order q=pkq = p^kq=pk as a subfield, its elements are the qqq roots of Xq−XX^q - XXq−X, and FFF cannot contain another subfield of order qqq. In summary, we have the following classification theorem first proved in 1893 by E. H. Moore:
The order of a finite field is a prime power. For every prime power qqq there are fields of order qqq, and they are all isomorphic. In these fields, every element satisfies xq=xx^q = xxq=x, and the polynomial Xq−XX^q - XXq−X factors as Xq−X=∏a∈F(X−a)X^q - X = \prod_{a \in F} (X - a)Xq−X=∏a∈F​(X−a).
It follows that GF(pn)\mathrm{GF}(p^n)GF(pn) contains a subfield isomorphic to GF(pm)\mathrm{GF}(p^m)GF(pm) if and only if mmm is a divisor of nnn; in that case, this subfield is unique. In fact, the polynomial Xpm−XX^{p^m} - XXpm−X divides Xpn−XX^{p^n} - XXpn−X if and only if mmm is a divisor of nnn.

Except in the construction of GF(4)\mathrm{GF}(4)GF(4), there are several possible choices for PPP, which produce isomorphic results. However, for some fields, typically in characteristic 222, irreducible polynomials of the form Xn+aX+bX^n + a X + bXn+aX+b may not exist. Multiplicative structure

The set of non-zero elements in GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q) is an abelian group under the multiplication, of order q−1q - 1q−1. By Lagrange's theorem, there exists a divisor kkk of q−1q - 1q−1 such that xk=1x^k = 1xk=1 for every non-zero xxx in GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q). As the equation xk=1x^k = 1xk=1 has at most kkk solutions in any field, q−1q - 1q−1 is the lowest possible value for kkk. The structure theorem of finite abelian groups implies that this multiplicative group is cyclic, that is, all non-zero elements are powers of a single element. In summary: The multiplicative group of the non-zero elements in GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q) is cyclic, i.e., there exists an element aaa, such that the q−1q - 1q−1 non-zero elements of GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q) are a,a2,…,aq−2,aq−1=1a, a^2, \ldots, a^{q-2}, a^{q-1} = 1a,a2,…,aq−2,aq−1=1. Such an element aaa is called a primitive element of GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q). Unless q=2,3q = 2, 3q=2,3, the primitive element is not unique. The number of primitive elements is ϕ(q−1)\phi(q - 1)ϕ(q−1) where ϕ\phiϕ is Euler's totient function. The result above implies that xq=xx^q = xxq=x for every xxx in GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q). The particular case where qqq is prime is Fermat's little theorem.

Discrete logarithm

If aaa is a primitive element in GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q), then for any non-zero element xxx in FFF, there is a unique integer nnn with 0≤n≤q−20 \leq n \leq q - 20≤n≤q−2 such that x=anx = a^nx=an. This integer nnn is called the discrete logarithm of xxx to the base aaa. While ana^nan can be computed very quickly, for example using exponentiation by squaring, there is no known efficient algorithm for computing the inverse operation, the discrete logarithm. This has been used in various cryptographic protocols, see Discrete logarithm for details. When the nonzero elements of GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q) are represented by their discrete logarithms, multiplication and division are easy, as they reduce to addition and subtraction modulo q−1q - 1q−1. However, addition amounts to computing the discrete logarithm of am+ana^m + a^nam+an. The identity am+an=an(am−n+1)a^m + a^n = a^n (a^{m-n} + 1)am+an=an(am−n+1) allows one to solve this problem by constructing the table of the discrete logarithms of an+1a^n + 1an+1, called Zech's logarithms, for n=0,…,q−2n = 0, \ldots, q - 2n=0,…,q−2 (it is convenient to define the discrete logarithm of zero as being −∞-\infty−∞). Zech's logarithms are useful for large computations, such as linear algebra over medium-sized fields, that is, fields that are sufficiently large for making natural algorithms inefficient, but not too large, as one has to pre-compute a table of the same size as the order of the field.

When computing Brauer characters, one uses the map αk↦exp⁡(2πik/(q−1))\alpha^k \mapsto \exp(2 \pi i k / (q - 1))αk↦exp(2πik/(q−1)) to map eigenvalues of a representation matrix to the complex numbers. Under this mapping, the base subfield GF(p)\mathrm{GF}(p)GF(p) consists of evenly spaced points around the unit circle (omitting zero).

Example: GF(64)

The field GF(64)\mathrm{GF}(64)GF(64) has several interesting properties that smaller fields do not share: it has two subfields such that neither is contained in the other; not all generators (elements with minimal polynomial of degree 6 over GF(2)\mathrm{GF}(2)GF(2)) are primitive elements; and the primitive elements are not all conjugate under the Galois group. As 2 and 3 are coprime, the intersection of GF(4) and GF(8) in GF(64) is the prime field GF(2). The union of GF(4) and GF(8) has thus 10 elements. Frobenius automorphism and Galois theory

In this section, ppp is a prime number, and q=pnq = p^nq=pn is a power of ppp. In GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q), the identity (x+y)p=xp+yp(x + y)_p = x_p + y_p(x+y)p​=xp​+yp​ implies that the map φ:x↦xp\varphi : x \mapsto x^pφ:x↦xp is a GF(p)\mathrm{GF}(p)GF(p)-linear endomorphism and a field automorphism of GF(q)\mathrm{GF}(q)GF(q), which fixes every element of the subfield GF(p)\mathrm{GF}(p)GF(p). Polynomial factorization

If FFF is a finite field, a non-constant monic polynomial with coefficients in FFF is irreducible over FFF, if it is not the product of two non-constant monic polynomials, with coefficients in FFF. As every polynomial ring over a field is a unique factorization domain, every monic polynomial over a finite field may be factored in a unique way (up to the order of the factors) into a product of irreducible monic polynomials. There are efficient algorithms for testing polynomial irreducibility and factoring polynomials over finite fields. They are a key step for factoring polynomials over the integers or the rational numbers. At least for this reason, every computer algebra system has functions for factoring polynomials over finite fields, or, at least, over finite prime fields.

Irreducible polynomials of a given degree

The polynomial Xq−XX^q - XXq−X factors into linear factors over a field of order qqq. More precisely, this polynomial is the product of all monic polynomials of degree one over a field of order qqq. This implies that, if q=pnq = p^nq=pn then Xq−XX^q - XXq−X is the product of all monic irreducible polynomials over GF(ppp), whose degree divides nnn. In fact, if PPP is an irreducible factor over GF(ppp) of Xq−XX^q - XXq−X, its degree divides nnn, as its splitting field is contained in GF(pnp^npn). Conversely, if PPP is an irreducible monic polynomial over GF(ppp) of degree ddd dividing nnn, it defines a field extension of degree ddd, which is contained in GF(pnp^npn), and all roots of PPP belong to GF(pnp^npn), and are roots of Xq−XX^q - XXq−X; thus PPP divides Xq−XX^q - XXq−X. As Xq−XX^q - XXq−X does not have any multiple factor, it is thus the product of all the irreducible monic polynomials that divide it. This property is used to compute the product of the irreducible factors of each degree of polynomials over GF(ppp); see Distinct degree factorization.

Applications

In cryptography, the difficulty of the discrete logarithm problem in a finite field or in an elliptic curve over a finite field is the basis of several widely used protocols, such as the Diffie–Hellman protocol. For example, as of 2025, a secure internet connection to Wikipedia involves the elliptic curve Diffie–Hellman protocol (ECDHE) over elliptic curves defined on large finite fields. In coding theory, many codes are constructed as subspaces of vector spaces over finite fields. Finite fields are used by many error correction codes, such as Reed–Solomon error correction code or BCH code. The finite field almost always has characteristic of 2, since computer data is stored in binary. For example, a byte of data can be interpreted as an element of GF( 282^828 ). One exception is PDF417 bar code, which is GF(929). Some CPUs have special instructions that can be useful for finite fields of characteristic 2, generally variations of carry-less product. Finite fields are widely used in number theory, as many problems over the integers may be solved by reducing them modulo one or several prime numbers. For example, the fastest known algorithms for polynomial factorization and linear algebra over the field of rational numbers proceed by reduction modulo one or several primes, and then reconstruction of the solution by using Chinese remainder theorem, Hensel lifting or the LLL algorithm. Similarly many theoretical problems in number theory can be solved by considering their reductions modulo some or all prime numbers. See, for example, Hasse principle. Many recent developments of algebraic geometry were motivated by the need to enlarge the power of these modular methods. Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem is an example of a deep result involving many mathematical tools, including finite fields. The Weil conjectures concern the number of points on algebraic varieties over finite fields and the theory has many applications including exponential and character sum estimates. Finite fields have widespread application in combinatorics, two well known examples being the definition of Paley Graphs and the related construction for Hadamard Matrices. In arithmetic combinatorics finite fields and finite field models are used extensively, such as in Szemerédi's theorem on arithmetic progressions.

Extensions

Wedderburn's little theorem

A division ring is a generalization of field. Division rings are not assumed to be commutative. There are no non-commutative finite division rings: Wedderburn's little theorem states that all finite division rings are commutative, and hence are finite fields. This result holds even if we relax the associativity axiom to alternativity, that is, all finite alternative division rings are finite fields, by the Artin–Zorn theorem.

Algebraic closure

Quasi-algebraic closure

Although finite fields are not algebraically closed, they are quasi-algebraically closed, which means that every homogeneous polynomial over a finite field has a non-trivial zero whose components are in the field if the number of its variables is more than its degree. This was a conjecture of Artin and Dickson proved by Chevalley (see Chevalley–Warning theorem).
