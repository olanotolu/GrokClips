Howard Hendricks

Howard George Hendricks (April 5, 1924 – February 20, 2013) was an American Christian educator and author who served as a professor at Dallas Theological Seminary for over sixty years.[1][2] Born into a broken home, Hendricks earned a bachelor's degree from Wheaton College in 1946 and a Master of Theology from Dallas Theological Seminary in 1950, later receiving an honorary Doctor of Divinity from Wheaton in 1967.[2][3] Affectionately known as "Prof," he taught Bible exposition, hermeneutics, and Christian education to more than ten thousand students, emphasizing inductive study methods that prioritize observation, interpretation, and application of Scripture.[1][4] His practical approach to teaching and discipleship influenced generations of evangelical leaders, including pastors, authors, and seminary presidents, through classroom instruction, seminars, and speaking engagements with groups like Promise Keepers.[1][3] Hendricks authored books such as Living by the Book and Teaching to Change Lives, which promoted transformative Bible engagement and effective communication of biblical truths.[4][3]

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Family Background

Howard George Hendricks was born on April 5, 1924, in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, to parents George and Celia (Dickens) Hendricks.[3][5] His parents separated around the time of his birth, with Hendricks later recalling, "My parents separated when I came along. I split the family," reflecting the fractured family environment into which he was born.[1][6] Following the separation, Hendricks was placed in the care of his paternal grandparents, Jake and Cora Hendricks, in Philadelphia, where he was raised primarily by his grandmother.[4][3] This arrangement stemmed from his parents' inability to provide a stable home, leaving him in a broken household without direct parental involvement during his formative years.[1] He described his early childhood as one of floundering amid family instability, and during elementary school, he characterized himself as a troublemaker, indicative of behavioral challenges in that period.[7][8]

Conversion and Early Influences

Hendricks was raised in a broken home, with his parents separating before his birth on April 5, 1924, in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania; he was primarily cared for by his paternal grandparents, Jake and Cora Hendricks.[4] His grandmother Cora provided a nurturing environment infused with Christian faith, offering stability and early exposure to religious principles amid family instability.[4] A pivotal early influence came from a man named Walt, associated with a nearby church, who engaged Hendricks and a group of boys through Sunday school activities and informal games like marbles; this outreach led 12 out of 13 boys in the group, including Hendricks, toward Christian commitment and eventually into vocational ministry.[4] In sixth grade, his teacher Miss Noe played a transformative role by confronting rumors about him with the words, “I’ve heard a lot about you. But I don’t believe a word of it,” which conveyed genuine belief in his potential and sparked a profound sense of being valued— a realization Hendricks later credited as instrumental in his openness to faith.[1] Additional adolescent guidance from a devout scoutmaster and a local pastor further encouraged his consideration of Christian service.[4] Though no precise date for his conversion to Christianity is documented in primary accounts, it occurred during his youth, prior to enrolling at Wheaton College in 1942, after which he sensed a divine call that prompted him to decline a full medical scholarship at Northwestern University in favor of Christian studies.[4][1] These formative encounters emphasized personal affirmation, community outreach, and relational evangelism, shaping Hendricks' lifelong emphasis on transformative teaching over mere information transfer.[1]

Formal Education

Hendricks completed his undergraduate education at Wheaton College in Wheaton, Illinois, earning a bachelor's degree in 1946.[9][3][7] Following this, he pursued theological training at Dallas Theological Seminary in Dallas, Texas, where he obtained a Master of Theology (ThM) degree in 1950.[4][1][2] In addition to these primary degrees, Hendricks engaged in further graduate studies at institutions including Wheaton College, Dallas Theological Seminary, and locations in New York, though specific degrees from these efforts beyond the ThM are not documented.[5] Later, in recognition of his contributions to Christian education, he received an honorary Doctor of Divinity from Wheaton College Graduate School in 1967.[3] These formal credentials laid the foundation for his extensive career in biblical teaching and seminary instruction.

Professional Career

Tenure at Dallas Theological Seminary

Howard Hendricks joined the faculty of Dallas Theological Seminary (DTS) on a part-time basis in 1950, transitioning to full-time status in 1951.[1] He served in various capacities, including as professor of Christian Education and Leadership, ultimately retiring in 2011 as distinguished professor emeritus while remaining active until his death in 2013.[3] Over his more than six-decade tenure, Hendricks taught over 10,000 students, emphasizing practical ministry training, biblical exposition, and personal discipleship.[1] Hendricks' teaching focused on courses such as Bible Exposition and Hermeneutics, Methods of Bible Study, and Christian Education principles, where he introduced inductive study methods, Bible memorization requirements (up to 1,000 verses), and hands-on application through discipleship groups and early-morning mentoring sessions.[4] These approaches aimed to equip students not only with doctrinal knowledge but also with skills for effective teaching and leadership in church settings, influencing generations of evangelical ministers.[1] His mentorship extended to prominent figures including Charles Swindoll, Tony Evans, and David Jeremiah, who credited Hendricks with shaping their approaches to preaching and pastoral work.[3] In 1986, Hendricks founded the Center for Christian Leadership at DTS (later renamed the Hendricks Center), which prioritized spiritual formation, character development, and discipleship training to address perceived gaps in seminary education focused solely on academics.[3] This initiative reflected his broader contributions to curriculum development, including the establishment of a Spiritual Formation program that integrated relational and practical elements into theological training.[1] His work helped transform DTS into a hub for producing leaders who extended influence across hundreds of Christian organizations, underscoring a legacy rooted in rigorous, application-oriented education rather than abstract theory.[1]

Involvement in Broader Ministries

Hendricks served as chaplain and Bible teacher for the Dallas Cowboys professional football team from 1976 to 1984, providing spiritual guidance to players and staff during that period.[1][10] Beyond his academic role, he held positions on various organizational boards, including those of Walk Thru the Bible Ministries, where he participated in staff training conferences and served as a board member; Promise Keepers, for which he spoke at the 1995 Washington, D.C. conference and joined the board; The Navigators; Leadership Dynamics; Multnomah Bible College; and SEARCH Ministries.[11][4] He engaged extensively in speaking at conferences and seminars, addressing audiences at events hosted by organizations such as The Navigators, Moody Bible Institute, and the National Pastors Convention, as well as major Christian education and Sunday school conventions during the 1960s and 1970s.[4][12] His international ministry reached over 80 countries through personal visits, including a 1981 trip to Australia to encourage the founding of the Adelaide College of Ministries, and contributions as an early master-coach for the Global Proclamation Academy under RREACH.[11][3] Locally, Hendricks acted as an elder at Stonebriar Community Church for nearly 15 years following its establishment around 1999.[11] These activities extended his influence in evangelical leadership and discipleship beyond seminary walls, often emphasizing practical Bible application and mentoring.[4]

Teaching Philosophy

Inductive Bible Study Method

Howard Hendricks advocated an inductive Bible study method that prioritizes deriving meaning directly from the biblical text through personal observation, rather than imposing external assumptions or relying heavily on commentaries from the outset. This approach, which he taught extensively at Dallas Theological Seminary over more than six decades, encouraged students to engage Scripture as primary evidence, fostering independent discovery of truths.[11] Hendricks detailed the method in his 1991 book Living by the Book, co-authored with his son William D. Hendricks, presenting it as both an art and a science accessible to laypeople and scholars alike.[13][11] The core process consists of three sequential steps: observation, interpretation, and application. Observation forms the foundation, requiring repeated, careful reading of the passage to identify factual content without initial judgment. Practitioners ask interrogative questions—who is speaking, what actions occur, when and where events unfold, why certain elements appear, and how the narrative progresses—while marking structural devices like key terms, repetitions, contrasts (e.g., alike and unalike), and cause-effect relationships. Hendricks exemplified this by challenging students to generate dozens of observations from short texts, such as 75 from Acts 1:8, to build exhaustive textual awareness before advancing.[14][11][14] Interpretation follows, aiming to ascertain the author's intended meaning within its original context. This step incorporates historical, cultural, and literary analysis—considering genre, immediate surroundings, and broader canonical parallels—while consulting reliable resources only after exhausting personal inquiry to avoid bias. Hendricks emphasized fidelity to the text's plain sense, warning against allegorizing or subjectivizing isolated verses detached from their setting.[14][11] Application translates interpreted principles into actionable life changes, probing how the truth confronts personal sin, offers promises, provides examples, or issues commands. Hendricks insisted this step prevents intellectualism, asserting that knowledge unapplied equates to spiritual stagnation; he posed targeted questions like "What behavior should I modify?" or "What truth must I embrace?" to ensure relevance. Some adaptations of his framework add a fourth step, communication, involving sharing derived insights with others through teaching or discussion to reinforce retention and accountability.[14][14][14] Integrated into seminary curricula like Bible Study Methods and Hermeneutics, Hendricks' method equipped over 10,000 students, promoting lifelong habits of rigorous, transformative engagement with Scripture.[11][15]

Emphasis on Mentoring and Application

Hendricks viewed mentoring as essential to effective Christian teaching and leadership, distinguishing it from mere instruction by emphasizing personal, transformative influence. He described mentoring as "the expression of one soul talking to another," requiring proximity to allow a mentor's character and faith to "rub off" on the mentee, alongside instilling belief in their potential and challenging them toward superior thinking and action.[16] In practice, he conducted regular early-morning breakfast meetings with small groups of students at Dallas Theological Seminary to foster discipleship, prioritizing relational investment over administrative roles or personal recognition.[17] Central to his mentoring philosophy was the replication of godly lives, aiming to guide individuals toward Christlikeness and leave a legacy through others' growth. Hendricks advocated that every Christian cultivate three relational dynamics: a mentor ahead for experience, a peer alongside for mutual encouragement, and a mentee behind for influence, preventing spiritual stagnation and ensuring generational continuity in ministry.[18] He challenged mentees with probing questions, such as "What are you willing to live for? To die for? To settle for?" to clarify priorities and align actions with biblical truth, underscoring that a mentor's effectiveness derives from demonstrating God's power in their own life.[16] Hendricks equally stressed the application of Scripture as the capstone of biblical study, insisting that knowledge without implementation fails to produce transformation. In his inductive Bible study method—comprising observation, interpretation, and application (with correlation for contextual understanding)—he warned that "observation plus interpretation without application equals abortion," arguing the Bible exists to change lives, not merely satisfy curiosity.[17][19] Application, often the most neglected phase, demands identifying specific, actionable responses: relating observed truths to personal circumstances, meditating on them, and committing to measurable practices that bridge head knowledge to heart obedience.[20] This focus on application permeated his classroom approach, where he prohibited reliance on commentaries during observation to encourage direct engagement with the text, followed by rigorous interpretation and mandatory life application to avoid superficial study.[17] Hendricks taught that true teaching engages learners actively, declaring it "a sin to bore people with the Word of God," and integrated application to ensure students not only understood Scripture but lived it out, thereby equipping them for ministry impact.[17] Through these principles, he influenced countless evangelical leaders by modeling teaching that prioritizes eternal fruit over academic abstraction.[16]

Publications and Writings

Key Books

Howard Hendricks authored or co-authored over a dozen books, primarily focused on Christian education, Bible study methods, mentoring, and practical ministry application, drawing directly from his decades of teaching at Dallas Theological Seminary.[4] His writings emphasize inductive approaches, character development, and transformative teaching, influencing evangelical educators and leaders.[21] Living by the Book: The Art and Science of Reading the Bible (1991, co-authored with William D. Hendricks, Moody Press), Hendricks' most widely read work, outlines a three-step inductive Bible study process—observation, interpretation, and application—to enable readers to engage Scripture personally and apply it to daily life.[4][13] The book has sold over a million copies and includes practical exercises, reflecting Hendricks' classroom methods for fostering lifelong biblical literacy.[22] Teaching to Change Lives: Seven Proven Ways to Make Your Teaching Come Alive (1987, Multnomah Press) presents seven "laws" of learning—the teacher, educator, activity, communication, system, readiness, and the learner—derived from Hendricks' educational psychology and biblical principles, aimed at making instruction transformative rather than mere information transfer.[4][23] This concise volume, reissued in 2003, has been used in seminaries and churches to train educators, underscoring Hendricks' belief that effective teaching prioritizes heart change over rote knowledge.[24] As Iron Sharpens Iron: Building Character in a Mentoring Relationship (1995, co-authored with William D. Hendricks, Moody Press) explores mentoring as a biblical model for personal and spiritual growth, drawing on Proverbs 27:17 to advocate intentional relationships that sharpen character through guidance, accountability, and example.[4][25] The book provides practical strategies for mentors and mentees, based on Hendricks' own experience discipling thousands, and critiques superficial discipleship in favor of deep, lifelong investment.[26] Other notable works include Heaven Help the Home (1973, Victor Books), which applies biblical principles to family dynamics and parenting, and A Life of Integrity (1997, Multnomah Books), urging Christian men to pursue ethical leadership amid cultural pressures.[4] These publications collectively sold widely in evangelical circles, with Hendricks' clear, application-oriented style contributing to their enduring use in training programs.[21]

Journal Articles and Other Contributions

Hendricks authored a four-part series of articles in Bibliotheca Sacra, the theological journal of Dallas Theological Seminary, titled "On the Edge of Eternity—A Conversation about Aging." This series, published quarterly in 2000, explored biblical perspectives on aging, retirement, personal reflection, and the value of ministry in later life, drawing from Hendricks' experiences as a longtime educator. The articles emphasized proactive engagement with Scripture and practical application over withdrawal, challenging conventional views of seniority in evangelical contexts.[1][27] The series comprised: "The Other Side of the Mountain" in the January 2000 issue (vol. 157, no. 625), which introduced the theme of transitioning into later years; "Rethinking Retirement" in the April 2000 issue (vol. 157, no. 626), advocating for continued productivity aligned with divine purpose; "Me, Myself, and My Tomorrows" in the July 2000 issue (vol. 157, no. 627, pp. 259-270), focusing on self-examination and future-oriented faith; and "Reaping the Rewards of Senior Ministry" in the October 2000 issue (vol. 157, no. 628, pp. 387-396), highlighting the contributions of older believers in mentoring and church life.[27][28][29][30] Beyond scholarly journals, Hendricks contributed articles to popular evangelical periodicals, including Decision, Moody Monthly, and Christianity Today, where he addressed topics in Bible teaching, leadership, and personal discipleship. These pieces extended his inductive study methods and mentoring principles to wider audiences, often integrating scriptural exegesis with real-world application.[31]

Personal Life

Marriage and Family

Howard Hendricks married Jeanne Bell on June 14, 1947, in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania.[8] The couple remained wed for 66 years until Hendricks' death in 2013, during which time Jeanne actively supported his teaching and ministry endeavors, including co-authoring several books and participating in family-oriented conferences.[32] [33] Together, they raised four children: sons Robert and David, and daughters Beverly and Gail.[5] [8] All four children married and pursued lives aligned with evangelical principles, reflecting the Hendricks' emphasis on practical Christian living in family contexts.[33] The family expanded to include six grandchildren and two great-grandchildren by the time of Hendricks' passing.[8] Hendricks often integrated his family experiences into his teachings on marriage, advocating for mutual celebration and biblical roles as foundational to marital success, drawing from his own long-term partnership with Jeanne.[34] This personal commitment influenced broader initiatives, such as his early involvement in what became the FamilyLife ministry, focused on strengthening marriages through scriptural application.[34]

Later Years and Death

In his later years, Howard Hendricks continued to teach and mentor students at Dallas Theological Seminary, where he had served on the faculty for over 60 years, emphasizing practical application of Scripture and leadership development until shortly before his death.[1] He also maintained involvement in broader evangelical efforts, including serving on the board of The Navigators, an organization focused on discipleship and evangelism.[35] Hendricks delivered teachings on topics such as aging and retirement, advocating that Christians redirect their energies toward mentoring younger generations rather than withdrawing from service, drawing from biblical examples of lifelong productivity.[36] Hendricks faced health challenges in his final years, including a history of skin cancer treated surgically in 1996, after which residual cells persisted, though he persisted in his work.[1] He died peacefully at his home in Dallas on February 20, 2013, at the age of 88, from complications of Alzheimer's disease.[37] A memorial service was held on March 2, 2013, celebrating his influence on evangelical education and leadership.[1]

Legacy and Reception

Influence on Evangelical Leaders

Howard Hendricks exerted significant influence on evangelical leaders primarily through his mentoring philosophy at Dallas Theological Seminary (DTS), where he served as a professor for over 60 years, teaching more than 12,000 students and emphasizing personal discipleship alongside inductive Bible study.[34] His approach prioritized relational investment, character development, and practical application of Scripture, which he modeled by investing in select students beyond the classroom to foster lifelong leadership.[16] This method produced a cadre of prominent figures who credited Hendricks for shaping their ministries, including Charles Swindoll, who became president of DTS from 1994 to 2001 and founded Insight for Living; Tony Evans, the first African-American student in Hendricks' class and founder of The Urban Alternative; and Joseph Stowell, former president of Moody Bible Institute.[1][7] Other key leaders under Hendricks' tutelage included David Jeremiah, founder of Turning Point Ministries; Bruce Wilkinson, author of The Prayer of Jabez; Erwin Lutzer, longtime pastor of The Moody Church; and Andy Stanley, lead pastor of North Point Community Church, each attributing aspects of their preaching style, organizational leadership, and commitment to mentoring to Hendricks' example.[7][38] Hendricks' insistence on "observing, interpreting, and applying" Scripture—core to his teaching—translated into these leaders' emphases on expository preaching and disciple-making, influencing broader evangelical networks through radio broadcasts, authorship, and institutional roles.[11] For instance, Swindoll and Evans extended Hendricks' inductive methods into their respective platforms, reaching millions via media ministries that stressed biblical literacy over superficial engagement.[39] Critics within evangelical circles occasionally noted that Hendricks' pragmatic focus on application could risk diluting doctrinal precision, yet his mentees generally upheld orthodox theology while innovating in outreach and leadership training.[40] Overall, his legacy in leadership formation is evidenced by the sustained impact on DTS alumni who occupied pulpits, seminaries, and parachurch organizations, perpetuating a model of influence grounded in intentional relationships rather than institutional authority alone.[41]

Theological Perspectives and Critiques

Hendricks' theological perspectives were anchored in the conservative evangelical framework of Dallas Theological Seminary, where he taught for over five decades, aligning with its doctrinal emphases on the inerrancy of Scripture and dispensational premillennialism as essential for hermeneutical fidelity.[4] He viewed the Bible as authoritative and transformative, insisting that genuine faith demands personal risk and obedience, as articulated in his teaching that "there's no such thing as faith apart from risk-taking."[42] His primary contribution lay in hermeneutics and Christian education, promoting an inductive Bible study method comprising observation (what the text says), interpretation (what it means), application (how to respond), and correlation (relation to broader Scripture).[14] This approach, detailed in resources like his Bible Study Methods course and Living by the Book (1991, co-authored with William D. Hendricks), prioritized life change over isolated doctrinal accumulation, critiquing seminary environments that exalted systematic theology without corresponding spiritual vitality.[17] Hendricks argued that Scripture study sustains believers against sin, famously stating, "This Book will keep you from sin, or sin will keep you from this Book."[43] He championed discipleship as biblically mandated (e.g., 2 Timothy 2:2), equipping leaders through mentoring rather than rote instruction, and affirmed God's sovereignty amid suffering, as in his cancer battle where he declared, "Either God is sovereign or He is not."[1] This practical theology extended to Christian education, which he elevated as a rigorous discipline for ministry preparation, founding DTS's Christian Education Department in 1958.[4] Critiques of Hendricks' perspectives are sparse in evangelical literature, reflecting his role as educator over systematic theologian. Some observers noted his application-focused method as potentially complementing—but occasionally challenging—DTS's doctrinal rigor by de-emphasizing abstract theology in favor of personal engagement, though this was framed as a needed balance rather than flaw.[17] Broader dispensational critiques (e.g., from covenant theologians) apply indirectly via his institutional loyalty, but no prominent attacks targeted his specific teachings, with his legacy underscoring affirmation for fostering obedient faith.[4]
