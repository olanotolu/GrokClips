Barberton, Ohio

Barberton is a city in Summit County, Ohio, United States, situated in the Akron metropolitan area with a population of 25,191 as recorded in the 2020 United States Census.[1] The city was platted in 1891 by industrialist Ohio Columbus Barber, who envisioned a planned community to support his manufacturing enterprises, including the relocation of the Diamond Match Company factory from Akron in 1894, which catalyzed explosive growth.[2][3] This rapid expansion in the late 19th century earned Barberton the enduring nickname "Magic City," reflecting its transformation from rural land around Lake Anna into a bustling industrial hub during Ohio's manufacturing boom.[4][3] Barber's influence extended to innovative projects like the Anna-Dean Farm, an experimental agricultural estate established in 1908 that exemplified early 20th-century agribusiness advancements.[4] While deindustrialization challenged the local economy in the late 20th century, Barberton has adapted by fostering growth in sectors such as healthcare, education, and technology, alongside multimillion-dollar investments in infrastructure and recreation.[4] The city remains culturally distinct for its Serbian fried chicken tradition, originating from immigrant-founded eateries like Belgrade Gardens in 1933, which features chicken fried in lard and served with rice and hot sauce, drawing regional acclaim.[5] Notable landmarks include Lake Anna Park and preserved O.C. Barber buildings, underscoring a heritage of entrepreneurial drive and community resilience.[4]

History

Founding and Early Development (1891â€“1910)

In 1891, industrialist Ohio Columbus Barber founded Barberton in New Portage Township, Summit County, Ohio, by acquiring approximately 600 acres of land near Akron and establishing the Barberton Land and Improvement Company to oversee town planning and development.[6] The village was incorporated that same year under Ohio law, with Barber leveraging his business acumen from the Diamond Match Company to attract workers and industries to the site, which benefited from proximity to the Ohio Canal and railroads.[6][7] Early infrastructure included the Erie Depot, constructed around 1890, and Victorian-era industrial buildings erected in 1891 at Second Street and Wooster Road West, among the oldest such structures in Summit County.[8] Barber accelerated growth by relocating the Diamond Match Company factory from Akron to Barberton in 1894, following a tax dispute and the Panic of 1893, where the plantâ€”featuring a North Machine Shop (1894â€“1896) and Foundry (1894)â€”ultimately produced 250 million matches daily.[9][10] This influx spurred rapid expansion, with factories and homes proliferating so swiftly that an Akron Beacon Journal reporter in 1893 described the transformation as springing up "like magic," earning the city its early nickname, the Magic City.[11] The Tracy Block, built in 1891 as downtown's oldest brick commercial structure, exemplified this building boom.[8] By the early 1900s, Barberton hosted diverse industries, including strawboard manufacturing, sewer pipe production via the American Sewer Pipe Company (formed 1891), pottery works, Grinnell fire extinguishers, and Babcock & Wilcox boilers, alongside the Columbia Chemical Company's synthetic soda ash plant operational from November 1, 1900.[6][8][12] Barber organized the Akron-Barberton Beltline Railroad to enhance connectivity, while land acquisitions, such as portions of the Huston Kepler farm, laid groundwork for further expansion into experimental agriculture on what became the 3,000-acre Anna Dean Farm by 1910, including initial structures like Barn No. 1 (1909) and the O.C. Barber Dam (1910).[6][13][8] These efforts positioned Barberton as an industrial hub, though a business downturn shortly after incorporation tested its economic resilience before recovery through diversified manufacturing.[6]

Industrial Boom and the "Magic City" Era (1910â€“1940)

![O.C. Barber Piggery on Anna Dean Farm, built in 1912]float-right The industrial expansion of Barberton accelerated after 1910, propelled by the established presence of O.C. Barber's enterprises, including the Diamond Match Company, which had relocated its operations to the city in 1894 and continued to dominate match production. By the 1910s, the company innovated with the patenting of the first non-poisonous match using phosphorus sesquisulfide, enhancing safety and market dominance while employing hundreds in Barberton's factories. Complementing this, the Columbia Chemical Company, operational since 1900, produced synthetic soda ash essential for glass manufacturing, supplying firms like Pittsburgh Plate Glass and fostering chemical industry growth along Wooster Road. These sectors attracted waves of immigrant labor, particularly from Eastern Europe, contributing to ethnic diversity and workforce expansion.[2][14][15][16] Barberton's nickname, the "Magic City," emerged from this era's explosive development, as O.C. Barber's vision transformed farmland into a bustling industrial hub seemingly overnight, with infrastructure like the O.C. Barber Dam completed in 1910 to support water needs for farms and factories. The Anna Dean Farm, Barber's experimental agricultural complex, exemplified this ambition; constructed between 1909 and 1912, it featured advanced livestock facilities, including the $50,000 Piggery dubbed the "Pork Palace," aimed at scientific breeding and feed production to sustain urban growth. Population surged accordingly, from approximately 3,500 in 1910 to 10,470 by 1920, reflecting job opportunities in manufacturing and agriculture.[17][18][19] Through the 1920s and 1930s, additional industries like the Lectromelt Steel Casting Company, founded in 1920 from the Barberton Foundry, diversified the economy with steel production for molds and components. Annexations, such as Johnson's Corners in 1929, expanded municipal boundaries to accommodate growth. Despite O.C. Barber's death in 1920, the momentum persisted, with the Diamond Match facilities capable of producing 225 million matches daily by the interwar period, underscoring Barberton's role in national supply chains. Labor actions, including strikes at Columbia Chemical in 1934 and 1936, highlighted tensions amid expansion, yet the city's industrial base solidified its prosperity until the onset of broader economic shifts.[20][18][21][22]

Post-World War II Decline and Deindustrialization (1940â€“1980)

Following World War II, Barberton initially experienced economic expansion driven by sustained demand for rubber and related manufacturing, with the city's population rising from 24,028 in 1940 to 27,893 in 1950.[23] This growth continued into the 1950s, fueled by national postwar prosperity and the rubber industry's role in tire production for expanding automobile ownership, culminating in a population peak of 33,805 in 1960. Major employers like Firestone Tire and Rubber maintained operations in Barberton, supporting a workforce tied to bias-ply tire manufacturing, though early signs of strain emerged from rising labor costs and initial foreign competition in synthetics.[24] By the late 1960s and 1970s, structural shifts in the tire industry accelerated decline, particularly the industry's pivot to radial tires, which demanded advanced machinery and processes incompatible with many legacy plants built for bias-ply production.[25] Firestone, facing competitive pressures from innovators like Michelin, invested $10 million in late-1970s upgrades at its Barberton facility but closed the plant in March 1980, eliminating hundreds of positions as part of a nationwide consolidation of six underperforming sites.[26] Likewise, Seiberling Rubber Company's Barberton operations shuttered in 1980, displacing about 1,000 workers amid similar technological and market challenges.[27] These events mirrored broader deindustrialization trends in Ohio's rubber belt, where manufacturing employment began contracting due to automation, high union wages, and imports from low-cost producers, leading to Barberton's population drop to 33,052 in 1970 and further to 29,732 by 1980. Local economic reliance on heavy industry, without diversification, amplified vulnerabilities, as plant closures triggered unemployment spikes and out-migration, eroding the tax base and straining municipal services by decade's end.[28]

Modern Revitalization and Challenges (1980â€“Present)

Barberton experienced profound economic difficulties in the wake of deindustrialization during the 1980s, marked by substantial job losses at key manufacturers. Firestone's closure contributed to widespread unemployment, while PPG Industries reduced its workforce from 1,200 to 200 employees through layoffs in the preceding years.[29] The Seiberling tire plant, once a cornerstone employer, declined significantly, reflecting broader shifts away from heavy industry in the region.[27] These losses exacerbated social challenges in the working-class community, including persistent unemployment that averaged 7-8% in the 1990s.[30] Population stagnation and subsequent decline underscored the era's hardships, with the city recording 27,899 residents in 2000 before dropping 11.7% to 24,624 by 2024.[31] The manufacturing sector, historically dominant, contracted amid restructuring, leaving Barberton and surrounding Summit County grappling with the erosion of traditional industries.[11] PPG adapted by pivoting to specialty chemicals for optical, pharmaceutical, printing, and rubber applications by 2000, but overall economic recovery proved uneven.[15] Revitalization initiatives gained momentum in the late 1990s through organizations like Neighborhood Conservation Services, a nonprofit focused on housing rehabilitation to counter urban decay.[12] The Barberton Community Development Corporation supported redevelopment via business financing, gap loans, and infrastructure projects.[34] Downtown renewal efforts intensified in the 2000s and 2010s, including the Downtown Building Rehabilitation Program and Restaurant & Retail Challenge Grants funded by the Barberton Community Foundation.[35] In 2023, the city and foundation established Main Street Barberton to drive economic development and civic improvements in the core area.[7] Ongoing challenges persist, including workforce skill gaps addressed by recent federal grants for training programs in September 2025.[11] City leaders emphasize infrastructure upgrades, community collaboration, and diversification to redefine Barberton's identity beyond its industrial past.[6] Unemployment has moderated to around 6% in recent years, yet population decline and the need for sustainable job growth remain key hurdles.[8] Grants totaling $61,000 have supported specific retail and restaurant projects, signaling incremental progress in local commerce.[13]

Demographics

Population Changes and Trends

Barberton's population expanded rapidly in the early 20th century, coinciding with its industrial development and influx of workers to factories established by O.C. Barber and others. The 1900 census enumerated 4,354 residents, which nearly tripled to 9,410 by 1910 and approached 19,000 by 1920, driven by employment in rubber processing, machinery, and related sectors that attracted labor from rural areas and immigrants.[57] Growth moderated thereafter, reaching 23,934 in 1930 before stabilizing near 24,000 in 1940 amid the Great Depression's economic constraints on further expansion.[57] Post-World War II, the population rose to a peak of approximately 29,800 in 1960, supported by sustained manufacturing output and suburban appeal within the Akron metropolitan area. Subsequent decades saw gradual erosion, with numbers dipping to 28,300 by 1980 as factory closures and automation reduced blue-collar jobs, prompting outmigration to regions with stronger employment prospects. By 2000, the census recorded 27,899 residents, reflecting a 0.3% increase from 1990 but signaling the onset of persistent decline linked to broader Rust Belt deindustrialization.[58][59] The downward trajectory accelerated after 2000, with the population falling to 26,550 in 2010 and 25,191 in the 2020 census, a roughly 10% drop over the decade attributable to factory shutdowns, lower fertility rates, and net domestic outmigration exceeding inflows. Recent estimates indicate further contraction, to 24,977 in 2023, at an annual rate of about -0.8%, outpacing Summit County's milder -0.2% decline over the same period and underscoring Barberton's vulnerability to manufacturing's structural contraction without diversified economic replacement.[60][61]

Socioeconomic Characteristics

The median household income in Barberton was $48,943 (in 2023 dollars) from 2019 to 2023, significantly below the Ohio state median of $69,680.[63][64] Per capita income during this period was $31,112, reflecting limited earnings dispersion typical of deindustrialized communities.[63] The poverty rate stood at 16.9%, higher than the Akron metro area's 12.4% and Ohio's approximate 13%, with 4,166 individuals affected.[60][65] Educational attainment among adults aged 25 and older indicates a predominantly high school-educated populace, with approximately 89% holding at least a high school diploma or equivalent and 11% lacking one; only 17.9% (Â±2.4%) possess a bachelor's degree or higher, about half the Summit County rate.[65][65] Labor market participation is subdued, with a rate of 58.2% and an unemployment rate averaging 4.7% in 2024, influenced by historical manufacturing reliance and structural shifts.[66][67] Income inequality is pronounced, with a Gini coefficient of 0.57, exceeding the U.S. average and signaling concentrated wealth amid broader economic stagnation.[68]

Government and Politics

Municipal Government Structure

Barberton operates under a mayor-council form of government, as defined in its municipal charter adopted in 1916 and amended thereafter.[72] In this structure, executive and legislative powers are divided between the elected mayor and city council, with the mayor responsible for executing laws and administering city affairs, while the council handles legislative functions such as passing ordinances and approving budgets.[72] [73] The mayor serves as the chief executive officer, supervising the administration of city departments and exercising control over subordinate officers.[73] The position is elected at-large for a four-year term during odd-numbered years' general municipal elections, with no specified term limits in the charter.[74] The mayor collaborates with the city council on legislation and represents the city in external affairs, including appointments that require council confirmation for certain positions.[75] The city council consists of nine part-time members elected to four-year staggered terms, ensuring continuity in legislative oversight.[76] Six members represent specific wards, two are elected at-large, and one serves as council president, also an elected position.[76] The council holds regular legislative meetings on the second and fourth Mondays of each month and committee-of-the-whole sessions on the first and third Mondays, focusing on policy development, zoning, and fiscal matters.[76] Ward boundaries are defined by ordinance to reflect population distribution, promoting localized representation.[76]

Electoral History and Political Trends

Barberton elects its mayor to four-year terms through partisan elections, with candidates typically advancing from primaries. The city has seen Democratic dominance in mayoral races since the late 20th century, following earlier periods of Republican leadership; for instance, Catherine Dobbs served as mayor from 1956 to 1961, marking her as the first woman in that role for Barberton and Summit County.[77] William B. Judge, a Democrat, has held the office since 2011, securing reelection in the Democratic primaries of 2015 and 2019 before facing Republican Megann Eberhart in the 2023 general election, which Judge won by a narrow margin.[78][79] The Barberton City Council comprises nine part-time members: six elected by ward, two at-large, and one as president, all serving four-year terms.[76] Local elections have historically favored Democrats, aligning with the city's industrial, working-class demographics and proximity to union-heavy Akron.[80] However, Republican candidates achieved notable gains in the 2021 council elections, including promotion of one Republican to council president, election of another to an at-large seat, and a third to a ward position, signaling a potential shift amid broader regional trends toward economic conservatism.[81] In national elections, Barberton precincts within Summit County have mirrored Ohio's swing dynamics, with the county leaning Democratic overall but showing variability; for example, while Kamala Harris carried Summit County in 2024, local Republican inroads suggest voter dissatisfaction with establishment policies in blue-collar areas like Barberton.[82][81] These patterns reflect causal factors such as deindustrialization and wage stagnation, prompting some traditional Democratic voters to support alternatives emphasizing trade protectionism and deregulation.[80]

Economy

Historical Industrial Base

Barberton was established in 1891 by industrialist Ohio Columbus (O.C.) Barber as a planned manufacturing center on land near Akron, Ohio, leveraging proximity to railroads and the Ohio Canal for industrial development.[83] Barber, who had risen in the match industry from his family's small factory founded in 1845 to co-founding the Diamond Match Company in 1881â€”which captured 85% of the U.S. market by the late 1880sâ€”relocated match production to Barberton in 1894 to capitalize on local resources and stabilize operations amid economic fluctuations.[83] The city's early industrial infrastructure included the North Machine Shop (built 1894â€“1896), Foundry (1894), Pattern Shop (c. 1898), and South Machine Shop (1903), all supporting Diamond Match's output, which reached significant scales though exact Barberton-specific figures are not detailed in primary records.[19] This match-centric base quickly diversified, attracting firms in strawboard, fire extinguishers via Grinnell Corporation, boilers from Babcock & Wilcox, sewer pipe, ammunition, aluminum, pottery, woolen mills, felt, and rubber through Diamond Rubber Company (later merged into B.F. Goodrich in 1911).[83] The Akron-Barberton Beltline Railroad, constructed in the 1890s, further enabled material transport and spurred growth, earning Barberton the moniker "Magic City" for its rapid expansion from incorporation in 1891 to a bustling industrial hub by the early 1900s.[83] By the 1910sâ€“1920s, business expansions included steel casting via the Barberton Foundry (reorganized as Lectromelt Steel Casting Company in 1920) and sustained manufacturing booms, with population and infrastructure surging alongside these operations.[84][20] Rubber manufacturing emerged as a pillar in the 1920s, with F.A. Seiberling founding Seiberling Rubber Company in 1921 by acquiring and reopening the decade-old Portage Rubber Company factory in Barberton after his departure from Goodyear.[85] Chemical production complemented this, as Pittsburgh Plate Glass Company (later PPG Industries) established a plant in 1899â€”beginning soda ash output in 1900 as Columbia Chemical Companyâ€”and expanded into chlorine, caustic soda, calcium chloride, and specialty chemicals, supported by the world's deepest limestone mine at 2,200 feet for raw materials.[15] Sun Rubber Company also launched in 1923, focusing on rubber goods amid the regional tire and polymer surge tied to Akron's ecosystem.[86] These sectors drove Barberton's economy through heavy industry, providing jobs in fabrication, processing, and extraction until mid-century shifts.[24]

Current Economic Landscape

Barberton's economy employs around 11,000 residents as of 2023, with the largest sectors being retail trade (1,975 workers), manufacturing (1,788 workers), and health care and social assistance.[60] Employment grew marginally by 0.1% from 2022 to 2023, reflecting stability amid broader regional manufacturing pressures.[60] The city's labor force totals approximately 12,391, with an unemployment rate of 3.4% recorded in October 2024, down from peaks during the COVID-19 period but above the national average at times due to industrial sector fluctuations.[87] Median household income stands at $48,943 for 2023, with per capita income at $35,967, indicating a working-class base reliant on blue-collar jobs.[57] Manufacturing persists as a core driver, hosting firms in polymers, automotive components, and aerospace, alongside smaller operations in foam production and tool manufacturing.[88] [89] Major employers include local entities like the City of Barberton, Barberton City Schools, and industrial outfits such as Ravago and Hycom Inc., though no single dominant corporation anchors the economy as in its historical rubber era.[90] [89] Municipal efforts emphasize revitalization, with $96,604 budgeted for economic development and $130,000 for facade improvements in 2024 to attract investment.[91] In September 2025, U.S. Representative Emilia Sykes secured federal funding for workforce training initiatives aimed at positioning Barberton as a hub for advanced manufacturing skills, targeting job creation through partnerships with local technical centers.[92] These programs address skill gaps in a region where Summit County's broader economy influences commuting patterns, with many residents employed in nearby Akron-area firms.[93]

Revitalization Efforts and Ongoing Challenges

In recent years, Barberton has pursued downtown revitalization through targeted grant programs and nonprofit initiatives aimed at attracting new businesses and rehabilitating historic structures. The city's Department of Planning & Community Development administers an Economic Development Grant Program and a FaÃ§ade Improvement Grant Program, providing financial incentives to property owners and businesses for exterior upgrades and interior renovations on commercial buildings.[94][95] In March 2025, downtown Barberton announced two grant programs totaling $300,000 to support new retail and restaurant ventures, building on prior awards such as the $61,000 from the Barberton Community Foundation in June 2025 for two restaurant projects.[96][97] Main Street Barberton, a nonprofit established with city and foundation support in 2023, coordinates these efforts to foster economic growth, reporting progress in its 2023 annual report on rehabilitating vacant properties and enhancing the commercial historic district.[98][7] Additionally, in July 2025, city council approved legislation designating downtown as a community entertainment district to encourage outdoor events and patronage.[99] Efforts to diversify beyond legacy industries include workforce development in advanced manufacturing and site redevelopment. In September 2025, U.S. Representative Emilia Sykes secured federal funding through the Economic Development Administration for initiatives positioning Barberton as a hub for manufacturing training, emphasizing job creation amid regional industrial shifts.[92] The city marketed a 36-acre former industrial site for redevelopment, while conducting 65 business retention meetings that yielded 10 expansions by March 2025.[100][101] The Barberton Community Development Corporation coordinates broader economic strategies, focusing on job growth and property utilization.[102] Despite these initiatives, Barberton faces persistent challenges from deindustrialization and fiscal constraints typical of Ohio's Rust Belt communities. The decline of traditional manufacturing has led to vacant properties and slower job recovery, with the city's 2023 comprehensive annual financial report noting ongoing budget balancing difficulties due to sluggish state economic trends and reliance on income tax revenues.[103][13] A 2024 state audit echoed these issues, projecting continued fiscal pressures without accelerated growth.[104] Broader social and infrastructure needs, including addressing underutilized sites citywide, remain hurdles to sustained revitalization.[6][101]

Education

Primary and Secondary Education

The Barberton City School District provides primary and secondary education to residents of Barberton, Ohio, serving students from preschool through grade 12 across five main facilities.[105] The district, established over 120 years ago, emphasizes foundational skills in early grades through programs like the Starting Strong Preschool for ages 3-5 and kindergarten field trips, transitioning to specialized secondary offerings such as STEM labs with 3D printers at Barberton High School and the College Credit Plus program, which earned families approximately $68,500 in savings via 1,353 credit hours in recent years.[106] [107] Barberton Primary School covers kindergarten through grade 2, Barberton Intermediate School serves grades 3-5, Barberton Middle School handles grades 6-8, and Barberton High School educates grades 9-12, with a separate preschool program.[105] During the 2024 school year, total enrollment stood at 3,634 students, with a student-teacher ratio of 16:1; minority students comprise about 30% of the population, and 33% are economically disadvantaged. [108] [109] Performance metrics from state assessments show roughly 50% proficiency in both math and reading across the district.[108] Barberton High School reports a 14% AP participation rate and ranks in the bottom half of Ohio high schools based on test scores and graduation outcomes.[110] [111] The Ohio Department of Education assigned the district an overall 2.5-star rating out of 5, reflecting average achievement and progress components.[112] Supplemental options include St. Augustine Catholic School, a private PK-8 institution enrolling around 176 students with a faith-based curriculum.[113] The district also partners with initiatives like the 4 Cities Compact for vocational training and offers extracurriculars in arts, athletics, and community service to support career readiness.[107]

Educational Outcomes and Institutions

The Barberton City School District serves approximately 3,645 students across six schools, including Barberton High School (grades 9-12), Barberton Middle School (grades 6-8), Barberton Intermediate School (grades 4-5), three elementary schools (grades K-3), and a preschool program.[109][105] The district, established over 120 years ago, emphasizes foundational education in a community with 33.2% economically disadvantaged students and 30% minority enrollment.[106][109] In the Ohio Department of Education's 2024 school report card, evaluating the 2023-24 school year, the district earned an overall rating of 2.5 stars out of 5, indicating performance below state averages in key areas while meeting standards in college, career, workforce, and military readiness (3 stars).[114] Achievement components received 2 stars, with early literacy at 2 stars, reflecting challenges in reading proficiency for K-3 students.[112] The four-year adjusted cohort graduation rate at Barberton High School stood at 82% for the class of 2023, compared to the state average of 86%, though district-wide averages align similarly.[115][108] State assessment proficiency levels lag behind Ohio benchmarks: district-wide, 55% of students are proficient in reading and 50% in mathematics, with elementary schools showing 55% reading and 50% math proficiency, and middle/high levels at approximately 52% combined.[109][116] Barberton Intermediate School outperforms district averages slightly, with 60% proficient in reading and 63% in math. Recent report cards note incremental gains in achievement and graduation rates but declines in early literacy, attributing variability to socioeconomic factors and instructional focus.[117] No local postsecondary institutions operate within Barberton; graduates typically pursue options at nearby facilities like the University of Akron.[118]

Culture and Community Life

Local Traditions and Events

A defining culinary tradition in Barberton is Serbian-style fried chicken, introduced by immigrants in the 1930s at Belgrade Gardens restaurant.[119] This unbreaded chicken, lightly salted and fried in lard, is served with french fries, coleslaw, and a "hot sauce" of rice mixed with tomatoes and hot peppers.[119] Several establishments, including Hopocan Gardens, Milich's Village Inn, and the original Belgrade Gardens, continue to prepare it using fresh, local birds without freezing or heavy seasoning, preserving the Serbian-American heritage brought by early 20th-century settlers.[119][120] The annual Barberton Mum Festival, held in September at Lake Anna Park, stands as the city's largest community gathering, drawing visitors to displays of over 17,000 chrysanthemums producing more than one million blooms.[121][122] This two-day event features live music, vendor booths, and family activities centered around the seasonal floral exhibits, with the 35th iteration occurring in recent years.[121][123] Tuscany on Tusc, an annual street festival in the downtown Barberton Arts District, celebrates Italian culture with traditional foods, music, and entertainment under evening lights along West Tuscarawas Avenue.[124] Main Street Barberton also organizes recurring community events such as farmers markets and art festivals, fostering local engagement in the historic district.[125]

Recreation and Landmarks

Barberton maintains a network of 15 public parks offering diverse recreational opportunities, including playgrounds, sports complexes, and trails for hiking and biking.[126] The city's Parks and Recreation department organizes programs such as youth sports, community events, and seasonal activities to promote outdoor engagement.[127] Lake Anna Park, spanning 21 acres around a spring-fed 10-acre lake in downtown Barberton, serves as a primary recreational and landmark site.[128] Features include walking paths, picnic areas, and a gazebo suitable for gatherings, with the lake named after the daughter of industrialist O.C. Barber.[129] The park hosts annual events like the Barberton Mum Festival, drawing visitors for floral displays and festivities, and includes a Veterans Memorial honoring local military service.[130] Historical landmarks tied to Barberton's founding emphasize its industrial heritage. The Anna Dean Farm, developed by O.C. Barber starting in 1909, includes the 1912 O.C. Barber Piggeryâ€”known as the "Pork Palace"â€”a massive structure exemplifying early 20th-century agricultural innovation on what was once the world's largest hog farm.[19] The O.C. Barber Mansion, constructed as one of the finest residences between New York and Chicago, represents Gilded Age opulence and is preserved by the Barberton Historical Society for tours.[131] These sites, along with trails tracing the Tuscarawas River and Ohio Canal, provide interpretive access to the city's origins in rubber manufacturing and farming.[18]

Infrastructure and Transportation

Transportation Networks

Barberton's road network includes U.S. Route 224, which passes immediately south of the city and connects to Interstate 76 (I-76) and Interstate 77 (I-77) at the Central Interchange in nearby Akron, facilitating regional freight and commuter traffic.[132] State Route 585 provides additional east-west access through the area, linking Barberton to Wooster and Akron.[133] The city maintains local streets with ongoing resurfacing and reconstruction efforts funded by a dedicated local levy passed as Issue 8.[134] Public transit is provided by the Akron Metro Regional Transit Authority (METRO RTA), which operates fixed-route bus service into Barberton, including Route 8 that runs from the Robert K. Pfaff Transit Center in Akron to points in Barberton such as Wooster Road, with service every 30 minutes during peak hours and fares at $2 per ride.[135] [136] The system integrates with Summit County routes for broader regional connectivity, though Barberton lacks dedicated local bus loops.[137] Freight rail service is handled by the Akron and Barberton Belt Railroad, a short-line operator serving industrial sites within the city, with no active passenger rail operations since the discontinuation of Erie Railroad service in 1965.[138] For air travel, Barberton is proximate to Akron-Canton Regional Airport (CAK), situated 14 to 18 miles southeast, offering domestic flights via multiple carriers.[139] [140] Cleveland Hopkins International Airport lies about 44 miles north.[140] The city's 2024 Active Transportation Plan emphasizes enhancements to sidewalks and bike routes along major corridors to support non-motorized travel.[141]

Public Services and Utilities

The City of Barberton operates its own Utilities Department, which provides water services to approximately 11,000 customers through divisions handling administration, water treatment, and distribution, ensuring compliance with quality standards for potable water.[142] Sewer services extend to about 13,000 customers, managed via administration, wastewater treatment, industrial pretreatment, and collection systems for sanitary, storm, and combined sewers.[142] The department, located at 576 W Park Avenue, handles emergencies via a dedicated line and customer service for billing and maintenance issues.[142] Electricity distribution in Barberton is handled by Ohio Edison, a FirstEnergy subsidiary serving the region, while supply is facilitated through the city's municipal aggregation program, currently with Dynegy offering a fixed rate of 9.32 cents per kWh from May 2025 to May 2026, excluding delivery fees and taxes.[143] [144] Natural gas supply operates under a city aggregation program with IGS Energy at $4.84 per Mcf through the November 2026 billing cycle, with distribution typically by regional providers like Enbridge Gas in Ohio.[145] [146] Public safety services include the Barberton Police Department, staffed by 65 full- and part-time personnel under Chief Vince Morber, organized into patrol, detective bureau, and administrative divisions responsible for law enforcement, crime investigation, traffic control, and community protection.[147] The Fire Department, reporting to the Director of Public Safety, operates from two stations and provides fire suppression, emergency medical services, and prevention activities such as enforcing open burning restrictions from March 1 to June 1 annually.[148] The Public Service Department oversees infrastructure maintenance, including streets, sidewalks, building inspections, engineering for bridges and water systems, and trash and recycling collection, with operations directed from 576 W Park Avenue to support daily municipal functionality.[149]
