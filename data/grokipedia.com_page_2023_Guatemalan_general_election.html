2023 Guatemalan general election

The 2023 Guatemalan general election was held on 25 June to select the president, vice president, 160 members of Congress, 20 deputies to the Central American Parliament, and 340 mayors across the country's municipalities, with a presidential runoff conducted on 20 August following no candidate securing a first-round majority.[1][2][3] Bernardo Arévalo, an anti-corruption academic representing the center-left Movimiento Semilla party, defeated former first lady Sandra Torres of the establishment National Unity of Hope party in the runoff, obtaining 58% of the valid votes according to official tallies by the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE).[4][5] The election outcome represented a rare rebuke to Guatemala's entrenched political elite, known for a "pact of the corrupt" involving judicial manipulation and institutional capture, though Semilla secured only a minority of congressional seats amid fragmented legislative results.[6] Despite Arévalo's clear victory, post-election controversies erupted as public ministry prosecutors, aligned with outgoing President Alejandro Giammattei's administration, sought to suspend Semilla's legal status on allegations of irregularities in party registration and first-round irregularities, prompting massive indigenous-led protests, road blockades, and international pressure from bodies like the Organization of American States.[6][7] These maneuvers delayed Arévalo's inauguration from 14 January to the same date after constitutional court interventions upheld the TSE's results, highlighting deep institutional weaknesses and elite resistance to reformist change.[8][9] The events underscored Guatemala's ongoing struggle with democratic backsliding, where voter preferences clashed against captured institutions, ultimately allowing Arévalo's assumption of office but leaving his anti-corruption agenda vulnerable to sabotage.[6][10]

Electoral Framework

Presidential Election Process

The president and vice president of Guatemala are elected on a joint ticket for a non-renewable four-year term through a two-round voting system administered by the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE).[2][11] In the first round, voters select from candidates nominated by registered political parties, with the candidate receiving a simple plurality advancing alongside the runner-up to a runoff if no one secures an absolute majority (over 50 percent) of valid votes cast.[2][12] The runoff, if required, must occur no later than two months after the first round, with polls open from 7:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m. local time on designated Sundays.[2][13] Eligibility for the presidency mandates that candidates be Guatemalans by origin, at least 40 years of age, and in full possession of their political rights without any disqualifying convictions or incompatibilities under the constitution.[11][14] Nominations occur through political parties, which must register candidates via their national executive committees with the TSE at least six months prior to the election, submitting required documentation including party conventions or primaries where applicable.[2][13] Voting is compulsory for all citizens aged 18 and older but is not strictly enforced, with an estimated 60.52 percent turnout in the 2023 first round; ballots are cast secretly at polling stations nationwide, including for Guatemalans abroad via consular mechanisms.[2][12] For the 2023 cycle, the first round took place on June 25, with the TSE certifying results and scheduling the August 20 runoff between the top two candidates after no absolute majority was achieved.[2][15] The process emphasizes party agents' presence at polling stations for oversight, alongside international observers, to verify vote counts and tabulation, culminating in TSE proclamation of the winner who assumes office on January 14 of the following year.[12][16]

Congressional and Other Elections

The congressional election, held on June 25, 2023, alongside the first round of the presidential vote, renewed all 160 seats in the unicameral Congress of the Republic for the 2024–2028 legislative period.[1] Deputies were elected via proportional representation across Guatemala's 22 departmental constituencies using closed party lists and the D'Hondt method for seat allocation, with constituency sizes ranging from 2 to 40 seats based on population.[1] Voter turnout for the legislative ballot stood at 59.4%, with 5,565,598 valid votes cast out of 9,372,164 registered voters.[1] A total of 22 parties competed, but no single party secured a majority, resulting in a fragmented legislature where the largest bloc, Vamos, held 39 seats.[1] The Tribunal Supremo Electoral (TSE) officialized the results on July 16, 2023.[17] The election produced the following seat distribution:

Pre-Election Context

Political and Economic Landscape

Guatemala's political landscape in the lead-up to the 2023 general election was dominated by entrenched corruption and institutional capture by a coalition of political, economic, and criminal elites, often referred to as the "pacto de corruptos." Under President Alejandro Giammattei, who assumed office in January 2020, anti-corruption mechanisms established after the 2015-2019 International Commission against Impunity in Guatemala (CICIG) era were systematically dismantled, with no renewed commitment to reforms.[19] The Public Ministry, led by Attorney General Consuelo Porras since 2018, obstructed investigations into high-level graft, protecting allied networks and leading to the exile or harassment of prosecutors like Juan Francisco Sandoval.[20] This elite-driven impunity eroded public trust, as evidenced by widespread disqualifications of anti-establishment presidential candidates by the Constitutional Court in early 2023, fueling perceptions of electoral manipulation.[21] Judicial and legislative branches exhibited similar capture, with the Supreme Court and Congress prioritizing elite interests over accountability, including resistance to swearing in anti-corruption judges.[19] Giammattei's administration faced allegations of graft in public contracts and failure to address impunity, contributing to polarized rhetoric and repression of civil society protests against institutional decay.[19] Guatemala ranked 150th out of 180 countries on the 2022 Corruption Perceptions Index with a score of 24 out of 100, reflecting sustained perceptions of public sector corruption.[22] Public sentiment was marked by deep disillusionment, with systemic graft cited as a primary driver of insecurity, violence, and emigration.[21] Economically, Guatemala achieved GDP growth of 4.1% in 2022, rebounding from a 1.8% contraction in 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic, supported by remittances totaling over $10 billion annually and expansion in services (62% of GDP).[23] However, poverty affected approximately 59% of the population as of 2019, with rates remaining persistently high amid the crisis and projected increases into 2022, exacerbated by inequality and inadequate social spending.[19] Weak governance, corruption, and limited access to education and health services perpetuated underdevelopment, with Indigenous communities facing disproportionate deprivation—82% in rural areas per multidimensional measures.[19] Remittances from the United States mitigated some fiscal pressures but failed to address structural barriers, as average growth of 3-4% fell short of the 7% needed for meaningful poverty reduction.[23]

Candidate Registration Disputes

The Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE) rejected multiple presidential candidacies during the registration phase, citing ineligibility under electoral laws and constitutional provisions, with several decisions upheld by the Constitutional Court. These exclusions, finalized by March 26, 2023, reduced the field to 22 approved candidates and prompted accusations from human rights groups and analysts that the process favored establishment figures while sidelining challengers on technical or questionable grounds.[24][25] Indigenous leader Thelma Cabrera of the Movement for the Liberation of the Peoples (MLP), polling as a potential contender among rural and leftist voters, was barred alongside her running mate, former Human Rights Ombudsman Jordán Rodas. On February 2, 2023, the TSE ruled Rodas ineligible due to an "annotation" on his professional certificate stemming from a January 6, 2023, criminal complaint by the incumbent ombudsman, Alejandro Córdova, alleging mishandling of payments during Rodas's tenure—complaints that had not previously impeded his public service. Human Rights Watch described the timing and basis of the complaint as unsubstantiated, arguing it effectively excluded an anti-corruption ticket without due process.[25][24] Right-wing candidate Roberto Arzú, son of a former mayor, faced rejection on February 6, 2023, for initiating campaign activities before the official period and failing to pay a fine from his 2019 congressional bid. The TSE deemed him "unsuitable" under electoral regulations, a decision critics like the Washington Office on Latin America (WOLA) called disproportionate compared to leniency shown toward candidates linked to corruption scandals. Appeals to the Constitutional Court were denied, solidifying his exclusion.[25][24] Businessman Carlos Pineda, initially approved but polling strongly as an outsider, was provisionally suspended in May 2023 amid allegations of electoral law violations, including irregularities in party financing and prior campaign conduct. On May 26, 2023, the Constitutional Court rejected his final appeal, confirming disqualification one month before the June 25 first round, which Reuters reported as exacerbating perceptions of judicial interference to protect entrenched interests.[26][27] Zury Ríos Sosa, representing the Valor party and daughter of 1980s de facto president Efraín Ríos Montt, was disqualified by the TSE on March 24, 2023, under Article 185 of the Constitution, which bars relatives of those who assumed power through coups from presidential office—a provision applied consistently in her multiple prior bids. The Constitutional Court upheld the ruling, though international observers noted it eliminated a conservative voice critical of the government despite her strong polling in security-focused demographics.[28] These disputes highlighted tensions between legal formalism and broader electoral access, with proponents of the TSE's actions emphasizing adherence to statutes amid Guatemala's history of military interventions and corruption, while detractors, including the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights indirectly through related statements, viewed them as selective enforcement enabling pacts among political elites. No widespread evidence emerged of fraud in the registration mechanics themselves, but the exclusions contributed to voter apathy and protests, as documented by local and regional monitors.[25][24]

Presidential Candidates and Parties

Leading Candidates and Platforms


Sandra Torres, representing the National Unity of Hope (UNE), emerged as the first-round frontrunner on June 25, 2023, advocating a conservative platform centered on social welfare expansion and aggressive crime-fighting. She pledged to extend stipends for vulnerable groups like single mothers, attract foreign investment via near-shoring initiatives to generate jobs, and implement El Salvador-style security measures, including military deployment on streets, new prison construction, and biometric surveillance to combat gangs and extortion. Torres committed to greater governmental transparency, despite her history of corruption probes that concluded without charges in 2022.[29] Bernardo Arévalo, candidate of the progressive Seed Movement (Movimiento Semilla), advanced to the runoff with an anti-corruption agenda aimed at institutional renewal. Son of Guatemala's first democratically elected president, Arévalo proposed a 10-point plan featuring an anti-corruption cabinet, lifetime bans for convicted officials, and monopoly busting in sectors like telecommunications and pharmaceuticals to reduce costs and bolster consumer protections. His security proposals included recruiting and training 12,000 police officers, upgrading facilities, and curbing illicit prison activities, while economically emphasizing rule-of-law improvements to enhance the country's credit rating and foster employment training.[29][30] Zury Ríos Sosa, backed by the right-wing Valor party in alliance with Unionist, secured third place and appealed to voters seeking stringent security and economic vitality. Daughter of ex-dictator Efraín Ríos Montt, she promised to replicate El Salvador's hardline anti-gang tactics under Nayib Bukele, prioritizing victim rights and rapid incarceration to address rampant violence. Her platform also highlighted pro-business reforms to stimulate growth amid widespread public disillusionment with establishment figures.[31][32]

Withdrawn, Declined, and Rejected Bids

Several presidential candidacies were rejected by the Tribunal Supremo Electoral (TSE) or the Constitutional Court prior to the finalization of the ballot on March 26, 2023, primarily on grounds of ineligibility under Guatemalan electoral law, including prior criminal convictions, failure to meet residency or citizenship requirements, and non-compliance with party nomination procedures.[24][33] These rejections reduced the field from over 20 initial aspirants to 16 approved tickets, prompting accusations from human rights groups and opposition figures of selective application of rules to favor establishment-aligned candidates, though the rulings were grounded in statutory criteria and affirmed by judicial review.[34][27] The most prominent rejection involved Carlos Pineda Nitsch, a conservative businessman and leader in early opinion polls representing the Valor party, whose candidacy was definitively barred by the Constitutional Court on May 27, 2023, following TSE denial due to a 2019 conviction for illicit association that rendered him ineligible despite a suspended sentence.[35] Pineda, who polled as high as 10-15% in pre-disqualification surveys, denounced the decision as politically motivated corruption, but the court upheld the TSE's interpretation of Article 184 of the Constitution prohibiting candidacies by those with sentences over five years.[27] Roberto Arzú Escobar, son of former president Álvaro Arzú and candidate for the PAN-PODEMOS alliance, was disqualified on May 25, 2023, by the Constitutional Court after failing to provide timely proof of renouncing his U.S. citizenship, violating residency and nationality stipulations under electoral law.[36] This marked the second major exclusion in late May, further consolidating support among right-wing voters toward remaining conservative contenders like Zury Ríos Sosa.[28] Indigenous leader Thelma Esperanza Cabrera Orellana of the Maya Q'eqchi' community, nominated by the Movimiento para la Liberación del Pueblo (MLP), had her bid rejected by the TSE in February 2023 for the party's alleged failure to conduct compliant internal primaries and verify delegate signatures, a decision criticized by observers as disproportionately applied to smaller, left-leaning movements.[37][33] Cabrera's exclusion, upheld despite appeals, eliminated a key voice advocating for indigenous rights and land reform, contributing to perceptions of systemic barriers against non-traditional parties.[38] Fewer notable withdrawals occurred voluntarily; minor candidates like those from fringe parties occasionally stepped aside to consolidate votes, but no major figures withdrew post-registration, with most shifts happening via endorsements after the first round rather than pre-ballot abandonment. Declined bids included former president Jimmy Morales, who opted not to seek re-election amid ongoing corruption probes, and other establishment figures who avoided the fragmented field.[39]

Campaign Phase

Core Issues and Debates

The 2023 Guatemalan presidential campaign prominently featured corruption and impunity as the dominant issue, with Bernardo Arévalo of the Movimiento Semilla party positioning his platform around breaking the "pacto de corruptos"—an alleged alliance of political elites, business interests, and judicial actors that had undermined democratic institutions since the 2015-2019 anti-corruption commission's efforts.[6] Arévalo's rhetoric emphasized restoring judicial independence, prosecuting high-level graft, and reforming the public prosecutor's office, which observers noted had been co-opted to shield entrenched power structures.[40] In contrast, Sandra Torres of the Unidad Nacional de la Esperanza (UNE) party downplayed systemic overhaul, instead highlighting her prior executive experience—including as first lady and in social program implementation—and proposing incremental anti-corruption measures alongside expanded welfare initiatives to address voter disillusionment without disrupting established networks.[41] Public security and violence emerged as another key debate, amid Guatemala's homicide rate of approximately 16 per 100,000 inhabitants in 2022, driven by gang activity, extortion, and organized crime infiltration into politics.[21] Arévalo advocated intelligence-led policing and community-based prevention to reduce impunity rates exceeding 90% for violent crimes, linking insecurity to corrupt state capture that enabled criminal economies.[42] Torres countered with promises to bolster military involvement in internal security and fund victim support programs, framing her approach as proven continuity from past administrations' efforts that had marginally lowered violence metrics during her influence.[3] Economic inequality and poverty, affecting over 55% of Guatemalans in 2022 per World Bank data, fueled debates on growth versus redistribution, with campaigns clashing over migration drivers like job scarcity and rural underdevelopment.[21] Arévalo's agenda prioritized education and health investments to build human capital, arguing that corruption diverted resources from productive sectors; Torres emphasized cash transfers and infrastructure to provide immediate relief, critiquing outsider reforms as risking instability in a economy reliant on remittances comprising 18% of GDP.[29] These positions reflected broader tensions between transformative institutional change and pragmatic patronage-based stability, with limited formal debates amplifying rhetorical exchanges via rallies and media.[6]

Pre-Vote Irregularity Claims

International election observation missions reported multiple instances of irregular campaign practices during the lead-up to both the first round on June 25, 2023, and the runoff on August 20, 2023, primarily involving the National Unity of Hope (UNE) party of candidate Sandra Torres and the Vamos party. These included the distribution of cash, food, household goods, and promises of social benefits tied to attendance at campaign events, observed across departments such as Chimaltenango, Suchitepéquez, and Totonicapán. For example, EU observers noted UNE distributing approximately GTQ 900 (about USD 115) per attendee in certain areas, alongside prohibited registrations for government social programs as incentives.[12][43] Such practices were cited as violations of Guatemalan electoral law prohibiting vote inducements, with claims from observers and opposition figures, including the Semilla Movement, attributing them to efforts by established parties to sway rural and indigenous voters through clientelism. The Organization of American States (OAS) Electoral Observation Mission corroborated widespread reports of material inducements during the inter-round campaign, emphasizing their potential to undermine fair competition.[44] Semilla representatives publicly alleged that these tactics, combined with state resource misuse, created an uneven playing field favoring incumbency-linked parties like UNE.[12] Additionally, the Semilla Movement faced claims from prosecutors of ongoing irregularities, including repeated raids on its headquarters and the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE) between July 13 and July 21, 2023, purportedly investigating signature fraud from party formation but widely viewed by observers as judicial harassment to disrupt campaigning. These actions, initiated by the Attorney General's office under Consuelo Porras, were condemned by the EU EOM as lacking legal basis and aimed at eroding public trust in the process.[12] Arévalo's campaign countered that such interventions represented institutional bias against anti-corruption challengers, echoing broader allegations of elite interference documented in pre-election analyses.[6] Disinformation campaigns also surfaced as a claimed irregularity, with Semilla targeted by online smears from anonymous accounts and far-right groups falsely linking the party to policies like abortion legalization, amplifying polarization during the final campaign weeks. While no formal pre-vote sanctions were imposed on UNE or Vamos for vote-buying by the TSE, observers noted inadequate enforcement of campaign finance reporting, with undeclared expenditures potentially exceeding legal caps set at GTQ 38 million (about USD 4.9 million) per presidential candidacy.[43] These claims highlighted systemic challenges in regulating influence peddling, though empirical evidence from observer deployments substantiated the patterns without evidence of widespread pre-vote ballot tampering.

Opinion Polling Trends

First Round Surveys

Opinion polls conducted ahead of the first round on June 25, 2023, reflected a highly fragmented field of over 20 candidates, with no one projected to secure the absolute majority required to avoid a runoff. Early surveys captured volatility driven by candidate disqualifications, particularly the suspension of Carlos Pineda of Citizens Prosperity on May 11, 2023, who had led prior polling. Following his exit, former First Lady Sandra Torres of the National Unity of Hope (UNE) consolidated as the frontrunner, typically polling in the low 20s percent range, while support for other contenders like Edmond Mulet of Cabal and Zury Ríos of the Valor-Unionist alliance trailed but remained competitive for a runoff spot. High rates of undecided, null, and blank votes—often exceeding 20% combined—underscored voter dissatisfaction and methodological challenges in capturing late shifts.[45] A ProDatos survey fielded April 14–23, 2023, and published May 2, showed Pineda leading with 23.1% of valid votes, ahead of Torres, though exact figures for the latter were not detailed in reports; this poll, if reflective of election-day conditions, would have advanced Pineda to the second round.[46] Post-disqualification polling adjusted accordingly, with a May 2023 CID Gallup survey indicating Torres benefiting from Pineda's redistributed support amid ongoing institutional disputes.[47] The last major pre-vote survey, ProDatos' Encuesta Libre fielded June 5–14, 2023, reinforced Torres's lead at 21.3%, with Mulet at 13.4%, Ríos at 9.1%, and Manuel Conde of Vamos at 5.8%; other candidates, including Bernardo Arévalo of Semilla, polled below 5% individually. Null and blank intentions reached 13.5% and 14.8%, respectively, up from earlier months, signaling potential protest voting.[48]

Runoff Projections

Opinion polls conducted in the lead-up to the August 20, 2023, presidential runoff consistently projected a substantial victory for Bernardo Arévalo of the Semilla Movement over Sandra Torres of the National Unity of the Hope party.[49][50] These projections reflected Arévalo's momentum from the first round, where he garnered 31.2% of the vote compared to Torres's 25.5%, bolstered by endorsements from eliminated candidates and widespread anti-corruption sentiment amid institutional opposition to his campaign.[51] A CID Gallup poll released on August 2 indicated Arévalo leading with 63% of valid votes against Torres's implied 37%.[49] Another CID Gallup survey shortly before the election, reported on August 16, showed Arévalo at 61% to Torres's 39% among valid votes, underscoring his sustained advantage despite legal challenges to his candidacy.[50][51]

First Round Election

Conduct and Turnout

The first round of the 2023 Guatemalan general election occurred on June 25, 2023, with voter turnout reaching 59.85 percent among the approximately 9.3 million registered voters, resulting in 5,565,598 total votes cast, including 17.33 percent null and 6.98 percent blank ballots.[43] This figure marked a slight increase over prior elections but reflected ongoing challenges with voter apathy and dissatisfaction, as evidenced by the high rate of invalid votes.[43] Election day proceeded largely peacefully and in an organized manner, according to assessments from international observers including the Organization of American States (OAS) and European Union (EU) Election Observation Missions, which monitored over 1,000 and hundreds of polling stations respectively across Guatemala's departments.[53][43] Polling stations opened near the scheduled 7:00 a.m. time in most cases, with procedures for voter identification, secrecy, and counting generally adhered to, though minor issues included inconsistent ink verification for prior voting and occasional compromises to ballot secrecy in crowded booths.[53][43] Isolated irregularities disrupted the process in specific locales, such as violence and ballot burning in San José del Golfo and San Martín Zapotitlán, leading to temporary suspensions or cancellations, alongside reports of unauthorized voter transportation and intimidation in areas like Sipacate and Chisec.[53][43] The Tribunal Supremo Electoral (TSE) deployed security forces professionally without reported interference, and the preliminary results transmission system (TREP) operated to provide timely transparency, despite some delays in vote counting prioritization.[53] Observers noted adequate staffing, with significant female and indigenous representation among polling officials, but highlighted needs for improved training on counting protocols and voter education in indigenous languages.[43] Overall, the OAS and EU missions characterized the voting as reflective of voter will amid civic engagement, condemning violent incidents while affirming the process's integrity absent systemic fraud.[53][43]

Presidential and Congressional Results

The Tribunal Supremo Electoral (TSE) certified the results of the first round presidential election on July 14, 2023, confirming that no candidate achieved the absolute majority required to avoid a runoff.[54] Sandra Torres of the National Unity of Hope (UNE) received the highest share with 1,329,082 votes (25.66% of valid votes), while Bernardo Arévalo of the Semilla Movement placed second with 782,465 votes (15.75%).[54] [43] Other notable candidates included Edmond Mulet of the Cabal movement (9.2%) and Zury Ríos Sosa of the Valor-Unionist coalition (8.3%).[43]

Runoff Election

Interim Period Events

Following the certification of first-round results by the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE) on July 1, 2023, which advanced Bernardo Arévalo of the Movimiento Semilla (SEMILLA) party and Sandra Torres of the Unidad Nacional de la Esperanza (UNE) to the August 20 runoff, the interim period saw intensified legal scrutiny of SEMILLA's registration.[55] The Public Ministry, led by Attorney General Consuelo Porras, pursued a long-standing investigation into alleged falsified signatures during SEMILLA's 2015 formation, dubbing it "Caso Corrupción Semilla."[56] On July 12, 2023, a lower court ordered the temporary suspension of SEMILLA's legal personality at the ministry's request, potentially disqualifying Arévalo from the runoff and prompting claims of institutional interference to block an anti-corruption candidate.[57] [58] The Constitutional Court intervened swiftly, suspending the lower court's order on July 13, 2023, thereby allowing SEMILLA to participate in the runoff while the investigation continued; this ruling was hailed by Arévalo's supporters as a defense of electoral integrity but criticized by Porras' office as premature. Prosecutors raided TSE offices on the same day to seize documents related to SEMILLA's alleged irregularities, escalating tensions and drawing accusations from opposition figures that the actions aimed to undermine the vote's outcome.[58] Further, on July 21, 2023, police executed a search warrant at SEMILLA's headquarters in Guatemala City, targeting evidence of signature fraud, though no immediate arrests of party leadership occurred.[59] These developments sparked widespread protests, beginning in mid-July 2023, with demonstrators in Guatemala City and other areas demanding Porras' resignation and decrying judicial overreach as an establishment effort to preserve corrupt networks. By July 16, protests entered their third consecutive day, involving hundreds blocking streets and chanting against the Public Ministry's role in politicized prosecutions. On July 24, further rallies drew participants protesting court and prosecutorial interference in the runoff, reflecting broader public frustration with institutions perceived as aligned against reformist challengers.[60] Despite the unrest, campaigning proceeded, with Arévalo emphasizing anti-corruption themes and Torres focusing on social programs, amid a polarized atmosphere that international observers, including the Organization of American States, monitored for potential disruptions to the August vote.[61]

Final Campaign and Outcomes

The final campaign period for the presidential runoff spanned from July 12 to August 18, 2023, following the Supreme Electoral Tribunal's (TSE) confirmation of candidates Bernardo Arévalo of the Movimiento Semilla and Sandra Torres of the National Unity of Hope (UNE).[43] This phase was characterized by low-intensity activities, with UNE demonstrating greater visibility through offline events and alliances with over 250 mayors, while Semilla's efforts were constrained by ongoing judicial actions, including raids by the Special Prosecutor's Office Against Impunity (FECI) on TSE facilities and Semilla offices in July.[43] [62] Arévalo's campaign emphasized anti-corruption reforms, social justice, and structural change, relying heavily on organic social media outreach, trending hashtags, and limited paid advertising focused on governance plans, without forming explicit alliances to maintain independence from traditional power structures.[43] In contrast, Torres promoted continuity through social welfare programs, security enhancements, and appeals to conservative values such as family and private property, targeting rural and indigenous voters via extensive audiovisual media and paid content promising expanded aid.[43] Both campaigns faced a backdrop of judicial challenges to Semilla's legal status, though the Constitutional Court issued protective rulings on July 13 allowing its participation, and an Organization of American States (OAS) delegation visit from August 1-4 secured commitments from outgoing President Alejandro Giammattei for the runoff's conduct and power transition.[43] [5] The runoff election occurred on August 20, 2023, with voter turnout reaching 44.96%, slightly higher than in prior analogous contests, amid reports of isolated irregularities such as misuse of state resources and vote-buying but no evidence of widespread fraud altering outcomes.[43] Arévalo secured victory with 60.91% of valid votes (2,442,718 ballots), while Torres received 39.09% (1,567,664 ballots), reflecting a decisive rejection of establishment continuity in favor of anti-corruption priorities.[43] [62]

The TSE certified these results officially on August 28, 2023, prompting Torres to concede while alleging procedural flaws like rapid preliminary vote transmission without substantiating impact on totals; international observers, including the European Union Election Observation Mission, affirmed the process's integrity despite pre-election institutional pressures.[5] [43] This outcome positioned Arévalo for inauguration, underscoring voter prioritization of prosecutorial independence over entrenched patronage networks.[6]

Post-Election Institutional Conflicts

Fraud Allegations and Challenges

Following the first round on June 25, 2023, several political parties, including those aligned with President Alejandro Giammattei and presidential candidate Sandra Torres of the National Unity of Hope (UNE), filed challenges contesting the results, prompting the Constitutional Court to temporarily suspend certification of the vote tallies pending a review of alleged irregularities in specific polling stations.[63][64] The Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE) ultimately validated the outcomes on July 1, 2023, confirming Bernardo Arévalo's advancement to the runoff with 10.9% of the vote against Torres's 20.3%, amid observations from international bodies like the Organization of American States (OAS) that reported no systemic fraud despite isolated procedural issues.[6] The Public Ministry, led by Attorney General Consuelo Porras, escalated challenges by targeting Arévalo's Movimiento Semilla party, alleging administrative fraud in its 2017 formation, including over 4,000 forged signatures in its registration documents—claims originating from a 2018 complaint revived in 2023.[59] On July 12, 2023, a criminal court ordered the suspension of Semilla's legal personality, a move that risked disqualifying Arévalo from the August 20 runoff, though the TSE provisionally allowed his participation to avoid disrupting the electoral timeline.[65][66] International observers, including the European Union and OAS, dismissed these actions as lacking evidentiary basis and politically motivated to undermine the anti-corruption platform, noting that party registration disputes should not retroactively void certified candidacies.[67] After Arévalo's runoff victory with 61% of the vote, Torres and UNE rejected the results, alleging widespread electoral fraud favoring Semilla, including manipulations in vote counting and unauthorized alterations to tally sheets in multiple municipalities.[7] The Public Ministry intensified efforts in November 2023 by raiding Semilla offices again and formally suspending the party's registration on November 3, citing ongoing investigations into the signature forgeries, which effectively barred Semilla from assuming congressional seats won in the election.[68][69] On December 8, 2023, Porras publicly threatened to annul Arévalo's presidential win, arguing it violated electoral law due to Semilla's suspended status, a position criticized by observers as an institutional coup attempt without substantiation from forensic audits or TSE validations that affirmed the vote's integrity.[70][10] These challenges lacked corroboration from independent audits; the TSE and OAS mission reported on September 12, 2023, that the process was transparent with discrepancies under 0.5% and attributable to human error rather than deliberate fraud, while EU officials labeled the fraud claims "spurious" amid evidence of prosecutorial overreach tied to entrenched interests opposing Semilla's graft probes.[6][67] The Constitutional Court later ruled on December 20, 2023, that Arévalo's candidacy remained valid despite party suspension, preserving the results against further annulment bids.[7]

Party Suspension Attempts

The Public Ministry (MP), under Attorney General Consuelo Porras, initiated legal proceedings to suspend the legal personality (personalidad jurídica) of Movimiento Semilla, alleging irregularities such as falsified signatures during the party's registration process in 2017.[57][55] These efforts intensified after Semilla's candidate Bernardo Arévalo advanced to the presidential runoff on June 25, 2023, with a First Instance Criminal Court ordering the suspension on July 13, 2023, potentially disqualifying Arévalo from the August 20 ballot.[65][66] The Constitutional Court intervened on July 25, 2023, suspending the lower court's ruling and allowing Semilla to participate in the runoff, citing the need to preserve electoral integrity.[7] Post-runoff, following Arévalo's victory on August 20, 2023, the MP persisted with suspension requests, leading the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE) to provisionally suspend Semilla's legal status on November 2, 2023, again on grounds of signature fraud in the party's founding.[68] This action barred Semilla from congressional activities but did not directly affect Arévalo's presidency, as the suspension targeted the party's collective rights rather than individual candidacies.[68] The TSE's decision followed a Constitutional Court amparo ruling on October 5, 2023, that had previously rejected MP arguments but left room for administrative review.[7] These attempts drew widespread condemnation from international observers, including the Organization of American States (OAS), which on August 31, 2023, decried restrictions on Semilla's legislative participation as violations of democratic norms.[71] The United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights labeled post-election challenges as "persistent and systematic" efforts to subvert the vote on December 9, 2023.[72] Domestically, the moves were viewed by Semilla supporters as politically motivated by entrenched interests opposing anti-corruption reforms, though the MP maintained they addressed verifiable administrative flaws predating the election.[59] The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld Semilla's status for inauguration purposes, enabling Arévalo's swearing-in on January 14, 2024, despite ongoing litigation.[7]

Constitutional Court Rulings

The Constitutional Court of Guatemala issued multiple rulings in response to legal challenges following the first round of the 2023 general election, primarily aimed at reviewing alleged irregularities in vote tabulation while safeguarding the scheduled runoff. On July 1, 2023, the Court granted an amparo (constitutional protection) petition from defeated congressional candidates, suspending the Supreme Electoral Tribunal's (TSE) officialization of results pending an audit of tally sheets from approximately 100 municipalities where discrepancies were claimed, such as mismatched votes or unsigned documents.[73][74] However, the ruling explicitly directed the TSE to continue preparations for the August 20 runoff between Bernardo Arévalo of Semilla and Sandra Torres of UNE, rejecting broader calls for annulment and emphasizing that any proven irregularities affected only specific races, not the presidential contest.[75][76] Amid escalating efforts by the Public Ministry to disqualify Semilla, the Court intervened decisively on July 13, 2023, suspending a lower court's order that had revoked the party's legal personality based on claims of irregularities in its formation signatures. This ruling, issued via amparo 3985-2023, restored Semilla's participation rights and enabled Arévalo's candidacy in the runoff, countering what prosecutors alleged were forged affiliations dating back to 2015.[58][77] The decision highlighted the Court's prioritization of electoral continuity over preemptive disqualifications, noting that ongoing investigations could proceed post-election without disrupting the process.[78] Following the August 20 runoff, the Court upheld the TSE's certification of Arévalo's victory on August 29, 2023, rejecting subsequent amparos seeking annulment on grounds of alleged fraud or Semilla's temporary TSE suspension for similar signature issues.[79] In late 2023, amid Public Ministry attempts to declare the election void and strip Arévalo's immunity—citing TSE administrative errors—the Court reaffirmed the results as "unalterable" and binding. On December 15, 2023, it accepted Semilla's appeal and ordered Congress to "guarantee" Arévalo's January 14, 2024, inauguration, blocking further dilatory tactics and affirming the constitutional mandate for power transition.[80][81] These rulings collectively preserved the electorate's expressed will against institutional resistance, though critics from establishment-aligned sources questioned their timing as potentially favoring anti-corruption reformers.[6]

Inauguration Obstacles and Resolution

Protests and Power Transition Delays

Following the August 2023 runoff victory of Bernardo Arévalo, widespread protests erupted in October against actions by Attorney General Consuelo Porras and the Public Ministry to suspend the Movimiento Semilla party on allegations of voter registration irregularities, which critics viewed as an attempt to invalidate the election results.[82] Indigenous authorities and civil society groups initiated an indefinite national strike on October 2, blockading major highways and roads across 20 departments to demand Porras's resignation and an orderly power transition.[7] These demonstrations, largely peaceful in the initial week, involved thousands paralyzing commerce and transportation, with protesters accusing the Public Ministry of coup-like interference in the democratic process.[83] By October 10, protests escalated as blockades intensified in Guatemala City and rural areas, leading to clashes with security forces and one reported death on October 16 from injuries during confrontations in Sololá.[84] [85] The unrest, coordinated by 48 indigenous councils representing over 6 million people, highlighted systemic opposition from entrenched elites and the Public Ministry, which had previously disqualified several candidates during the first round.[86] Protesters maintained nonviolent tactics where possible, but the government's interior minister resigned amid the pressure, underscoring the movement's impact on forcing accountability.[85] Power transition delays culminated in January 2024, when the outgoing Congress, dominated by Arévalo's opponents, stalled the credentialing of incoming legislators from Semilla, postponing the scheduled January 14 inauguration ceremony.[87] This obstruction, lasting over 12 hours, prompted renewed street protests outside Congress and the Supreme Electoral Tribunal, with demonstrators demanding immediate swearing-in to prevent a constitutional crisis.[88] Arévalo was ultimately inaugurated shortly after midnight on January 15, 2024, after the legislature relented under domestic and international scrutiny, marking the end of delays but highlighting vulnerabilities in Guatemala's institutional framework.[89] The protests' persistence from October through January played a pivotal role in sustaining public pressure against these delays, contributing to the eventual democratic handover despite ongoing legal challenges from the Public Ministry.[90]

International Diplomatic Pressures

Following the attempts by Guatemalan institutions to suspend Arévalo's Semilla Movement and challenge election results in late 2023, the United States imposed sanctions on July 19, 2023, targeting ten officials, including judges Fredy Orellana and Jimi Bremer, and prosecutor Cinthia Monterroso, for actions deemed to undermine democracy, such as politically motivated prosecutions and corruption.[91] The U.S. Treasury also designated associate Miguel Martínez under the Magnitsky Act, while the State Department revoked visas of implicated individuals, aiming to deter interference in the presidential transition.[92] U.S. diplomats facilitated a late October 2023 meeting between Indigenous leaders, private sector representatives, and Arévalo's team, yielding a joint statement from twelve organizations committing to defend democratic institutions.[92] The European Union complemented these efforts with a December 2023 Council decision for restrictive measures, including visa bans and asset freezes, against officials obstructing the electoral process.[93] EU High Representative Josep Borrell publicly condemned post-election maneuvers as an "attempted coup d’état" that month, while the European Parliament passed resolutions denouncing arbitrary legal actions against the victors.[93] An EU election observation mission had previously refuted fraud allegations from the June and August 2023 rounds, bolstering international insistence on result certification.[93] Regionally, the Organization of American States (OAS) affirmed the elections' integrity through statements and resolutions, including a November 2023 Permanent Council measure invoking the Inter-American Democratic Charter to demand adherence to voter will and rejection of annulment bids.[92] Additionally, thirty-one former presidents from Latin America and Spain issued a statement urging Guatemalan authorities to honor the outcomes, amplifying pressure on the Public Ministry and Congress.[92] British diplomats joined counterparts in a December 13, 2023, op-ed opposing undemocratic obstructions.[94] These coordinated measures, alongside domestic mobilization, constrained institutional resistance, facilitating Arévalo's inauguration on January 14, 2024, as Borrell's attendance underscored sustained commitment.[93][92]

Inauguration on January 14, 2024

The inauguration of Bernardo Arévalo as president and Karin Herrera as vice president, set for noon on January 14, 2024, at the Legislative Palace in Guatemala City, faced immediate delays as outgoing lawmakers from the conservative-controlled Congress withheld certification of credentials for incoming officials.[95][89] This stalling tactic extended the proceedings for over nine hours, amid heightened security and public anticipation outside the venue.[96][97] Arévalo and Herrera were ultimately sworn in shortly after midnight on January 15, with Arévalo taking the constitutional oath before Supreme Court justices and assembled dignitaries, marking the formal transfer of power from interim President Alejandro Giammattei.[98][99] In his inaugural address, Arévalo declared the event a "new spring for democracy," pledging to combat corruption, strengthen institutions, and uphold the rule of law as the foundation of his administration's agenda.[89][95] The ceremony proceeded without major incidents, though it underscored persistent institutional tensions, with Arévalo's Semilla Movement holding a minority in the legislature.[98] International observers, including a U.S. presidential delegation led by Secretary of State Antony Blinken, attended to affirm support for the democratic process.[100] The successful power transition, following months of legal and political challenges, was hailed by organizations like the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights as a triumph of popular will over obstructionist efforts.[101] Arévalo's ascension positioned him as Guatemala's most progressive leader since the return to civilian rule in the 1980s, inheriting a nation grappling with entrenched corruption and socioeconomic disparities.[99]

Reactions and Assessments

Domestic Viewpoints Across Spectrum

Supporters of Bernardo Arévalo and the Movimiento Semilla, spanning progressive civil society, urban youth, and anti-corruption activists, hailed the August 20, 2023, runoff victory—securing 61% of the vote—as a rejection of Guatemala's corrupt "pacto de corruptos" elite, crediting voter turnout amid institutional barriers for enabling democratic renewal.[6] Indigenous organizations, including the 48 cantons of Totonicapán and other Mayan groups, mobilized mass protests post-election to safeguard the results, framing Arévalo's win as aligned with demands for justice against systemic graft that disproportionately harms rural and indigenous populations.[102] These groups emphasized empirical evidence from the Supreme Electoral Tribunal's certification of results, dismissing reversal attempts as authoritarian overreach by unelected officials.[7] In contrast, backers of Sandra Torres and the Unidad Nacional de la Esperanza (UNE), representing a centrist-populist establishment faction, initially contested first-round irregularities on June 25, 2023, though Torres conceded the runoff defeat; subsequent alliances with judicial holdovers, including Attorney General Consuelo Porras, pursued Semilla's suspension via alleged administrative violations, portraying Arévalo's ascent as procedurally flawed and a risk to institutional stability.[103] This viewpoint, echoed in filings to the Constitutional Court, prioritized fidelity to bureaucratic norms over the electoral mandate, with UNE legislators decrying potential disruptions to social welfare programs Torres had championed.[104] Conservative and business-oriented sectors, including parties like Valor and Vamos and chambers of commerce, expressed wariness toward Arévalo's platform, warning of economic volatility from promised judicial reforms and indigenous rights expansions that could deter investment; pre-runoff analyses highlighted fears of ideological overreach mirroring his father Juan José Arévalo's 1940s tenure, prioritizing market predictability over prosecutorial pursuits against allied elites.[21] Rural landowners and evangelical communities, often aligned with disqualified conservative candidate Zury Ríos Sosa's base, viewed the outcome as a leftist surge undermining traditional values and security, citing Semilla's limited congressional seats (23 of 160) as insufficient for governance without compromising core principles.[105] These perspectives underscored causal links between unchecked anti-corruption drives and potential capital flight, drawing on historical precedents of policy-induced instability.[10]

International Observations and Interventions

The Organization of American States (OAS) deployed an Electoral Observation Mission comprising 90 experts and observers from 20 countries to monitor Guatemala's 2023 general elections, assessing aspects including electoral organization, technology, voting processes, and vote counting in both the first round on June 25 and the runoff on August 20.[44][16] The mission's preliminary report for the second round noted that voting occurred in a generally peaceful environment despite isolated incidents of violence and intimidation, though it highlighted irregularities such as discrepancies in tally sheets and delays in result transmission in certain polling stations.[44] The European Union Election Observation Mission (EU EOM), active from May to September 2023, evaluated the pre-electoral, electoral, and immediate post-electoral phases, deploying over 80 observers across the country.[43][106] Its preliminary statement on August 22, 2023, affirmed that the second round proceeded calmly with high participation, but identified persistent challenges like unequal campaign financing favoring established parties and vulnerabilities in the electronic transmission system exploited by unsubstantiated fraud claims.[106] Following post-election attempts to suspend the victorious Semilla party's registration and block Bernardo Arévalo's inauguration, international bodies escalated interventions to defend the results. The OAS Permanent Council adopted a resolution on November 15, 2023, invoking concerns under Chapter IV of the Inter-American Democratic Charter regarding threats to democratic institutions, urging Guatemalan authorities to respect the electoral outcome and cease judicial overreach.[107] The United States imposed visa restrictions on July 19, 2023, under Section 353 of the National Defense Authorization Act against nine Guatemalan judges and prosecutors for actions undermining democratic processes, including targeting independent media and electoral officials, with additional sanctions on dozens more by October and November 2023.[108][109] The European Union issued statements condemning institutional sabotage and, on February 2, 2024, sanctioned five individuals—including key judicial and prosecutorial figures—for serious human rights violations and efforts to subvert the rule of law, building on earlier diplomatic pressure to facilitate the January 14, 2024, inauguration.[110][111] These measures, coordinated with regional partners, emphasized adherence to constitutional norms and contributed to resolving the transition crisis without altering the certified vote tallies.[111]

Broader Implications

Democratic Process Evaluation

The electoral phase of the 2023 Guatemalan general election, encompassing the first round on June 25 and runoff on August 20, was characterized by professional administration by the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE), with international observers reporting generally peaceful voting and transparent preliminary results dissemination via the TREP system. The European Union Election Observation Mission (EU EOM) assessed polling positively in 97% of observed stations in the first round and 100% in the second, noting efficient vote counting in 91% and 100% of cases respectively, though isolated incidents of violence, vote-buying, and intimidation occurred. Similarly, the Organization of American States (OAS) Electoral Observation Mission observed calm operations at over 1,300 stations in the runoff, with TREP processing all tally sheets by late evening, confirming Movimiento Semilla's victory at approximately 58%. Voter turnout declined from 60.5% to 45.1%, reflecting apathy amid prior disqualifications and judicial interventions, but no evidence emerged of systemic fraud altering outcomes.[43][44][43] Post-election, the democratic process faced severe tests from institutional actors, including repeated raids on TSE facilities by the Attorney General's Office, attempts to suspend Semilla's legal personality over pre-existing signature irregularities, and disinformation campaigns alleging fraud, which eroded public trust and delayed certification. The EU EOM highlighted judicial overreach and abuse of state resources by incumbents as undermining legal certainty, while the OAS expressed concern over actions threatening TSE autonomy and the rule of law. These maneuvers, often linked to entrenched elites resisting anti-corruption challengers, exemplified capture of prosecutorial and legislative branches, contrasting with the TSE's relative independence during voting.[43][112][113] Resolution came through Constitutional Court rulings upholding TSE certifications and Semilla's status, bolstered by widespread indigenous-led protests and international diplomatic pressure from entities like the OAS and EU, enabling Arévalo's inauguration on January 14, 2024. This outcome demonstrated democratic resilience via judicial checks, civic mobilization, and external scrutiny, yet exposed systemic vulnerabilities in separation of powers and elite pacts that prioritize impunity over voter sovereignty. Empirical indicators—such as Arévalo's 58-61% runoff margin and observer validations—affirm the elections captured genuine public preference for reform, but the protracted transition underscores Guatemala's democracy as functional yet precarious, requiring strengthened institutional independence to prevent recurrence.[43][44][112]

Long-Term Political Shifts

The 2023 election of Bernardo Arévalo and the Semilla Movement marked a potential rupture in Guatemala's entrenched elite pacts, known as the "pacto de corruptos," which had dominated politics through corruption and judicial capture for decades.[6] Arévalo's victory, driven by widespread disillusionment with traditional parties, elevated a new center-left force focused on anti-corruption and institutional reform, contrasting with the dominance of establishment figures like Sandra Torres.[42] This outcome reflected heightened civil society mobilization, including Indigenous-led protests that defended electoral integrity against post-vote sabotage attempts, signaling a broader demand for accountability over elite continuity.[6][102] However, Semilla's marginal congressional presence—holding only 23 of 160 seats—has constrained transformative shifts, forcing reliance on negotiations with parties implicated in prior corruption networks.[114] Institutional resistance persists, exemplified by Attorney General Consuelo Porras's ongoing investigations into Arévalo and attempts to dissolve Semilla, underscoring the judiciary's capture by legacy elites despite international sanctions on Porras.[9] Incremental reforms, such as the 2024 Competition Law to curb monopolies and a 3.9% homicide reduction via anti-extortion task forces, indicate modest progress in transparency and security, bolstered by dialogues with Indigenous communities on land rights.[114] Yet, high cabinet turnover and elite opposition, including from business groups resisting wage hikes, have eroded public approval from 54% in September 2024, highlighting the fragility of these gains.[114][115] As of early 2025, these dynamics suggest nascent realignments toward merit-based governance and reduced violence, with 4% projected economic growth aiding stability, but entrenched power structures continue to impede systemic overhaul.[115] Long-term prospects hinge on Arévalo's ability to navigate congressional gridlock and judicial obstruction through 2028; sustained civil society pressure and international backing could entrench anti-corruption norms, potentially fragmenting traditional party dominance, though reversion to elite control remains a credible risk absent deeper institutional reforms.[9][6]
