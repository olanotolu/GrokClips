Photonic molecule

Photonic molecules are a form of matter in which photons bind together to form molecules.[1] Bound states of photons were first predicted in 1992.[1] The term "photonic molecule" was first introduced in 1998 to describe photons confined to two or more coupled optical cavities that reproduce the physics of interacting atomic energy levels.[2] In 2007, theoretical models advanced the understanding of strongly interacting photons in one-dimensional waveguides.[3][4] Photonic molecules consisting of bound photons were experimentally realized in 2013.[5][6] In these systems, individual massless photons interact so strongly that they act as though they have mass. Researchers drew analogies between the phenomenon and the fictional lightsaber from Star Wars.[5][6]

Photon Molecules Construction

Construction

Gaseous rubidium atoms were pumped into a vacuum chamber. The cloud was cooled using lasers to just a few degrees above absolute zero. Using weak laser pulses, small numbers of photons were fired into the cloud.[6] As the photons entered the cloud, their energy excited atoms along their path, causing them to lose speed. Inside the cloud medium, the photons dispersively coupled to strongly interacting atoms in highly excited Rydberg states. This caused the photons to behave as massive particles with strong mutual attraction (photon molecules). Eventually the photons exited the cloud together as normal photons (often entangled in pairs).[6] The effect is caused by a so-called Rydberg blockade, which, in the presence of one excited atom, prevents nearby atoms from being excited to the same degree. In this case, as two photons enter the atomic cloud, the first excites an atom, annihilating itself in the interaction, but the transmitted energy must move forward inside the excited atom before the second photon can excite nearby atoms. In effect the two photons push and pull each other through the cloud as their energy is passed from one atom to the next, forcing them to interact. This photonic interaction is mediated by the electromagnetic interaction between photons and atoms.[6] Subsequent experiments have extended this to three-photon bound states using similar Rydberg atom setups.[7]

Possible applications

The interaction of the photons suggests that the effect could be employed to build a system that can preserve quantum information, and process it using quantum logic operations.[5] The system could also be useful in classical computing, considering the power-dissipation challenges chip-makers face.[5] It may be possible to arrange the photonic molecules in such a way within the medium that they form larger three-dimensional structures, such as crystals, wholly out of light.[5] Subsequent work has observed bound states of three photons, further supporting potential applications in quantum simulation and information processing, though practical implementations remain under development.[7]

Interacting optical cavities as photonic molecules

The term photonic molecule has been also used since 1998 for an unrelated phenomenon involving electromagnetically interacting optical microcavities. The properties of quantized confined photon states in optical micro- and nanocavities are very similar to those of confined electron states in atoms.[8] Owing to this similarity, optical microcavities can be termed 'photonic atoms'. Taking this analogy even further, a cluster of several mutually-coupled photonic atoms forms a photonic molecule.[9] When individual photonic atoms are brought into close proximity, their optical modes interact and give rise to a spectrum of hybridized super-modes of photonic molecules.[10] This is very similar to what happens when two isolated systems are coupled, like two hydrogen atomic orbitals coming together to form the bonding and antibonding orbitals of the hydrogen molecule, which are hybridized super-modes of the total coupled system. "A micrometer-sized piece of semiconductor can trap photons inside it in such a way that they act like electrons in an atom. Now the 21 September PRL describes a way to link two of these "photonic atoms" together. The result of such a close relationship is a "photonic molecule," whose optical modes bear a strong resemblance to the electronic states of a diatomic molecule like hydrogen."[11] "Photonic molecules, named by analogy with chemical molecules, are clusters of closely located electromagnetically interacting microcavities or "photonic atoms"."[12] "Optically coupled microcavities have emerged as photonic structures with promising properties for investigation of fundamental science as well as for applications."[13] The first photonic realization of the two-level system of a photonic molecule was by Spreeuw et al.,[14] who used optical fibers to realize a ring resonator, although they did not use the term "photonic molecule". The two modes forming the molecule could then be the polarization modes of the ring or the clockwise and counterclockwise modes of the ring. This was followed by the demonstration of a lithographically fabricated photonic molecule, inspired by an analogy with a simple diatomic molecule.[2] However, other nature-inspired PM structures (such as â€˜photonic benzeneâ€™) have been proposed and shown to support confined optical modes closely analogous to the ground-state molecular orbitals of their chemical counterparts.[15] Photonic molecules offer advantages over isolated photonic atoms in a variety of applications, including bio(chemical) sensing,[16][17] cavity optomechanics,[18][19] and microlasers,[20][21][22][23] Photonic molecules can also be used as quantum simulators of many-body physics and as building blocks of future optical quantum information processing networks.[24] As of 2025, they have found use in generating stable platicons for microcomb applications and in miniaturized spectrometers surpassing traditional resolution-bandwidth limits.[25][26] In complete analogy, clusters of metal nanoparticles â€“ which support confined surface plasmon states â€“ have been termed â€˜plasmonic molecules.â€[27][28][29][30][31] Finally, hybrid photonic-plasmonic (or opto-plasmonic)[32][33][34][35] and elastic molecules[36] have also been proposed and demonstrated.
