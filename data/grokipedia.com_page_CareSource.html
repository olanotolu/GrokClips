CareSource

CareSource is a nonprofit managed care organization headquartered in Dayton, Ohio, founded in 1989 by Pamela Morris to expand health care access for underserved populations through Ohio's first Medicaid health maintenance organization.[1][2] It administers government-sponsored health plans, primarily Medicaid, Medicare, and Marketplace products, serving over 2 million members across 14 states and generating approximately $13.1 billion in annual revenue.[2][3][4] The organization has grown into one of the largest Medicaid managed care providers in the United States, emphasizing member-centric care, community investment, and a defined network of health care providers to improve outcomes for low-income and vulnerable populations.[2][5] CareSource's mission focuses on transforming health care delivery by coordinating services, managing costs, and supporting preventive care, with initiatives including grants totaling $28.5 million for community health programs.[4] Its nonprofit structure directs administrative expensesâ€”reported at about 9.7% of revenueâ€”toward operational efficiency rather than shareholder profits.[6] CareSource has faced legal challenges, notably multiple class action lawsuits following a 2023 MOVEit software vulnerability that exposed personal health information of over 3 million individuals, with plaintiffs alleging inadequate data security measures.[7][8][9] Earlier disputes have included provider payment claims, such as underpayment allegations from hospitals spanning 2001 to 2011.[10] Despite these issues, CareSource maintains recognition for its scale and focus on public health programs.[11]

Founding and History

Establishment and Early Development (1989â€“1990s)

CareSource was founded in May 1989 as the Dayton Area Health Plan by Pamela B. Morris, a social worker and social entrepreneur, in Montgomery County, Ohio.[12][1] Initially funded by a $500,000 state grant, the organization pioneered Ohio's first mandatory Medicaid managed care program for low-income families, overcoming regulatory challenges by obtaining an HMO license from the Ohio Department of Insurance and a federal waiver from the Health Care Financing Administration.[13][14] Starting with 38 employees, it emphasized coordinated care to address fragmented services for underserved populations, marking a shift from traditional fee-for-service Medicaid toward proactive health management.[12][15] In 1993, the Dayton Area Health Plan separated from its affiliate, Western Ohio Health Care, rebranded as CareSource to better reflect its expanded mission, and relocated to downtown Dayton.[16][12] That year, the original federal waiver expired, requiring plans to maintain a 75% mandatory to 25% voluntary enrollment ratio, but U.S. Congressman Dave Hobson secured an exception allowing continued operations.[17] CareSource also collaborated with state regulators to launch the Preferred Option initiative, which integrated enhanced primary care and preventive services for Medicaid recipients.[12] These developments solidified its role in Ohio's evolving Medicaid landscape, where managed care enrollment rose from about 11% in 1990 to broader adoption amid state efforts to control costs and improve outcomes.[14] The mid-to-late 1990s saw initial territorial expansions through acquisitions. In 1996, CareSource entered the Columbus market by purchasing United Healthcare's Medicaid plan, which operated under the MedPlan name and elevated CareSource to Ohio's largest Medicaid HMO by enrollment.[17] The next year, it acquired the Butler Health Plan, incorporating roughly 6,000 additional members from Butler County and extending its provider network.[17] These moves, amid Ohio's push toward statewide mandatory managed care, positioned CareSource for sustained growth, with membership building toward approximately 70,000 by 2000 while maintaining a non-profit focus on quality metrics and community reinvestment.[17][18]

State Expansions and Growth (2000s)

In 2000, the Dayton Area Health Plan, CareSource's predecessor, consolidated its various health plans under the single brand name CareSource, serving approximately 74,000 Medicaid members primarily in Ohio.[12] This rebranding streamlined operations and supported ongoing enrollment growth amid increasing demand for managed care services in the state's Medicaid program. By 2004, membership had expanded to nearly 395,000, positioning CareSource as one of the leading providers in Ohio's market, where it enrolled over half of all Medicaid managed care participants.[12] Further growth in the mid-2000s included statewide service expansion, with CareSource becoming the only Medicaid managed care organization to offer plans across all 88 Ohio counties by 2007.[17] In 2004, an industry analysis by Interstudy ranked it the sixth-largest Medicaid managed care plan nationally, reflecting its scale and operational maturity.[17] The organization also launched internal initiatives, such as CareSource University in 2004 for employee training, and established the CareSource Foundation in 2006 to fund community health and non-profit programs in Ohio.[17] By 2008, membership surpassed 690,000, driven by Ohio's Medicaid enrollment trends and CareSource's focus on provider network development and member retention.[12] To accommodate this expansion, the company broke ground on a $55 million, 300,000-square-foot corporate headquarters in downtown Dayton, completed in 2009, which centralized administrative functions and symbolized its infrastructural investments amid sustained revenue and enrollment increases.[17]

National Scaling and Key Milestones (2010sâ€“Present)

In the early 2010s, CareSource experienced significant growth driven by the Affordable Care Act's Medicaid expansions, increasing its membership to approximately 853,000 by 2010, primarily in Ohio, with projections for at least 400,000 additional enrollees nationwide as states broadened eligibility.[19] This positioned the organization as one of the largest Medicaid managed care providers, with revenue reaching $2.5 billion annually by that year, reflecting its focus on underserved populations through non-profit managed care models.[17] The mid-2010s marked initial interstate expansions beyond Ohio, including entry into the Kentucky Marketplace in 2017â€“2018, where it became the sole insurer in half of the state's counties, and preparations for Indiana operations announced in 2016 for launch the following year, initially emphasizing Medicare Advantage and later securing Medicaid contracts.[20][21] A pivotal milestone occurred on July 1, 2017, when CareSource began serving about 200,000 Georgia Medicaid beneficiaries, extending its model of coordinated care to the Southeast and establishing a presence in rural and urban provider networks.[22] Entering the 2020s, CareSource accelerated national scaling through targeted programs for complex needs populations. Business Model as a Non-Profit Managed Care Organization

CareSource functions as a nonprofit managed care organization (MCO) by contracting with state Medicaid agencies to deliver comprehensive health coverage to low-income and vulnerable populations, primarily through capitated arrangements where it receives fixed per-member-per-month (PMPM) payments to assume financial risk for medical services. These capitation revenues, which form the core of its funding, are recognized monthly as the organization becomes obligated to provide care, encompassing risk-adjusted rates that factor in enrollee demographics, health status, and acuity to promote equitable resource allocation. In fiscal year 2023, such payments underpinned operations across multiple states, with supplementary incentives like pay-for-performance bonusesâ€”capped at up to 1.8% of total capitation in Ohio and Indiana for achieving quality benchmarks in areas such as healthy weight and high-risk populations.[35][36][37] Administrative and medical costs are managed via utilization review, provider network contracting, and care coordination programs aimed at reducing unnecessary hospitalizations and emergency visits while emphasizing preventive and chronic disease management. Premium revenues exclude direct member copays in most Medicaid contexts but may incorporate state reimbursements for add-on services, quality withhold recoveries, and waivers, ensuring compliance with federal medical loss ratio standards that mandate at least 85-95% of premiums be directed toward clinical care. This model incentivizes efficiency, as underutilization risks revenue shortfalls while overutilization erodes margins, fostering data-driven interventions like population health analytics.[38][37] Unlike for-profit MCOs, CareSource's nonprofit status precludes shareholder dividends, mandating reinvestment of surpluses into operational enhancements, member benefits expansions, and community health initiatives rather than profit extraction. For example, in 2024, the organization allocated resources toward social determinants of health, including a $5 million affordable housing fund in Ohio's Appalachian regions and broader commitments to digital equity and poverty alleviation, reflecting a structure that prioritizes sustained member outcomes over quarterly returns. This approach, governed by IRS 501(c)(3) requirements and state regulations, has supported growth to over 2 million members across 14 states by 2025, with audited financials showing net assets directed toward reserves and program scalability.[39][40][24]

CareSource actively expands its provider base in new markets, such as through targeted recruitment in states like Georgia and Indiana, to meet regulatory network adequacy standards ensuring timely access, including requirements for provider-to-member ratios and geographic distribution. Members utilize an online directory tool to search for in-network providers by specialty, location, and availability, supporting continuity of care.[44][45][46] Care delivery occurs via a coordinated managed care model prioritizing person-centered planning and multidisciplinary teams, where care managersâ€”assigned as a single point of contactâ€”conduct assessments, develop individualized care plans, and collaborate with PCPs and specialists to address physical, behavioral, and social needs. This includes targeted programs for chronic disease management, such as diabetes or asthma, with 24/7 support lines and transitional care post-hospitalization to reduce readmissions. Telehealth integration enables virtual delivery of services like sick visits and behavioral health consultations, covered when clinically appropriate under state guidelines.[47][48][49] To incentivize efficient, high-quality outcomes, CareSource implements value-based reimbursement mechanisms, such as enhanced payments for providers meeting metrics on preventive screenings, care coordination, and reduced utilization of high-cost services; in Georgia, provider participation in these programs rose 23% from 2021 to 2022, correlating with improved member health metrics like lower emergency department visits. Utilization management tools, including prior authorization for non-emergent procedures, ensure evidence-based delivery while maintaining provider reimbursement at negotiated fee-for-service or capitated rates.[50][51][52]

Programs and Services

CareSource administers Medicaid managed care plans under contracts with state agencies, delivering capitated coverage for medically necessary services to eligible low-income populations, including pregnant women, children, families, seniors, and individuals with disabilities. Core benefits encompass primary and specialty physician care, inpatient and outpatient hospital services, emergency treatment, laboratory and radiology diagnostics, and prescription drug coverage through a managed pharmacy benefit, all provided at no cost or with nominal copays where applicable by state rules. In Ohio, its foundational market, these plans emphasize network adequacy with access to over 90% of the state's providers, ensuring coordinated delivery to prevent fragmented care.[53][54] Behavioral health integration forms a pillar of offerings, providing outpatient therapy, psychiatric services, substance use disorder treatment, and 24/7 crisis hotlines, often bundled with physical health to facilitate holistic management. Preventive services, such as immunizations, screenings, and well-child visits, are prioritized to improve long-term outcomes, supported by non-emergency medical transportation to appointments and pharmacies. Dental coverage typically includes preventive and restorative care for children, with adult expansions in select states like Georgia, alongside limited vision benefits for exams and glasses.[55][56] Value-added features distinguish CareSource's model, including rewards incentives like Babies FirstÂ® for maternal care milestones (e.g., prenatal visits earning points redeemable for essentials like groceries or gas) and Kids First for pediatric preventive adherence, fostering member engagement without financial barriers. Criticisms of Performance Data and Metrics

In 2011, CareSource Management Group Co. and affiliated entities settled with the U.S. Department of Justice for $26 million to resolve allegations under the False Claims Act that they failed to deliver required health education, counseling, and screening services to pregnant Medicaid enrollees between 2002 and 2008, while submitting false quarterly reports to the Ohio Department of Job and Family Services claiming full compliance.[114] These reports were tied to contractual performance benchmarks, where noncompliance risked penalties or reduced capitation payments, raising concerns about the integrity of self-reported data used to evaluate managed care performance.[115] External quality review organizations (EQROs) conduct annual validations of performance measures, including HEDIS scores, for Ohio Medicaid managed care plans like CareSource, assessing data accuracy through audits of medical records and administrative claims.[116] While CareSource's measures have generally passed these validations without noted discrepancies in recent EQRO technical reports (e.g., SFY 2023-2024), the historical falsification incident underscores broader risks in Medicaid managed care, where plans face incentives to meet or exceed metrics for star ratings, NCQA accreditation, and state report cards that influence enrollment and funding.[117] No substantiated recent allegations of metric manipulation have emerged from regulatory audits or litigation specific to CareSource's quality reporting. However, a 2024 whistleblower lawsuit in Indiana accused CareSource and other plans of submitting improper inpatient claims that could inflate performance on readmission and utilization metrics, though the case was dismissed by a federal judge in October 2025 for lack of evidence.[118] This reflects ongoing regulatory emphasis on verifying claims data underlying quality scores, as unvalidated submissions could distort assessments of care coordination and outcomes.

Controversies and Legal Challenges

Data Security Breaches and Cybersecurity Failures

In May 2023, CareSource was impacted by a global cybersecurity incident involving the MOVEit file transfer software vulnerability exploited by the Clop ransomware group, resulting in the unauthorized access and exfiltration of sensitive member data.[7][119] The breach affected approximately 3 million individuals nationwide, including personally identifiable information (PII) such as names, addresses, dates of birth, Social Security numbers, and protected health information (PHI) like medical diagnoses and treatment details.[120][8] CareSource confirmed the incident on June 27, 2023, after an investigation revealed that hackers had accessed and copied data from the MOVEit server used by a third-party vendor, though the company stated that its own systems were not directly compromised.[121][122] The incident prompted multiple class action lawsuits alleging negligence in CareSource's data security practices, including failure to implement adequate safeguards against known vulnerabilities and delays in notification to affected members.[7][9] Plaintiffs sought damages exceeding $9.9 million, claiming the breach exposed members to risks of identity theft and privacy violations.[9] In response, CareSource offered two years of complimentary credit and identity monitoring services to impacted members, including fraud consultation and restoration assistance, starting from notifications issued in August 2023.[123][122] Among the affected, approximately 212,193 Indiana Medicaid members had their PII and PHI compromised, as reported by state officials.[124][125] Separately, in early 2024, CareSource notified providers of a cybersecurity incident at its trading partner Change Healthcare, which disrupted claims processing but did not involve direct data exfiltration from CareSource's systems; mitigation efforts were handled by intermediary Availity.[126] In August 2023, the Clop group publicly leaked a 40 GB dataset purportedly stolen from CareSource via a fourth-party vendor in the Medicaid ecosystem, highlighting supply chain risks in healthcare data handling.[127] No additional major direct breaches were reported through October 2025, though the MOVEit event underscored ongoing vulnerabilities in vendor-managed file transfer tools within managed care organizations.[128]

Litigation Involving Fraud Allegations and Member Disputes

In 2011, CareSource Management Group Co. and affiliated entities agreed to pay $26 million to settle False Claims Act allegations brought by two former employees acting as whistleblowers.[114][135] The suit, filed in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Ohio, claimed that from 2001 to 2006, the company enrolled children with special mental health needs in Ohio's Medicaid program but knowingly failed to deliver required screening, assessment, and case management services, while submitting claims to Medicaid implying the services had been provided.[114] Of the settlement, Ohio received $17.4 million and the federal government $8.6 million; CareSource did not admit liability.[135] More recently, a 2024 whistleblower lawsuit in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Indiana accused CareSource and other managed care organizations of Medicaid fraud through improper billing practices, including claims for unrendered services and upcoding patient diagnoses to inflate payments.[118][136] In October 2025, a federal judge dismissed most claims against CareSource, ruling that the allegations lacked sufficient specificity to establish liability under the False Claims Act.[118] Regarding member disputes, a class action lawsuit filed in 2018 by plaintiffs Neha Desai and others in Montgomery County Common Pleas Court (removed to federal court as Case No. 3:18-cv-00118) alleged that inaccuracies in CareSource's provider directories misled members into using out-of-network providers, resulting in higher out-of-pocket costs and premiums than promised under in-network coverage.[137][138] The complaint asserted claims of breach of contract, insurance bad faith, negligent misrepresentation, constructive fraud, and unjust enrichment, arguing that members conferred benefits on CareSource via premiums but received substandard network access.[137] In 2024, the Ohio Second District Court of Appeals addressed aspects of the case, including whether directory errors breached contractual obligations to members, though class certification efforts faced challenges.[137] The case highlights ongoing tensions over network adequacy but remains unresolved in full as of available records.[137]

Debates on Managed Care Efficacy in Public Programs

Managed care organizations (MCOs) in public programs like Medicaid aim to control costs through capitation payments while promoting coordinated care, but debates persist over their net efficacy compared to traditional fee-for-service (FFS) models. Empirical studies show mixed results: some evidence indicates improvements in specific outcomes, such as a 2020 analysis of Ohio Medicaid data revealing that targeted care management in MCOs reduced infant mortality rates by enhancing prenatal and postpartum interventions for high-risk enrollees.[152] However, broader reviews find no definitive superiority in access, quality, or satisfaction; for instance, a 2023 MACPAC assessment of multiple studies concluded that managed care neither consistently improves nor worsens beneficiary outcomes relative to FFS, with variations depending on state implementation and population.[153] Critics argue that reported cost savings often stem from utilization restrictions or cost-shifting rather than systemic efficiencies, potentially compromising care. A 2009 examination of California's Medi-Cal program demonstrated that managed care expansions yielded apparent fiscal benefits for MCOs but correlated with higher downstream FFS expenditures, suggesting shifted burdens rather than genuine reductions in total program costs.[154] Similarly, a 2019 study across states linked MCO efficiency metrics to insignificant impacts on care quality, though higher medical loss ratiosâ€”indicating greater spending on services versus administrationâ€”showed a modest positive association with performance ratings.[155] These findings fuel skepticism about whether capitation incentivizes prevention or merely rationing, especially in vulnerable populations. In the context of providers like CareSource, which operates extensively in Ohio's Medicaid managed care, state evaluations highlight strengths in member satisfaction and targeted metrics; the Ohio Department of Medicaid's 2019 report card ranked CareSource tied for first in overall quality based on HEDIS and CAHPS measures.[86] Yet, debates extend to data reliability, as encounter reporting discrepancies in Ohio's 2022 validation study revealed match rates below 90% for some plans, raising questions about the accuracy of performance claims and their translation to real-world efficacy.[156] One empirical concern is care fragmentation: a 2019 analysis found Medicaid managed care enrollees had more ambulatory providers but fewer visits than FFS counterparts, potentially undermining coordination despite administrative promises.[157] Overall, while managed care dominates Medicaid enrollmentâ€”covering over 70% of beneficiaries by 2024â€”causal evidence on long-term fiscal and health gains remains inconclusive, with outcomes hinging on rigorous oversight and non-profit structures like CareSource's.[158]
