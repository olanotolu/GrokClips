Anwar Choudhury

Anwar Bakht Choudhury (born 15 June 1959) is a retired British diplomat of Bangladeshi origin whose career spanned military service, private sector roles, and high-level Foreign and Commonwealth Office (FCO) positions, culminating in a brief tenure as Governor of the Cayman Islands.[1][2] Choudhury joined the FCO in 2003 following experience in the Royal Air Force, Ministry of Defence, Cabinet Office as a director, and private sector work.[1] His diplomatic assignments included serving as British High Commissioner to Bangladesh from 2004 to 2008, during which he became the first British diplomat from an ethnic minority background to hold such a senior post overseas; Director of International Institutions at the FCO in 2008, focusing on multilateral policy; and British Ambassador to Peru prior to 2018, managing embassy operations across political, trade, and consular affairs.[1][3] He received an Institute of Government award for diplomatic excellence.[1] Appointed Governor of the Cayman Islands on 26 March 2018, Choudhury was recalled to London in June 2018 after less than three months in post, pending an FCO investigation into multiple complaints against him.[1][4] Reported allegations included bullying and shouting at domestic and office staff, drunken behavior, and mistreatment of his wife, though the investigation's detailed outcome was not publicly disclosed and some local commentary suggested procedural issues with the recall process.[5][6][7] He did not return to the governorship and retired from UK government service in August 2023.[8]

Early Life and Education

Birth and Family Background

Anwar Choudhury was born on 15 June 1959 in Sunamganj, Sylhet Division, East Pakistan (now Bangladesh).[9][10] His family originated from the Sylhet region, and he immigrated to the United Kingdom as a child.[3] Choudhury is married to Momina Choudhury and is the father of two children, a son and a daughter.[3]

Formal Education and Early Influences

Choudhury earned a Bachelor of Science degree in Electrical and Electronic Engineering from the University of Salford in 1985.[3] [11] He subsequently obtained a Master of Business Administration from Durham University, which equipped him with business acumen prior to his entry into public service.[3] [11] His engineering education reflected a technical foundation that contrasted with the typical humanities-oriented path of many diplomats, influencing an unconventional career trajectory that began in the private sector and military before diplomacy.[3] This interdisciplinary background, combining technical expertise with managerial training, likely shaped his strategic approach to subsequent roles in telecommunications and defense.[3] Specific early personal influences beyond family origins remain undocumented in available records, though his relocation from Bangladesh to the United Kingdom as a young immigrant may have fostered resilience and adaptability in professional pursuits.[2]

Pre-Diplomatic Career

Private Sector Employment

Prior to entering public service, Anwar Choudhury began his professional career in the United Kingdom's industrial sector as a design engineer.[3][2] This role marked his entry into the private sector following his education, though specific employers, projects, or duration remain undocumented in official biographies.[1] Choudhury's time in industry provided foundational engineering experience before transitioning to military and government positions, reflecting a career path that combined technical expertise with subsequent public sector leadership.[3] No detailed records of achievements or innovations from this period are publicly available, and it preceded his service in the Royal Air Force.[2]

Service in the Royal Air Force

Choudhury entered British civil service through a role as an engineering strategist with the Royal Air Force, following employment in the private sector at Siemens Plessey as a consultant engineer.[3][12] This civilian position, beginning around 1985, involved strategic engineering contributions to RAF operations, though specific projects or duration beyond the initial entry point remain undocumented in official biographies.[1][13] His RAF service preceded subsequent roles in the Ministry of Defence, where he advanced to positions such as Principal Private Secretary to the Secretary of State for Defence from 1992 to 1995.[13] The strategist role highlighted his technical expertise in defense engineering, bridging private industry experience with public sector demands for procurement and planning efficiency.[14]

Diplomatic Career

Entry into the Foreign and Commonwealth Office

Anwar Choudhury entered the Foreign and Commonwealth Office (FCO) in 2003, transitioning from a senior role as Director in the Cabinet Office, where he had served since 2000.[9][13] Prior to that, his career encompassed service in the Royal Air Force, the Ministry of Defence, and private sector positions, providing him with experience in security, defense, and executive management.[1][15] This move to the FCO positioned him for accelerated advancement within the diplomatic service, as he was transferred specifically to prepare for a high-profile posting.[3] Within a year, in 2004, Choudhury was appointed British High Commissioner to Bangladesh, his country of origin, reflecting the FCO's recognition of his expertise in international institutions and regional affairs despite his relatively recent entry into diplomacy.[13][16] His rapid elevation underscored a fast-track trajectory, atypical for late entrants, leveraging his prior governmental and military background to address strategic diplomatic needs in South Asia.[3]

High Commissioner to Bangladesh (2004â€“2008)

Anwar Choudhury, a British diplomat of Bangladeshi origin, was appointed High Commissioner to Bangladesh in December 2003, becoming the first individual from an ethnic minority background to hold such a senior role in the Foreign and Commonwealth Office.[9] He took up the position in early May 2004, following service in the Cabinet Office.[3] On 21 May 2004, shortly after assuming office, Choudhury was injured in a grenade attack targeting him at the shrine of Shah Jalal in Sylhet, which killed three bystanders and wounded around 50 others.[3] [17] The assault was carried out by Islamist militants linked to groups such as Jama'atul Mujahedin Bangladesh, amid rising concerns over extremist activities in the country.[18] Three perpetrators received death sentences, upheld by Bangladesh's Supreme Court in 2017 and subsequently executed.[19] Choudhury's tenure, spanning four years until May 2008, occurred against a backdrop of political instability in Bangladesh, including the imposition of a military-backed caretaker government in January 2007 following allegations of electoral fraud.[20] He maintained that the United Kingdom had no role in these domestic developments, emphasizing diplomatic neutrality.[20] Upon completion of his term on 13 May 2008, he was succeeded by Stephen Evans and returned to the Foreign and Commonwealth Office as Director of International Institutions.[21] [1]

Senior Roles in the FCO (2008â€“2012)

Upon returning from his posting as British High Commissioner to Bangladesh in 2008, Anwar Choudhury was appointed Director of International Institutions at the Foreign and Commonwealth Office (FCO) in London.[1] In this senior role, he oversaw the development and coordination of the UK's multilateral policy, managing engagement with international organizations on global issues.[1] [16] Choudhury held the position from 2008 to 2011, leading a directorate at Senior Civil Service Pay Band 2 (SCS2) level responsible for directing UK strategy toward institutions like the United Nations and other multilateral bodies.[13] [22] During this period, his work focused on aligning FCO priorities with international diplomacy, though specific policy outcomes attributed directly to him remain limited in public records.[1] In 2012, Choudhury transitioned to Director of Diplomatic Excellence within the FCO, a role he held into 2013, where he led initiatives to enhance the professionalism and effectiveness of UK diplomatic operations.[13] This position involved internal reforms to improve FCO performance, building on his prior multilateral experience, and later earned recognition through an Institute of Government award for inspiration in government service.[1] These senior director-level postings marked a return to headquarters policy leadership before his subsequent ambassadorship.[4]

Ambassador to Peru (2012â€“2017)

Anwar Choudhury was appointed Her Majesty's Ambassador to Peru in May 2014, succeeding James Dauris, and took up the post in mid-2014.[15] In this role, he oversaw the British Embassy in Lima and its consulates, directing efforts to advance UK interests across political, economic, commercial, and consular domains.[1] His tenure emphasized strengthening bilateral ties amid Peru's growing economy and strategic importance in Latin America. Choudhury prioritized trade and investment promotion, leading six trade missions to double UK exports within five years, with targeted sectors including health (a $3 billion market), education, and energy.[23] Notable initiatives included a $100 million education deal for bilingual teacher training, involving over 800 Peruvian educators with UK visits, and a memorandum of understanding for health infrastructure and medical training.[23] In energy, he facilitated British solar technology deployment for remote Andean communities.[24] Community efforts under his ambassadorship encompassed blood donation campaigns and support for Peruvian children.[24] On judicial cooperation, Choudhury engaged Peru's judiciary in January 2015 to address shared challenges like illicit drug trafficking, money laundering, and corruption, advocating technology exchanges and potential agreements to streamline processes.[25] He also positioned the UK as a partner for Peru's hosting of the 2019 Pan American Games in Lima, leveraging London 2012 expertise through a 2017 Department for International Trade agreement for knowledge transfer in economic, urban, and sporting legacies.[26] Cultural and educational events, such as Shakespeare commemorations and university partnerships like NCUK with UPC Peru, further bolstered ties.[27] [28] Choudhury departed the post in 2017, succeeded by Kate Harrisson, to prepare for his next assignment as Governor of the Cayman Islands.[29] His efforts contributed to enhanced UK-Peru connectivity, including the resumption of direct British Airways flights from London to Lima after 32 years.[23]

Governor of the Cayman Islands (2018)

Anwar Choudhury was appointed Governor of the Cayman Islands on 26 June 2017 by the British government, succeeding Helen Kilpatrick whose term expired in March 2018.[13] He assumed office on 26 March 2018, becoming the 13th person to hold the position since the Cayman Islands' status as a British Overseas Territory was formalized.[30] Choudhury was sworn in during a ceremony at the Legislative Assembly in George Town, where he pledged to uphold the territory's constitution and serve under Queen Elizabeth II.[30][31] As Governor, Choudhury's responsibilities included representing the British monarch, overseeing defense, internal security, external affairs, and the civil service, while assenting to legislation passed by the Cayman Islands Parliament.[13] In his initial statements, he emphasized security as his foremost priority, followed by advancing human rights and environmental protection.[32] During his short time in office, he engaged in meetings with civil service staff and community members to foster relations, and requested supplementary funding of CI$148,000 to support expanded and extended social events aimed at public engagement.[33][34] Choudhury's tenure concluded on 20 September 2018, after he was temporarily recalled to London in mid-June 2018 amid unspecified complaints lodged against him.[4][35] He was succeeded by Martyn Roper, who took office on 29 October 2018.[36]

Controversies and Investigations

Grenade Attack and Islamist Terrorism Context

On May 21, 2004, Anwar Choudhury, then British High Commissioner to Bangladesh, was injured in a grenade attack while visiting the Hazrat Shah Jalal Shrine in Sylhet.[37] The assailants hurled a hand grenade at his convoy, killing three bystanders and wounding several others, including Choudhury's bodyguard and local civilians.[19] Bangladesh authorities convicted Mufti Abdul Hannan, a senior operative of the Islamist militant group Jamaat-ul-Mujahideen Bangladesh (JMB), and two accomplices of orchestrating the assault; all three were executed in April 2017 after exhausting appeals.[38] [39] The attack occurred amid a surge in Islamist terrorism in Bangladesh during the early 2000s, characterized by bombings and targeted killings aimed at enforcing sharia law and opposing secular governance. JMB, founded in the 1990s and influenced by Deobandi ideology with ties to al-Qaeda networks, claimed responsibility for multiple high-profile operations, including synchronized bombings across 63 district towns on August 17, 2005, which killed two and injured hundreds.[38] This period saw Islamist groups exploiting political instability, poverty, and madrassa-based radicalization to expand influence, with over 50 terrorist incidents reported between 2000 and 2005, often targeting symbols of Western or moderate Muslim authority.[17] Choudhury's targeting, despite his Bangladeshi heritage and Muslim faith, underscored the indiscriminate nature of these attacks on perceived apostates or foreign representatives, as JMB viewed diplomatic engagements at religious sites as provocative. Investigations revealed the plot was planned from JMB safe houses in Sylhet, a hub for militant recruitment, highlighting systemic failures in intelligence sharing and border controls that enabled cross-border training with Pakistani affiliates.[40] The British government responded by bolstering embassy security and pressing Dhaka for counterterrorism reforms, which contributed to a caretaker government's crackdown leading to JMB leaders' arrests in 2006.[37]

Allegations of Misconduct During Cayman Governorship

In June 2018, the UK Foreign and Commonwealth Office (FCO) recalled Anwar Choudhury from his role as Governor of the Cayman Islands to London for an investigation into multiple complaints lodged against him.[4] The FCO described the allegations as unspecified but confirmed they related to his conduct in office.[41] Reports in UK media outlets detailed claims from staff and household members, including accusations of bullying and shouting at employees, engaging in drunken argumentsâ€”including one with his mother-in-law at the governor's residenceâ€”and requesting that a maid perform a shirtless back massage.[42] [43] Additional complaints reportedly involved inappropriate behavior toward his wife and domestic staff.[44] These accounts, primarily from anonymous sources cited in tabloid and broadsheet coverage, prompted parliamentary questions in the UK about Choudhury's suitability, though no criminal charges were filed.[12] An internal FCO probe concluded without public disclosure of findings, but on September 20, 2018, officials announced Choudhury would not resume his governorship and would instead take a diplomatic posting in London.[45] Local Caymanian media and public campaigns highlighted support for Choudhury, portraying him as an effective administrator who had initiated governance reforms, and questioned the allegations' validity amid a perceived lack of transparency from the FCO.[7] Deputy Governor Franz Manderson assumed acting duties during the interim, with Martyn Roper sworn in as permanent replacement on October 29, 2018.[46] The episode drew criticism for the opacity of the process, with editorials decrying a "conspiracy of silence" over the unresolved claims.[47]

Claims of Involvement in Bangladesh Politics

During his tenure as British High Commissioner to Bangladesh from 2004 to 2008, Anwar Choudhury faced accusations from critics of excessive involvement in the country's domestic politics, particularly in the lead-up to and during the imposition of emergency rule on January 11, 2007 (known as "1/11"), when the military-backed caretaker government sidelined the major political parties to conduct anti-corruption reforms and electoral preparations.[21][48] Allegations suggested that Choudhury, alongside U.S. Ambassador Patricia Butenis, actively consulted with Bangladeshi military leaders such as Army Chief Lt. Gen. Moeen U. Ahmed and influenced the political transition by apprising opposition leader Sheikh Hasina of developments five days prior to the changeover.[48][49] These claims portrayed Western diplomats, including Choudhury, as supporting or facilitating the military's intervention to avert pre-election violence between the Awami League and Bangladesh Nationalist Party, amid broader assertions of foreign interference aimed at institutional overhaul.[50][51] Choudhury has repeatedly and categorically denied orchestrating or participating in the 1/11 events, stating in 2009 during a visit to Dhaka that he had no role in the political shift and rejecting any UK involvement in the caretaker government's takeover.[52][20][53] In a 2011 interview, he acknowledged that questions could legitimately be raised about diplomats' roles but emphasized that his objective was to assist in restoring democracy rather than meddle, while in 2008 he affirmed Britain's opposition to military intervention in politics.[54][55] No independent evidence from official UK inquiries or declassified documents has substantiated the allegations of direct orchestration, though Bangladeshi media outletsâ€”often aligned with partisan views on the 1/11 period, where the Awami League later condemned it as undemocraticâ€”have amplified claims of diplomatic overreach.[52][56] Beyond 1/11, Choudhury drew criticism for perceived proximity to Bangladeshi political figures, with detractors attributing undue influence to his Bangladeshi heritage and frequent engagements, though such accusations lacked specific documentation of impropriety and were voiced primarily upon his departure in May 2008.[21] These claims persisted in retrospective analyses but remained unverified by primary diplomatic records, reflecting tensions between Bangladesh's polarized political discourse and standard high commissioner advocacy for governance reforms.[50]

Personal Life and Later Years

Family and Relationships

Anwar Choudhury is married to Momina Choudhury.[57][3] The couple has four children, including an eldest son who was studying in the United Kingdom in 2018, and three daughters named Amani, Ambreene, and Zara.[32][57] In March 2018, upon arriving in the Cayman Islands as governor, Choudhury was accompanied by his wife and the three daughters, with the son expected to join later.[32] No public details are available regarding any prior marriages or additional relationships.[16][58]

Retirement and Reflections

Anwar Choudhury announced his retirement from UK government service on August 26, 2023, concluding a diplomatic career that spanned roles including High Commissioner to Bangladesh (2004â€“2008), senior positions in the Foreign and Commonwealth Office (2008â€“2012), and Ambassador to Peru (2012â€“2017), followed by a brief tenure as Governor of the Cayman Islands in 2018.[8] In his retirement statement, Choudhury shared reflections prompted by a recent visit from Caymanian friends, expressing nostalgia for the islands and regret over not having properly farewelled the community earlier. He conveyed gratitude for the "great support and affection" extended to his family during his time as governor, noting that media coverage of local reactions had left him "misty-eyed" and emotionally moved.[8] Choudhury emphasized the warmth of the Caymanian people, describing them as "warm, generous and delightful," and expressed hope that he and his family could visit in the future. His message wished the islands "every success and happiness for the years ahead," underscoring a personal fondness for the community despite the brevity and challenges of his governorship.[8]
