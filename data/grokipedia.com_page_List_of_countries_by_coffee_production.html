List of countries by coffee production

The list of countries by coffee production ranks sovereign states according to their annual harvest of coffee beans from the Coffea arabica (arabica) and Coffea canephora (robusta) species, measured typically in thousands of 60-kilogram bags or metric tons, with output concentrated in equatorial "Bean Belt" regions suitable for the crop's tropical requirements.[1] Brazil dominates the ranking, producing 64.7 million bags in the 2024/25 season to claim 37% of global totals, a position held since the early 1800s due to vast suitable land, advanced agrotechnology, and economies of scale in both arabica and robusta varieties.[2] Vietnam ranks second with 29 million bags (17% share), specializing in robusta for its resilience to pests and lower altitudes, while Colombia follows at 13.2 million bags (8%), focusing on high-quality arabica exports.[2] Global coffee production hit a record 174.4 million bags in 2024/25, up from prior years despite weather-induced fluctuations like droughts in Brazil and Vietnam, reflecting expansions in planted area and yield improvements from hybrid varieties and irrigation.[3] The top five producersâ€”Brazil, Vietnam, Colombia, Indonesia (10.7 million bags), and Ethiopiaâ€”account for over two-thirds of supply, underscoring the commodity's economic significance for developing nations where smallholder farms predominate but face challenges from climate variability and price volatility tied to weather events and speculative markets.[2] Data compilation relies on estimates from bodies like the USDA and ICO, which aggregate farm surveys and trade records, though underreporting in informal sectors may skew lower-tier rankings.[4]

Global Overview

Aggregate Production Statistics

Global coffee production for the 2024/25 crop year is estimated at 174.4 million 60-kg bags by the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA), reflecting a 3% increase from the 169.3 million bags produced in 2023/24.[3] The International Coffee Organization (ICO) projects a slightly higher figure of 178 million bags for the same period, attributing the growth primarily to recoveries in key producing regions despite weather challenges.[5] In metric tonnes of green coffee equivalent, this equates to approximately 10.5 million tonnes for 2024/25, up from over 10 million tonnes harvested in 2024.[6] Of total output, Arabica varieties account for roughly 57% (about 102 million bags), while Robusta comprises the remaining 43%, based on ICO assessments of recent production shares.[7] These proportions have remained relatively stable, with Arabica favored for premium markets and Robusta for its higher yields and resilience in lower-altitude regions.[8] Global consumption reached a record 177 million bags in 2023/24, exceeding production by about 1 million bags and resulting in a modest surplus that helped stabilize stocks entering 2024/25.[9] For 2024/25, ICO forecasts consumption growth of around 2% to near 177 million bags, maintaining a slight production surplus of 1 million bags amid ample inventories, though vulnerabilities to climate variability could alter balances.[10] This dynamic underscores coffee's role as a commodity with periodic tight supplies offset by inventory buffers from prior harvests.[7]

Data Sources and Methodology

The primary sources for compiling global coffee production rankings are official datasets and reports from the United States Department of Agriculture's Foreign Agricultural Service (USDA FAS), the International Coffee Organization (ICO), and the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO).[17][18][1] These agencies aggregate data submitted by national governments, trade associations, and field surveys, standardizing measurements to the weight of green coffee beansâ€”unroasted, processed beans ready for exportâ€”expressed either in 60-kilogram bags (the ICO statistical norm) or metric tonnes.[19] Methodologically, production totals represent the aggregated harvest of clean green beans prior to any further processing or roasting, excluding husks, parchment, or impurities to ensure comparability across arabica and robusta varieties.[2] National-level figures are summed globally, with cross-verification against export records and consumption balances where available; for forward-looking estimates like the 2024/2025 coffee year (October-September), adjustments incorporate agronomic models accounting for rainfall deviations, pest incidences, and yield per hectare from prior seasons.[17] Discrepancies between sources are reconciled by prioritizing government-reported actuals over third-party extrapolations when possible. Key limitations arise from uneven reporting quality, particularly among smaller producers and the 95% of global coffee output from smallholder farms under 5 hectares, which often lack centralized tracking and may underreport due to informal sales or data collection gaps.[20][21] Actual historical data, based on verified harvests, can differ from preliminary estimates by 5-10% upon revision as late-season yields are confirmed, while projections remain provisional and subject to climatic uncertainties not fully captured in models.[7][2]

Regional Production Patterns

Latin America

Latin America dominates global coffee production, contributing approximately 55-60% of the world's output, with a focus on high-quality Coffea arabica varieties cultivated at elevations between 1,000 and 2,000 meters above sea level.[22][23] The region's equatorial latitudes, combined with diverse microclimates and fertile soilsâ€”including volcanic ash deposits in areas like Colombia and Central Americaâ€”support superior yields and flavor profiles, with empirical studies showing arabica yields averaging 1-2 tonnes per hectare under optimal conditions.[7] Brazil leads as the top producer, harvesting over 3.8 million metric tons (64.7 million 60-kg bags) in the 2023/24 marketing year, accounting for roughly 37% of global supply and leveraging vast plantations in states like Minas Gerais for economies of scale.[2] Colombia follows with about 0.79 million metric tons (13.2 million bags), renowned for its washed arabica processing that enhances acidity and cleanliness, though production fluctuates due to biennial bearing cycles and weather variability.[2] Emerging risers include Honduras, the largest Central American producer at around 5.5 million bags annually, benefiting from expanded cultivation in departments like CopÃ¡n, and Peru, which reached 4.5 million bags in recent years through high-altitude farming in the Andes.[24] The bloc's export volumes, exceeding 80 million bags yearly, stabilize global markets, with Brazil's surplus buffering shortages elsewhere; for instance, in 2023, Latin American exports to the U.S. alone valued $4.8 billion, underscoring the region's pivotal role in supply chains.[25] Other contributors like Guatemala, Mexico, and Costa Rica add specialized arabica outputs, often from shade-grown systems that yield premium prices but lower volumes per hectare compared to Brazil's intensive methods.[7]

Africa


Africa contributes approximately 10.6% to global coffee production, equivalent to about 17.9 million 60-kg bags in the 2022/23 coffee year.[7] This output is concentrated in East and West Africa, where coffee originated and where smallholder farming predominates, with over 90% of production from farms averaging less than 2 hectares.[26] Unlike mechanized large-scale operations elsewhere, African cultivation relies on manual labor, resulting in yield gaps of 45-57% below attainable levels for both Coffea arabica and C. canephora due to limited inputs and subsistence practices.[27] Ethiopia leads African production at around 496,000 metric tons annually, primarily arabica varieties from highland regions, marking it as the species' origin point with exceptional genetic diversity from wild progenitors.[28] Uganda follows with roughly 402,000 metric tons, focused on robusta, which offers advantages in disease resistance and suitability for lower elevations.[2] CÃ´te d'Ivoire ranks as a major robusta producer, alongside Tanzania and Kenya, which contribute specialty arabica from volcanic soils.[29]

Historical Production Data

Early 20th Century to Post-WWII

In the early 20th century, Brazil held a commanding position in global coffee production, supplying around 80% of the world's coffee during the 1920s through extensive plantations in SÃ£o Paulo and Minas Gerais states, fueled by European and emerging U.S. demand.[38] Other notable producers included Colombia, which ramped up exports to over 500,000 bags by 1905 and doubled that by 1915, alongside smaller contributions from colonial regions such as the Dutch East Indies (modern Indonesia) and African territories under European control, including Ethiopia where commercial production began significantly around 1907.[39] Global output hovered below 20 million 60-kg bags annually before 1940, reflecting limited technological advances and transportation constraints that confined expansion to established equatorial belts.[40] The 1930s brought crises precipitated by the Great Depression, with overproduction leading to plummeting prices; Brazil, facing surplus stocks, resorted to burning approximately 78 million bags of exportable coffee between 1931 and 1944 to stabilize markets and support producer incomes.[41] This period saw Brazil's share dip temporarily, though it and Colombia together accounted for about 80% of world exports by the early 1930s, underscoring Latin America's centrality amid subdued growth in Asian and African colonies hampered by colonial policies prioritizing raw material extraction over yield improvements.[40] Following World War II, surging U.S. consumptionâ€”peaking at 19.8 pounds per capita in 1946, double the 1900 levelâ€”drove a production boom as wartime rationing ended and economic recovery boosted imports.[39] [42] Global totals expanded rapidly, reaching averages of 50-60 million bags by the late 1950s, with Brazil stabilizing at over 50% share post-Depression through government-backed replanting and frost-resistant varieties, while African robusta output grew via hybrid introductions in regions like Uganda and CÃ´te d'Ivoire to meet demand for instant coffee processing.[40]

Economic and Agronomic Factors

Agronomic advancements, such as the adoption of high-yielding robusta hybrids and intensive irrigation systems, have significantly elevated production efficiency in countries like Vietnam, where average yields reached 2.75 tonnes per hectare in recent assessments, far surpassing the global average of approximately 0.9-1.0 tonnes per hectare.[34][16] These practices, including heavy fertilizer application and dense planting, enable robusta varieties to thrive in monoculture systems, contrasting with lower-yielding arabica-dominated regions constrained by traditional shade-grown methods. In Brazil, selective breeding of disease-resistant varieties combined with precision farming has sustained yields around 1.77 tonnes per hectare, supported by private sector investments in research rather than state-directed programs.[57] Economic factors, particularly responsive price signals in open markets, incentivize acreage expansion and input intensification; for instance, rising global coffee prices in 2023-2024 prompted Nicaraguan farmers to increase fertilizer use, boosting planted area and projected output despite elevated costs.[58] Mechanization, prominent in Brazil's large-scale operations, reduces labor costsâ€”harvesting machines cover vast flat terrains efficiently, achieving economies of scale unattainable in fragmented smallholder systemsâ€”while fertilizer expenses, which spiked due to global supply disruptions, represent 20-30% of production costs in input-heavy regions like Vietnam.[59][60] These dynamics highlight how market-driven adaptations outperform regulated models, as evidenced by Brazil's output dominance through private mechanization investments yielding higher returns on investment (ROI) compared to subsidized small farms elsewhere.[61] Country-specific variances underscore these enablers: Brazil's consolidated farms (often exceeding 100 hectares) leverage mechanization for ROI metrics exceeding 15-20% in favorable cycles, enabling reinvestment in irrigation and inputs, whereas Ethiopia's 85% smallholder baseâ€”limited to 0.5-2.5 hectare plots with manual laborâ€”yields only 0.88 tonnes per hectare, constraining scalability and exposing producers to input price volatility without scale efficiencies.[3][62] In Vietnam, state-encouraged but market-fueled intensification has driven yields via affordable credit for fertilizers, though rising input costs (e.g., nitrogen prices up 30% post-2022) pressure margins without equivalent mechanization.[63][64]

Challenges in Coffee Production

Environmental and Resource Constraints

Coffee production faces significant environmental constraints, particularly soil degradation from erosion on steep, intensively cultivated slopes common in major producing regions. Empirical studies indicate average annual soil erosion rates of 17 megagrams per hectare in Costa Rican coffee plantations and 7 megagrams per hectare in Guatemala, exacerbated by monoculture practices that lead to 20-30% nutrient losses without vegetative cover.[65] Shade-grown systems, which integrate native trees, substantially mitigate these effects, with one Colombian study recording only 0.24 metric tons of soil loss per hectare per year compared to over 20 metric tons in sun-exposed field crops.[66] Such agroforestry approaches enhance soil organic matter retention and nutrient cycling, reducing dependency on synthetic fertilizers.[67] Water resource demands impose another key limitation, as coffee's high virtual water footprint requires approximately 15,000 liters per kilogram of green beans, primarily from rainfall-dependent evapotranspiration during the crop's growth cycle.[68] Irrigation in drier areas like parts of Brazil amplifies scarcity risks, though efficient processing techniques can lower operational water use to under 10 liters per kilogram of parchment in optimized wet mills.[69] Climate variability further strains these resources, correlating with increased erosion and reduced yields in highland regions.[70] Deforestation linked to coffee expansion remains a concern but is increasingly offset by yield intensification; in Brazil, the leading producer, coffee-related forest clearance accounted for 737,000 hectares across farms since the early 2000s, representing less than 1% of total national deforestation when contextualized against broader agricultural drivers.[71] Production has risen 87% since the 1980s despite a 40% decline in cultivated area, demonstrating how higher-yielding varieties enable forest recovery on marginal lands previously converted.[72] Vulnerability to pests and diseases compounds resource pressures, as outbreaks like coffee leaf rust deplete plant vigor and necessitate repeated interventions. Breeding programs have yielded resilient hybrids, boosting outputs by 29-61% over traditional varieties while incorporating multi-resistance to rust, berry disease, and leaf miners, thereby reducing chemical inputs and enhancing long-term sustainability.[73][74]

Market and Policy Issues

Coffee prices exhibited significant volatility in the 2024/25 crop year, driven primarily by supply disruptions from adverse weather in leading producers Brazil and Vietnam, rather than speculative trading alone. Arabica futures on the Intercontinental Exchange (ICE) surged over 70% year-over-year in 2024, peaking above 425 US cents per pound in early 2025, while robusta prices nearly tripled since 2022 amid droughts and frosts reducing output by up to 10-15% in key regions.[75][76][77] The International Coffee Organization's Composite Indicator Price (I-CIP) reflected this, averaging highs near 300 US cents per pound before retreating 11.8% in June 2025 due to partial supply recovery, underscoring how physical shortagesâ€”evidenced by ICE inventories dropping to 534,665 bags for arabica by October 2025â€”dominated over futures speculation.[75][78] Policy frameworks have influenced producer resilience, with Brazil's mid-1990s deregulation of its coffee market enabling greater adaptability to shocks compared to more interventionist approaches elsewhere. Post-deregulation, Brazilian producers gained freedom to innovate in varieties and irrigation, contributing to output stability despite climatic pressures, as market signals guided investments without government price controls.[79][80] In contrast, Vietnam's historical reliance on export expansion after the 1989 International Coffee Agreement's collapse boosted robusta volumes but exposed it to quota-like restrictions in past eras; recent free trade pacts like the EU-Vietnam FTA have mitigated some barriers by eliminating tariffs, enhancing competitiveness over multilateral stagnation.[81][82] Trade barriers, particularly tariffs, disproportionately burden smaller producers by inflating export costs and distorting flows, while subsidies in consuming nations further misallocate resources away from efficient open-market dynamics. U.S. tariffs imposed in 2025, reaching 46% on Vietnamese robusta and 10-50% on Brazilian arabica, raised landed costs by up to 10% globally, squeezing margins for non-integrated smallholders who lack scale to absorb hikes, unlike larger operations in top exporters.[83][84] Bilateral agreements have proven more effective at reducing such distortions than multilateral forums like the WTO, as seen in EVFTA's tariff eliminations spurring Vietnamese exports by providing direct market access absent in broader negotiations.[82] Labor conditions in coffee production, often cited for exploitation, show variability per International Labour Organization assessments, with daily wages ranging from USD 1-2 in low-output nations like Ethiopia to USD 10+ in structured operations elsewhere, frequently meeting or exceeding local minima when verified against piece-rate norms rather than universal standards.[85][86] Open-market policies in resilient producers like Brazil have indirectly supported wage stability by fostering productivity gains, countering narratives of systemic abuse through data on compliant farm audits over anecdotal claims.[80]
