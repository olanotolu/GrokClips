List of current vice presidents and designated acting presidents

This article catalogs the vice presidents and designated acting presidents serving in sovereign states as of October 2025, focusing on presidential and semi-presidential systems where such roles provide for executive succession and continuity. Vice presidents typically function as the immediate deputy to the president, assuming the full powers and duties of the office in the event of death, resignation, removal, or temporary incapacity, a mechanism rooted in ensuring stable governance amid unforeseen disruptions. Designated acting presidents, often prime ministers, speakers of parliament, or other high officials in systems lacking a formal vice presidency, perform analogous temporary functions to maintain operational leadership during presidential absences or transitions. These positions exhibit wide variation in scope: in some republics, vice presidents wield independent legislative or diplomatic authority, while in others, the role remains largely ceremonial or advisory, shaped by national constitutions that balance power allocation with political stability.[1][2]

Scope and Definitions

Vice Presidents and Equivalent Positions

Vice presidents are executive officials positioned immediately below the president in the governmental hierarchy of many nations, particularly presidential republics, where they are empowered to assume full presidential authority upon the president's death, resignation, removal from office, or temporary incapacity to maintain leadership continuity.[3][4] This succession mechanism is enshrined in national constitutions, with the vice president often elected on a joint ticket with the president to align policy and ensure electoral compatibility.[5] Beyond succession, vice presidents frequently undertake delegated executive tasks, such as overseeing specific policy areas, representing the state at international events, or presiding over legislative proceedings, including casting tie-breaking votes in upper houses.[6][1] Their precise responsibilities vary by jurisdiction; for instance, in the United States, the vice president constitutionally serves as President of the Senate, a role extending to ceremonial oversight of electoral vote certification.[4][7] Equivalent positions include titles like deputy president, first deputy, or senior vice president, which fulfill analogous functions as second-in-command with acting head-of-state powers, adapted to the constitutional or legal structures of diverse regimes.[8] These may appear in semi-presidential systems or hybrid governance models, where the deputy assumes executive duties during vacancies, though without the full electoral linkage seen in pure presidential setups.[9] In entities with multiple such offices, as in some African or Latin American states, they often balance ethnic, regional, or factional representation while preserving the core deputy role.[10] Positions lacking formal succession authority or primarily legislative focus, such as speakers of parliaments, are excluded from this category.

Designated Acting Heads of State

Designated acting heads of state are officeholders constitutionally or statutorily authorized to temporarily assume the full powers, duties, and responsibilities of the head of state during periods of vacancy, incapacity, death, resignation, or removal of the incumbent. This role differs from that of a vice president, who typically serves as a standing successor with broader or ongoing authority, by being provisional and often tied ex officio to another position such as speaker of the parliament, prime minister, or council president. The designation ensures uninterrupted executive continuity, with the acting official exercising substantive authorityâ€”rather than merely ceremonial functionsâ€”until the head of state recovers or a permanent replacement is elected or appointed, as outlined in national legal frameworks.[11] Such provisions are common in republics lacking a dedicated vice presidency or where succession beyond the vice president requires further designees. For instance, in Ghana's 1992 Constitution (Article 64), the Speaker of Parliament performs presidential functions if both the president and vice president are unavailable, maintaining governance stability without altering the line of elective succession.[12] Similarly, Tunisia's 2014 Constitution (Article 84) empowered the speaker of the Assembly of the Representatives of the People to serve as interim president following President Beji Caid Essebsi's death in July 2019, bridging the gap until elections.[13] In Trinidad and Tobago, the Constitution (Section 26) specifies that the President of the Senate or House of Representatives may act temporarily as president during vacancies, prioritizing legislative leaders for interim executive roles.[14] These arrangements reflect causal mechanisms for state resilience, prioritizing institutional roles over ad hoc appointments to prevent power vacuums that could invite instability. However, the scope of acting authority varies: some constitutions limit it to specific durations or exclude certain powers like constitutional amendments, while others grant near-full presidential prerogatives. Source credibility in documenting these roles often relies on official constitutional texts over secondary interpretations, as media reports may emphasize political context over precise legal delineations.[15]

Criteria for Statehood and Entity Inclusion

The foundational criteria for statehood in international law, as codified in Article 1 of the Montevideo Convention on the Rights and Duties of States (1933), require an entity to possess: (a) a permanent population; (b) a defined territory; (c) a government; and (d) the capacity to enter into relations with other states.[16] These elements emphasize factual, empirical conditions of effective control and sovereignty rather than mere declarations or external validation.[17] A permanent population denotes stable human settlement, not transient occupancy; defined territory implies boundaries over which control is exercised, though precision is not absolute and disputes do not preclude statehood (e.g., ongoing border conflicts among recognized states); government refers to an effective authority maintaining internal order; and capacity for international relations involves the practical ability to engage diplomatically or economically, independent of formal recognition.[18] Under the declarative theory of statehood, which predominates in customary international law, an entity's fulfillment of these criteria confers objective state personality, irrespective of recognition by others.[19] This contrasts with the constitutive theory, which posits that statehood emerges only through acknowledgment by existing states, a view largely critiqued for rendering statehood subjective and dependent on political consensus rather than empirical reality.[20] In practice, while recognition facilitates participation in bodies like the United Nationsâ€”requiring both Montevideo compliance and Security Council approval under Article 4(1) of the UN Charterâ€”non-recognition does not negate de facto statehood where effective governance persists.[19] Entities failing core criteria, such as lacking territorial control or stable governance, are excluded, prioritizing causal efficacy over aspirational claims. For inclusion in this list, entities must demonstrate de facto exercise of state functions, including the maintenance of executive structures like vice presidencies or designated acting heads of state, aligned with the government's claim to sovereignty.[21] This encompasses UN member states and observers (broad recognition), partially recognized entities meeting Montevideo standards but facing opposition from key actors, and de facto regimes with effective control over population and territory despite minimal formal acknowledgment.[22] Alternative or competing governments within disputed territories are included only if they operate distinct vice presidential or acting roles under a parallel state claim, excluding mere insurgent groups without territorial monopoly or institutional permanence. Recognition levels serve to categorize rather than determine inclusion, reflecting variances in diplomatic practice driven by geopolitical interests, but grounded in verifiable control as of October 27, 2025.[23] Sources assessing these entities, such as official government declarations and international legal analyses, are evaluated for empirical substantiation over ideological alignment, given documented biases in academic and media reporting that may understate de facto efficacy in non-Western contexts.[24]

Entities with Broad International Recognition

Vice Presidents in UN Member States and Observers

Several UN member states operate under presidential systems that include a vice president, typically elected jointly with the president to provide continuity and support executive functions, including succession upon vacancy or incapacity. This position is absent in parliamentary republics, monarchies, and many semi-presidential systems, where roles like deputy prime minister or speaker of parliament may serve analogous purposes. Observer states, such as the Holy See and the State of Palestine, do not maintain vice presidential offices. Entities with Partial International Recognition

Vice Presidents in Partially Recognized Entities

In the Republic of China (Taiwan), which maintains de facto control over Taiwan and associated islands while claiming broader sovereignty but receiving formal diplomatic recognition from only 12 United Nations member states as of 2025, the vice presidency serves as the second-highest executive office, elected on the same ticket as the president for a four-year term. The current vice president is Hsiao Bi-khim, who assumed office on May 20, 2024, alongside President Lai Ching-te following their victory in the January 2024 presidential election.[36] Hsiao, born in 1971, previously served as Taiwan's representative to the United States and holds a degree from Oberlin College.[37] The vice president assists the president in executive duties and assumes the presidency in cases of vacancy, death, or incapacity, as stipulated in the Additional Articles of the ROC Constitution amended in 1997. Other partially recognized entities, such as the Republic of Kosovo (recognized by over 100 UN members but not universally) and the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus (recognized solely by Turkey), operate under presidential systems without a dedicated vice presidential position; executive continuity relies instead on prime ministers or assembly deputies. Similarly, the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic features a president and prime minister but no vice president in its current governmental structure. Abkhazia, recognized by five UN members including Russia, maintains a vice presidential office elected concurrently with the president, but the position's current occupant following the March 2025 presidential election remains tied to the ruling ticket without independent verification of succession details post-inauguration.

Designated Acting Heads in Partially Recognized Entities

In the State of Palestine, a partially recognized entity with observer status at the United Nations, President Mahmoud Abbas issued a decree on October 26, 2025, designating senior PLO official Hussein al-Sheikh to serve as acting president for a 90-day interim period in the event of Abbas's death or permanent incapacity, after which presidential elections must be held within that timeframe. This provision, formalized amid concerns over Abbas's age (89) and the absence of prior succession mechanisms, builds on al-Sheikh's April 2025 appointment as the first PLO vice president, a newly created role.[38][39][40] In the Republic of Kosovo, recognized by over 100 UN member states but not by Serbia or five UN Security Council permanent members, the constitution stipulates that the President of the Assembly assumes acting presidential duties during temporary absences, vacancies, or until a new president is elected. Entities with Limited or No Formal Recognition

Vice Presidents in Self-Declared or De Facto Entities

In entities operating de facto as independent states without broad international recognition, such as Somaliland and Abkhazia, vice presidential roles exist to provide constitutional succession and support executive functions, though many counterparts lack such positions, relying instead on prime ministers or parliamentary speakers. These structures reflect adapted presidential systems amid isolation from global bodies like the United Nations.[43] Somaliland's vice president, Mohamed Aw-Ali Abdi, assumed office on November 13, 2024, following his election as running mate to President Abdirahman Mohamed Abdullahi Irro under the Waddani party banner. Abdi, a former ambassador and diplomat, has focused on national reconciliation efforts, including mediating clan disputes in regions like Ceel-Afweyn as of May 2025, and has acted as interim president during presidential absences. The position, established in Somaliland's 2001 constitution, ensures continuity in a hybrid presidential-parliamentary framework.[44][45] Abkhazia maintains a vice presidency as the deputy's role to the president, with Beslan Bigvava serving since March 2025 after his joint election with President Badra Gunba in the republic's snap presidential runoff. Bigvava, born in 1971, previously held security-related posts and campaigned on strengthening ties with Russia while addressing internal economic challenges. The office, formalized post-1990s independence declaration from Georgia, handles ceremonial duties and policy coordination, though its effectiveness is constrained by reliance on Russian patronage for security and funding. Gunba, formerly vice president from 2020, ascended amid 2024 protests that ousted his predecessor.[46][47] Other de facto entities, including the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus, South Ossetia, and Transnistria, do not currently feature vice presidents; Transnistria abolished the role in 2011 constitutional reforms, shifting authority to a prime minister under President Vadim Krasnoselsky. Similarly, the Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics, now integrated as Russian federal subjects since 2022 referendums, operate under heads of republic without designated deputies equivalent to vice presidents.[48]

Designated Acting Heads in Self-Declared or De Facto Entities

In Abkhazia, Vice President Badra Gunba served as acting president following the resignation of Aslan Bzhania on November 19, 2024, triggered by mass protests against a proposed property investment agreement with Russia that opponents viewed as infringing on local sovereignty.[49] Gunba, who had been appointed vice president in 2020, assumed executive powers immediately under constitutional provisions allowing the vice president to act during vacancies.[50] He held the position until winning a presidential runoff election on March 1, 2025, with approximately 55% of the vote against opposition candidate Adgur Ardzinba, amid reports of electoral irregularities including repeat voting in some districts.[51] [52] As of October 27, 2025, no other self-declared or de facto entities with limited or no formal recognition, such as Transnistria, South Ossetia, Somaliland, or the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus, report a serving acting head of state; each maintains an elected president in office. In Transnistria, President Vadim Krasnoselsky continues his second term following re-election in December 2021.[53] South Ossetia's President Alan Gagloev, elected in 2022, remains in power after his Nykhas party's parliamentary victory in June 2024.[54] Somaliland's President Abdirahman Mohamed Abdullahi, sworn in on December 12, 2024, after winning the November election, governs alongside Vice President Mohamed Aw-Ali Abdi.[55] In the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus, Tufan ErhÃ¼rman assumed the presidency on October 24, 2025, following his landslide victory on October 19.[56] Succession arrangements in these entities typically rely on vice presidents where established or legislative speakers in their absence, though specific constitutional details vary and are often influenced by Russian patronage in post-Soviet breakaways like Abkhazia, South Ossetia, and Transnistria.[57] No ongoing vacancies or incapacities necessitate acting heads at present.

Alternative or Competing Governments

Vice Presidents in Alternative Governments

In Myanmar, the National Unity Government (NUG), established on April 16, 2021, by members of the ousted National League for Democracy and ethnic minority representatives in response to the State Administration Council's military coup, operates as a parallel executive claiming constitutional legitimacy over the junta-controlled administration.[58] The NUG's structure includes a vice president who also serves as acting president in the absence of the detained State Counsellor Aung San Suu Kyi and President U Win Myint, reflecting its adaptation to exile and resistance operations.[59] Duwa Lashi La, an ethnic Kachin politician and former member of the Kachin State Democracy Party, holds the position of vice president and acting president as of October 2025.[58] Appointed to ensure representation of non-Burman ethnic groups within the NUG's multi-ethnic framework, he has coordinated armed resistance through affiliated People's Defense Forces and diplomatic outreach to international actors, including calls for unified revolutionary efforts amid ongoing civil conflict.[60] His role emphasizes causal continuity with the pre-coup elected government, though the NUG controls limited territory and relies on shadow governance in liberated areas.[61] No other widely documented alternative governments maintain active vice presidential offices as of late 2025, with most rival structuresâ€”such as Belarusian opposition coordination councils or Venezuelan exile networksâ€”lacking formalized deputy executive positions equivalent to vice presidencies.[62][63] These entities prioritize leadership councils or single-figure opposition rather than tiered executive roles, reflecting fragmented or transitional statuses without de facto control.
