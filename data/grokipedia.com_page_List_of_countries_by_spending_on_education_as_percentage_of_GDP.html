Overview

Definition and Measurement

Government expenditure on education as a percentage of gross domestic product (GDP) measures the share of a country's total economic output allocated by the public sector to educational activities, calculated by dividing total government spending on education (including current, capital, and transfer expenditures) by nominal GDP for the same reference year and multiplying by 100.[9] This indicator, as defined by the UNESCO Institute for Statistics (UIS), encompasses total general government outlays (from local, regional, and central levels) directed toward educational institutions, excluding private household contributions unless channeled through public subsidies or transfers.[10] The World Bank adopts a parallel formulation, specifying that it includes expenditures funded by international transfers to government but focuses exclusively on public sources to ensure cross-country comparability.[11] Current expenditures cover operational costs such as teacher salaries, teaching materials, and administrative expenses, while capital expenditures fund infrastructure like school buildings and equipment; transfers include subsidies to households (e.g., for tuition or student loans) and international aid allocated to education.[9] Coverage spans all formal education levels from early childhood (pre-primary) through tertiary, provided the activities align with International Standard Classification of Education (ISCED) criteria, though research and development spending in higher education is often excluded or separately reported to avoid inflating figures.[5] Data are primarily sourced from national budget records, ministry reports, and government finance statistics, harmonized with System of National Accounts (SNA) standards for consistency; for instance, the OECD computes this metric using administrative data from member countries, averaging around 4.7% of GDP in recent years for public spending across primary to tertiary levels.[12][13] Measurement relies on fiscal year data aligned to calendar years where possible, with GDP denominated in current local currency units converted to percentages to mitigate exchange rate distortions; however, discrepancies arise from varying national definitions of "education" (e.g., inclusion of vocational training or adult education) and incomplete reporting of subnational spending.[11] International organizations like UNESCO and the OECD apply imputation methods for missing data, such as interpolation from historical trends or regional averages, but emphasize that the indicator reflects public prioritization rather than total societal investment, as private expenditures (e.g., household tutoring or for-profit schools) are captured in separate metrics when available.[5] This public-focused approach facilitates policy benchmarking but understates overall education financing in systems with significant private involvement, such as those in East Asia or the United States.[14]

Significance for Policy and Limitations as an Indicator

Governments and international organizations often use education spending as a percentage of GDP as a benchmark to evaluate fiscal commitment to human capital development, with UNESCO's Education 2030 Framework recommending allocations of 4-6% of GDP or 15-20% of total public expenditure to support sustainable development goals.[15] This metric informs policy decisions on budget prioritization, such as increasing allocations during economic expansions to expand access or infrastructure, as evidenced by analyses showing that a 1 percentage point rise in spending correlates with over 3 percentage points higher gross secondary enrollment rates in cross-country panels.[16] Policymakers in developing nations, for instance, reference it to justify aid requests or debt relief tied to education investments, viewing higher percentages as signals of long-term economic growth potential through improved workforce skills.[17] However, this indicator has significant limitations as a standalone measure of educational effectiveness or policy success, as it conflates quantity of input with quality of output and ignores variations in per-student spending driven by demographic factors like youth population shares.[18] Empirical studies, including those by economist Eric Hanushek, demonstrate weak or negligible correlations between aggregate spending levels and student cognitive outcomes after controlling for initial conditions, with cognitive skills explaining up to 75% of GDP growth variations rather than expenditure shares alone.[19] High-spending countries like the United States (around 6% of GDP) often underperform on international assessments compared to lower-spending peers like Finland, underscoring that inefficiencies in resource allocationâ€”such as teacher quality or curriculum designâ€”render the percentage metric insufficient for causal inference on learning gains.[20][21] Additionally, it excludes private sector contributions, which can exceed 1-2% of GDP in some economies, and fails to adjust for purchasing power parity or economic cycles that artificially inflate or deflate the ratio without reflecting real investment changes.[7] Thus, while useful for cross-national comparisons of commitment, overreliance on this indicator can mislead policy toward input expansion over outcome-oriented reforms.

Data Sources and Methodology

International Databases and Reporting Standards

The primary international databases for tracking government spending on education as a percentage of gross domestic product (GDP) are maintained by the UNESCO Institute for Statistics (UIS), the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), and the World Bank, which largely aggregates UIS data.[22][1][8] The UIS serves as the global custodian, collecting administrative data annually from national ministries of education and finance through standardized surveys, covering over 200 countries and territories with a focus on Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) indicator 4.c.1, which measures domestic education expenditure as a proportion of total government spending.[23][22] This includes current operational costs (e.g., teacher salaries, materials), capital investments (e.g., infrastructure), and subsidies, expressed relative to GDP from national accounts or IMF estimates.[1] Data validation involves direct country consultations to address discrepancies, though coverage gaps persist in fragile states or those with incomplete fiscal reporting.[24] The OECD's Education at a Glance publication provides detailed, comparable indicators primarily for its 38 member countries and select partners, drawing on the UNESCO-OECD-Eurostat (UOE) joint data collection framework established in the 1980s to harmonize concepts across diverse systems.[8][25] Under UOE standards, expenditure encompasses public funding for institutions from early childhood to tertiary levels, aligned with the International Standard Classification of Education (ISCED 2011), and excludes household contributions unless subsidized; it calculates public spending as a share of GDP using purchasing power parity (PPP) adjustments for cross-country comparability.[26][27] The World Bank complements these by disseminating UIS-sourced series (e.g., SE.XPD.TOTL.GD.ZS) in its World Development Indicators, incorporating GDP data from the IMF's World Economic Outlook for consistency, and emphasizing low- and middle-income countries where UIS coverage is vital but often lags by 2-3 years due to reporting delays.[11][28] Reporting standards prioritize fiscal year alignment and functional classification per the Government Finance Statistics Manual (GFSM 2014), ensuring expenditure is attributed to education functions (e.g., excluding research or health-related training unless primarily educational).[26] However, variations arise in defining "government" (central vs. local levels), inclusion of international aid (e.g., as transfers), and treatment of non-formal education, leading to calls for enhanced harmonization via the Education Data and Statistics Commission, which develops guidelines for benchmark indicators like total public education spending relative to GDP.[29][22] These frameworks facilitate global monitoring but underscore the need for country-level verification, as self-reported data can reflect definitional inconsistencies rather than true fiscal priorities.[24]

Key Methodological Considerations and Data Gaps

Government expenditure on education as a percentage of GDP typically measures consolidated public spending on educational goods and services across all levels of government and ministries, excluding private household contributions unless specified as total expenditure, and is benchmarked against standards like 4-6% in the Education 2030 Framework for Action.[30] This includes current expenditures such as teacher salaries and materials, as well as capital outlays like infrastructure, but excludes debt interest payments and certain financial assets per IMF Government Finance Statistics (GFS) classifications under COFOG.[30] Comparability across countries is hindered by variations in data sourcesâ€”such as UNESCO Institute for Statistics (UIS), IMF GFS, OECD's UOE collection, or World Bank aggregatesâ€”which yield differing estimates due to subtle definitional discrepancies, including whether to incorporate consumption of fixed assets or adjust for purchasing power parities (PPPs).[30][31] National differences in fiscal calendars (e.g., calendar vs. financial year), accounting practices (cash vs. accrual basis), and education system structuresâ€”such as the role of private institutions, vouchers, or subnational fundingâ€”further complicate cross-border analysis, often requiring imputation or harmonization methods like medians or linear trends.[30][31] Inconsistencies in International Standard Classification of Education (ISCED) application, particularly between older ISCED-97 and updated ISCED 2011 frameworks, affect categorization of levels like early childhood or combined school-work programs, potentially inflating or deflating reported spending.[31] Peripheral services (e.g., student transport or meals) and in-kind contributions (e.g., free textbooks) are included in some datasets but vary in valuation, while exclusions like workplace training or non-formal education unless tied to formal programs limit full capture of education-related outlays.[31] OECD data, for instance, adjusts for PPPs and imputes retirement costs for personnel to enhance comparability, but reference years differ (e.g., 2014 for Hungary, 2021 for Belgium), and subnational variations (e.g., only Flemish data for Belgium) introduce further heterogeneity.[31] Data gaps are pronounced in developing and low-income countries, where reporting coverage for public expenditure as a share of GDP fell to 66% globally in recent years, with 34% of countries lacking 2021 data and even lower rates for shares of total government expenditure.[32] Private and household expenditures, critical for total spending metrics, cover only about 13% of the global population via surveys, with inconsistencies in recall periods, item definitions, and out-of-pocket tracking exacerbating underreporting, particularly in middle-income contexts.[30] Disaggregated data by education level, expenditure type (e.g., current vs. capital), or non-state actors remains scarce, especially in low-income settings where spending hovers below benchmarks despite per capita increases, necessitating imputations that may mask true fiscal commitments or inefficiencies.[32] Overall, these gaps stem from resource-intensive collection processes (e.g., UIS's 440 data points per questionnaire) and decentralized systems, limiting robust international benchmarking.[30]

Historical Trends

Global Patterns from 1970 to Present

Global government expenditure on education, expressed as a percentage of GDP, has demonstrated a gradual upward trajectory since 1970, albeit with significant cross-country and regional heterogeneity. Early data from the UNESCO Institute for Statistics (UIS), aggregated via the World Bank, indicate that global averages hovered around 3% in the late 20th century, reflecting limited fiscal prioritization in many low-income economies amid post-colonial reconstruction and basic needs focuses. By the 1990s, following international frameworks like the 1990 Jomtien Declaration on Education for All, spending began to rise as governments in developing regions allocated more resources to primary enrollment expansion, pushing world averages toward 4% by 2000.[1] [7] This trend accelerated in the 2000s and 2010s, coinciding with the Millennium Development Goals and later Sustainable Development Goal 4, which targeted universal access and quality education. World Bank aggregates show the global figure climbing to approximately 4% in 2010 and 4.8% in 2020, encompassing both current and capital outlays across public systems. The increase was particularly pronounced in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia, where spending shares doubled in some nations to address enrollment gaps, though absolute per-pupil investments remained low due to GDP constraints. In contrast, high-income OECD countries maintained relatively stable shares around 5-6%, with incremental growth tied to tertiary expansion rather than broad fiscal shifts.[1] [33] Post-2020, amid economic disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic, global spending stabilized near 4.5-5%, with preliminary 2022 estimates at 5%. This plateau reflects competing demands on budgets, such as health and debt servicing in low-growth environments, despite rhetorical commitments to recovery investments. Data gaps persist for fragile states, potentially understating averages, but the pattern underscores a causal link between policy advocacy and fiscal allocation, tempered by economic realities rather than uniform efficiency gains.[1] [7]

Shifts in Regional and Income-Based Spending

Low- and middle-income countries have exhibited faster growth in education spending as a percentage of GDP compared to high-income countries, reflecting commitments to international targets such as the Education for All initiative and Sustainable Development Goal 4. Between the 1990s and pre-COVID period, low- and middle-income countries raised their average spending from around 3% to approximately 4.45% of GDP, outpacing the more stable levels in high-income nations, which hovered near 4.7% in 2018â€“19.[7][34][35] By 2023, however, low-income countries still allocated only 2.4% of GDP to education, compared to 5.0% in high-income countries, indicating persistent gaps despite the relative acceleration in poorer groups driven by population pressures and equity goals.[1] Lower-middle-income countries reached 3.6% and upper-middle 4.9% in recent data, underscoring a convergence trend tempered by fiscal constraints in the lowest-income settings.[1] Regionally, Sub-Saharan Africa has shown incremental rises from a low base, with public spending increasing over 6% annually in the decade leading to 2011 amid post-colonial expansions and aid inflows, yet averaging around 3.7% of GDP by 2023â€”below the global benchmark of 4â€“6%.[36][1] In contrast, Latin America and the Caribbean experienced a sharper uptick, from 3% in the 1990s to over 5% by 2017, peaking at 5.2% around 2010 before a slight retrenchment to 4.3% amid economic volatility and shifting priorities.[37][1][38] East Asia and the Pacific maintained moderate growth, aligning closer to high-income averages by the 1990s through investments supporting rapid industrialization, reaching 3.9% recently, though absolute outlays surged with GDP expansion.[7][1] Europe and Central Asia, encompassing many high-income economies, exhibited relative stability at 4.7%, with minimal shifts as mature systems prioritized efficiency over expansion.[1] These patterns highlight how lower-income regions prioritized catch-up spending post-1970s, while advanced areas focused on sustaining established levels.[7]

Current Landscape

Latest Global and OECD Averages (2021-2023 Data)

In 2021, OECD countries allocated an average of 4.9% of GDP to total expenditure on educational institutions, encompassing public and private funding across primary through tertiary levels. This marked a slight decline from pre-pandemic levels but remained consistent with long-term trends, reflecting commitments to education amid economic recovery pressures. Data for 2022 indicate stability, with OECD-wide averages hovering around 5%, though variations exist due to differing national reporting methodologies and inclusion of ancillary services like student transport. By 2023, preliminary estimates from updated OECD analyses suggest a marginal dip to approximately 4.7%, influenced by fiscal tightening in several member states post-inflationary spikes.[3][13] Global averages for government expenditure on education present greater challenges owing to uneven data coverage, particularly in low- and lower-middle-income countries where reporting lags or omissions are common. According to World Bank compilations drawing from UNESCO Institute for Statistics, the weighted average for countries with available data stood at roughly 4.5% of GDP in 2021, with low-income nations averaging 3.6% amid constrained budgets. This figure rose modestly to about 4.2-4.5% in 2022 for reporting economies, though comprehensive global aggregation remains elusive due to gaps in over 50 countries, potentially understating totals if unreported private contributions are considered. UNESCO targets of 4-6% for sustainable development remain unmet on average, highlighting persistent disparities between high-income aggregates (often exceeding 5%) and developing regions.[1][32][39] These averages underscore methodological nuances: OECD figures prioritize total institutional spending for comparability among developed economies, while global metrics from the World Bank emphasize public outlays, potentially excluding household or philanthropic inputs that inflate effective totals in some contexts. Data reliability is higher for OECD due to standardized annual surveys, whereas global estimates suffer from self-reported inconsistencies and delays, with full 2023 coverage projected only in subsequent UNESCO releases.[5][12]

Notable Country Variations and Rankings

Analytical Perspectives

Correlations with Student Performance Metrics

Analyses of cross-national data reveal a weak overall correlation between education spending as a percentage of GDP and student performance on international assessments like the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA). A comprehensive review of 187 studies on school expenditures and student outcomes found no systematic positive relationship, with 87% of estimates showing insignificant effects on achievement after accounting for variations in resource use.[44] This pattern holds internationally, where high-spending OECD countries such as the United States (approximately 5.6% of GDP in 2021) score below the OECD average in PISA mathematics (465 vs. 472 in 2022), while lower-spending nations like Estonia (around 5% of GDP) achieve top-tier results (510 in mathematics).[45][46] Evidence from PISA data across OECD countries indicates that spending per student correlates positively with scores up to moderate levels (e.g., below the OECD median), but the association flattens or reverses among high-spenders, suggesting diminishing returns. For instance, cumulative per-student expenditure for ages 6-15 shows a clear link to higher PISA reading scores in lower-spending contexts, but OECD frontrunners like Finland historically achieved superior outcomes (e.g., 526 in reading, 2018) with spending closer to 6% of GDP, compared to higher outlays in underperformers like Austria (5.7% of GDP, 480 in reading).[47] Regional outliers, such as East Asian economies (e.g., South Korea at ~4.5% of GDP yielding 527 in mathematics), further illustrate that factors beyond fiscal inputsâ€” including instructional quality and cultural emphasis on disciplineâ€”drive performance more than raw spending shares.[46] Controlling for GDP per capita weakens the apparent link, as wealthier nations naturally allocate larger absolute resources to education, confounding simple percentage-based comparisons (correlation between spending share and GDP per capita: r â‰ˆ 0.71 across OECD). Studies emphasizing efficiency, such as those benchmarking PISA against per-pupil outlays, highlight inefficiencies in high-spending systems; for example, the U.S. ranks high in expenditure per full-time-equivalent student but lags in outcomes due to administrative bloat and uneven resource allocation, not insufficient funding levels.[48][49] Longitudinal OECD data from 2000-2022 reinforces this, showing stagnant or declining PISA scores in many high-spending countries despite rising expenditure shares, implying that incremental spending yields negligible gains without reforms in pedagogy and accountability.[46][50]

Evidence on Returns to Spending and Efficiency

Empirical analyses of the causal effects of education spending on student outcomes, often leveraging court-ordered finance reforms or lottery-based school assignments, indicate modest positive returns, particularly for disadvantaged students. A 2016 study using U.S. data from such reforms estimated that a sustained 10% increase in per-pupil spending over 12 years raises annual household income in adulthood by about $2,000 (in 2005 dollars), improves high school graduation rates by 7.7 percentage points for low-income students, and reduces adult poverty rates.[51] These effects are attributed to smaller class sizes, extended school days, and better teacher compensation, but the magnitudes diminish at higher spending levels, with little to no detectable benefits in districts already spending above median levels.[52] Cross-country evidence reinforces the pattern of diminishing marginal returns to aggregate spending as a share of GDP. In low-income contexts, where baseline spending is below 3% of GDP, incremental investments yield larger gains in enrollment and basic literacy, as documented in World Bank analyses of sub-Saharan African reforms.[53] Conversely, in high-spending OECD nations averaging 5% of GDP, further increases correlate weakly with PISA score improvements; for instance, the United States, spending 6.1% of GDP in 2021, ranks below systems like Estonia (5.3% of GDP) in math proficiency despite higher absolute inputs. Meta-reviews highlight that only about 20-30% of resource variations explain achievement differences, underscoring inefficiency in allocation rather than absolute levels.[54] Efficiency in converting spending to outcomes hinges on institutional factors, with stochastic frontier analyses revealing wide variance: top-quartile efficient countries achieve equivalent learning gains at 40-50% lower expenditure than laggards.[55] Peer-reviewed evaluations emphasize that returns amplify when funds target teacher selection and incentives over uniform inputs like infrastructure; for example, randomized trials in India and Kenya show high returns (up to 0.2 standard deviations per dollar) from performance-linked contracts, versus near-zero from undifferentiated salary hikes.[56] In contrast, administrative bloat in high-GDP-share spenders, such as non-instructional costs exceeding 30% of budgets in some U.S. districts, erodes potential gains, as evidenced by longitudinal tracking of expenditure breakdowns against stagnant NAEP scores since 1971 despite real per-pupil spending tripling.[52]

Criticisms and Debates

Flaws in Prioritizing Input Metrics Over Outputs

Focusing on education spending as a percentage of GDP as a primary metric emphasizes resource allocation over actual learning outcomes, potentially misleading policymakers and the public about system quality. Empirical analyses consistently reveal a weak or insignificant correlation between national spending levels and student performance indicators such as PISA scores, particularly when controlling for socioeconomic factors like GDP per capita.[19][14] For instance, cross-country regressions show that variations in expenditure explain minimal variance in cognitive skills, with institutional factors like teacher selection and accountability exerting greater influence.[57] This input-centric approach overlooks efficiency disparities, as evidenced by data envelopment analyses of OECD countries indicating an average public education spending efficiency score of 0.732; nations could achieve equivalent outputs with 26.8% less expenditure by reallocating resources toward high-impact areas like instructional quality rather than uniform increases.[58] High-spending countries often underperform relative to lower spenders: the United States allocated 5.6% of GDP to education in 2021â€”above the OECD average of 5%â€”yet ranked below average in 2018 PISA mathematics (score 478 vs. OECD 489) and science (502 vs. 489), trailing efficient systems like Estonia (3.5% GDP spending but PISA math 523).[45][59] Similarly, Norway's 6.6% GDP share in 2019 yielded middling PISA results (math 499), comparable to Poland's stronger outcomes at lower relative input.[4] Prioritizing inputs fosters inefficiency by incentivizing budgetary expansion without corresponding accountability for results, diverting funds from productive uses such as evidence-based reforms. Longitudinal studies confirm that beyond basic thresholds, additional spending yields diminishing or null returns on achievement, as resources may inflate administrative costs or reduce class sizes ineffectually while neglecting causal drivers like curriculum rigor.[51] This metric's flaws are compounded by its insensitivity to compositional effects, such as aging teacher salaries or non-instructional bloat, which erode value without enhancing skills linked to economic growthâ€”where a one-standard-deviation PISA improvement correlates with 1-2% annual GDP gains, far outweighing mere expenditure hikes.[60] Consequently, output-focused evaluations, including standardized assessments and labor market returns, better reveal systemic strengths and guide causal interventions over aggregate input proxies.[57]

Instances of Waste and Inefficiency in High-Spending Nations

services, as evidenced by econometric analyses showing suboptimal resource distribution across public goods.[67] Comparative studies rank Norway, alongside Denmark and Sweden, as inefficient in educational production relative to other OECD nations, where inputs yield lower outputs in standardized assessments despite high per-student expenditures.[68] Across OECD countries, public education spending efficiency averages 73.2%, implying potential for a 26.8% reduction in expenditures without diminishing outputs, highlighting systemic waste in high-spending contexts through over-reliance on inputs rather than performance-linked accountability.[58] Such patterns underscore causal factors like bureaucratic inertia and misaligned incentives, where elevated spending correlates weakly with enhanced human capital formation.[13]

Determinants of Spending Levels

Macroeconomic and Demographic Influences

Higher levels of GDP per capita are positively associated with greater public expenditure on education as a percentage of GDP across countries. Analysis of OECD data indicates that expenditure shares tend to be highest in the wealthiest economies and lowest in less affluent ones, reflecting greater fiscal capacity to sustain such investments relative to overall economic output.[69] This pattern holds globally, as evidenced by cross-country comparisons where national income emerges as a key driver of both absolute spending and the share allocated to education, enabling governments to expand programs without proportionally straining budgets.[7] Macroeconomic pressures, such as Baumol's cost disease, further contribute to elevated spending shares in sectors like education, which are labor-intensive and exhibit slower productivity growth compared to the broader economy. This structural inefficiency drives up relative costs over time, compelling governments to allocate a larger GDP fraction to maintain service levels, as documented in econometric analyses of public education expenditure drivers.[70] In high-income contexts, this effect is amplified by commitments to universal access and quality standards, resulting in sustained upward pressure on the expenditure-to-GDP ratio independent of overall growth rates. Demographic factors, particularly the proportion of school-age children in the population, exert a direct influence on education spending shares. Larger cohorts of primary and secondary students necessitate higher total outlays for infrastructure, teaching, and support services, elevating the percentage of GDP devoted to education, especially in systems with compulsory schooling durations of 9â€“10 years or more.[14] Elevated youth dependency ratiosâ€”defined as the number of individuals under 15 relative to the working-age population (15â€“64)â€”intensify this dynamic by increasing per-worker fiscal burdens, prompting reallocations toward youth-dependent services like education to foster future productivity.[71] Countries with demographic transitions featuring declining youth shares, such as in aging advanced economies, often experience stabilized or reduced spending percentages as the dependent population shrinks relative to the economic base.[7]

Governance, Policy Choices, and Institutional Factors

Governance quality, encompassing government effectiveness and regulatory frameworks, enables higher public education expenditures as a share of GDP by enhancing fiscal capacity and policy credibility. The World Bank's Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI) capture these dimensions, with empirical studies showing that nations scoring higher on government effectiveness exhibit stronger commitments to sustained public investments, including education, due to better bureaucratic efficiency and reduced leakage. For example, Nordic countries like Norway, with top-tier WGI scores, allocated 6.96% of GDP to education in 2021, reflecting institutional stability that supports high taxation and targeted spending.[42] Control of corruption is a critical institutional factor, as pervasive corruption diverts resources from education, which involves harder-to-misappropriate wage and operational costs, toward capturable areas like procurement. Cross-country analyses by the IMF reveal that higher perceived corruption correlates with lower education shares in total government expenditure, effectively reducing the % of GDP devoted to schooling in affected nations.[72] This pattern holds in developing economies, where weak anti-corruption mechanisms lead to underfunding despite revenue availability, prioritizing elite-capture over broad human capital development.[73] Policy choices driven by political ideologies systematically influence spending priorities. In OECD nations, left-leaning governments demonstrate a causal tendency to elevate education investments, as panel data from 21 countries (1980-2018) indicate higher expenditures under progressive administrations compared to conservative ones, attributing this to ideological emphasis on egalitarian outcomes.[74] Conversely, right-leaning policies often constrain growth in social spending, with U.S. state-level evidence showing Republican-controlled governments implementing post-recession cuts that lowered education budgets relative to GDP.[75] Fiscal decentralization represents another institutional lever, where devolving education authority to subnational levels boosts aggregate spending when paired with accountability. Research across countries finds that increased decentralization raises the education expenditure share by 2-3 percentage points in responsive systems, as local governments align budgets with regional demands and voter oversight, though outcomes falter without central capacity-building.[76] In federal setups like Canada's or Australia's, this structure sustains higher % GDP allocations compared to unitary states, underscoring how institutional design mediates policy efficacy.[77]
