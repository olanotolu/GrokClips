Any Day Now (2012 film)

![Any Day Now (2012 film) poster](./assets/Any_Day_Now_2012Film2012_Film2012Fâ€‹ilm
Any Day Now is a 2012 American legal drama film written and directed by Travis Fine, starring Alan Cumming as Rudy Donatello, a drag club singer, and Garret Dillahunt as Paul Wheatland, a deputy district attorney, who together take legal responsibility for a neglected teenager with Down syndrome named Marco after his mother abandons him.[1][2]
Set in Los Angeles during the late 1970s, the film depicts the couple's efforts to secure permanent custody amid opposition from a discriminatory court system that views their homosexuality as disqualifying them as suitable parents, highlighting the era's legal biases against same-sex relationships and individuals with disabilities.[3][1]
Inspired by real events from Fine's childhood observation of a similar custody battle, the production premiered at the Tribeca Film Festival in 2012, where it won the Heineken Audience Award for Best Narrative Feature, and went on to secure additional audience prizes at festivals including Outfest and Frameline.[4][5]
Critically, the film received a 78% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 65 reviews, with praise for its performancesâ€”particularly Cumming's, which earned him a Grand Jury Award for Best Actor at the Los Angeles Film Festivalâ€”and its portrayal of historical injustices, though some reviewers noted its melodramatic tendencies.[3][6][5]

Synopsis

Plot summary

In 1979 Los Angeles, Rudy Donatello, a struggling drag performer at a gay bar, finds his neighbor's 14-year-old son Marco, who has Down syndrome, abandoned and unkempt after Marco's drug-addicted mother, Marianna, is arrested for possession and solicitation. Rudy takes Marco into his rundown apartment, providing immediate care including bathing and feeding him, and seeks assistance from Paul Fleiger, a closeted assistant district attorney whom Rudy recently met at the bar following an altercation with a homophobic police officer. Posing as heterosexual cousins to avoid scrutiny, Rudy and Paul secure temporary custody through the juvenile court, relocate to Paul's home, and begin integrating Marco into their lives by teaching him daily skills like shaving, cooking, and singing, while Rudy continues performing numbers such as "It's Raining Men" and Paul handles initial legal proceedings.[7][8] As the couple develops a genuine familial bond with Marco, who responds positively to their structure and affection, they pursue permanent guardianship, undergoing a home evaluation that highlights their stable environment. However, Paul's conservative boss discovers their romantic relationship through a leaked photo, resulting in Paul's firing for perceived moral unfitness and the abrupt removal of Marco by social services. Undeterred, they hire a private attorney and endure a contentious custody hearing before a prejudiced judge who, upon confirming their homosexuality, rules against them, citing it as contrary to Marco's best interests and awarding custody back to the still-unfit Marianna, who had been released early to contest the case. Rudy's impassioned courtroom outburst defending their parenting further alienates the court.[7][8][9] Marco is eventually placed in a state institution due to Marianna's neglect, where he contracts pneumonia and dies shortly thereafter, devastating Rudy and Paul. The loss tests but ultimately reinforces their commitment to each other, as Rudy returns to performing a poignant rendition of "I Shall Be Released" in tribute.[8][10]

Production

Development and inspiration

The screenplay for Any Day Now originated with George Arthur Bloom, who penned the initial draft over 30 years prior to the film's release, drawing loose inspiration from his late-1970s encounters in Brooklyn with a flamboyant gay man named Rudy who informally cared for a mentally handicapped child abandoned by its drug-addicted mother.[11][4] Bloom's script fictionalized the circumstances, eschewing a direct retelling in favor of dramatized elements centered on adoption and custody struggles, and it nearly reached production earlier with actors including Tommy Lee Jones before stalling.[11][12] Director Travis Fine optioned Bloom's screenplay following his own return to filmmaking with The Space Between (2011), rewriting it to incorporate a romantic partner for the lead characterâ€”transforming the narrative into a couple's storyâ€”and infusing personal reflections on parental alienation to underscore themes of unconventional family bonds.[11][13] Fine, an actor-turned-director, envisioned the film as a character-driven exploration of 1970s legal prejudices against gay men seeking custody, set specifically in 1979 West Hollywood to evoke the era's discriminatory court systems without descending into didacticism, aiming instead for a universal portrayal of love transcending societal biases that resonated with broader adoption debates.[4][13] Production under Music Box Films commenced principal photography on May 24, 2011, spanning a five-week schedule characteristic of low-budget independent cinema, with Fine emphasizing restraint to honor historical LGBT struggles while appealing to mainstream audiences through relatable human drama.[11][14]

Casting

Alan Cumming was cast as Rudy Donatello, a nightclub singer and drag performer, after director Travis Fine made a cold call to his agency; Cumming committed early, drawn to the script's emotional depth and his own advocacy for LGBT equality.[4][13] Garret Dillahunt portrayed Paul Fliger, a deputy district attorney, joining just eight to nine days before principal photography began following intensive discussions with Fine, who added the character to strengthen the central relationship.[4] Isaac Leyva played Marco Deison, the abandoned teenager with Down syndrome, selected from a nationwide search involving auditions of dozens of actors aged 12 to mid-20s through agents, managers, and Down syndrome organizations; Leyva, who has the condition himself, was chosen for his inherent charm and ability to convey genuine emotion.[4][13] Frances Fisher appeared in a supporting role as Judge Meyerson, the presiding court official.[15] Fine rewrote portions of Leyva's role post-casting to align with his strengths, such as emphasizing warmth over angerâ€”which Leyva could not authentically depictâ€”and incorporating dance sequences supported by his acting teacher to highlight natural expressiveness.[4][13] This adjustment prioritized verifiable emotional realism over scripted rigidity, contributing to the portrayal's authenticity without relying on non-disabled actors. Cumming and Dillahunt, despite the compressed timeline, forged an immediate rapport during pre-shoot preparations like wig fittings, allowing their on-screen dynamic as a couple to emerge organically without predefined boundaries for intimate scenes, which Cumming described as effortlessly comfortable.[16] As an independent production tackling custody battles and 1970s-era biases against gay parenting and disability, casting demanded flexibility amid budget constraints, yet attracted performers committed to the material's unflinching humanism rather than commercial appeal.[4]

Filming and technical aspects

Principal photography for Any Day Now took place from August to October 2011, primarily in Los Angeles to authentically recreate the late 1970s West Hollywood setting central to the story.[17] Production emphasized practical locations and constructed sets dressed with era-specific details, such as vintage automobiles, period clothing, and interior designs, to maintain historical accuracy without relying on digital alterations or visible modern intrusions.[11] Cinematographer Rachel Morrison, who holds a master's degree in cinematography from the American Film Institute, handled the visual style, employing handheld camera work and natural lighting to convey the grounded, everyday struggles of the characters in a pre-digital era.[18] Her approach prioritized intimate framing in domestic and courtroom sequences, fostering emotional immediacy through close-ups and medium shots that highlighted interpersonal dynamics without stylized flourishes.[19] The film runs 98 minutes and features no significant visual effects, relying instead on practical effects for authenticity in scenes depicting home life and legal proceedings.[1] Composer Joey Newman scored the picture, integrating Rufus Wainwright's original composition "Metaphorical Blanket," written and performed specifically for the end credits to underscore thematic resolution.[20][21] This soundtrack choice blended contemporary songwriting with 1970s-era needle drops to enhance period immersion while advancing narrative closure.[22]

Release

Premiere and distribution

The film had its world premiere at the Tribeca Film Festival on April 20, 2012, screening multiple times during the event and culminating in its receipt of the Heineken Audience Award for best narrative feature on April 28, 2012.[23][24] Music Box Films, an independent distributor specializing in specialty cinema, handled U.S. distribution following the festival circuit, opting for a limited theatrical rollout starting December 14, 2012, in key markets including New York City and Los Angeles, with subsequent expansion to additional select cities.[2][25] This approach aligned with standard indie strategies to prioritize arthouse theaters and build word-of-mouth prior to wider ancillary release. Promotional efforts centered on the film's roots in a 1970s true-story inspiration for its screenplayâ€”originally penned three decades earlierâ€”and spotlighted lead performances by Alan Cumming and Garret Dillahunt, targeting independent film enthusiasts and communities engaged with LGBTQ+ narratives through festival buzz, trailers, and targeted advertising.[26][27] Home video distribution followed with DVD and Blu-ray editions issued on April 23, 2013, while digital streaming availability emerged on platforms such as Netflix.[28][29] Internationally, releases included the United Kingdom on September 6, 2013, managed through entities like Celsius Entertainment for sales and local theatrical partners.[30][31]

Box office performance

Any Day Now opened in limited release across 16 theaters in the United States on December 14, 2012, distributed by Music Box Films.[25] Its opening weekend generated $40,489, representing 20.1% of the domestic total gross.[25] The film ultimately earned $201,395 in domestic box office receipts, achieving a legs multiplier of 4.97 relative to its debut weekend and an average theatrical run of 6.1 weeks per venue.[25] International earnings amounted to $56,505, contributing to a worldwide theatrical gross of $257,900, with the domestic market accounting for 78.1% of the total.[25] This performance aligned with the typical trajectory for independent dramas on limited screens amid competition from major wide releases during the December holiday period.[32]

Reception

Critical response

Any Day Now received generally positive reviews from critics, earning a 78% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 65 reviews, with an average score of 6.7/10.[3] On Metacritic, it holds a score of 60 out of 100 from 16 critics, indicating mixed to positive reception.[6] Reviewers frequently praised the lead performances by Alan Cumming and Garret Dillahunt for their emotional depth and authenticity, highlighting Cumming's portrayal of Rudy Donatello as particularly compelling and multifaceted.[33] Critics commended the film's restraint in addressing gay adoption rights during the 1970s, noting its avoidance of preachiness while effectively exploring the era's legal and social barriers. Variety described it as an "intimate character drama" that delves into the lack of adoption rights for gay couples in Los Angeles without becoming didactic. The New York Times lauded its "outraged, unblinking depiction of institutionalized homophobia," emphasizing the raw intensity of the courtroom and personal struggles depicted.[34][33] Some reviewers, however, criticized the narrative for contrivance and excessive sentimentality, arguing that it occasionally prioritizes emotional manipulation over subtlety. IndieWire faulted the film for "hectoring audiences with good intentions but maudlin melodrama," pointing to awkward pacing in key plot developments. The New York Post characterized it as "brassily sentimental," suggesting moments that veered into overly dramatic territory reminiscent of older cinematic styles.[35][10] Overall, professional assessments ranged from three-out-of-four stars in several outlets to full endorsements for its heartfelt execution.

Audience reception

The film garnered a 7.4 out of 10 rating on IMDb from 6,747 user votes, reflecting broad appreciation among viewers for its emotional depth and portrayal of a gay couple's custody battle over a teenager with Down syndrome in the 1970s.[1] Many users highlighted the heartfelt storytelling, strong performances by Alan Cumming and Garret Dillahunt, and the narrative's resonance with contemporary debates on parenting rights and family structures beyond traditional norms.[36] On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 76% audience score from verified viewer ratings, with feedback emphasizing the authentic depiction of love and legal struggles against societal prejudice.[3] Some audience members expressed reservations about the film's handling of confrontations involving gay parenting, describing them as occasionally "odd" or contrived, while others perceived elements of preachiness amid the melodrama, particularly in scenes underscoring institutional bias.[36] Conservative-leaning viewer critiques remain sparse, with limited commentary questioning the 1970s context's emphasis on non-traditional families without broader societal risk discussions, though the overall reception leans positive rather than deeply polarized.[36] Sustained interest persists through streaming availability on platforms like Netflix, maintaining niche viewership drawn to its themes of resilience and civil rights, albeit with ideological divides evident in selective user endorsements.[37]

Awards and nominations

Any Day Now garnered recognition primarily from independent film festivals and LGBTQ+-focused awards, with multiple audience awards highlighting its grassroots appeal rather than mainstream industry accolades. The film did not receive nominations from major ceremonies such as the Academy Awards or Golden Globes, consistent with its limited theatrical release and independent production status.[5] Key wins include the Heineken Audience Award for Best Narrative Feature at the 2012 Tribeca Film Festival, selected by public vote from over 100 entries in the narrative competition.[23] At the 2012 Seattle International Film Festival, it secured the Golden Space Needle for Best Film (awarded to director Travis Fine) and Best Actor (Alan Cumming).[38] The Chicago International Film Festival's 2012 Audience Choice Award for Best Narrative Feature further underscored viewer enthusiasm.[28]

Themes and analysis

Historical and legal context

In the 1970s United States, sodomy laws criminalized sexual acts between consenting adults of the same sex in most states, reinforcing societal and judicial views of homosexuality as morally unfit for parenting roles.[40] These statutes, which remained in effect until the Supreme Court's 2003 decision in Lawrence v. Texas, were frequently invoked in custody disputes to deny gay individuals guardianship or adoption rights, as courts prioritized the child's "best interests" under assumptions that homosexual orientation inherently posed risks to moral development.[41] Gay adoption was explicitly barred or effectively prohibited in the majority of states, with openly homosexual applicants routinely disqualified regardless of their caregiving capabilities or the absence of evidence of harm to the child.[42] Judicial precedents reflected systemic prejudice, though rare victories emerged by the late 1970s. For instance, state courts in California, Maine, Ohio, Oregon, and South Carolina awarded custody to gay parents in mid-decade cases, marking initial challenges to blanket exclusions based on sexual orientation alone.[43] The Washington Supreme Court in 1978 issued the first ruling favoring a lesbian couple's custody rights, while New York became the initial state to prohibit rejection of adoption applicants solely due to homosexuality.[44] Nonetheless, such outcomes were exceptional, as prevailing case law emphasized parental "fitness" through heteronormative lenses, often resulting in loss of children for gay guardians amid heightened scrutiny post-coming out. Regarding children with Down syndrome, institutionalization was the dominant practice throughout the 1970s, with the vast majority placed in state facilities shortly after birth due to pervasive medical and social stigma portraying them as uneducable or burdensome.[45] Family relinquishment rates were high, driven by limited community support and assumptions of inevitable dependency, though an emerging deinstitutionalization movementâ€”spurred by exposÃ©s of institutional abuses and legal advocacyâ€”began advocating for community-based care.[46] By the decade's end, federal initiatives and court challenges targeted unnecessary institutionalization, yet stigma and resource shortages sustained high placement rates, with deinstitutionalization gains accelerating only in subsequent decades.[47]

Portrayal of social issues and accuracy

The film Any Day Now is loosely inspired by an anecdote from the late 1970s relayed to original screenwriter George Arthur Bloom by a friend, rather than a verbatim true story, which has prompted critiques of its contrived dramatic elements and selective narrative choices.[26] Director Travis Fine emphasized that the screenplay draws from real-era prejudices but amplifies events for cinematic impact, such as the custody battle's courtroom confrontations, which blend historical bias with fictionalized intensity to underscore themes of discrimination.[26] This approach accurately captures the era's systemic judicial hostility toward homosexual parents, where courts presumed sexual orientation equated to moral unfitness; for instance, in contested custody disputes around 1970, only about 1% resulted in awards to the gay or lesbian parent, often prioritizing heterosexual stability over evidence of parenting competence.[48] [42] While the portrayal aligns with documented 1970s cases of gay couples facing outright denial in adoption or foster scenarios due to anti-homosexual statutes and social stigmaâ€”exemplified by early lesbian custody losses post-divorce, as in Seattle's Sandy and Madeleine caseâ€”it has drawn counter-criticism for overstating universal homophobia and sidelining legitimate child welfare considerations.[49] [43] Reviewers have noted the film's broad-strokes depiction risks implying prejudice as the sole barrier, potentially underrepresenting varied societal attitudes or the era's valid scrutiny of unstable home environments, such as one protagonist's intermittent employment as a performer amid economic pressures.[50] In reality, judicial decisions increasingly shifted toward evidence-based assessments by the late 1970s, rejecting blanket assumptions, though gay parenting remained exceptional and fraught with external risks to child stability from pervasive hostility.[51] The film's representation of disability through the character Marco, played by actor Jamie Ridler who has Down syndrome, has been praised for authenticity, avoiding caricature and integrating the condition into family dynamics without exaggeration.[52] Progressive commentators laud this and the overall narrative for illuminating intersectional vulnerabilitiesâ€”LGBTQ+ discrimination compounded by ableism and neglect in welfare systemsâ€”fostering empathy for marginalized parenting rights that echoed real legal battles.[53] [54] However, some analyses critique the selective focus on triumphant advocacy, arguing it glosses over causal factors like the adoptive parents' relational volatility or the era's data-limited but intuitively grounded concerns for child outcomes in non-traditional, high-risk households, where societal caution stemmed from observable instability rather than mere animus.[55] This tension highlights the film's role as advocacy drama over unvarnished historiography, prioritizing emotional resonance over exhaustive causal dissection of 1970s family law precedents.[56]
