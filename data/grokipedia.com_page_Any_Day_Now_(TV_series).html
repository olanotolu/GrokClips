Any Day Now (TV series)

Any Day Now is an American drama television series that aired on Lifetime from August 8, 1998, to March 3, 2002, spanning four seasons and 88 episodes.[1] The program centers on the enduring friendship between two women who met as teenagers in segregated Birmingham, Alabama, during the 1960s: Mary Elizabeth "M.E." Sims, portrayed by Annie Potts as a white Southern homemaker who later pursues law, and Rene Jackson, played by Lorraine Toussaint as a Black civil rights attorney.[2] Created by Nancy Miller, the series interweaves present-day narratives in the 1990s with flashbacks to their youth, examining themes of racial division, personal growth, and social justice through legal cases and family dynamics.[3] The show garnered critical acclaim for its candid portrayal of interracial relationships and persistent societal tensions, earning an 8.4 out of 10 rating on IMDb from over 1,200 user reviews.[1] It received 31 award nominations, including Primetime Emmy nods for outstanding guest actress and multiple NAACP Image Award nominations for Toussaint's performance as Rene Jackson, recognizing her depiction of professional and racial challenges faced by Black women.[4] While avoiding major controversies, Any Day Now distinguished itself on Lifetime by confronting issues like systemic racism and civil rights without resorting to sensationalism, contributing to its cult following despite the network's typical focus on lighter fare.[5] The series concluded on a narrative high note, with creators citing a sense of completion rather than external pressures for its end.[6]

Premise and Setting

Core Premise

Any Day Now centers on the lifelong interracial friendship between Mary Elizabeth "M.E." O'Brien Sims, a white Southern woman aspiring to write while managing family responsibilities in Birmingham, Alabama, and Rene Jackson, a black attorney who returns to the city in the 1990s to campaign for a judgeship.[3] [7] Their bond originated in the 1960s, when as children they navigated the racial segregation and violence of Birmingham during the Civil Rights Movement, including events like church bombings and enforced separation, before circumstances forced a temporary estrangement.[8] [2] The narrative employs dual timelines in each episode, interweaving present-day professional endeavorsâ€”such as Rene's legal advocacy and M.E.'s personal pursuitsâ€”with flashbacks to specific 1960s incidents that shaped their worldview, demonstrating causal links between historical racial injustices and contemporary issues in law, family relations, and community interactions.[5] [9] This structure highlights enduring influences without explicit moralizing, allowing events like segregation-era exclusions to parallel 1990s cases involving discrimination or social inequities.[10]

Historical and Geographic Setting

Any Day Now unfolds primarily in Birmingham, Alabama, during the late 1990s, portraying the city's post-civil rights landscape of urban redevelopment, legal institutions, and lingering social divisions. The modern storyline centers on professional and personal interactions within Birmingham's courthouses, neighborhoods, and public spaces, reflecting the city's transition from industrial decline to efforts at economic revitalization amid demographic shifts.[11][5] Flashback sequences transport the narrative to 1960s Birmingham, a period marked by enforced racial segregation and intense civil rights struggles, including the enforcement of Jim Crow laws that separated public facilities and schools. These vignettes depict the city's streetcars, segregated fountains, and community enforcers of racial norms, grounding character origins in the pre-1964 Civil Rights Act era. The series references historical flashpoints such as the September 15, 1963, bombing of the 16th Street Baptist Church, which killed four African American girls and galvanized national attention to Southern racial violence.[12][13][3] Alabama's geographic position in the Deep South amplifies the series' exploration of causal links between historical racial animosities and contemporary interpersonal dynamics, with Birmingham's steel-mill heritage and civil rights battleground status providing a realistic framework for conflicts rooted in inherited prejudices rather than abstracted moralism. The choice of location underscores the persistence of regional tensions, as evidenced by the city's documented history of Ku Klux Klan activity and resistance to desegregation mandates into the 1970s.[11][14]

Narrative Format

Episode Structure and Flashbacks

Each episode of Any Day Now follows a consistent non-linear format, alternating between present-day narratives set in the late 1990sâ€”focusing on the adult protagonists' legal cases and personal livesâ€”and flashback vignettes to their preteen years in the 1960s amid racial segregation in Birmingham, Alabama.[5][9] This structure, integrated within the standard runtime of approximately 42 minutes for broadcast dramas of the era, divides the storyline roughly evenly between timelines to interweave contemporary conflicts with formative past experiences.[15] The flashback mechanism establishes causal links by mirroring 1960s incidentsâ€”such as encounters with discrimination or friendship testsâ€”against parallel 1990s events, illustrating persistent social patterns without resolving them didactically.[5][16] Temporal transitions rely on visual and dialogic indicators, including black-and-white cinematography for the 1960s segments to evoke historical authenticity, eschewing voiceover narration to sustain viewer engagement through contextual immersion rather than explicit guidance.[17] This approach distinguishes the series' pacing by compressing decades-spanning character arcs into self-contained episodes, prioritizing structural economy over chronological linearity.[18]

Stylistic Elements

The series' writing approach emphasizes dialogue centered on racial injustice and interpersonal dynamics in the Deep South, drawing from the era's social realities such as segregated facilities to portray authentic tensions rather than idealized exchanges.[19] This results in scripts that prioritize substantive exploration of prejudice and friendship, though occasionally criticized for earnest preachiness in addressing reparations or historical apologies.[20] The tone maintains a poignant heaviness, blending schmaltzy emotional beats with humor to humanize characters under societal stress, setting it apart from Lifetime's lighter fare by insisting on unflinching causal links between past events and present behaviors.[19][20] Directorial choices include a dual-timeline format with rapid shifts between contemporary scenes and 1960s flashbacks, fostering a hyperactive pacing that interweaves courtroom arguments with domestic revelations to mirror the nonlinear impacts of historical trauma.[19] Cinematography in flashbacks employs a washed-out sepia filter to denote the period, enhancing visual distinction but noted as somewhat clichÃ©d in evoking mid-20th-century grit.[19] Overall, these elements cultivate a grounded realism, focusing on behavioral authenticity amid conflict without glossing over empirical frictions like cross-racial bonds in segregated Alabama.[19][20]

Cast and Characters

Main Characters and Performers

The primary protagonists of Any Day Now are Mary Elizabeth "M.E." O'Brien Sims, played by Annie Potts, and her lifelong friend Rene Jackson, played by Lorraine Toussaint. M.E. Sims is characterized as a writer, wife, mother, and grandmother navigating personal and familial challenges in contemporary Birmingham, Alabama, often reflecting on her upbringing in the segregated South.[10] Her portrayal by Potts emphasizes the tensions between traditional family roles and individual aspirations, rooted in the series' Southern setting.[3] Rene Jackson, portrayed by Toussaint, serves as a single mother and judge confronting professional and racial obstacles in the legal system, drawing from her experiences growing up as a Black girl in 1960s Alabama.[21] Toussaint's performance highlights Jackson's determination and ethical stance in judicial matters, such as disciplinary hearings and case representations, without relying on overt narratives of perpetual disadvantage.[22] The characters' friendship, forged in childhood adversity, underscores interpersonal dynamics influenced by societal shifts from civil rights era to modern times. Supporting the leads are key family members, including M.E.'s husband Colliar Sims, enacted by Chris Mulkey, whose relationship with M.E. illustrates marital strains amid career and relocation decisions.[21] Rene's interactions with her daughter and ex-husband further explore parental responsibilities and past relational conflicts, tying personal choices to broader cultural pressures on professional women. These portrayals link individual arcs to the series' examination of enduring friendships across racial and class lines.[1]

Supporting Characters and Actors

Colliar Sims, the husband of Mary Beth Sims, was portrayed by Chris Mulkey across all four seasons, serving as a recurring figure whose construction background and traditional views often clashed with his wife's legal pursuits, highlighting tensions between personal stability and social change.[21][23] Kelly Sims, Mary Beth's daughter, was played by Olivia Friedman, with her arcs depicting adolescent challenges amid the family's navigation of racial and ethical issues from the parents' era.[21][24] Rene Jackson's family included her mother Sara, enacted by Donzaleigh Abernathy, whose presence underscored intergenerational transmission of civil rights experiences from the 1960s into contemporary professional life.[25] The young counterparts in flashback sequences provided continuity: Shari Dyon Perry depicted adolescent Rene Jackson in 66 episodes spanning 1998 to 2001, capturing formative events like school integration that informed adult decisions.[26][27] Mae Middleton portrayed young Mary Beth O'Brien, emphasizing early friendship bonds tested by segregation-era realities.[23][24] Recurring clients and colleagues, such as those in Rene's district attorney's office or Mary Beth's firm, functioned episodically to ground narratives in verifiable social patterns, like employment discrimination cases drawn from historical precedents, without fabricating archetypal biases.[28] These roles advanced subplot realism by representing discrete causal outcomes of policy shifts, such as desegregation's uneven effects on families, rather than aggregating into unsubstantiated group narratives.[17]

Production

Development and Creative Team

Any Day Now was created by Nancy Miller and Deborah Joy LeVine, who developed the concept of an interracial female friendship spanning from the 1960s civil rights era to the present, initially pitching it to CBS in 1990 before the project moved to Lifetime for production.[29][30] The core premise centered on two girlsâ€”one white and working-class, the other Black and from a more affluent familyâ€”forming a bond in Birmingham, Alabama, amid the city's real historical tensions, including desegregation efforts and racial violence, to examine friendship's endurance against social barriers.[29][18] Miller served as executive producer, overseeing the series' focus on character-driven narratives grounded in interpersonal and societal conflicts rather than formulaic melodrama.[31] Lifetime premiered the series on August 8, 1998, as one of its first forays into original hour-long scripted dramas, shifting from reliance on movies and reruns to appeal to its core audience of women aged 24 to 49 with stories of female resilience and relational dynamics.[32][33] The network's strategy emphasized relatable, issue-oriented content, including race relations handled through personal lenses, aligning with Lifetime's branding as a platform for women's perspectives on family, career, and social justice.[34][35] The creative team, led by Miller, greenlit an initial commitment to 88 episodes across four seasons, reflecting confidence in the series' potential despite challenges in securing broadcast slots for such themes on major networks.[31] Production concluded in March 2002 after the contracted run, with no public renewal announced, allowing the narrative to resolve key arcs like the protagonists' evolving bond and family reconciliations.[32][36]

Filming and Technical Production

The series was primarily filmed in the Los Angeles area of California, utilizing studios and soundstages to recreate the Birmingham, Alabama, settings central to its narrative. Specific production sites included Warren Entertainment Studios in Santa Clarita for episodes requiring controlled interior environments.[37] The pilot episode was initially shot on location in New Orleans, Louisiana, before the full series production shifted to California following network pickup. Produced under Spelling Television in association with Lifetime, the show operated within the modest budgetary framework typical of cable network originals during the late 1990s, emphasizing practical set designs and minimal visual effects to prioritize character-driven dialogue and period authenticity over elaborate technical flourishes.[38] Location shoots incorporated Southern exteriors where feasible to enhance realism, supplemented by studio simulations for civil rights-era flashbacks and contemporary scenes.[39] Technical execution relied on standard 35mm film workflows common to hour-long dramas of the period, with multi-camera setups employed for efficiency in rehearsal-heavy scenes to accommodate tight schedules and resource constraints. Post-production focused on refining audio elements, including ambient sound layering for emotional immersion, handled by specialists such as composer and sound designer Duncan Watt.[40]

Episodes

Broadcast History and Overview

Any Day Now premiered on the Lifetime cable network on August 8, 1998, and concluded after four seasons on March 10, 2002, delivering a total of 88 original hour-long episodes.[1] [36] The series maintained a weekly broadcast schedule, enabling consistent viewer engagement through its structured format of interwoven present-day narratives and 1960s flashbacks.[32] This regularity, typical of Lifetime's programming strategy for dramas, supported the accumulation of a dedicated audience over the run. Holiday-themed episodes, such as season 3's "Five Golden Rings" aired in December 2000, integrated seasonal elements into the core themes of interracial friendship and civil rights without deviating from the standard episode structure.[41] After Lifetime ended production in 2002, the series saw limited initial syndication but gained renewed distribution through complete DVD sets released for home viewing.[42] By the 2020s, episodes entered daily syndication on Start TV, airing at 7:00 a.m. ET, which has preserved accessibility for retrospective audiences.[3] Full-season streaming remains sporadic across platforms like Philo, reflecting the era's transitional media landscape where physical media and basic cable reruns predominate over digital aggregation.[43]

Reception

Critical Evaluations

Critics praised Any Day Now for its realistic depiction of racism in the segregated South, particularly through flashbacks to 1960s Birmingham, Alabama, showing everyday indignities such as blacks riding in the back of buses, using separate water fountains, and facing suspicion in stores.[19] This approach avoided white savior narratives, focusing instead on authentic interracial friendship and personal struggles amid Jim Crow laws, as highlighted in analyses of the series' central bond between protagonists Rene Jackson and Mary Elizabeth "M.E." Weston.[60] The show's portrayal of subtle racism and working-class Southern dynamics was noted for capturing regional nuances without caricature, contributing to its aggregate user rating of 8.4 out of 10 on IMDb based on over 1,200 votes.[1][61] The dual-timeline structure, alternating between the characters' youth during civil rights upheavals and their adult reconnection, was commended for effectively illustrating causal links between historical racism and contemporary interpersonal tensions, with childhood segments often deemed more engaging and poignant than present-day plots.[19] Strong performances by Annie Potts and Lorraine Toussaint anchored this narrative, providing subtle emotional depth to the era-spanning friendship.[61] However, some reviews critiqued the series for heavy-handedness, arguing that its earnest pursuit of "important" themes sometimes sacrificed subtlety, laying on dramatic weight too thickly and resulting in overly serious vibes that undermined narrative flow.[19] Frequent timeline shifts were seen by others as diluting momentum, echoing slower-paced predecessors like I'll Fly Away while prioritizing message over seamless storytelling.[61] Despite these flaws, the show's commitment to racial realism was viewed as a strength, earning it a B grade in one assessment for its nourishing exploration of race and female solidarity.[61]

Audience and Viewership Data

"Any Day Now" debuted with a 1.9 household Nielsen rating in August 1998, marking a 21 percent increase over the corresponding period from the prior year and positioning it as Lifetime's strongest new series launch at the time.[51] The series performed well within Lifetime's core demographic of adult women, contributing to the network's overall ratings growth, including an 18 percent increase in the women 18-34 category during early 2000.[62] Later seasons adjusted storylines to emphasize younger characters, such as Mary Elizabeth's teenage daughter, in efforts to broaden appeal to younger female viewers.[18] On IMDb, the series holds an average user rating of 8.4 out of 10 based on 1,261 ratings, reflecting strong audience appreciation for its dramatic storytelling.[1] User reviews frequently commend the show's authentic depiction of interracial friendship and social challenges, with many describing episodes as emotionally resonant and uplifting, fostering a sense of escapism and positivity.[63] Viewers have expressed ongoing demand for home video releases, indicating enduring personal attachment despite limited post-broadcast availability.[63]

Criticisms and Cultural Debates

The episode "It's Not Just a Word," which concluded the third season on March 18, 2001, incorporated the racial epithet "nigger" 73 times in its original script (with several instances ultimately cut following network input), depicting a black basketball player's manslaughter trial after fatally responding to its use by a white teenager.[64] This narrative choice provoked internal production conflicts and broader viewer discussions on the slur's potency, with actress Lorraine Toussaint, portraying Rene Jackson, insisting on its immutable historical baggage as a marker of dehumanization and rejecting notions of reclamation by younger generations or in cultural contexts like rap music.[64] [65] Producer Nancy Miller countered that authentic examination required confronting the word's shifting societal valence, questioning whether its taboo status might erode over time amid evolving usage patterns.[64] Debates centered on intent versus impact, with participants highlighting generational and intraracial divides: some viewed the episode as a catalyst for dissecting language's role in perpetuating violence, while others, including Toussaint during script revisions, feared it risked normalizing or diluting the term's visceral harm without adequate safeguards against misinterpretation.[65] Production tensions escalated, as Toussaint clashed with Miller over a pivotal scene, underscoring uncertainties in white creators' authority to adjudicate black experiential narratives.[64] The series' overarching treatment of racial themes drew accusations of preachiness, with a 1999 New York Times review labeling it "flat-footed and preachy as drama" for prioritizing issue resolution over nuanced character development.[66] Episodes like "It's Not About the Butter," where a misperceived service interaction escalates into a discourse on contemporary microaggressions, similarly fueled arguments over whether systemic grievance emphases overshadowed explorations of personal agency and cross-racial reconciliation.[3] Retrospective commentary, including a 2017 analysis, frames these portrayals as reflective of late-1990s cable trends toward unfiltered social realism, yet questions linger on their prescience versus conformity to era-specific didacticism that often foregrounded institutional culpability at the expense of individualistic causal factors in racial dynamics.[5] Mainstream outlets' acclaim for such content, despite noted inconsistencies, illustrates institutional preferences for narratives aligning with prevailing progressive sensibilities, potentially marginalizing dissenting views on narrative balance.[13]

Awards and Nominations

Primetime Emmy Awards

Any Day Now received one Primetime Emmy Award nomination during its run, in the category of Outstanding Costumes for a Series at the 52nd Primetime Emmy Awards held on September 10, 2000.[67] The nomination was for the episode "It's Not About the Butter" from season 3, recognizing the work of costume supervisor Mary Anne Aston and costume designer Elizabeth P. Palmer for their contributions to the series' visual authenticity in portraying diverse professional and personal environments in Birmingham, Alabama.[4][68] This technical acknowledgment highlighted the production's attention to period-appropriate and character-driven attire amid competition from established network dramas like The West Wing and Providence, though the series did not secure a win in the category.[67] No further Primetime Emmy nominations were received for acting, writing, or other categories, reflecting the series' primary recognition in industry-specific honors rather than broader Emmy contention.[68]

NAACP Image Awards

Lorraine Toussaint received four NAACP Image Award nominations for Outstanding Actress in a Drama Series for her role as Rene Jackson in Any Day Now, spanning the 30th through 33rd annual ceremonies from 1999 to 2002.[71] These nominations acknowledged Toussaint's portrayal of a principled African American civil rights attorney navigating professional and personal challenges in Birmingham, Alabama, during the series' depiction of civil rights-era and contemporary storylines.[4] In the 30th NAACP Image Awards held on February 14, 1999, Toussaint was nominated alongside actresses including Della Reese for Touched by an Angel, who won the category.[72] Subsequent nominations followed in 2000, 2001â€”announced December 10, 2001, in Varietyâ€”and 2002, reflecting consistent recognition for her lead performance amid limited opportunities for African American actresses in dramatic leads on television at the time.[73][71] Toussaint did not win the award during the series' run, with winners including Reese in 1999 and others such as Vanessa Estelle Williams for Soul Food in 2003.[72] The nominations highlighted Any Day Now's emphasis on substantive roles for minority actors, avoiding common stereotypes in favor of multifaceted characters grounded in historical and legal realism, as per the series' narrative focus on race relations and friendship across racial lines.[71] No wins or additional category nominations for the series or its cast were recorded in NAACP Image Awards during this period.[4]

Other Recognitions
