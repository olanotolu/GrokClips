Dinard

Dinard (French pronunciation: [dinaʁ]; Breton: Dinarzh , [ˈdiːnars]; Gallo: Dinard) is a commune in the Ille-et-Vilaine department, Brittany, northwestern France. Dinard is on the Côte d'Émeraude of Brittany. Its beaches and mild climate make it a holiday destination, and this has resulted in the town having a variety of famous visitors and residents. The towns of Pleurtuit and Saint-Malo are nearby and the Dinard–Pleurtuit–Saint-Malo Airport is about 4 km south of Dinard. With its international film festival, villas, sumptuous hotels and casino, Dinard is regarded as one of the most prestigious seaside resorts in all of France. The official name of the town was successively Saint-Énogat (until 1879), Dinard-Saint-Énogat (from 1879 to 1921) and Dinard (since 1921). Today, moreover, Saint-Énogat is still a district belonging to the city, it is close to the sea and is home to many small shops. The city also has a football club called FCD (Football Club Dinardais), which includes categories from U6 to Senior, the club is well helped by the town hall and the city of Dinard which helps with equipment, transport, grounds, and also the organization of the club. The city also hosts the Jumping Internationnale in show jumping every year.

History

Dinard originated as a small fishing hamlet within the parish of Saint-Énogat. In the late 19th century, American and British aristocrats made Dinard a fashionable summer resort, and they built stunning villas on the cliff tops and exclusive hotels such as the 'Le Grand Hotel' on the seafront during the French "Belle Époque". The name Dinard comes from the words "Din" ("hill"/"fort") and Ard/Art ("fortified"). It has been claimed, probably erroneously, that the second element means "bear"/"Arthur"); the bear in Celtic mythology is a symbol of sovereignship. Originally, Dinard was part of the parish of Saint-Énogat. In the late 19th century, the resort became popular with wealthy British nationals who built villas on the coast. Dinard rapidly expanded. Dinard experienced a decline in tourism following World War II. Today, Dinard is considered one of the most "British" of coastal resorts in France. It has, however, retained its French charm. There are 407 listed villas. The town was liberated by American forces in August 1944 during the Battle of Saint-Malo.

Climate

Dinard is influenced by the warm North Atlantic Drift, a continuation of the Gulf Stream, resulting in a mild oceanic climate (Köppen Cfb) several degrees warmer than inland areas. The yacht club features examples of tropical palms and plants along the Promenade du Clair de Lune.

Hotels

The most exclusive hotels are the Grand Hôtel Barrière (5-star) which overlooks the Rance Estuary, and Castelbrac (5-star), the former Villa "Bric à Brac". Other notable hotels are the Royal, which sits next to the Casino above the main Esplanade. Further round the coast in the Saint-Énogat area of Dinard is the Emeria Dinard Thalasso & Spa (formerly Thalassa Dinard) and Villas de La Falaise. This seawater therapy centre offers thalassotherapy treatments.[6] In the summer, the population swells to over 40,000 with the influx of holiday makers.

Visitors

Dinard's reputation as the "Cannes of the North" has attracted a wide variety of stars. Joan Collins was a frequent visitor, and Winston Churchill enjoyed holidaying on the River Rance. T. E. Lawrence (Lawrence of Arabia) lived in Dinard as a small child, long before his Arabian exploits, and Picasso painted here in the 1920s. Debussy is supposed to have had the idea for "La Mer" during a visit to Saint-Énogat in 1902. In 1996 Éric Rohmer filmed parts of Conte d'été in Dinard. Oscar Wilde also visited the place and mentions it in his De Profundis. Welsh singer-songwriter Iwan Rheon's first studio album is named Dinard after the commune. The Wales national football team used Dinard as its base during UEFA Euro 2016. In recent years, the town's film festivals continue to draw notable figures, such as Kristin Scott Thomas, who premiered her directorial debut My Mother's Wedding at the 2025 Dinard British & Irish Film Festival.

Attractions

Dinard offers limited nightlife options compared to larger cities, but features numerous bars and fine restaurants along its streets. The town's abundance of beaches, coupled with its mild summer weather, attracts many visitors during the holiday season. Key attractions include the Casino Barrière, which has a sea-facing restaurant, and the Palais des Arts et du Festival, a hall that hosts various expositions, conferences, and events. Notable scenic spots are the Promenade du Clair de Lune, a picturesque cliffside walk offering views of the bay, and the Pointe du Moulinet, known for its panoramic vistas and proximity to Belle Époque villas that exemplify the town's elegant architectural heritage.[7] The nearby towns of Saint-Briac-sur-Mer and Saint-Lunaire also feature respectively a fairly large golf course and the beach of Longchamp, renowned as a "surfers' spot".

Film festivals

Dinard Francophone Film Festival

The Festival du film francophone de Dinard (Dinard Francophone Film Festival), originally named Festival international du film d'expression française (International French-language Film Festival), also known as the Festival du film et d'échanges francophones (FIFEF; Festival of Francophone Film and Exchanges) in 1973, was a Francophone film festival inaugurated in July 1969.[9] Its last edition was in 1974.[10]

Dinard British Film Festival

Every year Dinard hosts a festival of British and Irish cinema in the first days of October, the Dinard Festival of British & Irish Film (previously known as the Dinard British Film Festival until its renaming in 2024).[11] Founded in 1989 by Thierry de la Fournière, the Dinard Festival has become an important vector for all films wishing to conquer the European market.[11]

Educational facilities

Many educational facilities may be found in Dinard, most of them elementary and primary schools, including public schools such as École primaire publique Alain Colas and private ones like École primaire privée Notre Dame de la Mer. There is one secondary school at the collège level: the Collège Le Bocage, a state-run public school with approximately 570 pupils from Dinard and its vicinity.[12][13] For upper secondary education (lycée), Dinard hosts the Lycée hôtelier Yvon Bourges, a public vocational lycée specializing in hospitality and catering, with around 160 students. Nearby options for general education include the Lycée Jacques Cartier in Saint-Malo.[14]

Beaches

The town has several beaches, all of which are sandy and well-maintained. The central and most popular beach is Plage de l'Écluse. Among the larger beaches are Plage du Prieuré (570 m long) and Plage de Saint-Énogat (approximately 500 m long). Dinard also has Plage de Port-Blanc and other smaller beaches near the city center. They are all reachable by a coastal path called the Chemin de Ronde. [15]

International relations

Dinard is twinned with:
  • Newquay, United Kingdom (since 1986)
  • Starnberg, Germany (since 1977)

Notable residents

  • Captain Philippe Koenigswerther, French Resistance fighter, Chevalier de la Légion d'Honneur, executed by the Nazis, born in Dinard in 1918.
  • Virginia d'Albert-Lake (1910–1997), American-born French Resistance fighter who lived near Dinard after World War II until her death.[20]
  • Florence Delaage, classical pianist, born in Dinard.
  • Yvonne Prévost (1876–1942), French tennis player.
