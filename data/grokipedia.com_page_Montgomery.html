Montgomery

Bernard Law Montgomery, 1st Viscount Montgomery of Alamein (17 November 1887 – 24 March 1976) was a British Army field marshal renowned for his command of Allied ground forces in key Second World War campaigns, including the North African theater and the liberation of northwest Europe.[1][2] The son of an Anglican bishop from Northern Ireland, he graduated from the Royal Military College, Sandhurst, and served in the First World War, where he was severely wounded at the Battle of the Somme.[1][3] Montgomery's most celebrated achievement came in 1942 as commander of the British Eighth Army, when he orchestrated the victory at the Second Battle of El Alamein, halting the Axis advance in Egypt and marking a strategic pivot that contributed to the eventual expulsion of German and Italian forces from North Africa.[1][4] He subsequently directed the Allied invasion of Sicily and mainland Italy in 1943 before assuming leadership of the 21st Army Group for Operation Overlord, the Normandy landings of June 1944, where his forces played a central role in breaking out from the beachheads despite logistical strains and fierce German resistance.[4][1] His emphasis on thorough preparation, overwhelming firepower, and infantry-artillery coordination restored confidence in British troops after earlier defeats but often prolonged operations, as seen in the costly but successful push across northern Europe.[5] Though elevated to viscount and Chief of the Imperial General Staff postwar, Montgomery's legacy includes significant controversies stemming from his abrasive personality and strategic divergences; he frequently clashed with U.S. counterparts like General Dwight D. Eisenhower over resource allocation and operational tempo, while initiatives such as Operation Market Garden in 1944 ended in failure due to overambitious airborne assaults into fortified German positions.[4][6] In later years, he publicly endorsed South African apartheid and opposed liberal immigration policies in Britain, positions that aligned with his conservative worldview but alienated progressive contemporaries and fueled debates over his broader judgment.[4][7] Nicknamed "Monty" for his rapport with enlisted men, he exemplified resolute leadership amid coalition tensions, though assessments of his generalship vary, with empirical analyses crediting his caution for minimizing casualties relative to riskier alternatives pursued elsewhere.[5][1]

People

Military figures

Field Marshal Bernard Law Montgomery (17 November 1887 – 24 March 1976) commanded British forces in key campaigns of the Second World War, including the defeat of Axis armies at the Second Battle of El Alamein from 23 October to 11 November 1942, which halted German advances in North Africa and earned him promotion to full general.[4] Appointed commander of the Eighth Army in August 1942, he reorganized demoralized troops, emphasizing training and logistics before launching offensives that pursued Rommel's forces across Libya and into Tunisia by May 1943.[8] Montgomery later served as ground forces commander for Operation Overlord, directing Allied advances from Normandy landings on 6 June 1944 through operations like Market Garden in September 1944, though criticized for cautious tactics and over-optimism in some assessments by contemporaries like Eisenhower.[1] Post-war, he became the first permanent Chairman of the NATO Military Committee in 1951, shaping Western European defense strategy until 1958.[2] Major General Richard Montgomery (2 December 1738 – 31 December 1775), born in Ireland, transitioned from British Army service—where he fought in the Seven Years' War, rising to captain by 1773—to the Continental Army during the American Revolution, commissioned as a brigadier general on 30 June 1775.[9] Leading the invasion of Canada, he captured Fort St. Johns after a 45-day siege ending on 2 November 1775 and took Montreal unopposed on 13 November 1775, securing American control over much of the province.[10] Montgomery's joint assault on Quebec City with Benedict Arnold on 31 December 1775 failed amid a blizzard, resulting in his death by cannon fire near the city's walls, an event that galvanized Patriot resolve and led Congress to promote him posthumously to major general on 9 January 1776.[11] His sacrifice, commemorated in places like New York's Montgomery County established in 1778, underscored early Revolutionary commitment despite the strategic setback of Quebec's retention by British forces.[9]

Literary and cultural figures

Lucy Maud Montgomery (November 30, 1874 – April 24, 1942) was a Canadian author best known for her novel Anne of Green Gables (1908), which has sold over 50 million copies worldwide and inspired extensive adaptations in film, television, and theater.[12] Drawing from her upbringing in Prince Edward Island, she produced 20 novels, more than 500 short stories, over 300 poems, and numerous essays, often exploring themes of rural life, imagination, and personal growth.[13] Her works gained international acclaim, with early publications in Canadian, British, and American magazines during the 1890s and 1900s.[13] Marion Montgomery (November 14, 1925 – March 15, 2011) was an American poet, novelist, and literary critic who emphasized Southern agrarian traditions and Christian humanism in his writings.[14] He authored poetry collections such as The Wandering Boy (1963) and novels like The Men of the Green..., while also producing critical works on authors including Flannery O'Connor and Allen Tate.[14] Montgomery taught English, composition, and creative writing at the University of Georgia from 1957 until his retirement in 1987, influencing generations of students through his focus on moral and philosophical dimensions of literature.[14] In music, John Leslie "Wes" Montgomery (March 6, 1923 – June 15, 1968) emerged as a pioneering jazz guitarist, self-taught and renowned for his thumb-plucking technique that produced a warm, octave-rich tone.[15] His recordings, including the 1960 album The Incredible Jazz Guitar of Wes Montgomery, blended bebop, cool jazz, and soul jazz, influencing artists like George Benson and Pat Metheny.[15] Raised in Indianapolis, he rose to prominence in the 1950s through club performances and collaborations, earning posthumous recognition as one of the 20th century's major jazz innovators.[15]

Political and business figures

Montgomery Blair (1813–1883) served as United States Postmaster General from 1861 to 1864 in President Abraham Lincoln's cabinet, overseeing the expansion of postal services during the Civil War, including the initiation of free city delivery in 1863. Born in Franklin County, Kentucky, Blair was a lawyer and Democrat who advised Lincoln on legal matters and advocated for gradual emancipation, though he opposed radical Republican policies and resigned amid cabinet tensions.[16] G. V. "Sonny" Montgomery (1920–2006) represented Mississippi's 3rd congressional district as a Democrat in the U.S. House of Representatives from 1967 to 1997, focusing on veterans' affairs and co-authoring the Montgomery GI Bill in 1984, which provided education benefits to service members.[17] A World War II veteran and long-serving member of the House Veterans' Affairs Committee, he chaired it from 1989 to 1995 and influenced military base retention policies in Mississippi.[17] Velmanette Montgomery (1936–2022) served as a Democratic member of the New York State Senate for the 25th district from 1985 to 2020, championing juvenile justice reform, child welfare, and criminal justice issues, including sponsorship of the 1992 Family Court Act amendments. As the longest-serving female senator in New York history, she prioritized community-based alternatives to incarceration and education funding for at-risk youth.[18] Isaiah T. Montgomery (1847–1924), born into slavery, founded the all-Black town of Mound Bayou, Mississippi, in 1887 as a post-Reconstruction self-sufficient community, serving as its first mayor and promoting economic independence through agriculture and cooperative enterprises.[19] An advisor to Booker T. Washington, Montgomery advocated for Black land ownership and education, establishing schools and businesses that sustained the town amid Jim Crow-era challenges.[20] In business, R. Lawrence "Larry" Montgomery (1949–2019) led Kohl's Corporation as CEO from 1999 to 2009 and chairman until 2011, driving expansion from 213 stores in 1999 to over 1,100 by 2009 through aggressive growth into new markets and merchandising shifts toward edgier apparel.[21] Starting as a store manager trainee at Kohl's in 1979, he rose through executive ranks, contributing to revenue growth from $3.2 billion in 1999 to $17.2 billion in 2008.[22]

Other notable individuals

Robert R. Montgomery is an American hematologist whose research has significantly advanced the treatment of inherited bleeding disorders, particularly von Willebrand disease (VWD) and hemophilia. His laboratory work at the Blood Research Institute identified a polymorphism in the von Willebrand factor gene that predicts gastrointestinal bleeding risk in VWD type 1 patients, enabling more precise risk stratification and management strategies.[23] In June 2025, Montgomery received the inaugural Roberts Award from the International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis for bridging laboratory science with clinical applications in hemostasis.[23] In athletics, Jeff Montgomery pitched as a Major League Baseball reliever, compiling 255 career saves across 12 seasons, primarily with the Kansas City Royals (1988–1996) where he led the American League in saves in 1992 and 1993. Born May 29, 1965, in Wellston, Ohio, he finished his career with the Atlanta Braves, earning an All-Star selection in 1996 and contributing to their World Series appearance that year.[24]

Places

United States

Montgomery, Alabama, serves as the capital and second-most populous city in the state, located centrally along the Alabama River. The city was incorporated on December 3, 1819, through the consolidation of two earlier settlements, East Alabama Town and New Philadelphia.[25] Named for Major General Richard Montgomery, an American Revolutionary War figure killed at the Battle of Quebec in 1775, it became the state capital in 1846.[26] As of 2024, Montgomery's population stands at 195,287, while its metropolitan area encompasses approximately 374,000 residents.[27][28] The city functions as the seat of Montgomery County, Alabama, which was established by the Mississippi Territory legislature on December 6, 1816, and covers 793 square miles with a 2020 population of 228,954.[29][30] Beyond Alabama, the name Montgomery applies to 22 distinct municipalities, townships, or communities across the United States, including smaller cities such as Montgomery in Georgia (population around 4,000), Texas (around 2,000), and New York.[31] These locales vary in size and significance but share etymological roots tied to the same historical namesake in many cases. Montgomery County exists in 18 states, with at least 13 honoring General Richard Montgomery; the remainder derive from other sources, such as local figures or unrelated origins.[32] Prominent examples include Montgomery County, Maryland, an affluent suburb of Washington, D.C., with a 2023 population of 1.06 million, a median household income of $128,733, and integration into the National Capital Region's economy.[33] Other sizable instances are Montgomery County, Texas (population exceeding 600,000, anchored by Conroe and The Woodlands), and Montgomery County, Pennsylvania (around 860,000 residents, encompassing Norristown). These counties typically function as key administrative and economic hubs, reflecting regional demographics and development patterns.

United Kingdom

Montgomery is a small market town and community in Powys, eastern Wales, located approximately one mile west of the England-Wales border near Shropshire and the River Severn.[34] The town lies within the historic county of Montgomeryshire, which it served as the administrative center from 1536 until the county's reorganization in 1974.[35] As of the 2021 census, the Montgomery community, encompassing the town and surrounding rural areas, had a population of 1,286 residents across 13.6 square kilometers.[36] The town's origins trace to the Norman Conquest, when Roger de Montgomery, Earl of Shrewsbury, constructed the motte-and-bailey castle known as Hen Domen between 1071 and 1074 to secure the Welsh Marches against Welsh resistance.[37] The present Montgomery Castle, built in stone starting around 1223 under orders from King Henry III, replaced Hen Domen amid conflicts with Welsh prince Llywelyn the Great; it featured a twin-towered gatehouse and served as a key frontier stronghold.[38] The site witnessed repeated sieges, including a 1267 treaty between Henry III and Llywelyn ap Gruffydd, destruction by Owain Glyndŵr in 1402, and the largest battle fought in Wales during the English Civil War on 18 September 1644, where Parliamentarian forces under Sir Thomas Mytton defeated Royalists led by Sir Francis Ottley.[39] The castle was slighted after the Royalist defeat, leaving extensive ruins that overlook the town today.[40] Montgomery received its charter as a borough in 1227, fostering rapid growth as a trading center with a layout that has remained largely unchanged for over 800 years, featuring narrow streets within medieval town walls.[34] St Nicholas Parish Church, constructed from 1227, exemplifies early English Gothic architecture with a notable timber roof and historical monuments, including those to local gentry families.[34] The town center blends half-timbered medieval buildings with Georgian and Victorian facades, designated as a conservation area to preserve its heritage.[41] Key attractions include the Old Bell Museum, housed in a 16th-century former inn with exhibits on local history and the Civil War battle, and proximity to walking paths like the Offa's Dyke long-distance footpath.[34] Economically, it functions as a rural service hub with tourism driven by its historical sites and surrounding countryside, though population density remains low at about 95 persons per square kilometer.[36]

Other countries

Montgomery is a residential neighbourhood in the northwest quadrant of Calgary, Alberta, Canada, bounded by Shaganappi Trail to the east, 32nd Avenue NW to the south, Bow Trail to the west, and the Bow River to the north.[42] Developed primarily in the post-World War II era, it features a mix of single-family homes, townhouses, and apartments, with a population of approximately 4,500 residents as per the 2021 Census of Canada.[42] The area is known for its proximity to the University of Calgary, scenic river pathways, and community amenities including parks, schools, and local businesses along 33rd Avenue NW.[43] In Australia, Montgomery refers to an electoral division in the Tasmanian Legislative Council, encompassing parts of the northwest coast including the cities of Devonport and Burnie, established in its current form following redistributions in the 1990s and covering about 2,400 square kilometres with over 120,000 electors as of 2025.[44] Additionally, Mount Montgomery is a 524-metre peak in the Dial Range of northwest Tasmania, within a 308-hectare state reserve managed for conservation and recreation, offering hiking trails with views of Bass Strait and surrounding coastal towns.[45] In Pakistan, Montgomery was the former name of the city now known as Sahiwal in Punjab province, established in 1865 and renamed in 1967; the surrounding district, once Montgomery District with a 1941 population of about 950,000, was also redesignated Sahiwal District, reflecting administrative changes post-independence from British rule.[46] The name derived from British administrator Sir Robert Montgomery, who served as Lieutenant Governor of Punjab from 1862 to 1865.[46] In Belgium, Montgomery designates a major public square and metro station in the Woluwe-Saint-Pierre municipality of Brussels, named after British Field Marshal Bernard Law Montgomery following World War II; the site serves as a key transport hub on Line 1 of the Brussels Metro and Line 95 tram, handling thousands of daily commuters near the European Quarter.

Organizations and institutions

Educational institutions

Montgomery College in Montgomery County, Maryland, is a public community college founded in 1946 as an evening program at Bethesda-Chevy Chase High School with an initial enrollment of 186 students.[47] It has expanded to three campuses and serves over 50,000 students annually through credit and noncredit programs, emphasizing workforce development and transfer pathways.[48] Auburn University at Montgomery, a public institution in Montgomery, Alabama, was established in 1967 as an extension of Auburn University, with its first full classes offered in 1969 and initial enrollment near 600 students.[49] By 2024, it enrolls approximately 3,300 undergraduates and offers bachelor's, master's, and doctoral degrees across diverse fields, governed by the Auburn University Board of Trustees.[50] Montgomery County Community College, located in Blue Bell and Pottstown, Pennsylvania, was founded in 1964 to address local educational needs and now enrolls about 13,750 students, with roughly 75% attending part-time.[51] It provides over 100 associate degrees, certificates, and workforce training programs tailored to regional businesses.[52] Smaller institutions include Montgomery Community College in Troy, North Carolina, chartered in 1967 as a technical institute and focused on technical degrees and university transfers.[53] Fulton-Montgomery Community College in Johnstown, New York, established in 1963 under the SUNY system, serves around 2,000 students with associate degrees and certificates in business, health, and liberal arts.[54] Additionally, Montgomery School, a private preparatory institution in Maryland founded in 1915 for boys, now operates as a coeducational K-12 school emphasizing classical education.[55]

Commercial entities

Montgomery Ward & Co. is an American e-commerce retailer specializing in general merchandise such as furniture, tools, appliances, and clothing, with origins as a pioneering mail-order business established in Chicago in 1872 by Aaron Montgomery Ward.[56] The company revolutionized retail by offering catalog sales directly to rural consumers, avoiding middlemen and providing a money-back guarantee, which expanded its reach nationwide by the early 20th century.[57] After filing for bankruptcy in 2000 and closing its physical stores, it relaunched as an online-only operation in 2004 under new ownership, continuing to offer catalog services and credit options like buy-now-pay-later plans.[58] Montgomery Elevator Company, founded in 1892 in Moline, Illinois, initially as a machinery manufacturer before specializing in elevators from 1910 onward, became a major U.S. producer of vertical transportation systems.[59] Known for installing elevators in commercial and residential buildings across the country, the firm operated independently until its acquisition by KONE in 1994, after which its legacy products continued under the successor brand.[60] Montgomery Inn is a barbecue restaurant chain headquartered in the Cincinnati suburb of Montgomery, Ohio, renowned for its loin back ribs and barbecue sauce, which has been ranked among America's top sauces.[61] Established in 1951 by Ted and Matula Gregory, who transformed a local tavern into a rib-focused venue, it expanded to multiple locations including sites on the Ohio River and in Cincinnati's Pleasant Ridge neighborhood, offering dine-in, carryout, and nationwide shipping of products.[62] The chain maintains a family-owned structure and emphasizes Cincinnati-style barbecue traditions.[61]

Governmental and other organizations

The Montgomery Town Council serves as the local governmental authority for the town of Montgomery in Powys, Wales, handling community services, planning, and public meetings as outlined in its operational policies. Among other organizations, the Montgomery Improvement Association (MIA) was established on December 5, 1955, by African American ministers and community leaders in Montgomery, Alabama, to coordinate the bus boycott protesting segregated public transportation.[63] The organization played a central role in mobilizing participants and electing Martin Luther King Jr. as its president, contributing to the U.S. Supreme Court's 1956 ruling against bus segregation.[64] The Montgomery Institute, founded as a Mississippi non-profit corporation on October 27, 1999, and based in Meridian, focuses on economic development, entrepreneurship, and community upbuilding in the region.[65]

Ships and naval vessels

United States Navy vessels

The USS Montgomery (C-9) was the lead ship of her class of two small unprotected cruisers authorized by the Naval Appropriations Act of 7 September 1888. Built by Columbia Iron Works in Baltimore, Maryland, she was launched on 5 December 1891 and commissioned on 26 June 1894.[66] During the Spanish–American War, she conducted blockade operations off Cuba and captured the Spanish bark Lorenzo as a prize on 5 May 1898.[67] Decommissioned in September 1904, she underwent repairs and was recommissioned in 1909 for training duties. Renamed USS Anniston on 14 February 1918 to free the name for a new destroyer, she served as a receiving ship during World War I before final decommissioning on 11 July 1919 and sale for scrap.[68] USS Montgomery (DD-121) was a Wickes-class destroyer constructed by Bethlehem Shipbuilding Corporation's Fore River Shipyard in Quincy, Massachusetts. Laid down on 27 February 1918, launched on 14 September 1918, and commissioned on 4 July 1919 (after World War I armistice), she conducted fleet exercises before decommissioning on 31 October 1922.[69] Recommissioned on 26 September 1930 following conversion to a light minelayer (reclassified DM-17 effective 21 November 1930), she participated in fleet problems and advanced base exercises. During World War II, she defended Pearl Harbor against the Japanese attack on 7 December 1941, firing anti-aircraft guns and reporting enemy aircraft positions.[70] She later laid defensive minefields off Guadalcanal—sustaining a collision with USS Preble (DM-20) that removed 20 feet of her bow during operations on the night of 24–25 August 1943—and supported invasions in the Gilbert, Marshall, and Marianas islands. Decommissioned on 23 March 1945 at Charleston, South Carolina, she was struck from the Naval Register on 1 May 1945 and sold for scrapping on 20 December 1946.[71] USS Montgomery (LCS-8) is an Independence-class littoral combat ship built by Austal USA in Mobile, Alabama, optimized for missions in littoral zones against asymmetric threats such as submarines, small surface craft, and mines. Laid down on 25 June 2013, launched on 6 August 2014, and christened on 8 November 2014, she was delivered to the Navy on 20 December 2015 and commissioned on 10 September 2016 during a ceremony in Mobile.[72] With a length of 421 feet, beam of 103 feet, displacement of approximately 3,200 tons, and top speed exceeding 40 knots, she employs interchangeable mission packages and supports manned and unmanned vehicles. Homeported in San Diego, California, since 2019, she operates under Littoral Combat Ship Squadron 1 and has conducted deployments including operations in the U.S. 5th Fleet. As of 2025, she remains in active service.[73]

Other naval vessels

HMS Montgomery (G95) was a destroyer of the Royal Navy's Town class, acquired from the United States Navy under the Lend-Lease agreement as the former USS Wickes (DD-75), the lead ship of the Wickes-class destroyers built during World War I.[74][75] Laid down on 26 June 1917 and launched on 25 June 1918 by Bath Iron Works, the vessel displaced approximately 1,247 tons, measured 314 feet in length, and was armed with four 4-inch guns, one 3-inch anti-aircraft gun, and depth charges for anti-submarine warfare.[74][76] She was the only ship in the Royal Navy to bear the name Montgomery.[75] Transferred to the Royal Navy on 3 September 1940 and commissioned at Halifax, Nova Scotia, on 23 October 1940 under Commander H.F. Nash (retired), HMS Montgomery underwent fitting out before departing Canada on 1 November 1940 and arriving at Belfast on 11 November.[74][75] Based initially at Liverpool, she conducted convoy escort duties in the Atlantic, including convoys TC 8, HX 108, HX 114, HX 117, and others, while rescuing survivors from sunk merchant vessels such as 39 from the Scottish Standard on 21 February 1941 and seven from the Friar Rock on 17 January 1942.[74] After repairs from April to September 1941, she served with the 4th Escort Group at Greenock and continued anti-submarine patrols, rescuing 32 survivors from the Manchester Merchant on 25 February 1943.[74][76] In February 1942, HMS Montgomery joined the Western Local Escort Force at Halifax before being loaned to the Royal Canadian Navy later that year for continued convoy protection operations.[74][76] She underwent repairs at Charleston from 1942 to 1943 and remained in service until placed in reserve on 23 February 1944.[74] The destroyer was ultimately scrapped in the spring of 1945, having contributed to Allied efforts in the Battle of the Atlantic without notable combat sinkings attributed directly to her actions.[74][75] Subsequent commanders included Lieutenant Commander W.L. Puxley from 28 February 1941 to 23 January 1943 and Lieutenant K.I. Hamilton until early 1944.[74] No other naval vessels named Montgomery have been documented in service with the Royal Navy, Royal Canadian Navy, or other non-United States navies.[75][76]

Other uses

In media and fiction

In the Star Trek franchise, Montgomery Scott, nicknamed "Scotty," is the chief engineer aboard the USS Enterprise, renowned for his engineering prowess and the catchphrase "I'm givin' her all she's got," first portrayed by James Doohan in the original 1966 television series and appearing across multiple iterations including films and reboots.[77] Charles Montgomery Burns, commonly called Mr. Burns or Monty, is the elderly, greedy owner of the Springfield Nuclear Power Plant and primary antagonist in the animated series The Simpsons, characterized by his vast wealth, Machiavellian schemes, and frail physique, debuting in the show's 1989 premiere episode.[78] Addison Forbes Montgomery is a fictional neonatal surgeon and main character in the medical dramas Grey's Anatomy (introduced in the 2005 season 2 premiere as Derek Shepherd's ex-wife) and its spin-off Private Practice (2007–2013), specializing in high-risk pregnancies and maternal-fetal medicine, portrayed by Kate Walsh.[79] Bianca Montgomery is a central character in the soap opera All My Children, depicted as the daughter of Erica Kane, with her storyline notably including a coming-out arc as a lesbian in 2003, one of daytime television's early prominent LGBTQ+ narratives, played primarily by Eden Riegel from 2000 to 2010 (with returns).[80] In Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events book series (1999–2006) and its Netflix adaptation (2017–2019), Dr. Montgomery "Monty" Montgomery is the Baudelaire orphans' brief second guardian, an eccentric herpetologist murdered after discovering a conspiracy involving the villainous Count Olaf.[81]

Brands and miscellaneous

Montgomery Ward & Co., founded on August 18, 1872, by Aaron Montgomery Ward in Chicago, Illinois, pioneered the mail-order catalog business in the United States, initially offering 163 items on a single-sheet catalog focused on dry goods for farmers.[82] The company expanded into department stores and later e-commerce, selling general merchandise including appliances, furniture, and clothing until its original operations ceased in 2001, though a revived online version persists.[56] Original Montgomery, a British apparel brand established in the 1890s in Leicester, England, gained renown for producing duffle coats originally commissioned by the British Admiralty for naval use, emphasizing handmade wool garments with traditional toggle fastenings.[83] Montgomery Inn, a barbecue restaurant chain founded in 1951 in Cincinnati, Ohio, by Ted Gregory, specializes in loin back pork ribs and barbecue sauce, operating multiple locations including its original site on Montgomery Road and shipping products nationwide.[61]
