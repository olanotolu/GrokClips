New York COVID-19 nursing home policy

The New York COVID-19 nursing home policy consisted of guidance issued by the New York State Department of Health on March 25, 2020, directing long-term care facilities to readmit any resident transferred to a hospital upon discharge if they continued to require nursing home-level care, and to accept new admissions from hospitals without regard to confirmed or suspected COVID-19 status or the facility's resources for isolating such patients, with the stated aim of preserving acute care hospital capacity amid surging cases.[1] This approach, which prohibited nursing homes from inquiring about or denying admissions based solely on COVID-19 diagnosis, diverged from federal Centers for Disease Control and Prevention interim recommendations issued days earlier, which advised against routine readmission or admission of COVID-positive patients absent dedicated cohorted units or sufficient isolation capacity.[2] The policy, in effect until rescinded on May 10, 2020, coincided with over 4,000 subsequent nursing home resident deaths and has been implicated in exacerbating transmission in facilities housing vulnerable populations, where staffing shortages and lapses in infection controlâ€”such as inadequate personal protective equipment and failure to isolateâ€”compounded risks, particularly in lower-rated homes.[3] A January 2021 investigation by New York Attorney General Letitia James disclosed that state-reported COVID-19 fatalities among nursing home residentsâ€”initially limited to those occurring within facilities, totaling around 6,645 by November 2020â€”undercounted the true toll by about 50% by excluding hospital deaths, yielding an adjusted figure exceeding 13,000 resident deaths when including off-site fatalities.[3][4] Federal probes, including by the House Select Subcommittee on the Coronavirus Pandemic, have further highlighted internal documents evidencing harm from the directive, accused the Cuomo administration of data manipulation to minimize reported impacts, and referred former Governor Andrew Cuomo for potential criminal prosecution over allegedly false statements to Congress denying the policy's mandate to admit COVID-positive patients.[5][6][7] New York's nursing home deaths accounted for roughly 30% of the state's total COVID-19 fatalitiesâ€”disproportionately high relative to national averagesâ€”prompting ongoing scrutiny of decision-making processes that prioritized hospital decongestation over long-term care safeguards.[3]

Historical Context

Early COVID-19 Spread in New York

The first confirmed case of COVID-19 in New York State occurred on February 29, 2020, in a resident of New York City who had traveled from Europe.[8] This was followed by the state's official announcement of its initial case on March 1, 2020, involving a woman in her 30s who had returned from Iran and tested positive after exhibiting symptoms.[9] Early genomic analysis indicated that initial introductions into the region were primarily from European and U.S. sources rather than direct from Asia, with multiple independent importations seeding community transmission.[10] Community transmission accelerated rapidly in March 2020, particularly in densely populated areas like New York City, where the virus spread through household contacts, workplaces, and social gatherings before widespread testing and mitigation measures took effect.[11] By March 8, the positivity rate on diagnostic tests in New York City reached 27%, surging to a peak of 65% during the week of March 22 amid exponential case growth.[12] Retrospective estimates suggest the infection rate in the state peaked around March 19, with daily infections exceeding 60,000â€”far higher and earlier than initially reported due to limited testing capacity and under-detection of asymptomatic or mild cases.[13] Neighborhoods in Queens and Brooklyn emerged as early epicenters, driven by factors including high population density, international travel hubs, and undetected superspreading events in indoor settings like restaurants and religious gatherings.[14] By late March 2020, New York State reported over 30,000 confirmed cases, with New York City accounting for the majority, positioning the region as a global hotspot comprising roughly 5% of worldwide confirmed infections at that time.[15] The outbreak's severity was exacerbated by delays in recognizing the full extent of silent spread, as serological studies later revealed widespread prior exposure in urban communities by mid-March.[16] This early phase underscored vulnerabilities in high-density urban environments, setting the stage for overwhelming healthcare demands and policy responses targeting at-risk populations.[17]

Hospital Overload and Resource Strain

In March 2020, New York State faced an acute surge in COVID-19 hospitalizations that rapidly strained hospital capacity, particularly in New York City, where the majority of cases concentrated. The state possessed approximately 53,000 total hospital beds and 3,000 ICU beds entering the crisis, but projections indicated a potential demand for up to 140,000 COVID-specific beds amid exponential case growth. By March 23, 2020, confirmed cases exceeded 20,000, with daily hospitalizations climbing sharply and prompting Governor Andrew Cuomo to mandate that all hospitals analyze and prepare to expand capacity to 100% occupancy, with a minimum 50% increase required. This directive reflected fears of systemic overload, as hospitals reported rising admissions of severe cases requiring ventilators and intensive care. ICU resources proved especially vulnerable, with occupancy nearing limits by late March; for instance, on March 28, 2020, New York City hospitals were approaching critical thresholds for ICU beds amid surging cases. Statewide, the Health Department ordered facilities to prioritize surge planning, including cohorting COVID patients and converting non-ICU spaces, while temporary expansions like the Javits Convention Center field hospital and the USNS Comfort hospital ship were mobilized to add thousands of beds. Despite these measures, inpatient mortality risks correlated with ICU strain, as overloaded units in safety-net hospitals experienced disproportionate pressure from un-segregated patient flows. Peak hospitalizations reached approximately 18,000 by mid-April 2020, underscoring the precarity of early resource allocation. This hospital strain directly influenced ancillary policies, including the March 25, 2020, nursing home directive, which instructed facilities not to refuse admissions or readmissions based on confirmed or suspected COVID-19 status, aiming to discharge recovering patients from hospitals and preserve acute care beds for critically ill individuals. Official rationales emphasized averting a collapse in hospital functionality, though subsequent analyses highlighted ethical trade-offs in shifting burdens to long-term care settings with limited isolation capabilities. Empirical data from the period confirm that without such interventions, ventilator shortages and bed unavailability would have exacerbated mortality, as New York accounted for over 40% of U.S. cases by early April despite comprising only 6% of the population.

Policy Formulation and Issuance

March 25, 2020 Directive Details

The New York State Department of Health (NYSDOH) issued an advisory on March 25, 2020, titled "Advisory: Hospital Discharges and Admissions to Nursing Homes," addressed to nursing home administrators, directors of nursing, infection preventionists, and hospital discharge planners.[18] The document's stated purpose was to clarify operational expectations for nursing homes (NHs) in receiving residents discharged from hospitals during the escalating COVID-19 outbreak, thereby facilitating hospital bed availability amid surging cases in New York.[18][3] Under the directive, NHs were required to readmit any resident who had been living in the facility immediately prior to hospitalization once the discharging hospital physician deemed the individual medically stable for return, regardless of COVID-19 status.[18] Similarly, NHs had to accept all residents deemed appropriate for admission by hospitals, explicitly prohibiting refusals of admission or readmission "solely on the basis of a confirmed or suspected diagnosis of COVID-19."[18][3] Hospitals were obligated to confirm medical stability via telephone and furnish comprehensive discharge summaries, including known COVID-19 test results or symptoms, to ensure smooth transfers.[18] The advisory further barred NHs from mandating a negative COVID-19 test as a precondition for admission or readmission, emphasizing that such requirements could not supersede the obligation to accept stable patients.[18] NHs were directed to adhere to existing infection prevention protocols, including standard precautions, droplet and contact isolation for suspected or confirmed cases, and enhanced environmental cleaning, while expediting resident intakes to avoid delays.[18] For personal protective equipment (PPE) shortages, NHs were instructed to submit requests through local offices of emergency management, specifying quantities, types, and delivery needs.[18] Questions were to be directed to a dedicated NYSDOH email, with no explicit timeline for the advisory's duration provided in the document.[18]

Alignment with Federal Guidance

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) issued guidance to nursing homes on March 13, 2020, emphasizing infection control measures amid the COVID-19 outbreak, but did not initially address admissions of COVID-positive patients specifically.[19] Subsequent CMS memos in mid-March reiterated that nursing facilities must adhere to their standard admission criteria and could not refuse residents based solely on a confirmed or suspected COVID-19 diagnosis, provided the facility could meet the resident's needs under infection prevention protocols.[20] This aligned with broader federal anti-discrimination principles under Medicare conditions of participation, which prohibit denying admission due to clinical conditions if care can be safely provided, but CMS explicitly noted that facilities were not required to admit patients if they lacked the capacity for appropriate isolation or cohorting.[21] New York's Department of Health directive on March 25, 2020, required nursing homes to "accept all patients discharged from a hospital" who were medically stable for discharge, explicitly barring refusals based on COVID-19 diagnosis, symptoms, or absence of a negative test result, with the stated aim of alleviating hospital capacity pressures.[18] Governor Andrew Cuomo's administration maintained that this policy comported with CMS guidance by prohibiting COVID-based denials and promoting hospital throughput during the surge.[22] However, the directive diverged by mandating unconditional readmissions without provisions for facilities to assess their own isolation capabilities or conduct pre-admission testing, potentially conflicting with CMS's emphasis on facilities' ability to safely manage infectious cases.[3] Critics, including subsequent legislative inquiries, argued the policy exceeded federal parameters by coercing admissions into congregate settings ill-equipped for widespread COVID-19 isolation, as CMS guidance permitted but did not compel such transfers absent adequate precautions.[23] In 2024, New York City Comptroller Brad Lander petitioned CMS, HHS, and CDC for a formal determination on whether the directive violated federal rules, citing ongoing disputes over its consistency with CMS's non-mandatory stance on COVID-positive admissions.[22] No definitive federal ruling has affirmed full alignment, though CMS later guidance in April 2020 reinforced flexibility for transfers only when testing and isolation were feasible.[24]

Execution and Operational Impacts

Readmission Processes and Facility Burdens

The March 25, 2020, New York State Department of Health (DOH) directive required nursing homes to readmit medically stable residents discharged from hospitals, prohibiting denial of admission or readmission based solely on a confirmed or suspected COVID-19 diagnosis.[18] Facilities were not permitted to mandate pre-admission COVID-19 testing, and hospitals were obligated to provide discharge instructions confirming stability via telephone consultation.[18] Upon arrival, readmitted residents testing positive or suspected of COVID-19 were to be isolated immediately, with facilities maintaining standard infection control precautions, enhanced environmental cleaning, and cohorting where single rooms were unavailable.[18] DOH instructed facilities to report critical shortages of personal protective equipment (PPE) to local emergency management offices for prioritization.[18] Between March 25 and May 8, 2020, approximately 6,326 COVID-19-positive patients were readmitted to 310 nursing homes, with admissions peaking the week of April 14.[3] [25] These processes exacerbated operational burdens, as many facilities already contended with preexisting outbreaks; DOH data indicated that 252 of the 310 facilities had infected residents or staff prior to these readmissions.[25] However, the influx strained isolation capabilities, given that a significant portion of New York nursing homes featured semi-private rooms and limited single-occupancy options, complicating cohorting efforts and increasing cross-infection risks when full isolation proved infeasible.[3] Staffing shortages intensified burdens, with COVID-19 illnesses and quarantines reducing available personnel; facilities with low Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) staffing ratings (1- or 2-star) accounted for over half of nursing home fatalities, as depleted staffs struggled to enforce isolation protocols or provide routine care.[3] Front-line workers reported burnout from heightened workloads, including repeated PPE donning/doffing and resident monitoring amid infections.[26] PPE scarcity was acute statewide, leading some facilities to reuse gowns, employ inadequate masks, or forgo full protocols, thereby elevating staff and resident exposure; DOH and state supplies eventually mitigated this, but initial gaps persisted into April.[3] These constraints correlated with elevated mortality in admitting facilities, where each additional COVID-positive readmission was associated with approximately 0.09 excess deaths, though DOH analyses emphasized that readmission peaks followed mortality peaks and involved patients likely past peak contagiousness after median 9-day hospital stays.[27] [25]

Compliance Enforcement and Variations

The New York State Department of Health (DOH) enforced the March 25, 2020, directive primarily through general oversight mechanisms rather than targeted actions specific to readmissions of COVID-19 patients. The directive required nursing homes to admit or readmit individuals from hospitals regardless of confirmed or suspected COVID-19 status, provided the facility could deliver adequate care, but allowed refusals only for lack of bed availability or inability to meet care needs under federal regulations. DOH conducted over 2,000 compliance assessments via phone calls, video reviews, and in-person visits through April 2020, alongside approximately 1,300 onsite infection control inspections in the ensuing months, resulting in citations for violations of broader protocols such as isolation and PPE usage. Facilities found in serious non-compliance faced potential enforcement like "Immediate Jeopardy" designations, though no documented penalties were applied directly for refusing readmissions under the directive itself; accountability was further complicated by Executive Order 202.10, issued March 23, 2020, which granted immunity from civil liability for good-faith pandemic-related decisions.[28][3] Compliance varied significantly across the state's roughly 650 nursing homes, influenced by factors including pre-existing COVID-19 presence, staffing levels, and regional case loads. Between March 25 and May 8, 2020, 6,326 COVID-19-positive or suspected patients were readmitted to 310 facilities, representing a subset of operations where 98% of affected homes already had internal outbreaks. Some facilities, such as certain New York City operators, admitted patients amid staffing shortages and owner directives to prioritize occupancy, while others in areas like Western New York paused admissions by late March or April due to high staff illness rates (e.g., 20% absenteeism) or resident instability, opting for agency staffing instead. Notably, 132 facilities reported COVID-19 fatalities without any directive-related admissions, and higher-rated CMS 5-star homes exhibited elevated mortality rates (12%) compared to lower-rated ones (7%), indicating no clear link between quality metrics and adherence outcomes. These discrepancies highlighted operational disparities, with for-profit and urban facilities often facing greater pressure to comply amid financial incentives, though the policy's rescission on May 10, 2020, curtailed further variations.[3][28]

Fatality Data and Accounting Practices

Initial Reporting Methodology

The New York State Department of Health (DOH) established its initial COVID-19 nursing home fatality reporting through self-reported data submitted directly by the 619 licensed nursing homes across the state. Facilities were required to report confirmed and presumed deaths occurring on-site via weekly surveys integrated with the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's (CDC) Health Electronic Response Data System (HERDS), mandated under federal guidance and enforced under penalty of perjury.[3][28] This process began in late March 2020, aligning with the onset of widespread outbreaks, and focused exclusively on fatalities verified within the nursing home premises to prioritize immediate, facility-level accountability and resource allocation amid testing shortages.[3] The methodology deliberately omitted deaths of nursing home residents that occurred off-site, particularly in hospitals following patient transfers for escalated care, as systematic linkage to vital records or hospital discharge data was not yet implemented statewide.[3][29] DOH guidance specified that only in-facility events qualified for inclusion in the publicized tallies, reflecting a determination by executive officials that such on-site reports offered higher reliability during the crisis's early chaos, when out-of-facility attribution risked inaccuracies from incomplete inter-agency data sharing.[3][27] Cumulative figures from this approach were first publicly released in early April 2020 and updated periodically on the DOH website, culminating in a reported total of 6,423 nursing home resident deaths (3,640 confirmed and 2,783 presumed) as of August 3, 2020.[3] Supporting mechanisms included a DOH hotline for complaints and supplemental surveys on staff infections and admissions, but core fatality counts remained tethered to the in-facility criterion until subsequent audits prompted methodological revisions.[3][28]

Discovery of Underreporting

The underreporting of COVID-19 deaths among New York nursing home residents was uncovered through an investigation by the Office of the Attorney General (OAG), which subpoenaed records from facilities and compared them against the New York State Department of Health (DOH) public data.[3] The OAG's preliminary review focused on over 20 nursing homes initially, expanding to a sample of 62 facilities representing approximately 10% of the state's total, covering the period from March 1 to August 3, 2020.[30] This analysis revealed that DOH figures systematically excluded deaths of residents who contracted COVID-19 in nursing homes but died in hospitals after transfer, leading to an undercount of approximately 50% statewide.[3] In the sampled facilities, nursing homes reported a total of 1,914 COVID-19-related resident deaths (including both in-facility and hospital occurrences), compared to 1,229 deaths in DOH's publicly available dataâ€”a discrepancy of 55.74%.[3] For context, as of November 16, 2020, DOH had reported 6,645 cumulative nursing home deaths using its in-facility-only methodology, but OAG data indicated the true figure for resident-associated deaths exceeded this when hospital transfers were included.[3] The OAG identified the undercounting by cross-referencing facility-submitted totals from the DOH's Health Emergency Response Data System with independent reports, highlighting inconsistencies such as one facility reporting 31 total deaths to OAG versus only 7 in DOH records by early August 2020.[3] The OAG report, released on January 28, 2021, formalized these findings and attributed the discrepancy to DOH's reporting protocol, which limited counts to fatalities occurring within nursing home premises regardless of infection origin.[30] This methodology had persisted despite earlier external scrutiny; for instance, in September 2020, the Empire Center for Public Policy sued DOH under Freedom of Information Law for withholding detailed resident-level data, suspecting similar omissions, and later corroborated the OAG's undercount estimate upon partial data release.[31][32] The discovery prompted ongoing OAG probes into specific facilities for potential underreporting or infection control failures, though it did not directly attribute the undercount to intentional manipulation.[30]

Government Responses and Reports

Department of Health's July 2020 Analysis

In July 2020, the New York State Department of Health (DOH) published an analysis titled "Factors Associated with Nursing Home Infections and Fatalities in New York State During the COVID-19 Global Health Crisis," examining data from March to June 2020 to identify drivers of COVID-19 spread in long-term care facilities.[33] The report, supported by consulting firm McKinsey & Company, drew on self-reported nursing home surveys, federal Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) data, Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) quality ratings, and state records to assess timelines of infections, facility characteristics, and policy impacts.[33] It concluded that community-acquired infections among staff, rather than hospital readmissions of COVID-positive patients, were the predominant source of transmission to residents.[25] The analysis highlighted temporal patterns in infections and deaths, noting that staff infection rates peaked on March 16, 2020â€”prior to the April 8 peak in resident fatalities by 23 daysâ€”and aligned with broader community transmission in high-burden areas like New York City and Long Island, from which over 80% of infected staff originated.[33] Approximately 37,500 of New York's 158,000 nursing home staff tested positive during the period, with around 7,000 cases in March alone rising to 20,000 by late April, suggesting asymptomatic or presymptomatic staff introduced the virus into facilities.[33] Resident infections followed staff trends, with 98% of facilities reporting cases before the March 25 readmission directive took effect, and over 80% already experiencing outbreaks by mid-March.[33] Regarding hospital readmissions, the report documented 6,326 admissions of confirmed or presumed COVID-positive patients to 310 nursing homes between March 25 and May 8, 2020, peaking on April 14â€”after the resident death peak.[33] It argued these admissions had negligible impact, citing a median hospital stay of nine days (exceeding typical contagion periods per CDC guidelines) and lack of correlation between readmission volume and subsequent facility outbreaks or excess mortality.[33] Facility quality ratings and staffing levels showed no significant association with higher case or death rates, further downplaying policy-driven factors.[33] Health Commissioner Howard A. Zucker emphasized in accompanying remarks that "the data shows that the nursing home residents got COVID from the staff, and presumably, also from those who visited them," attributing early spread to incomplete understanding of asymptomatic transmission in communities.[34] The report recommended improved staff testing, training on infection control, and better coordination with hospitals for future crises but did not propose changes to readmission protocols.[33] As of its release, DOH reported 6,432 nursing home resident fatalities from COVID-19, representing about 20% of the state's total at the time.[33]

Attorney General's 2021 Findings

In January 2021, New York Attorney General Letitia James released a preliminary report based on an investigation into the state's nursing homes' response to the COVID-19 pandemic, drawing from over 4,000 resident and family complaints received since March 2020, surveys of 62 facilities (representing 10% of the state's total), and other data sources.[30] The report concluded that the New York State Department of Health (DOH) had undercounted COVID-19 deaths among nursing home residents by approximately 50%, primarily because public reporting excluded residents who contracted the virus in facilities but died in hospitals after transfer.[30] [3] In the surveyed facilities, DOH data showed 1,229 deaths as of August 3, 2020, compared to 1,914 total deaths (including hospital transfers) reported directly to the Attorney General's Office (OAG), with one example facility reporting only 11 deaths to DOH versus 40 to OAG.[30] This methodology obscured the full scope of facility-associated fatalities, as state figures focused solely on in-facility deaths.[3] The report also documented widespread non-compliance with DOH directives, including the March 25, 2020, guidance on admitting or readmitting COVID-19 patients, which OAG analysis suggested may have heightened resident exposure risks and further complicated accurate death tracking in some cases.[30] Complaints highlighted failures such as admitting untested or symptomatic patients without isolation, inadequate staff screening (e.g., allowing symptomatic workers to enter without testing), and insufficient personal protective equipment (PPE) or training, contributing to outbreaks.[30] For instance, one Long Island facility reportedly dispersed confirmed COVID-19 cases across units despite having dedicated isolation areas available.[30] Staffing shortages exacerbated vulnerabilities, with the report linking higher fatality rates to pre-existing low staffing levels; among for-profit facilities, those rated poorly by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) on staffingâ€”280 out of 401â€”accounted for 3,487 resident deaths by November 16, 2020, over half of the statewide total in such homes.[30] Pandemic conditions worsened this, as seen in a New York City facility where two nurse supervisors oversaw operations amid 33 resident deaths in early April 2020.[30] OAG recommended repealing emergency immunity provisions under the Emergency or Disaster Treatment Protection Act (enacted March 23, 2020) to enable accountability, while noting ongoing probes into over 20 facilities and emphasizing the need for greater transparency in death reporting.[30] The findings were described as preliminary, with discrepancies persisting in overall death statistics and further review required.[3]

Legislative Inquiries

The New York State Assembly conducted an investigation into Governor Andrew Cuomo's administration as part of its impeachment proceedings, with a specific focus on the handling of COVID-19 nursing home death data in the Department of Health's (DOH) July 6, 2020, report titled "Factors Associated with Nursing Home Infections and Fatalities in New York State during the COVID-19 Global Health Crisis."[35] The probe, led by the Assembly Judiciary Committee and released on November 22, 2021, examined internal communications and revisions to the report, revealing that Cuomo's Executive Chamber directed substantial changes to emphasize that infections were primarily introduced by staff rather than the state's March 25, 2020, directive mandating nursing homes to accept recovering COVID-19 patients from hospitals.[36] [37] The investigation found that the final published report disclosed only approximately 6,500 in-facility nursing home deaths, excluding around 10,000 out-of-facility deaths (such as those occurring after hospital transfers), a decision made to align with prior public reporting practices and avoid complicating the narrative amid political scrutiny from the federal government and media.[35] [38] Internal deliberations highlighted concerns over data reliability for out-of-facility cases, but witnesses indicated messaging priorities influenced the exclusions, delaying full disclosure to legislators until February 10, 2021, despite earlier requests.[35] The report lacked an independent medical review to validate its conclusion attributing most transmissions to staff infections, though the Assembly probe noted no direct evidence linking the readmission directive to increased fatalities overall.[35] In the State Senate, Republican members, including Senator James Tedisco, repeatedly called for comprehensive investigations and unredacted reports on nursing home deaths, criticizing the lack of accountability under subsequent Governor Kathy Hochul as of early 2024, but no formal Senate-led inquiry or hearing produced a comparable investigative report.[39] Senator Eileen Serino sought an oversight hearing into the state's response to nursing home complaints during the pandemic, highlighting enforcement failures, though outcomes remained limited to advocacy for policy reforms rather than binding findings.[40] These legislative efforts underscored partisan divides, with Democratic majorities in the Assembly prioritizing the impeachment on sexual harassment allegations while incorporating nursing home data issues, but ultimately not leading to convictions in the Senate or further state-level prosecutions tied to the policy.[35]

Rationales and Defenses

Stated Policy Objectives

The New York State Department of Health advisory issued on March 25, 2020, directed nursing homes to accept all residents medically stable for discharge from hospitals, regardless of confirmed or suspected COVID-19 status, with the explicit objective of expanding hospital capacity during the escalating pandemic emergency.[18] This policy enforced compliance with existing state regulations under 10 NYCRR 415.3(h), which mandate readmission of residents post-hospitalization, while prohibiting refusals based solely on COVID-19 diagnosis to prevent backups in acute care facilities amid rapid case surges in New York City and statewide.[18] [7] Hospital discharge planners were required to verify medical stability via telephone consultation with nursing home staff and provide detailed instructions, further aiming to expedite transfers and alleviate pressure on overwhelmed hospitals handling the influx of severe COVID-19 cases.[18] Nursing homes were barred from mandating pre-admission COVID-19 testing for such stable residents, underscoring the priority of immediate readmission to sustain hospital throughput for higher-acuity patients.[18] The directive positioned nursing homes as essential in managing the broader healthcare system's strain, with guidance on enhanced infection controls like standard precautions and PPE reporting to support operational continuity.[18]

Comparative Mortality Metrics

New York's nursing home resident COVID-19 death rate, adjusted for underreporting of hospital fatalities, reached 12.4 percent of residents, ranking sixth highest among U.S. states.[4] This adjustment stemmed from findings that the state initially excluded deaths occurring outside facilities, undercounting the toll by about 50 percent according to the New York Attorney General's office, which reviewed records from 62 facilities representing 10 percent of the state's total.[30] By contrast, the national nursing home resident death rate stood at roughly 9.2 percent through 2020, equivalent to 9,200 deaths per 100,000 residents.[41] Early data through September 2020 highlighted New York's elevated position relative to most states, with rates in Northeast peers like Massachusetts exceeding 12.5 percent per 1,000 residents, while many Southern and Western states reported lower figures.[42] For instance, states avoiding mandates for admitting COVID-positive patients, such as Florida and Texas, exhibited nursing home mortality below national averages, with Florida's total nursing home deaths representing a smaller per capita share amid its larger elderly population.[43] Nationally, nursing homes accounted for about 21 percent of all U.S. COVID-19 deaths despite comprising less than 1 percent of the population, underscoring the sector's vulnerability, though New York's metrics placed it among the 14 states where over 10 percent of nursing home residents succumbed.[41] Comparisons using federal CMS data further reveal disparities: New York's reported 2020 nursing home deaths totaled over 11,400 when including state tallies beyond federal counts, far surpassing per-facility averages in states with restrictive admission policies.[44] These metrics, derived from state health departments and cross-verified with federal reporting, indicate New York's rates were not only above the national median but also sustained high even after policy shifts in May 2020, when admissions of confirmed cases were halted.[45]

Critiques and Causal Analyses

Evidence Linking Policy to Excess Deaths

Analyses of state data have identified a statistical correlation between the admission of COVID-19-positive patients into New York nursing homes under the March 25, 2020, Department of Health directive and subsequent increases in facility-level mortality. An examination by the Empire Center for Public Policy, utilizing official Department of Health data on patient admissions and resident deaths across 633 nursing homes, found that each additional COVID-positive admission was associated with 0.09 excess deaths, with statistical significance (margin of error Â±0.05).[27] This correlation held statewide, though critics, including a review by the Step Two Policy Project, contested its strength in downstate facilities where community transmission was rampant, arguing it overstated policy causation relative to baseline infection rates.[46] Nonetheless, the facility-specific regression accounted for variables like bed capacity and regional case prevalence, suggesting admissions contributed incrementally to outbreaks in vulnerable settings. Internal Department of Health documents from June 2020, later obtained via congressional subpoena, indicated that the policy exacerbated infections despite not being the primary vector. A preliminary agency analysis traced approximately 20-30% of nursing home COVID-19 cases to hospital transfers in select facilities, with admissions coinciding with rapid transmission among residents lacking adequate isolation capacity.[7] These findings contradicted contemporaneous state defenses claiming most readmitted patients were non-contagious, as PCR testing protocols at the time often discharged individuals still shedding virus.[28] Comparisons of nursing home mortality rates further highlight potential policy-driven excess. New York reported over 15,000 nursing home-associated COVID-19 deaths by mid-2021 when including hospital fatalitiesâ€”a figure comprising roughly 25% of the state's total confirmed deathsâ€”exceeding the national average of 21% derived from Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services data.[41] [4] Facilities admitting positive patients post-directive experienced death rates up to 10-15% higher than non-admitting peers in similar demographics, per aggregated state records, aligning temporally with a surge in long-term care fatalities from late March to May 2020.[47] While confounding factors like urban density and staffing shortages contributed, the directive's mandate for acceptance without regard to infection status provided a mechanistic pathway for amplified spread, as evidenced by outbreak tracing in affected homes.[48] Peer-reviewed causal assessments remain limited, with broader studies emphasizing facility quality and pre-existing vulnerabilities over specific policies; however, cross-state analyses note New York's outlier status in readmission-driven mortality, supporting attributions of excess to the directive's implementation.[49] The New York Attorney General's 2021 report, while focused on undercounting (revealing a 50% discrepancy in reported versus actual deaths), underscored data opacity that obscured precise policy impacts but affirmed elevated risks from cohorting infected individuals with uninfected residents.[3] These elements collectively indicate the policy likely precipitated avoidable deaths, though quantifying the exact excess requires disentangling from concurrent epidemic dynamics.

Allegations of Data Manipulation

In January 2021, New York Attorney General Letitia James released a report alleging that the state Department of Health (DOH) undercounted COVID-19 deaths among nursing home residents by approximately 50%, based on a survey of 62 facilities that revealed consistent discrepancies between resident-reported fatalities and official DOH figures.[30] The report estimated at least 6,645 nursing home resident deaths from March to November 2020, with the undercount primarily stemming from exclusion of deaths occurring after residents were transferred to hospitals, a methodology critics argued obscured the full impact of the state's March 25, 2020, directive requiring nursing homes to admit or retain COVID-positive patients.[3] James attributed the discrepancies to DOH's reliance on incomplete facility submissions and failure to cross-reference hospital records, though the administration maintained the figures aligned with federal Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) guidelines limiting counts to in-facility deaths.[50] Further allegations emerged in March 2021 when the DOH confirmed that Governor Andrew Cuomo's senior aides, including members of his COVID-19 task force, intervened to revise a draft report on nursing home mortality released in August 2020.[51] The original draft had cited around 11,000 total deaths (including hospital transfers), but aides directed changes to emphasize only the 6,000 in-facility deaths, omitting the higher figure and altering language to downplay the policy's role, reportedly to shield the administration from federal scrutiny amid preparations for a book deal and Emmy awards for Cuomo's pandemic briefings.[52] Critics, including state legislators and the Empire Center for Public Policy, claimed this constituted deliberate data manipulation to sustain a narrative of effective crisis management, as evidenced by a Freedom of Information Law lawsuit that uncovered internal emails showing awareness of the fuller toll.[53] Subsequent probes amplified these claims; a 2022 state comptroller audit found DOH "misled the public" by understating deaths by at least 4,100 through similar exclusionary practices, while federal investigators in 2024 referred Cuomo to the Department of Justice for potential false statements related to the altered reporting.[54][55] The administration countered that revisions ensured methodological consistency with CDC standards and avoided inflating statistics beyond verifiable in-facility events, but whistleblower accounts from health officials described pressure to conform data to political optics rather than comprehensive accuracy.[56] Independent analyses, such as one by the Foundation for Research on Equal Opportunity, estimated the undercount at up to 68% when including all attributable deaths, reinforcing allegations that selective reporting concealed policy-driven vulnerabilities in long-term care facilities.[4]

Probes and Accountability Measures

State Investigations

In March 2022, the New York State Comptroller's Office issued an audit examining the Department of Health's (DOH) reporting of COVID-19 deaths among nursing home residents during the early pandemic period. The audit analyzed data from March 1 to August 8, 2020, and found that DOH's public reports excluded deaths of residents who contracted COVID-19 in nursing homes but died after hospital transfer, undercounting the total by approximately 4,113 deaths. DOH reported 6,361 in-facility COVID-19 deaths for nursing home residents through May 2020, but the Comptroller determined the actual figure, including post-transfer deaths, exceeded 10,000, representing about 20% of all New York COVID-19 fatalities at the time. This exclusionary methodology, which DOH justified as aligning with federal Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) guidelines for facility-attributed deaths, was deemed misleading to the public regarding the policy's impact.[57] The same audit criticized DOH for inadequate oversight of nursing homes' compliance with infection control measures and for delays in implementing federal reporting requirements under the Healthcare Early Warning System (HERDS). It highlighted that DOH's data collection relied heavily on self-reported facility surveys, which contained inconsistencies, such as discrepancies between death counts and hospital transfer logs. Comptroller Thomas DiNapoli stated that the underreporting obscured the true extent of nursing home vulnerabilities exposed by the March 25, 2020, directive requiring facilities to accept COVID-positive admissions without testing. DOH contested the audit's characterization, asserting its reports accurately reflected in-facility events and that total resident deaths were not concealed, as hospital data was separately tracked.[57] A follow-up Comptroller audit released in December 2023 assessed DOH's progress in addressing pandemic-related deficiencies in nursing home preparedness. It noted some data quality improvements, including better integration of HERDS surveys, but identified persistent gaps in infection prevention, staffing protocols, and emergency planning. The audit reviewed 20 nursing homes and found that while COVID-19 vaccination rates had risen to over 90% by 2023, many facilities still lacked sufficient personal protective equipment stockpiles and coordinated evacuation plans for future outbreaks. DiNapoli recommended enhanced state monitoring and independent verification of facility self-reports to prevent recurrence. This investigation underscored ongoing systemic issues traceable to the initial policy's strain on under-resourced long-term care settings.[58] State legislative bodies also initiated probes, with the New York State Senate's Joint Committee on Nursing Home Oversight holding hearings in 2021 and 2022 to scrutinize the policy's implementation. Assemblyman James Tedisco, leading Republican efforts, subpoenaed DOH records in 2023, revealing delays in releasing a $4.3 million independent review commissioned by Governor Kathy Hochul, which ultimately affirmed high death rates but attributed them partly to broader demographic factors rather than solely the admissions directive. These inquiries, while non-binding, amplified calls for accountability, though they yielded no formal charges against state officials.[39][59]

Federal Scrutiny and Referrals

In August 2020, the U.S. Department of Justice requested data from governors of states, including New York, that had issued COVID-19 orders potentially contributing to nursing home deaths, focusing on whether such policies led to increased fatalities among elderly residents.[60] This inquiry examined New York's March 25, 2020, directive requiring nursing homes to admit or readmit patients regardless of confirmed or suspected COVID-19 status, amid reports of over 6,000 nursing home deaths in the state by that time.[60] [61] The House Select Subcommittee on the Coronavirus Pandemic conducted extensive scrutiny, including a September 10, 2024, hearing where former Governor Andrew Cuomo testified on the policy's implementation and its reported outcomes.[5] The subcommittee's investigation revealed that Cuomo's administration issued guidance forcing nursing homes to accept COVID-positive patients without adequate isolation measures, contributing to excess deaths, and subsequently undercounted fatalities by excluding those occurring in hospitals after transfer.[5] [6] On October 31, 2024, the subcommittee referred Cuomo to the Department of Justice for potential criminal prosecution, citing evidence that he knowingly and willfully made false statements to Congress regarding the nursing home policy's impact and his administration's data reporting practices.[6] This referral followed findings that Cuomo's testimony contradicted internal documents showing awareness of the policy's risks and efforts to minimize public disclosure of death tolls, which exceeded 15,000 in nursing homes and affiliated facilities.[6] [55] Subsequently, in May 2025, the DOJ initiated a criminal investigation into Cuomo for allegedly lying to Congress about the nursing home crisis, prompted by the subcommittee's evidence of discrepancies between his statements and administration records.[61] [62] The House Committee on Oversight and Accountability reinforced this in April 2025 by referring Cuomo to the incoming Trump administration's DOJ, highlighting failures to address nursing home fatalities and blaming staff rather than policy in internal reports.[63] Earlier federal efforts included a 2021 DOJ Civil Rights Division probe into whether New York's policy violated the rights of disabled residents in government-run nursing homes, but this was closed without findings of systemic discrimination.[64] A 2025 DOJ inspector general report later scrutinized leaks in Trump-era probes of New York and Pennsylvania nursing home deaths, noting potential political influences but affirming the underlying concerns over policy-driven fatalities.[65] As of October 2025, the DOJ's investigation into Cuomo's congressional testimony remains ongoing, with no charges filed.[61]

Civil Litigation Outcomes

In 2022, families of nursing home residents who died from COVID-19 filed a federal wrongful death lawsuit against former Governor Andrew Cuomo and state officials, alleging that the March 25, 2020, directive requiring nursing homes to admit recovering COVID-19 patients from hospitals violated residents' constitutional rights under the Due Process Clause by creating a foreseeable danger.[66] On September 30, 2024, U.S. District Judge Katherine Polk Failla dismissed the case, ruling that the plaintiffs failed to establish a viable substantive due process claim, as the state policy did not shock the conscience or create the specific danger leading to the deaths, and Cuomo enjoyed qualified immunity.[66] [67] A similar federal suit filed by plaintiff Daniel Arbeeny, seeking to hold Cuomo personally liable for nursing home deaths under Section 1983 for deliberate indifference, was also dismissed by Judge Failla in a 44-page order on March 31, 2025, with the court finding no evidence that the directive abrogated the state's duty to protect or that it affirmatively placed residents in peril beyond standard negligence.[68] [67] The dismissal highlighted that while the policy was controversial, it did not meet the high threshold for federal civil rights violations, leaving plaintiffs without recourse under federal law despite claims of over 15,000 nursing home deaths linked to the admissions.[68][67] State-level civil suits against the New York Department of Health and nursing facilities have invoked the policy as a contributing factor but have largely targeted facilities for negligence rather than the directive itself, with outcomes influenced by the 2020 Emergency or Disaster Treatment Protection Act (EDTPA), which granted healthcare providers immunity from civil liability for COVID-related care decisions absent gross negligence.[69] In April 2021, Governor Cuomo signed legislation repealing the broad immunity protections for nursing homes, opening the door to wrongful death claims against facilities, though many such suits have been dismissed or settled on narrower grounds of facility-specific failures rather than state policy liability.[70] For instance, the New York Attorney General secured a $12 million settlement in August 2025 against Syracuse's Van Duyn nursing home for pre- and post-pandemic neglect exacerbating COVID vulnerabilities, including reforms but no direct policy attribution.[71] No class-action settlements or compensation fund have been established specifically holding the state accountable for the nursing home admissions policy, despite legislative proposals for a $4 billion victims' fund offering minimum $250,000 per death; such efforts remain unpassed, with litigation outcomes underscoring judicial reluctance to impose policy-driven liability absent gross misconduct.[72] Ongoing appeals in nursing home negligence cases continue, but federal and state courts have not recognized a broad civil remedy tying excess deaths directly to the directive's implementation.[73]

Long-Term Consequences

Policy Adjustments Post-Criticism

In response to early criticism and reports of elevated nursing home mortality rates in April 2020, Governor Andrew Cuomo announced on May 10, 2020, that hospitals would no longer discharge COVID-19 patients to nursing homes without a negative test result, effectively rescinding the March 25 directive's requirement to admit recovering patients regardless of infection status.[74] This change followed the transfer of approximately 9,000 COVID-positive or symptomatic patients to nursing homes under the original policy, amid data showing nursing facilities accounting for over 30% of New York's total COVID-19 deaths by that point.[75] Following the New York Attorney General's January 28, 2021, report documenting undercounted nursing home deaths and widespread non-compliance with isolation protocols, the state legislature enacted reforms addressing liability and oversight. On March 26, 2021, lawmakers repealed emergency executive orders granting civil immunity to nursing homes, hospitals, and physicians for COVID-19-related malpractice claims, exposing facilities to lawsuits for negligence during the pandemic.[76] The Attorney General's findings, based on surveys of over 1,000 facilities, highlighted failures in staff screening and PPE provision, prompting these measures despite Cuomo administration defenses that the policy aligned with federal guidance and did not drive excess mortality.[30] Under Governor Kathy Hochul, who assumed office in August 2021, additional adjustments included lifting a pandemic-era pause on pre-existing nursing home reforms in April 2022, reinstating requirements for minimum staffing levels (e.g., 3.5 hours of care per resident daily) and financial transparency, such as mandating 70% of revenues for direct care and 40% for staffing. In December 2022, Hochul signed legislation requiring nursing homes to develop and submit infection prevention plans, including regular staff testing and outbreak notifications to the Department of Health, aimed at mitigating future vulnerabilities exposed by the scandal.[77] These steps responded to ongoing scrutiny, including federal probes, though implementation faced criticism for insufficient enforcement amid persistent staffing shortages.[78]

Reforms and Compensation Efforts

In May 2020, Governor Andrew Cuomo reversed the March 25 directive via Executive Order 202.30, lifting the requirement for nursing homes to admit or readmit patients solely based on COVID-19 status and allowing facilities greater discretion in refusals, particularly amid ongoing surges.[79] The order also mandated COVID-19 testing plans for nursing home employees to mitigate transmission risks.[79] On April 7, 2021, Cuomo signed legislation repealing broad emergency immunity protections for nursing homes and hospitals against COVID-19-related malpractice claims, which had been enacted in the state budget earlier that year.[70] This change enabled families to pursue wrongful death and negligence lawsuits, shifting from near-total shields to accountability for gross negligence or willful misconduct during the pandemic.[70] Compensation efforts have primarily occurred through civil litigation rather than a centralized state fund. Numerous lawsuits against nursing home operators have resulted in multimillion-dollar settlements for alleged neglect contributing to COVID-19 deaths, including a $45 million agreement in November 2024 involving four facilities and their owners for restitution and penalties tied to inadequate care.[80] Similarly, the state Attorney General secured a $12 million settlement in August 2025 with a Syracuse nursing home, incorporating reforms such as enhanced staffing and infection control protocols alongside Medicaid restitution.[71] Legislative proposals for broader state-funded compensation, such as the Justice for Nursing Home Victims Actâ€”reintroduced as Assembly Bill A896 in 2025 to create a program offering at least $250,000 per eligible deceased residentâ€”have not advanced to enactment, remaining in committee without passage as of October 2025.[81] Ongoing federal appeals, including a Second Circuit case in October 2025 challenging qualified immunity for state officials in nursing home deaths, continue to test liability but have yet to yield direct payouts from the state.[82] No comprehensive taxpayer-funded compensation mechanism for victims of the policy has been established, with recourse limited to facility-specific suits and regulatory enforcement.[72]
