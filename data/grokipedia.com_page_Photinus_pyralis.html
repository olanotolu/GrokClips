Photinus pyralis

Common eastern firefly Photinus pyralis, also known by the common names the common eastern firefly or big dipper firefly, and sometimes called a "lightning bug", is a species of flying beetle. An organ on its abdomen is responsible for its light production. It is the most common species of firefly in North America, and is typically found east of the Rocky Mountains. Photinus fireflies are often confused with fireflies of the similar-sounding genus, Photuris, which are also found in North America. These fireflies are most noticeable around twilight in the spring and summer months. The common name, big dipper firefly, is due to the characteristic flight of the males, whose trajectory appears to follow a J-shape, lighting on the upswing.[3][4] During flight, this J-pattern is used alongside light flashing to attract females, who rest on vegetation and signal back to males if interested.[5]

Common Eastern firefly

Description

Common Eastern fireflies are small soft-bodied beetles that are 9â€“19 mm (0.4â€“0.7 in) long. They have flat black or brown bodies with wing coverings, or elytra, that extend the length of their entire thorax and abdomen. The elytra have a yellow border. The head shield, or pronotum, is pale yellow, with a black dot in the center, surrounded by pink or red.[6] There is sexual dimorphism in size and wing development: males are larger than females, who have reduced wings and are typically flightless.[7] Both males and females of this species have light-emitting organs on the ventral side of their abdomens. While this organ extends the length of the last two segments of the males' abdomens, it appears only in the second to last segment of the females' abdomens.[8] Like most flying firefly species, Photinus pyralis is nocturnal.[9]

Geographic range

P. pyralis ranges from southern Canada (such as Ontario) through the eastern United States to northern Mexico, primarily east of the Rocky Mountains. [7] [10]

Habitat

Common Eastern fireflies are found in a variety of habitats, ranging from temperate to tropical environments.[11] Some natural habitats of these fireflies include meadows, fields, wetlands, and forests,[6] and while they can successfully thrive in all of these areas, they require moisture to survive at all life stages. They are especially drawn to areas with damp soil and long grasses to better conceal females and offspring.[6] The species is a habitat generalist, often found in open areas, woodland edges, near streams, and even urban or disturbed sites.[11]

Home range and territoriality

Adult Photinus pyralis are not strong fliers, resulting in limited dispersal distances and small home ranges, typically on the scale of tens to hundreds of meters. Genetic analyses show little gene flow between populations, consistent with restricted individual mobility. P. pyralis exhibits no territorial behavior; males actively patrol low vegetation and open areas in search of females without defending specific territories.[12][13]

Life cycle

Like all beetles, the life cycle of the common eastern firefly consists of four stages: egg, larval, pupal, and adult. Females lay eggs in moist soil or leaf litter, typically in batches of about 50-100. The eggs are small (about 1 mm), spherical, and emit a faint glow. They hatch after 3 to 4 weeks into larvae.[6] The larval stage is the longest, lasting 1 to 2 years, during which the firefly spends most of its time feeding. Larvae are predatory, living in soil or leaf litter and consuming soft-bodied invertebrates such as worms, slugs, and snails. They undergo multiple instars, molting several times as they grow.[6] Larvae are vulnerable to predators and produce light as an aposematic signal to deter them, even though adults are also bioluminescent.[6][14][15] At the end of the larval stage, the firefly forms a pupal chamber in the soil and becomes a pupa. The pupal stage lasts 9 to 15 days, after which it ecloses as an adult.[6] Adults live for 5 to 30 days, during which they focus on mating and use their light to attract partners.[7]

Risks

Although classified as "Least Concern" by the IUCN Red List,[16] Photinus pyralis may face potential threats similar to those affecting fireflies generally. These include light pollution, pesticide use, climate change, and habitat loss due to human development and urbanization.[17] However, as a widespread and adaptable species, it appears tolerant of some habitat disturbances and light pollution, and specific threats to its populations are not well documented.[18]

Defense

Photinus pyralis contain steroid compounds called lucibufagins, which make them taste bad to potential predators, such as birds, bats, and other insects. However, some species of Photuris fireflies lack lucibufagins, and they prey on P. pyralis males in order to acquire the steroids for themselves.[19] The lucibufagins serve as a defense mechanism against predators. Beetles from the family Lampyridae have been known to use certain defenses such as unpleasant odour and the excretion of a sticky substance to avoid predation. Excretion of unpleasant fluids containing lucibufagins from the areas along the elytra and pronotum is the result of tactile stimulation and has been referred to as reflexive bleeding.[21][22] This reflex bleeding is a defensive function of P. pyralis, as it can cause certain predators, such as ants, to become entangled in the sticky substance, or it can cause revulsion in others upon predation.[21] Whereas adult flashing is used in mate signaling, larvae glow is thought to be an aposematic display for nocturnal predators.[15]

Mating

Mate searching behavior

Males are the first to start the series of patrolling flashes needed to locate and mate with a female. Males will actively fly while flashing, whereas females are sedentary. Male P. pyralis will flash every 5 to 7 seconds and wait for a responding flash from the female, which comes after a 1 to 2 second delay.[24] The length of time between male flashes is temperature dependent, with shorter intervals as temperature increases.[6] It has been shown that females only respond to their conspecific males, identifying them by the temporal patterning, duration and intensity of the male flash, with detection being achromatic.[25] Recent studies suggest that olfactory cues, mediated by sex-biased odorant receptors, may also contribute to mate recognition and attraction.[26] Females will twist their abdomen towards the male's flash, presenting their own flash toward the male. Males can be observed flying in a nearly vertical orientation, their antennae held forward and stiff while their legs are held toward the body during patrolling.[24] They also show an obvious gaze shift towards the last female flash and continue towards it until the female firefly flashes again.[24] The flashes continue until the male reaches the female. Males congregate in large masses and it is likely that more than one will find the same female; in this case male P. pyralis display aggression towards one another while not in flight.[27]

Male/male interactions and sexual selection

During the "aggression" stage, males with smaller elytra and smaller lanterns are favored, whereas during the signaling phase, males with longer elytra and bigger lanterns are favored.[27] Males with larger lanterns are favored in signaling phases of courtship because their broadcasting flashes can be seen by females who are further away. It is also suggested that due to their longer elytra these males may also have an advantage of finding the females faster.[28]

Female/male interactions and sexual selection

Among fireflies of the genus Photinus, males will first attract a female's attention with their light flashing patterns, but once they reach the females they will be selected based on the size and quality of the "nuptial gifts" they can offer. These nuptial gifts, also known as spermatophores, are a combination of sperm and high levels of protein, defensive compounds, and other nutrients which the females will subsequently use to feed the eggs should she choose to mate with the male.[29] During the adult stage of their lives, most Photinus fireflies do not eat, so all activity is fueled by energy that is consumed and subsequently stored during the larval stage; this becomes especially important regarding reproductive activity. Consequently, these nuptial gifts are a big factor in determining which male fireflies females choose to mate with. Although it ends up being a high cost to males, those with larger spermatophores tend to see increased reproductive success because they are able to provide females with more nutrients to sustain future offspring.[30] It was found that females were more likely to mate with virgin males who had never mated because their nuptial gift sizes were larger in comparison to males who had mated the previous night.[31]

Polyandry

The female common Eastern firefly is polyandrous, meaning it will mate with multiple males over multiple nights, although it will only mate with a single male in one night. This practice has been shown to increase female fecundity as well as overall lifespan.[30]

Light production

The light organ of P. pyralis is composed of two layers: a layer of refractile cells on the dorsal side and a photic layer with light-producing cells on the ventral side. The light organ (specifically the photogenic layer) is supplied with numerous tracheal branches, which are thought to provide the required oxygen for light production. The light-producing enzyme is luciferase, and is found within cells of the lantern.[33] Luciferase requires oxygen, luciferin and adenosine triphosphate (ATP) to catalyze a chemical reaction that produces bioluminescence in these insects.[34] It has been shown that the glow is not controlled by the tracheal end cells (which were thought to contain valves) nor by central nerve impulses through studies involving low oxygen conditions. Pupae of these beetles have different light organs than the adult. They do not have the characteristic tracheal end cells of the flashing adults, and whereas the adults emit bright flashes, pupae emit low intensity glowing.

Biotechnology research

Much research has been done to both determine the mechanism by which fireflies can emit light as well as how that tool can be used advantageously in biotechnological contexts. Luciferase has become a tool for many different research strategies. In 2018 the Photinus pyralis genome sequence was published.[36] The first use of luciferase was as a reporting marker in many high throughput assays. Luciferase assays are commonly used as reporter systems for monitoring gene expression, protein function, and cellular ATP levels. Because it is known that luciferase is activated by oxygen, luciferin, and ATP, it is a highly sensitive marker and is very easy and efficient to use, so it is very widely used among scientists. Studies from the 1980s demonstrated that firefly luciferase localizes to peroxisomes in the light organ cells of Photinus pyralis and, when expressed in mammalian cells, is targeted to their peroxisomes, organelles[37] responsible for carrying out oxidative reactions and producing hydrogen peroxide as a byproduct that is quickly removed. This information has advanced the field of molecular and cell biology.[38] Since 2020, significant advances in firefly luciferase biotechnology include the development of synthetic near-infrared luciferins for enhanced in vivo imaging depth and engineered variants enabling multi-color bioluminescence for simultaneous detection of multiple targets.[39][40][41]
