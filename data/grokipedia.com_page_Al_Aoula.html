Al Aoula

Al Aoula (Arabic: الأولى, lit. 'The First') is the flagship Arabic-language free-to-air television channel of the Moroccan public broadcaster Société Nationale de Radiodiffusion et de Télévision (SNRT).[1] Launched on 3 March 1962 as Télévision Marocaine (TVM), it became the first television network in Morocco to produce and transmit its own programs domestically.[2][1] As the primary channel of SNRT, Al Aoula broadcasts a mix of news, cultural programs, dramas, sports, and educational content, serving as a key national medium for information and entertainment in Morocco.[3] It introduced color television broadcasts in the country in 1972 and has maintained its status as the longest-running Moroccan TV station, retaining significant viewership despite the rise of private and satellite channels.[4] The channel operates under state oversight, reflecting government priorities in public broadcasting, with daily programming including the main evening news journal and regional content.[5][1]

History

Launch and early development (1962–1970s)

Al Aoula, originally established as Radiodiffusion Télévision Marocaine (RTM), launched on March 3, 1962, coinciding with the first anniversary of King Hassan II's accession to the throne, under direct state control to foster national cohesion following Morocco's independence in 1956.[1][6] The inaugural broadcast featured the king's speech, marking the inception of Morocco's first regular television service, which succeeded a brief experimental phase under the prior French protectorate known as TELMA from 1954 to 1955.[7][6] Funded entirely by the government, RTM operated from studios in Rabat with black-and-white transmissions aimed at promoting Arabic-language cultural and educational content to bridge linguistic and regional divides among Berber, Arab, and urban-rural populations.[8][9] Early infrastructure was rudimentary, relying on terrestrial analog signals receivable primarily in major urban centers like Rabat, Casablanca, and Fez, with an estimated initial coverage of less than 20% of the population due to limited transmitter towers and the high cost of television sets, which numbered fewer than 10,000 in the first year.[7] Programming emphasized nation-building objectives, including literacy campaigns, folklore preservation, and royal ceremonies, broadcast for about four hours daily in the evenings to align with household electricity access patterns.[10] Government oversight ensured content aligned with monarchical priorities, such as reinforcing Islamic values and Arab unity, while avoiding political dissent amid the post-independence consolidation of power.[8] By the early 1970s, RTM expanded its technical capabilities with the introduction of color television transmissions in 1972 using the SECAM IIIb system, initially tested during events like the Munich Olympics and gradually rolled out for national broadcasts to enhance visual appeal for educational documentaries and cultural specials.[7][11] This upgrade, supported by state investments in additional relay stations, extended signals to secondary cities and improved reception quality, though color set penetration remained below 3% of licensed receivers due to import restrictions and economic constraints.[7] The period solidified RTM's monopoly as the sole broadcaster, serving as a tool for centralized information dissemination during domestic challenges like the 1970s drought and border tensions.[9]

Monopoly era and expansion (1980s–1990s)

During the 1980s, Radiodiffusion Télévision Marocaine (RTM), operating as Télévision Marocaine (TVM), maintained its position as Morocco's sole national television broadcaster, exerting unchallenged dominance over electronic media content and audience access.[2] This monopoly facilitated the state's centralized control over information dissemination, with programming heavily oriented toward official narratives, educational material in Arabic and French, and coverage of royal events under King Hassan II's reign.[12] State investments in broadcasting infrastructure during this period aimed to extend signal reach beyond urban centers, though coverage remained uneven in remote rural regions until further expansions in the early 1990s.[13] The launch of 2M on March 4, 1989, marked the end of RTM's absolute monopoly, introducing a second channel initially available via cable subscription and broadcasting unscrambled for limited hours daily.[14] Despite this development, RTM retained primacy in delivering national news bulletins and state messaging, serving as the primary vehicle for government policy announcements and national unity campaigns.[15] Programming evolved modestly to incorporate more imported entertainment series, such as American soaps like Santa Barbara from the late 1980s, alongside domestic productions emphasizing cultural and moral themes, though propaganda elements persisted in depictions of royal ceremonies and bilingual (Arabic-French) content to reinforce monarchical legitimacy.[16] In the pre-satellite era, prior to widespread adoption of dish antennas in the early 1990s, RTM's monopoly created high audience dependency, empirically shaping public discourse by limiting exposure to alternative viewpoints and fostering reliance on state-filtered narratives for current events and national identity formation.[17] This causal influence was evident in the channel's role as the de facto arbiter of information, with viewership metrics from the period indicating near-universal access in equipped households and minimal pluralism until 2M's gradual terrestrial expansion.[18]

Rebranding and liberalization (2000s–present)

In 2007, under the Société Nationale de Radiodiffusion et de Télévision (SNRT), the channel formerly known as Télévision Marocaine (TVM) underwent a significant rebranding to Al Aoula, introducing a new visual identity and programming approach aimed at enhancing national appeal and aligning with Arabization policies.[19] This shift emphasized Arabic-language content and cultural relevance to Moroccan audiences, marking a departure from earlier French-influenced naming and aesthetics while reinforcing the channel's role as the flagship public broadcaster.[1] Amid Morocco's media liberalization in the early 2000s, which saw the partial privatization of competitors like 2M and the emergence of private satellite channels, Al Aoula adapted by expanding into satellite broadcasting via platforms such as Nilesat and Hotbird, enabling wider reach including to diaspora communities.[20] The channel began producing programs in high-definition (HD) format in 2007, with SNRT initiating full HD simulcasting by 2015 as part of preparations for the national digital terrestrial television switchover, targeted for completion around that year to improve signal quality and accommodate multiple channels. These technological upgrades helped Al Aoula maintain its public service mandate—focusing on national unity and information dissemination—despite competitive pressures from imported content like Turkish dramas on private outlets.[21] In recent years, Al Aoula has demonstrated resilience against streaming services and fragmented viewership, achieving a 37.7% audience share during Ramadan 2025 prime time, drawing 13 million viewers and contributing to public channels' overall 68.6% dominance in that period.[22] This performance, up significantly from prior years, underscores the channel's enduring appeal during high-engagement cultural events, even as digital platforms capture growing off-peak consumption.[23]

Ownership and Operations

Parent organization and governance

Al Aoula is operated by the Société Nationale de Radiodiffusion et de Télévision (SNRT), a state-owned public corporation that serves as Morocco's primary broadcaster, encompassing multiple television and radio channels.[24] SNRT is wholly owned by the Moroccan government and operates under the supervisory authority of the Ministry of Youth, Culture and Communication, which enforces alignment with state objectives in media dissemination.[24] This structure positions SNRT as an instrument of public policy rather than commercial enterprise, with operational decisions subordinated to governmental directives. Governance at SNRT is centralized under a Director General appointed by royal decree, a process that embeds monarchical oversight to ensure content and strategy conform to national interests.[24][25] An Administrative Council of 12 members provides strategic direction, comprising ten appointees from various government ministries and two from the Ministry of Youth, Culture and Communication, thereby integrating political representation and limiting autonomy from state influence.[24] This framework contrasts sharply with private media entities, where boards prioritize shareholder returns and market competition over mandated public service duties such as promoting national unity and cultural heritage. SNRT achieves nationwide coverage through a hierarchical operational setup, including central facilities in Rabat and regional production centers that support localized content adaptation and transmission infrastructure.[26] Following 2025 consolidations incorporating outlets like 2M and Medi1 TV, the organization employs around 4,500 staff across production, technical, and administrative functions, enabling comprehensive media operations distinct from profit-driven private broadcasters.[24]

Funding and financial model

Al Aoula, as the flagship channel of the Société Nationale de Radiodiffusion et de Télévision (SNRT), derives its primary funding from state subsidies allocated through the Moroccan national budget, which accounted for a significant portion of the broadcaster's resources, including approximately 88% of total public media funding directed to SNRT over a nine-year period ending around 2017, equating to over 1 billion USD in aggregate support.[26] This governmental allocation ensures operational stability for public service obligations but ties financial health to annual budget approvals by the Ministry of Finance, potentially exposing allocations to political influences rather than performance-based metrics.[24] Supplementing subsidies, SNRT generates revenue from commercial advertising, with Al Aoula serving as a key contributor due to its broad audience reach; advertising income primarily stems from television spots on channels like Al Aoula, which attract major advertisers during high-viewership periods such as Ramadan.[27] An additional funding stream comes from the Tax for Audiovisual Development (TAD), a special levy imposed on telecommunications operators and other entities to support public broadcasting infrastructure and content production.[24] This mixed model—public funds for mandate fulfillment and ads for competitiveness—creates inherent tensions, as the pursuit of advertiser-friendly programming may dilute commitments to independent or investigative journalism in favor of content aligned with state priorities.[2] Criticisms of the funding framework center on budgetary opacity and procurement irregularities, with audits revealing insufficient transparency in advertising revenue allocation and tender processes for production contracts.[28] [29] For instance, Reporters Without Borders has highlighted the advertisement market's lack of mechanisms to ensure fair distribution, while recent probes into SNRT executives, including those linked to Al Aoula's parent entity, have led to corruption charges over irregular bidding for audiovisual projects.[28] [30] State dominance in funding, while providing fiscal predictability absent in purely commercial models, incentivizes content that avoids scrutiny of government policies, as deviations could jeopardize subsidy continuity or ad placements from state-linked firms.[24]

Programming and Content

News and informational programming

Al Aoula's primary news output consists of multiple daily Journal Télévisé bulletins in Arabic, with principal editions airing at 20:30, delivering structured coverage of national politics, economic indicators, royal decrees, and international relations.[3] Complementary editions in French (JT en Français), Spanish (Telediario), and Amazigh (JT Amazigh) follow similar formats, adapting content for linguistic minorities while maintaining a focus on Moroccan state priorities.[3] These bulletins typically feature on-site reporting from government institutions, weather updates integrated at segment ends, and brief analyses of policy impacts, broadcast from SNRT studios in Rabat.[31] The programming underscores official narratives, allocating significant airtime to King Mohammed VI's addresses, cabinet decisions, and achievements in infrastructure or diplomacy, such as economic reforms under the New Development Model.[10] Parliamentary sessions and ministerial press conferences receive live transmission, often framing government actions as stabilizing forces amid regional challenges.[32] Dissenting perspectives, including opposition critiques, appear sparingly and are contextualized within broader national unity themes, reflecting SNRT's mandate to promote cohesion under ministerial oversight.[24] Informational segments extend beyond bulletins through debate formats like Parlement, which hosts discussions on legislative matters, and targeted specials on social issues.[32] In 2025, amid GenZ212-led protests demanding education and anti-corruption reforms, Al Aoula incorporated youth-oriented panels and reports to highlight government responses, such as expanded participation initiatives, aiming to foster dialogue on unemployment and inequality without endorsing unrest.[33] This empirical approach prioritizes data-driven segments on verifiable metrics like GDP growth or public health metrics, aligning with public service goals of informing citizens on state-directed progress.[34]

Entertainment, cultural, and educational content

Al Aoula broadcasts a variety of imported Turkish soap operas, dubbed into Arabic, which have become a social phenomenon in Morocco due to their dramatic storytelling and family-oriented themes, contributing to the channel's prime-time viewership.[35] These series, often airing in evening slots, appeal to broad demographics by blending romance, intrigue, and historical elements, fostering high engagement among Moroccan households despite competition from private channels. The channel produces original local series, including historical dramas like Fatah Al-Andalus, which premiered in 2022 and depicted the Muslim conquest of Iberia but sparked controversy for purportedly minimizing the Amazigh heritage of key figures such as Tariq ibn Ziyad.[36] Critics, including activist Rachid Bouhaddouz, filed a lawsuit against the Société Nationale de Radiodiffusion et de Télévision (SNRT) to halt its airing, alleging historical distortion, though Rabat's Court of First Instance dismissed the case on April 23, 2022, allowing broadcasts to continue. Comedy programming features shows like Stand Up, a talent competition for comedic sketches that has ranked as the channel's second-most-watched program since its debut, with its season finale drawing 5.4 million viewers through parody and social commentary rooted in everyday Moroccan life.[37] Such formats provide accessible entertainment while occasionally facing regulatory scrutiny, as in 2019 when the High Audio-Visual Communication Authority (HACA) warned Al Aoula over a skit deemed racially insensitive.[38] Educational segments emphasize Moroccan heritage, including slots on history, science, and Berber (Amazigh) culture, with programs subtitled in Arabic to promote national identity and linguistic diversity.[39] Cultural initiatives extend to music specials, heritage documentaries, and commissioned animated films—such as those produced in 2022 to preserve folklore—enhancing accessibility to traditional narratives amid criticisms of repetitive formats in serialized content.[40] High-engagement dramas, like Ana Wiak reaching 10.3 million viewers in a single episode, underscore how these originals sustain channel loyalty by blending local storytelling with broad appeal.[22]

Multilingual and regional broadcasts

Al Aoula's core programming is conducted in Modern Standard Arabic, with supplementary content in French to serve urban, educated, and Francophone viewers, including regular news bulletins such as JT en Français.[3] This bilingual approach reflects Morocco's historical linguistic landscape, where French maintains influence in elite and international contexts despite Arabic's primacy.[1] Since the early 2000s, amid broader governmental efforts to recognize Amazigh identity following the 2001 establishment of the Royal Institute of Amazigh Culture, Al Aoula has incorporated Tamazight-language segments, including dedicated news broadcasts in Amazigh dialects and cultural documentaries.[1] These air in scheduled slots, such as early-morning or late-night programming, covering variants like Tarifit for Rif region audiences and Tashelhit for Atlas and Souss communities.[3] Such inclusions, though limited compared to the dedicated Tamazight TV channel launched in 2010, represent initial steps toward linguistic representation for Berber speakers, who comprise an estimated 25-30% of Morocco's population.[37] Regional adaptations emphasize cultural content tailored to Morocco's diverse geography, with programs featuring Berber-influenced Arabic stylization—incorporating dialectal elements from Rif or Atlas traditions—to enhance accessibility and relatability without full dialectal shifts.[37] Occasional Spanish-language elements target northern border audiences near Spain, underscoring Al Aoula's role in promoting national cohesion through multilingual outreach while prioritizing centralized Arabic dominance.[3] These efforts have elicited mixed reception, with surveys indicating positive public attitudes toward Amazigh media presence but critiques from cultural advocates highlighting insufficient airtime relative to demographic weight, potentially limiting deeper integration.[41]

Audience Reach and Impact

Viewership metrics and ratings

Al Aoula consistently leads Moroccan television ratings, particularly during Ramadan, when viewership peaks due to traditional viewing habits around iftar and prime-time programming. On March 10, 2025—the ninth day of Ramadan 1446—the channel recorded a 37.7% audience share in prime time, drawing 13 million viewers and outperforming private competitors amid heightened advertising revenue.[22][42] Public broadcasters under SNRT, including Al Aoula, collectively captured 68.6% of the audience share during this period, underscoring their dominance in a market increasingly fragmented by satellite channels and streaming services.[22] Prior to the liberalization of broadcasting in the 2000s, Al Aoula enjoyed near-monopoly status as Morocco's sole national television channel, commanding the vast majority of viewership in an analog era limited to terrestrial signals. Post-liberalization, its share has fluctuated with competition from private outlets like 2M and international broadcasters, yet it retained a 34.6% share in key evening slots as of March 2024, reaching 41.9% of available viewers.[43] During Ramadan 2024, SNRT channels achieved up to 40% share in post-iftar prime time across the first 25 days.[23] These metrics reflect sustained accessibility via widespread terrestrial coverage and habitual tuning among households without broadband alternatives. Demographic data from the Haute Autorité de la Communication Audiovisuelle (HACA) indicates Al Aoula's strength among family-oriented and rural audiences, who prioritize national content over niche digital platforms.[44] In quarterly measurements through 2023, public channels like Al Aoula maintained higher penetration in non-urban areas compared to urban youth demographics favoring streaming.[44] Factors such as obligatory carriage on cable systems and alignment with cultural viewing peaks contribute to this resilience, with prime-time reach often exceeding 50% in targeted household segments.[43]

Societal and cultural influence

Al Aoula, as Morocco's flagship public broadcaster since its launch on March 3, 1962, has significantly contributed to national cohesion by providing accessible visual content that simplifies educational and societal topics for diverse audiences, including those in regions with limited print media access.[21] Operating as the sole television channel until 1989, it democratized information flow in a post-independence context, bridging urban-rural divides and fostering shared narratives on cultural identity and development.[45] This role extended to cultural exports like serialized dramas, which popularized discussions on family structures and social expectations, with imported Turkish soaps broadcast on the channel emerging as a widespread phenomenon that influenced everyday conversations and norms.[35] The channel's format has proven particularly effective in low-literacy areas, where adult illiteracy persists at around 24.8% nationally as of 2024, with rural rates substantially higher, enabling television to serve as a primary conduit for shaping public views on policies and institutions like the monarchy.[46][47] Programs vulgarizing complex issues—such as health, education, and civic duties—have thus reinforced collective understanding without requiring advanced reading skills, promoting unity amid Morocco's ethnic and linguistic diversity.[39] Despite these unifying effects, Al Aoula's state-aligned content tends to uphold prevailing power dynamics, prioritizing stability and official perspectives over critiques of underlying causal factors in social challenges. In 2025, initiatives like special youth-focused segments on the channel engaged emerging dissent by featuring discussions on demands from younger demographics, including parliamentary input, yet these efforts empirically channel input into existing frameworks rather than enabling transformative shifts.[33] This adaptive approach sustains influence amid generational changes while limiting disruptions to entrenched structures.[48]

Criticisms and Controversies

Allegations of state control and bias

Al Aoula, as the flagship channel of the state-owned Société Nationale de Radiodiffusion et de Télévision (SNRT), operates under a governance structure that ensures alignment with government priorities, with its 12-member administrative council comprising 10 members appointed by the state and its president director general selected by the Minister of Youth, Culture and Communication with the king's approval.[24] This framework has led to persistent allegations that the channel functions as a mouthpiece for the authorities, systematically eschewing criticism of the monarchy, state institutions, or sensitive national issues.[24][49] Journalists at SNRT, including those producing Al Aoula content, engage in widespread self-censorship due to legal threats, regulatory oversight, and harassment risks, despite constitutional guarantees against censorship; topics such as the monarchy, Western Sahara territorial claims, Islam, and military matters remain taboo, fostering a culture of preemptive restraint to avoid retribution.[49][50][24] Morocco's press freedom ranking of 129 out of 180 in the 2024 World Press Freedom Index underscores these constraints, with state media like Al Aoula exemplifying limited editorial independence amid judicial and societal pressures.[49] The channel's financial model amplifies these dynamics, deriving roughly 66% of its budget from state subsidies—such as MAD 1.1 billion in 2020—alongside allocations from public funds like the Taxe Parafiscale sur la Publicité et l'Annonce (TPPAN) and the Fonds de Soutien à la Production et à la Diffusion des Programmes Audiovisuels et Éducatifs du Patrimoine (FPPAAEP), which critics argue incentivize loyalty to the regime over neutral public service broadcasting.[24] This dependency, evident in SNRT's total budgets of MAD 1.6 billion in 2019 and MAD 1.9 billion for 2023–2024, causally ties resource provision to content alignment, prioritizing narratives that bolster institutional stability.[24] Compared to private outlets, Al Aoula exhibits less willingness to challenge official lines despite its superior resources, reflecting a hybrid model where state ownership enforces tighter conformity to establishment interests, including recent expansions like SNRT's 2024–2025 acquisition of stakes in channels such as 2M and Medi1TV to consolidate influence over the broadcast landscape.[24][49]

Coverage of protests and political events

During the 2017 Hirak Rif protests, which erupted in the Rif region following the October 28, 2016, death of fishmonger Mohcine Fikri in a garbage truck compactor, Al Aoula's coverage drew rebukes from Morocco's High Authority for Audiovisual Communication (HACA) for inaccuracies in depicting events in Al Hoceima.[51] [52] HACA issued formal warnings to Al Aoula, alongside other state-linked channels like Tamazight TV, for disseminating misleading reports on protest clashes that failed to reflect on-the-ground realities, thereby distorting public perception of demands for economic investment and regional equity.[52] This approach aligned with broader patterns of pro-government media framing Rif activists as separatists or foreign-influenced agitators, sidelining causal factors such as chronic underdevelopment and unemployment rates exceeding 20% in the region.[53] In the 2025 Gen Z protests, initiated on September 27 by decentralized online collectives like GenZ 212 across cities including Casablanca, Rabat, and Tangier, Al Aoula responded with youth-focused specials in early October that critics described as efforts to channel dissent into managed discourse rather than probe government accountability.[54] [33] These programs, broadcast amid demands for reallocating World Cup infrastructure funds toward collapsing public health and education systems—where over 200 arrests occurred by late September—emphasized "dialogue" with officials while omitting scrutiny of fiscal priorities that fueled unrest, such as stagnant youth unemployment near 35%.[55] [33] Analysts contend this coverage functioned to contain mobilization by state media, which, as public broadcasters under executive oversight, inherently prioritizes stability over exposing structural failures like inadequate hospital staffing and school infrastructure deficits documented in protest manifestos.[33] [56] In contrast to private outlets airing direct critiques of official reticence, Al Aoula's segments suppressed linkages to economic grievances, reinforcing a narrative of orderly engagement over systemic critique.[57]

Efforts at reform and ongoing debates

Following the adoption of Morocco's 2011 constitution, which enshrined principles of media pluralism and independence under Article 25, public broadcasters like Al Aoula—operated by the state-owned Société Nationale de Radiodiffusion et de Télévision (SNRT)—faced expectations for structural reforms to reduce direct governmental oversight. However, SNRT leadership, including its president, continues to be appointed by the King, maintaining monarchical influence without legislative divestment of state ownership, which critics argue perpetuates indirect control rather than fostering autonomy.[24] Recent initiatives have included efforts at modernization, such as SNRT's youth-focused events and Al Aoula's special programming addressing young people's demands amid social unrest, as seen in an October 2025 broadcast featuring opposition parliamentarians.[33] These steps, alongside digital engagement platforms, aim to adapt to younger audiences and sustain viewership, with SNRT reporting interactive content tailored for diaspora and domestic youth in events like the August 2025 global Moroccan youth forum.[58] Yet, such measures lack verifiable shifts in editorial independence, as SNRT avoids critiquing state policies or the monarchy, reinforcing perceptions of cosmetic adaptation over substantive change.[24] Ongoing debates highlight a divide: proponents credit these adaptations for maintaining Al Aoula's relevance in a competitive media landscape, where state funding ensures operational stability despite competition from private outlets.[59] Detractors, including opposition figures, decry persistent Rabat-centric content—derisively termed "tilifizyoun dyal Rbat" for its alignment with central authority narratives—and antiquated programming that fails to reflect diverse regional voices or challenge power structures.[60] Skepticism intensified in 2025, with analysts questioning the "new era" of openness amid youth engagement efforts, arguing that without causal breaks from executive and royal oversight—evidenced by ongoing harassment of critical journalists—reforms remain superficial and unlikely to achieve genuine pluralism.[33][59]
