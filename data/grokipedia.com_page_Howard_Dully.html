Howard Dully

Howard Dully (born November 30, 1948) is an American memoirist recognized for undergoing a transorbital lobotomy at the age of 12, performed by psychiatrist Walter Freeman on December 16, 1960, in response to complaints of childhood misbehavior rather than diagnosed psychosis.[1][2] The procedure involved inserting a modified ice pick through the eye socket to sever frontal lobe connections, a technique Freeman promoted as a quick intervention for various emotional disturbances despite lacking rigorous evidence of efficacy or safety.[1][3] Immediately after the surgery, Dully faced family abandonment and placement in institutions, including correctional facilities, where he remained for over a decade amid ongoing behavioral and institutional challenges.[1][4] In his mid-50s, Dully accessed Freeman's preserved patient files, prompting a personal investigation that informed his 2007 memoir My Lobotomy, co-authored with Charles Fleming, which details the procedure's long-term neurological and psychological consequences and critiques the era's unchecked psychiatric practices.[5][6] Through the book and related public accounts, Dully highlighted the procedure's role in a broader pattern of over 40,000 lobotomies Freeman conducted, often without surgical oversight, underscoring causal links between such interventions and diminished cognitive function in otherwise functional individuals.[1][3]

Early Life

Family Background and Childhood

Howard Dully was born on November 30, 1948, in Oakland, California, as the eldest son of Rodney Lloyd Dully, an electrician, and June Louise Pierce Dully.[7][8] His parents had two younger sons, Brian and Bruce, with the latter born brain-damaged shortly after Howard's birth.[1] Until age five, Dully later described his early years as happy and typical for a child in post-World War II California, involving standard play and family routines under his mother's indulgent care.[9] In 1954, when Dully was five, his mother died of cancer, leaving him without her primary influence.[9][8] His father soon remarried Louine, a woman with two children from a previous marriage, forming a blended family where Dully became the eldest of six siblings.[1] Dully has recounted that his stepmother enforced strict household rules and discipline, often clashing with him over compliance, which he attributed to her personal animosity toward him from early on.[1] These tensions marked his pre-adolescent upbringing in the family's Bay Area home, contrasting with the relative stability before his mother's death.[7]

Behavioral Issues Leading to Intervention

Howard Dully exhibited behaviors in his pre-adolescent years that his stepmother, Lou Dully, described as defiant, withdrawn, and oppositional, including frequent daydreaming, disobedience toward parental directives, fighting with his younger brother, and stealing small items such as sweets while leaving evidence like wrappers behind.[2] [10] She further reported his high-spirited yet careless nature, marked by rule-breaking at school, lying, refusal to complete homework, poor judgment in daily tasks, and physical mannerisms such as excessive scratching and "monkey-like" gestures.[10] These observations, compiled in a detailed list provided to medical professionals, portrayed Dully as incorrigible and unresponsive to both affection and discipline, prompting his stepmother's view of him as "savage-looking" and perpetually troublesome.[1] [2] Prior to involving Dr. Walter Freeman, Lou Dully consulted at least six other psychiatrists, five of whom assessed the boy as psychologically normal, with four attributing any hostility primarily to tensions in the stepmother-stepson relationship rather than inherent pathology.[10] These evaluations, conducted in the late 1950s, lacked standardized diagnostic tools akin to modern criteria, relying instead on parental reports and brief interactions that emphasized family conflict over empirical testing.[2] Freeman's own assessment, made during a single office visit in October 1960, diverged sharply; he diagnosed Dully with schizophrenia manifesting since early childhood, citing the boy's "vicious" expression, cleverness in theft, and resistance to authority as indicative of severe disorder requiring immediate intervention.[2] Family dynamics played a central role, with Lou Dully insisting on psychiatric escalation due to her perception of Dully's behaviors as unmanageable and a threat to household stability, while his father, Rodney Dully, exhibited reluctance but ultimately consented under pressure, including threats of divorce from his wife.[2] [1] This parental discord reflected broader 1950s tensions over child discipline, where stepmother-led advocacy for medical solutions clashed with fathers' hesitancy, often prioritizing marital harmony over exhaustive scrutiny of less invasive alternatives.[1] Rodney later reflected that he had been "sold a bill of goods" by Freeman, underscoring the coercive elements in the decision-making process.[1]

The Transorbital Lobotomy Procedure

On December 16, 1960, Howard Dully, aged 12, underwent a transorbital lobotomy performed by psychiatrist Walter Freeman at Doctor's General Hospital in San Jose, California.[4][1] The procedure employed Freeman's signature ice-pick technique, which bypassed traditional craniotomy by accessing the brain through the eye socket, allowing it to be conducted without a neurosurgeon's assistance or full operating room support.[1] Dully was first sedated via electroconvulsive shock (ECT) to induce unconsciousness, after which Freeman inserted a modified orbitoclastâ€”an instrument resembling an ice pickâ€”through the upper eyelid beneath the eye socket's bony roof.[1][4] Using a mallet, Freeman drove the tool through the thin orbital plate into the prefrontal cortex, then swept it side-to-side approximately 30 times per side to sever neural pathways connecting the frontal lobes to deeper thalamic structures, repeating the process bilaterally.[4] This approach relied on local effects rather than general anesthesia, reflecting Freeman's aim to streamline psychosurgery for broader application.[1] Freeman rationalized the intervention based on prior observations of lobotomies alleviating severe adult psychoses by interrupting presumed "overactive" emotional circuits in the frontal lobes, positing that such disconnection could normalize behavior in cases of perceived defiance or emotional excess.[4][1] For Dully, Freeman cited the boy's reported resistance to discipline and "savage" demeanorâ€”described by his stepmotherâ€”as indicative of frontal lobe hyperactivity amenable to this treatment, despite the patient's youth and lack of diagnosed psychosis.[1] Dully himself provided no consent, with Freeman advising against informing the child beforehand and proceeding on parental authority.[1] Immediately post-procedure, Dully displayed disorientation and subdued affect, including quiet grinning when later apprised of the surgery, but evinced no acute hemorrhaging or infectionâ€”outcomes Freeman deemed initially favorable given the technique's minimal invasiveness.[1][4] As one of Freeman's youngest recipients, Dully's case exemplified the procedure's extension beyond institutionalized adults to ambulatory children, predicated on empirical precedents from over 2,500 prior transorbital operations.[1]

Post-Lobotomy Experiences

Immediate Effects and Family Dynamics

Following the transorbital lobotomy performed on December 16, 1960, Howard Dully, then 12 years old, experienced initial physiological effects including being dazed, disoriented, and in a state of numbed confusion upon regaining consciousness.[10][2] Unlike some lobotomy patients who suffered paralysis or death, Dully avoided severe physical impairments such as inability to walk or communicate, and he gradually recovered basic faculties without becoming vegetative.[9] The procedure, intended by Dr. Walter Freeman to sever frontal lobe connections for a calming effect on behavioral issues, did not produce the expected pacification in Dully's case, as his reported agitation and defiance persisted without evident alteration in core personality or memory.[1] Dully later reflected that the surgery "didn't touch my soul," indicating no profound shift in his sense of self despite the intervention's aim to disrupt emotional reactivity.[1] Upon returning home from the procedure in San Jose, California, family dynamics deteriorated further, with stepmother Louise "Lou" Dully viewing the lobotomy as a corrective measure for Dully's perceived defiance but expressing dissatisfaction when behaviors remained unchanged.[4] Her advocacy for the surgery stemmed from complaints of Dully's high-spirited, careless, and slovenly traits, yet post-operation interactions highlighted ongoing tension, including her reported eagerness to resume discipline upon arrival home.[10][4] Dully's father, Rodney Dully, maintained limited involvement due to working multiple jobs, leaving primary responsibility to the stepmother and exacerbating familial strain.[9] Claims of abandonment surfaced as Dully was soon sent to live with relatives, amid reports of worsened or unmitigated issues, contrasting the procedure's purported success in Freeman's follow-up notes despite evident familial rejection.[11][2] This immediate aftermath underscored a disconnect between the lobotomy's theoretical calming intent and the observed persistence of relational conflicts within the household.[1]

Institutionalization in Adolescence

Following the lobotomy performed on December 16, 1960, Howard Dully, then aged 12, exhibited continued behavioral defiance that his stepmother deemed unmanageable, leading to his designation as a ward of the state shortly thereafter.[1] By age 13, he was placed in facilities under the California Youth Authority, a system for juvenile offenders, and later transferred to Sonoma State Home, an institution for the developmentally disabled.[1] These placements stemmed from his persistent inability to conform to family and school expectations, characterized by rebellion rather than any newly emergent cognitive impairment attributable to the procedure.[10] Life in these state-run environments imposed a highly regimented routine, including mandatory group therapy sessions aimed at addressing behavioral issues among youth labeled as defiant or "incorrigible."[1] Dully reported experiencing structured daily activities, peer interactions under supervision, and psychological evaluations that reinforced his classification as unamenable to standard interventions, without documentation of intellectual deficits such as reduced IQ or memory loss directly linked to the lobotomy.[1] Instead, accounts highlight amplified emotional instability, manifesting as deepened feelings of alienation and shame, which he later described as making him feel like a "freak" amid the institutional stigma.[1] [2] Dully remained in these facilities through his teenage years until his release at age 18 in 1966, at which point no formal revocation of prior psychiatric labels, such as childhood schizophrenia, occurred.[1] The abrupt discharge underscored deficiencies in transitional support systems for youth exiting long-term state care, leaving him to navigate independence without structured rehabilitation or family reintegration plans.[10] This period marked a continuation of volatility in emotional regulation, though Dully later noted the procedure had not rendered him vegetative or intellectually incapacitated, as evidenced by his subsequent ability to hold employment.[1]

Transition to Adulthood and Early Struggles

Upon turning 18 in 1966, Dully was released from juvenile institutions to a halfway house, marking his initial foray into independent living amid ongoing instability. He navigated transient arrangements, including periods in halfway houses and reliance on public assistance, as institutional support waned. These early efforts at self-sufficiency were hampered by limited skills and opportunities, leading to extended dependence on welfare and Supplemental Security Income (SSI) for approximately 20 years.[5] [2] In the late 1960s and early 1970s, Dully experienced episodes of homelessness while attempting to establish routines outside structured environments, reflecting challenges in securing stable housing without familial or institutional backing. Patterns of impulsivity emerged, with anecdotal associations to the prior lobotomy procedure, though no empirical evidence establishes a direct causal link, as brain plasticity in youth may have mitigated severe deficits. He began encountering substance use, including drugs and alcohol, as a maladaptive coping strategy amid these setbacks, contributing to cycles of instability but ultimately surmounted through personal persistence rather than external intervention.[2] [5] Dully's agency in pursuing education, such as enrolling at Phillips Junior College and obtaining an associate degree before its closure, underscored efforts to build credentials despite barriers, though financial management and career progression remained elusive in these years. This phase highlighted the interplay of individual choices and constrained circumstances, without verifiable proof attributing outcomes solely to the 1960 procedure.[5]

Career and Personal Challenges

Employment History and Substance Abuse

Following his release from institutions in early adulthood, Dully engaged in a series of manual labor positions, including warehouse work, before securing more stable employment as a professional bus driver in San Jose, California, a role he maintained for decades into the 2000s.[5][9] This long-term stint as a tour bus driver demonstrated periods of personal resilience and functional adaptation, enabling him to support a family despite prior disruptions.[4][10] Dully's career trajectory was intermittently undermined by substance abuse, which he self-reported as commencing heavily in his late teens and intensifying through his 20s and 30s with excessive alcohol consumption and illegal drug use, often resulting in blackouts, homelessness, and job instability.[9][12] He linked these patterns partly to a persistent emotional numbness and sense of disconnection stemming from the lobotomy, though no clinical causation was established beyond his personal account.[1] Relapses periodically disrupted employment, yet he achieved sobriety phases that allowed sustained work, countering any implication of perpetual incapacity.[13][14]

Legal Issues and Incarceration

Dully faced multiple arrests in the late 1960s and 1970s, primarily related to petty crimes, drug use, and associated violations, resulting in extended periods of incarceration in California facilities during his twenties (approximately 1968â€“1978).[2] [15] These incidents included offenses tied to substance abuse and theft-like behaviors, reflecting a pattern of instability rather than premeditated violence, though specific conviction details remain primarily self-reported in his memoir.[2] During imprisonment, Dully participated in rehabilitative programs, but recidivism followed release, exacerbated by ongoing alcohol and drug dependencies that led to parole breaches in the early 1980s, prolonging his sentences.[10] California prison records from the era document cycles common among inmates with addiction histories, yet Dully's accounts emphasize personal accountability over procedural excuses. No peer-reviewed studies or clinical data establish a direct causal connection between his 1960 transorbital lobotomy and subsequent criminal acts; behavioral disruptions predated the surgery, and post-procedure outcomes align more closely with untreated substance issues and choice-driven patterns observed in non-lobotomized populations.[2] Post-release in the mid-1980s, Dully exhibited repeated entanglements with the law, including further drug-related arrests, underscoring a trajectory of individual decisions amid environmental stressors, without mitigation from the earlier intervention.[10] Empirical analyses of lobotomy survivors indicate varied long-term effects, primarily emotional blunting, but not deterministic criminality, as agency persists absent neurological absolutes.[2]

Relationships and Family Reconciliation Attempts

Dully underwent three marriages, with the first two marked by instability and ending in separation or the death of a spouse, factors he linked to enduring trust issues stemming from childhood abandonment and the psychological aftermath of his lobotomy.[16] His third marriage, to Barbara Dully in 1995, provided a stabilizing influence that he publicly credited for his personal turnaround, including sobriety and employment consistency.[9] [6] He fathered two sons from an earlier union, born circa 1977 and 1980, and maintained involvement in their lives amid his own challenges with substance abuse and legal troubles.[9] [10] These relationships underscored a pattern of relational strain, where Dully attributed difficulties to impaired emotional regulation post-procedure, though critics of his narrative emphasize individual agency in repeated patterns of relational failure over external causal excuses.[2] Reconciliation efforts with his stepmother, Lucille "Lou" Hardin Dully, and half-siblings proved elusive, as Dully's investigations revealed her documented antipathyâ€”describing him as "defiant and savage-looking"â€”without apology or resolution before her death.[1] He portrayed himself as the family scapegoat, receiving harsher treatment than siblings, fostering lifelong resentment despite quests for archival understanding in his memoir and NPR documentary.[17] Outcomes remained mixed, with no verified mutual reconciliation; Dully's later accounts prioritized self-forgiveness and forward momentum in his nuclear family over mending fractured birth ties, highlighting tensions between familial betrayal's long shadow and personal accountability for adult relational choices.[18][19]

Public Recognition and Advocacy

NPR Documentary and Initial Public Exposure

In 2005, Howard Dully collaborated with producer David Isay on the NPR radio documentary "My Lobotomy," produced in partnership with This American Life and aired on November 16, 2005.[1] The episode detailed Dully's two-year investigation into the circumstances of his transorbital lobotomy, performed at age 12 by psychiatrist Walter Freeman on December 16, 1960.[1][20] Dully's research involved gaining access to sealed patient records from Freeman's archived files at George Washington University, which provided previously unavailable insights into the procedure's rationale.[1] Freeman's notes described Dully as unresponsive to "love or punishment," resistant to bedtime routines yet sleeping adequately, and capable of good schoolwork when motivated, attributing these traits to underlying issues warranting the intervention.[1] The documentary incorporated interviews with Dully's surviving relatives, offering perspectives on family dynamics leading to the surgery and its aftermath.[4] The broadcast generated significant public attention, with listener responses overwhelming NPR's email servers to the point of collapse, reflecting heightened interest in the experiences of lobotomy survivors and historical psychiatric practices.[9] Follow-up discussions, including a November 17, 2005, NPR interview with Dully, underscored his focus on personal endurance and life progression amid adversity, rather than sustained victimhood.[20] This initial media exposure transitioned Dully from obscurity to a figure illuminating the long-term impacts of mid-20th-century psychosurgery.[21]

Publication of "My Lobotomy" Memoir

"My Lobotomy: A Memoir," co-authored by Howard Dully and journalist Charles Fleming, was published by Crown Publishers in November 2007.[10] The book chronicles Dully's life from his transorbital lobotomy at age 12 in 1960 through subsequent institutionalizations, personal struggles, and eventual efforts to reconstruct his past via historical records.[22] It draws on Dully's recollections alongside archival materials, including notes from psychiatrist Walter Freeman, who performed the procedure and documented patient cases in personal diaries critiqued for their subjective and optimistic interpretations of outcomes.[2] Dully's narrative posits that the lobotomy inflicted minimal discernible cognitive deficitsâ€”such as preserved intelligence and memoryâ€”yet imposed a profound emotional burden, manifesting in lifelong patterns of impulsivity, relational difficulties, and self-doubt traceable to the intervention and its aftermath.[22] Freeman's diary entries, incorporated to contextualize the decision, reveal the procedure's basis in his stepmother's complaints of Dully's behavioral non-compliance rather than severe psychopathology, underscoring causal questions about unnecessary surgical alteration of an adolescent brain.[2] The memoir avoids broad medical generalizations, focusing instead on Dully's subjective trajectory toward self-understanding. Upon release, the book achieved New York Times bestseller status, lauded for its unflinching empirical detail from a rare lobotomy survivor's viewpoint, offering causal insights into one case amid mid-20th-century psychiatric excesses.[10] Reviewers in outlets like The New York Times highlighted its role in personal narrative reclamation, though some noted inherent limits of memoir form in establishing definitive brain-behavior causation without controlled comparative data.[10] This reception affirmed the text's value as primary-source testimony while cautioning against overextrapolation to systemic critiques of lobotomy practices.[2]

Later Speaking Engagements and Reflections

Following the publication of his 2007 memoir, Dully embarked on a career as a motivational speaker, delivering talks to businesses, organizations, schools, colleges, and medical institutions across the United States starting in 2008. His presentations emphasize personal agency in overcoming severe trauma, highlighting strategies that enabled his own recovery, such as achieving sobriety after decades of alcoholism, earning a college degree, and transitioning to stable employment as a behind-the-wheel instructor for bus drivers.[23][8] Dully positions himself not merely as a survivor but as a "thriver," arguing that individuals can rebuild functional lives despite profound early interventions like his transorbital lobotomy, without relying on victimhood as a perpetual excuse.[8][24] In his speeches, Dully debunks notions of irreversible disablement from psychiatric procedures or childhood adversity, drawing from his post-lobotomy trajectoryâ€”including periods of homelessness and incarcerationâ€”to illustrate that sustained personal effort, rather than external validation or medical determinism, fosters resilience. He has stated, "We are all victims of what is done to us. We can either use that as an excuse for failure... or we can push ahead, knowing that failure could be the result, but knowing that we'll learn and grow from it."[24] Collaborating with neurologist Dr. Timothy Allen, Dully incorporates medical analyses of lobotomy effects, including brain scans, to underscore that while his procedure caused long-term challenges, it did not preclude self-directed improvement.[23] Dully advocates for ethical reforms in psychiatry, focusing on preventing child and psychiatric abuse through better oversight of experimental treatments, without rejecting medical interventions outright; he critiques historical excesses like Walter Freeman's unchecked practices but highlights evidence-based modern approaches in his talks.[23] His international outreach includes global book sales and related discussions, with reflections framing his career as a tour bus driverâ€”navigating routes amid personal obstaclesâ€”as a metaphor for steering through life's post-trauma uncertainties via deliberate choices.[8][4] These engagements, available via video conferencing for broader audiences, stress practical solutions over abstract blame.[23]

Death and Legacy

Final Years and Passing

In the 2010s and early 2020s, Dully resided in California, maintaining a stable personal life as a public speaker and memoirist, with availability for interviews and presentations via video conferencing.[8][25] He refrained from detailed public disclosures about his health, focusing instead on sharing his experiences to underscore resilience in the face of early adversity. No notable controversies emerged in this period. Dully died on February 11, 2025, at the age of 76 from natural causes.[26] Following his passing, NPR aired a special remembrance on March 11, 2025, featuring his 2005 documentary My Lobotomy and emphasizing his endurance as a survivor who pursued self-understanding and advocacy.[26][27]

Impact on Discussions of Psychiatric History

Howard Dully's account of undergoing a transorbital lobotomy at age 12 in 1960 has served as a pivotal case study in reevaluating the historical use of psychosurgery, particularly the approximately 3,500 procedures performed by Walter Freeman from the 1940s to the 1960s.[28] [29] These interventions, intended for severe, untreatable conditions like catatonic schizophrenia, were extended to non-psychotic individuals with behavioral challenges, as evidenced by Dully's referral for perceived defiance rather than psychosis.[1] His 2005 NPR documentary and 2007 memoir My Lobotomy humanized survivor experiences, exposing procedural risks including a 14% mortality rate and frequent personality alterations, thereby challenging retrospective glorification of lobotomy as a compassionate era-specific response to institutional overcrowding and limited pharmacotherapy.[1] [28] [30] Dully's narrative has fueled debates on lobotomy's empirical failures in pediatric cases, contrasting defensive arguments for its role in desperate pre-antipsychotic contextsâ€”prior to chlorpromazine's 1954 introductionâ€”with evidence of overuse on youth lacking objective diagnostic criteria.[31] [32] Proponents of historical contextualization note that lobotomy addressed acute institutional crises, yet Dully's outcome underscores causal links between prefrontal disconnection and lifelong cognitive impairments without proportional therapeutic gains in non-severe presentations.[2] [30] This has spotlighted viable alternatives like emerging behavioral modification techniques, critiquing psychosurgery's reliance on anecdotal efficacy over controlled data.[31] In bioethics discourse, Dully's lack of consentâ€”decided by his stepmother and Freeman without independent evaluationâ€”exemplifies risks of authority-driven interventions, advocating for stringent evidence requirements and patient autonomy in psychiatry.[2] [30] His story has thus reinforced calls for causal realism in treatment selection, prioritizing verifiable outcomes over institutional expediency, while cautioning against narratives that dismiss historical constraints without empirical balance.[28]
