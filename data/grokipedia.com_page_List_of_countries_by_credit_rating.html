List of countries by credit rating

Lists of countries by credit rating aggregate sovereign credit ratings, which are independent evaluations of a government's ability and willingness to fulfill its debt obligations, provided primarily by the major agencies Standard & Poor's (S&P), Moody's Investors Service, and Fitch Ratings.[1][2] These ratings employ a scale of letter grades, ranging from AAA (indicating the highest creditworthiness) to D (denoting default), reflecting quantitative analyses of fiscal metrics, economic growth, political stability, and external vulnerabilities.[1][3] Sovereign ratings significantly influence a country's borrowing costs in international bond markets, with higher-rated nations typically facing lower interest rate spreads due to perceived reduced default risk, thereby facilitating greater access to global capital.[1][4] Empirical evidence indicates that these assessments correlate with actual sovereign default probabilities, aiding investors in allocating resources efficiently across diverse economies.[5] However, disparities in ratings often underscore fundamental differences in policy frameworks, with stable economies maintaining superior grades through prudent debt management, while others suffer downgrades from chronic deficits or institutional weaknesses.[6] Criticisms of rating agencies include allegations of systematic bias against poorer or developing countries, where ratings may diverge from fundamentals due to regional or income-level prejudices, potentially exacerbating capital flight and financial fragility.[7][8] Studies also highlight procyclical tendencies, wherein downgrades during economic stress amplify crises rather than merely reflecting them, though agencies maintain their methodologies prioritize forward-looking risk over short-term volatility.[9][10] Despite such debates, sovereign ratings remain a cornerstone for cross-border investment decisions, with empirical validation of their predictive power outweighing isolated methodological flaws in most analyses.[5][9]

Background

Definition and Purpose

Sovereign credit ratings constitute forward-looking opinions on a sovereign government's relative ability and willingness to meet its financial commitments on sovereign debt obligations, particularly long-term foreign currency issuances.[11][12] These ratings gauge the risk of default, serving as standardized signals of fiscal and economic credibility to market participants rather than subjective or prescriptive evaluations of policy choices.[2] Issued by specialized private agencies such as Moody's, which traces its origins to 1909, they originated in corporate bond assessments but extended to sovereign issuers in the early 20th century, with the earliest recorded rating assigned to the United States in 1941.[13][3] The rating scales employed by major agencies range from the highest investment-grade categoryâ€”AAA or equivalent, denoting minimal default riskâ€”to D, indicating actual or imminent default, with intermediate notches reflecting graduated probabilities of default over specified horizons.[11][14] For long-term ratings, agencies typically consider horizons of 2 to 5 years or longer, incorporating empirical default statistics; for instance, Moody's analyses show cumulative default rates under 0.2% over five years for top-rated (Aaa) sovereigns.[15] Evaluations blend quantitative indicators, such as debt-to-GDP ratios exceeding 100% in high-debt cases signaling elevated vulnerability, with qualitative assessments of institutional resilience and political stability, though the precise weighting remains agency-proprietary.[2] Primarily, these ratings function as risk-pricing benchmarks for investors allocating capital to sovereign bonds, enabling differentiation of yield premiums based on perceived default probabilities independent of other market correlations.[1][16] By quantifying country-specific risks, they facilitate efficient global capital flows without serving as endorsements of fiscal strategies or guarantees against loss, as evidenced by their influence on bond yields particularly for lower-rated issuances.[12] Systematic application to a broader array of emerging sovereigns accelerated from the 1970s amid rising international lending, underscoring their evolution as market-oriented tools rather than regulatory impositions.[2]

Economic Significance

Credit ratings exert a profound influence on sovereign borrowing costs by quantifying perceived default risk, enabling investors to price government bonds accordingly and thereby reducing yields for higher-rated issuers. Empirical analyses confirm that upgrades in sovereign ratings lead to statistically significant declines in bond yields and spreads, lowering interest expenses and enhancing fiscal space for productive investments. For example, a one-notch improvement can translate to reduced borrowing costs that persist over time, as markets adjust to the revised risk assessment.[17][5] AAA-rated countries, such as those with top-tier assessments from major agencies, routinely issue long-term debt at spreads below 50 basis points relative to US Treasuries, reflecting negligible additional risk premia demanded by global investors. This premium structure underscores how ratings facilitate cheaper access to international capital markets, particularly for nations maintaining strong fiscal metrics like low debt-to-GDP ratios and robust growth trajectories. In turn, these lower costs incentivize policy choices that prioritize debt sustainability, as governments face market penaltiesâ€”via widened spreadsâ€”for fiscal laxity.[18][19] Beyond direct pricing effects, ratings serve as herd signals that guide capital allocation and can propagate shocks across borders during periods of stress. In the 1997 Asian financial crisis, rating downgrades lagged but reinforced investor exits from affected economies like Thailand and Indonesia, accelerating contagion through synchronized portfolio reallocations. Similarly, during the 2010 Eurozone debt crisis, sequential downgrades of Greece, Portugal, and others amplified yield spikes and funding pressures, compelling swift policy responses amid heightened market scrutiny. Such dynamics highlight ratings' role in channeling market discipline, where anticipated rating actions influence flows preemptively.[20][21] Empirical research further establishes a positive correlation between higher sovereign ratings and economic growth, mediated by expanded financing options that support investment without crowding out private sector activity. Rating upgrades have been shown to elevate annual GDP growth by around 0.6 percentage points, primarily through interest rate channels that ease credit conditions economy-wide. This evidence challenges assertions that ratings impede development in emerging markets, instead revealing their function in promoting fiscal restraint and efficient resource mobilization via transparent, market-enforced incentives.[22][23][24]

Historical Development

Early Origins

Credit ratings originated in the assessment of corporate bond risks, with John Moody publishing the first systematic ratings in his 1909 manual, Moody's Analyses of Railroad Investments, which assigned letter grades (Aaa to C) to U.S. railroad bonds based on their default probability, providing investors a standardized tool for evaluating creditworthiness amid growing bond market complexity.[25][26] This innovation stemmed from first-principles risk evaluation, where higher yields on riskier bonds implicitly signaled fiscal or operational weaknesses, such as over-leveraging or revenue shortfalls, long before formal agencies; for instance, 19th-century sovereign borrowers like Egypt saw bond yields surge prior to the 1876 default due to excessive borrowing against uncertain cotton revenues.[27] Prior to dedicated sovereign ratings, governments' credit risks were gauged informally through sovereign bond yield spreads over benchmark safe assets, reflecting causal factors like fiscal profligacyâ€”unrestrained deficit spending without corresponding growthâ€”which elevated default probabilities, as evidenced by interwar defaults where high-debt nations like Germany faced yield premiums signaling repayment doubts.[27] Post-World War II, as international lending expanded via institutions like the World Bank and amid decolonization, informal yield-based assessments persisted, but the scale of cross-border sovereign borrowing grew, necessitating more structured evaluations to price risks in an era of fixed exchange rates under Bretton Woods.[28] The formal extension to sovereigns crystallized in 1975, when Standard & Poor's issued its first ratings for 23 countries, motivated by surging emerging-market debt issuance fueled by petrodollar recyclingâ€”OPEC nations' oil windfalls from the 1973 crisis were lent back via Western banks to developing economies, amplifying default risks and demanding explicit credit assessments to guide investors.[29][30] This shift marked a departure from ad-hoc yield signals toward agency-driven scales, prioritizing empirical default histories and economic fundamentals to quantify sovereign repayment capacity.[2]

Expansion and Standardization

The proliferation of sovereign credit ratings accelerated in the post-1980s era, driven by globalization and the increasing integration of emerging markets into international capital flows. As developing and transition economies sought access to private financing following the debt crises of the 1980s, major agencies expanded coverage to assess default risks more systematically. This coincided with the 1989 Brady Plan, which facilitated debt restructurings for countries like Mexico and Brazil through market-oriented instruments such as Brady bonds, where credit ratings played a key role in pricing risk and attracting investors.[31][32] By the 1990s, agencies like Fitch intensified involvement in sovereign ratingsâ€”building on Moody's longstanding but initially limited practiceâ€”amid a boom in demand from investors navigating restructurings and new issuances. Over 100 sovereigns received ratings from at least one major agency by 2000, with Moody's covering 108 and S&P 83, reflecting a shift toward speculative-grade assessments for nearly 40% of rated emerging markets.[2] Events such as Argentina's 2001 default, where agencies had issued prior downgrades but faced scrutiny over timing, prompted refinements in monitoring, including more frequent reviews to enhance responsiveness to fiscal deteriorations.[2] Standardization efforts gained momentum with the International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO) issuing its Code of Conduct Fundamentals for Credit Rating Agencies in December 2004, establishing principles for independence, transparency, and quality to foster consistent global practices.[33] Post-2008 financial crisis, the European Union institutionalized ratings' role through regulations like the 2009 Credit Rating Agency Regulation, which required agencies to register and adhere to oversight, while embedding ratings in rules for investment eligibility and risk weighting in financial institutions, thereby endorsing their use despite aims to mitigate over-reliance.[34][35]

Methodologies and Criteria

Core Analytical Framework

The core analytical framework employed by major credit rating agencies for sovereign assessments emphasizes a systematic evaluation of a country's capacity and willingness to service external debt obligations, prioritizing empirical economic data and fiscal metrics over interpretive narratives. This approach structures analysis around foundational pillars: economic factors, including GDP per capita growth rates and external balances such as current account positions; fiscal indicators, centered on debt sustainability metrics like gross government debt relative to GDP and primary balance trajectories; and political-institutional elements, evaluating governance stability through measures of policy continuity, institutional strength, and rule-of-law indices derived from sources like the World Bank's Worldwide Governance Indicators.[36][37][2] Data collection forms the initial phase, drawing from verifiable sources including national statistical offices, central banks, and international bodies such as the International Monetary Fund and World Bank to compile time-series data on macroeconomic variables, often updated annually or more frequently during surveillance reviews. Key Quantitative and Qualitative Factors

Credit rating agencies assess sovereign creditworthiness through a combination of quantitative metrics and qualitative judgments, emphasizing empirical indicators of fiscal sustainability and external resilience. Quantitative factors prominently include the public debt-to-GDP ratio, where levels surpassing 100% often limit ratings to the lower investment-grade categories, particularly for emerging economies, due to heightened repayment pressures and reduced fiscal flexibility amid shocks.[39] [40] External vulnerabilities, such as sustained current account deficits exceeding 5% of GDP, signal accumulating foreign indebtedness that amplifies rollover risks and susceptibility to capital flight, thereby capping ratings unless offset by ample reserves or export competitiveness.[37] [5] Qualitative factors focus on institutional quality and governance effectiveness, with metrics like the World Bank's Rule of Law index serving as proxies for the capacity to enforce contracts, curb corruption, and maintain policy credibilityâ€”deficiencies in which empirically drive higher default probabilities by enabling fiscal imprudence and eroding investor confidence.[41] [42] Weak rule of law correlates with elevated default frequencies across historical episodes, as it undermines the causal mechanisms linking economic shocks to sovereign distress, such as inconsistent revenue mobilization or arbitrary debt management.[41] Agencies integrate these elements via proprietary scoring frameworks that weight quantitative thresholds against qualitative overlays, yielding forward-looking assessments calibrated to observed default outcomes. Historical data affirm this approach: cumulative default rates for investment-grade sovereigns (Aaa/AAA to Baa/BBB) remain below 5% over decades, escalating to approximately 24% for speculative-grade issuers, underscoring how intertwined fiscal metrics and institutional frailties predict repayment failures.[43][44]

Agency-Specific Variations

Standard & Poor's (S&P) sovereign rating methodology incorporates Sovereign Risk Indicators, which feature quantitative thresholds for metrics like GDP per capita to assess economic development and institutional capacity, with higher per capita levels correlating to elevated rating ceilings.[45][46] Moody's Investors Service, by contrast, emphasizes event riskâ€”such as geopolitical shocks or policy discontinuitiesâ€”and integrates systemic liquidity factors, including macroprudential buffers against capital and credit vulnerabilities, within its broader framework of expected loss rather than pure default probability.[37][47] Fitch Ratings focuses on cross-country comparability via its Sovereign Rating Model, a regression-based tool that calibrates scores linearly to the rating scale while balancing macroeconomic stability, fiscal dynamics, and external vulnerabilities.[48][49] These methodological divergences manifest in scale notations and forward-looking qualifiers: Moody's employs an alphanumeric system from Aaa (highest) to Caa3 (deep speculative), distinct from S&P and Fitch's AAA-to-D hierarchy, though standard mappings align equivalents across agencies (e.g., Moody's Aa1 â‰ˆ S&P AA+).[50] All three agencies append outlooksâ€”stable for no anticipated change, positive for upgrade potential, or negative for downgrade riskâ€”typically spanning 12-18 months, to signal trajectory adjustments without immediate scale shifts.[51] Empirical analyses of sovereign ratings reveal limited inter-agency divergence, with most discrepancies confined to one or two notches when agencies differ, and rare instances exceeding three notches; such variations stem from proprietary weighting of factors like qualitative event assessments versus quantitative macroeconomic thresholds, rather than evidence of systemic bias.[5][52] This congruence underscores the agencies' shared reliance on core driversâ€”debt sustainability, growth prospects, and institutional qualityâ€”despite nuanced emphases tailored to their analytical paradigms.[2]

Japan Credit Rating Agency

The Japan Credit Rating Agency (JCR), established on April 1, 1985, operates as a leading provider of credit ratings in Japan, with expertise in assessing credit risks particularly for yen-denominated debt issuances and issuers in the Asia-Pacific region.[80] [81] JCR's sovereign coverage includes approximately 25 countries, prioritizing those relevant to Japanese investors, such as Asian economies issuing bonds in Japanese yen, where it evaluates factors like foreign exchange risks, regional economic interdependencies, and liquidity in yen markets.[82] [83] JCR employs a long-term rating scale ranging from AAA (indicating the highest credit quality) to C (substantial credit risk), with modifiers (+ or -) for gradations within major categories, followed by D for outright default and SD for selective default on specific obligations.[84] [85] This scale reflects a methodology that incorporates conservative adjustments for uncertainty in emerging market exposures and currency mismatches, often resulting in ratings that emphasize structural vulnerabilities over short-term cyclical improvements.[86] For instance, JCR rates Japan at AAA with a stable outlook, attributing this to the sovereign's dominant position in global yen-denominated assets and institutional policy credibility, diverging from lower assessments by Western agencies due to JCR's focus on domestic fiscal sustainability metrics.[87] In Asia-Pacific sovereigns, JCR's ratings highlight differentiated risks; China receives AA-/Stable, reflecting robust external balances offset by leverage concerns, while Indonesia and India are both at BBB+/Stable, citing growth potential tempered by fiscal deficits and commodity dependencies.[82] The Philippines holds an A-/Stable rating as affirmed on June 5, 2025, based on resilient GDP expansion above 6% annually, low public debt-to-GDP ratios under 60%, and a stable financial sector with non-performing loans below 4%.[88] [89] These assessments underscore JCR's emphasis on yen bond affordability for regional borrowers, where ratings incorporate stress tests for JPY appreciation impacts on repayment capacity.[90]

Countries Without Ratings

Reasons for Lack of Coverage

Major credit rating agencies such as S&P Global Ratings, Moody's Investors Service, and Fitch Ratings provide sovereign assessments for approximately 139-143 countries as of early 2025, leaving an estimated 50-60 sovereign entities without formal ratings from these providers.[53][43] This lack of coverage stems primarily from the agencies' operational focus on entities with active participation in international capital markets, where sovereign debt issuance enables data collection and investor relevance. Countries without established external debt markets, such as numerous microstates and small island nations, often fall outside this scope because they issue little to no internationally traded sovereign bonds, rendering ratings commercially unviable for agencies that derive revenue from such analyses.[2] A key causal factor is the absence of sufficient economic scale or debt issuance, particularly for microstates like Andorra, Liechtenstein, and Monaco, which maintain fiscal stability through alternative revenue sources such as tourism and banking secrecy but do not access global bond markets, leading agencies to forgo coverage due to limited analytical demand. Similarly, resource constraints at rating agencies prioritize larger economies with measurable default risk exposure to international investors, sidelining smaller or niche sovereigns where data scarcity hampers rigorous assessment.[99] Geopolitical isolation and ongoing conflict further preclude ratings for entities like North Korea and Somalia, where closed economies, lack of transparent financial data, and absence of capital market engagement make credible evaluation impossible under standard methodologies. North Korea's hermetic regime restricts all external economic interactions, eliminating the prerequisites for sovereign debt analysis.[100] In Somalia's case, protracted civil strife and fragmented governance since the 1990s have prevented the emergence of a unified debt-issuing authority, resulting in no viable sovereign debt instruments for rating.[101] Sanctions and regulatory barriers can prompt outright withdrawal of existing ratings, as seen with Belarus, where Fitch Ratings terminated coverage in October 2023 amid Western sanctions imposed post-2022 that severed access to hard currency markets and triggered technical defaults, rendering ongoing monitoring untenable.[102] These factors collectively reflect agencies' causal prioritization of assessable default risk over comprehensive global enumeration, though critics argue this creates coverage gaps favoring developed markets.[103]

Implications for Unrated Sovereigns

Sovereign entities lacking formal credit ratings from major agencies face heightened borrowing costs, as investors perceive greater opacity and uncertainty, often pricing debt at spreads comparable to speculative-grade obligations. This unrated status signals elevated default risk in the absence of standardized assessments, compelling reliance on non-market financing such as bilateral loans, which typically carry explicit risk premia exceeding those available to rated peers. For instance, empirical analyses of emerging markets reveal that unrated countries incur effective yields 200-500 basis points higher than investment-grade equivalents due to this informational asymmetry, exacerbating fiscal strains and limiting fiscal space for development priorities.[17][104] The opacity inherent in unrated status constitutes a self-inflicted barrier, as governments may avoid scrutiny to evade potentially adverse ratings, yet this choice amplifies investor caution and restricts access to diversified, lower-cost international bond markets. Data from low- and middle-income countries demonstrate that initial rating acquisition facilitates a shift toward greater foreign currency bond issuance, reducing dependence on costlier domestic or concessional funding. Moreover, rated sovereigns experience enhanced capital inflows, with foreign direct investment inflows averaging 3 percentage points higher relative to GDP compared to unrated counterparts, underscoring the market discipline imposed by transparency.[105][17] To mitigate these implications, unrated sovereigns often pursue structural reforms aimed at securing coverage from rating agencies, as empirical correlations show that rating attainment expands market access and compresses spreads through improved credibility. This dynamic incentivizes policy adjustments in fiscal management and governance, though persistent avoidance perpetuates a cycle of elevated premia and vulnerability to ad hoc lending terms, such as those in state-to-state arrangements that embed geopolitical contingencies.[5][106]

Criticisms and Empirical Assessment

Allegations of Bias and Political Influence

Critics, including organizations focused on development advocacy, have alleged that major credit rating agencies (CRAs) systematically underrate developing countries, imposing higher borrowing costs through opaque methodologies that diverge from economic fundamentals. A 2025 UNCTAD policy review documented stark disparities, with developing countries disproportionately clustered in lower rating notches despite comparable improvements in fiscal metrics relative to historical benchmarks.[103] Academic analyses have similarly identified statistical biases, where ratings for low-income sovereigns deviate from model-implied levels after controlling for observables, potentially exacerbating debt vulnerabilities in regions like sub-Saharan Africa.[7] Such claims often attribute this to institutional preferences for advanced economies, though proponents note CRAs' reliance on qualitative judgments amid data scarcity in emerging markets. Countervailing evidence from econometric studies indicates that sovereign ratings are predominantly driven by verifiable fundamentals, with macroeconomic variablesâ€”such as per capita GDP, debt-to-GDP ratios, fiscal balances, and growth ratesâ€”accounting for 70-90% of rating variance in panel regressions across agencies and time periods.[5][107] For instance, higher default histories and institutional weaknesses in low-income nations causally underpin lower scores, as these factors elevate repayment risks independent of any alleged prejudice; regressions incorporating serial default probabilities and governance indicators further attenuate apparent divergences, suggesting ratings reflect causal risk premia rather than caprice. This alignment holds even post-adjustments for regional fixed effects, undermining narratives of exogenous bias. Allegations of political influence in sovereign ratings remain sporadic and unsubstantiated at scale, with CRAs facing regulatory fines totaling over $2 billion after the 2008 crisis primarily for corporate conflicts rather than sovereign politicization.[108] Sovereign methodologies explicitly weigh political risks like willingness to repay, but empirical reviews find no consistent favoritism toward ideologically aligned governments; for example, during the Eurozone crisis, agencies downgraded high-debt peripherals such as Greece (to selective default in 2012) and Italy despite multilateral bailouts and EU political pressure, adhering to fiscal sustainability thresholds over solidarity.[109] Post-crisis reforms, including Dodd-Frank oversight, have curtailed issuer-pays model abuses without evidencing systemic ideological skew, as ratings correlate more strongly with objective event risks than partisan cycles. Ratings' alignment with outcomes bolsters their credibility: in emerging markets, defaults and restructurings from 2023 to mid-2025â€”such as Zambia's completion-point under the G20 Common Framework in 2023 and Ghana's bondholder agreements in 2024â€”were overwhelmingly concentrated among speculative-grade sovereigns (below investment grade), with over 75% of such events preceding or coinciding with agency warnings based on liquidity strains and debt burdens.[110] This pattern validates lower ratings as forward indicators of verifiable perils, including commodity dependence and governance lapses, rather than artifacts of bias.[7]

Predictive Accuracy and Market Impact

Empirical analyses of sovereign credit ratings indicate a track record of reasonable predictive accuracy for defaults, outperforming simpler metrics like debt-to-GDP ratios in distinguishing defaulters from non-defaulters over horizons of one to five years, though with limitations in timeliness during rapid deteriorations.[111] Ratings exhibit low false positive rates, as investment-grade sovereigns rarely defaultâ€”historical data from 1970 to 2020 show zero defaults among AAA-rated issuers and fewer than 1% for A-rated onesâ€”reflecting effective calibration to low-probability events despite criticism of conservatism.[112] In the case of Argentina's 2001 default, agencies like S&P issued progressive downgrades throughout the year, reaching selective default status by December, providing signals of heightened risk that contrasted with delayed responses from multilateral lenders.[113][114] Sovereign rating downgrades exert measurable market impact, typically elevating secondary bond yields by 30 to 100 basis points in the immediate aftermath, with stronger effects in emerging markets where investor sentiment amplifies the signal.[115] This yield spike reflects repricing of default risk but often proves transient, as long-term evidence links sustained low ratings to fiscal discipline, prompting governments to implement reforms that stabilize debt trajectories and restore access to capital markets.[5] While downgrades can contribute to self-fulfilling prophecies through forced asset sales by rating-constrained investors, central bank interventionsâ€”such as liquidity provision during the European sovereign debt episodeâ€”have mitigated contagion, limiting broader spillover to non-affected sovereigns.[22] During the 2008 global financial crisis, credit rating agencies underestimated systemic risks in structured finance and corporate debt, leading to widespread downgrades after initial optimism, but sovereign ratings performed relatively better, with advanced economy issuers experiencing no unanticipated defaults and emerging market adjustments aligning more closely with ex-post outcomes than in private sectors.[116] Sovereign-focused assessments avoided the tranching errors plaguing subprime ratings, where over 90% of 2006 mortgage-backed securities rated investment-grade were eventually downgraded, highlighting differential resilience in government debt evaluation amid liquidity shocks.[117] Overall, while not infallible, ratings' integration of macroeconomic fundamentals has supported market pricing of sovereign risk more effectively than alternatives in stress periods.[112]

Evidence on Systemic Effects

Empirical analyses indicate that sovereign credit rating downgrades are associated with heightened growth-at-risk, particularly in downside scenarios, where the 5th percentile of four-quarter-ahead GDP growth forecasts declines by approximately 2.95 percentage points following a downgrade.[118] However, such effects primarily manifest as amplifications of underlying economic fragilities, with panel regressions showing reduced GDP impacts of around 1.11 percentage points when controlling for pre-existing conditions, suggesting ratings serve more as indicators than independent drivers.[119] Determinants of ratings, including fiscal balances, external balances, and GDP growth rates, underscore that causality predominantly flows from policy weaknesses and macroeconomic fundamentals to rating changes, rather than the reverse exerting causal primacy.[5][120] On the positive side, upgrades tied to structural reforms have demonstrably spurred investment and growth; for instance, Egypt's sovereign rating improvements in late 2024 and October 2025 by Fitch and S&P, respectively, followed the adoption of a flexible exchange rate in March 2024 and fiscal tightening, correlating with real GDP acceleration to 4.4% in fiscal year 2025 and rebounding private sector activity.[121][122][123] Globally, ratings exhibit strong alignment with debt sustainability indicators, as higher public debt levels predict weaker ratings and elevated downgrade probabilities, reflecting objective fiscal pressures rather than exogenous shocks.[124] Claims that ratings systematically perpetuate global inequality lack substantiation beyond their accurate reflection of governance and institutional disparities across countries; studies on capital flows to emerging markets find ratings influence private investment but do not evidence self-reinforcing cycles detached from underlying policy gaps, with market pricing often operating independently of agency pronouncements.[106][93] While subjective elements in rating methodologies may introduce minor biases against developing economies, empirical reviews confirm no robust proof of ratings causally widening inequality disparities unlinked to verifiable economic risks.[93]

Recent Trends and Changes

Notable Upgrades and Downgrades (2024-2025)

In 2024, S&P Global Ratings recorded 22 sovereign upgrades against 10 downgrades, the highest number of upgrades since 2007, reflecting improved fiscal management in select emerging economies amid persistent global debt pressures.[125] Emerging market sovereign actions remained net positive into 2025, with Fitch Ratings noting eight upgrades versus four downgrades through mid-year, attributed to stronger fundamentals like reduced inflation and external balances in reforming nations.[126] These shifts often followed causal fiscal reforms, such as expenditure cuts and currency liberalization, which bolstered debt sustainability despite broader risks from high interest rates. Argentina experienced multiple upgrades tied to President Javier Milei's austerity measures, including achieving a federal budget surplus for the first time in over a decade and slashing monthly inflation to 2.1% by September 2025.[127] Moody's raised the rating from 'Ca' to 'Caa3' on January 24, 2025, citing improved government finances and a record $18.9 billion trade surplus in 2024; a further upgrade followed on July 17, 2025, after easing foreign exchange controls and securing IMF support.[128][129] Fitch concurred, upgrading to 'CCC+' from 'CCC' on May 12, 2025, emphasizing deregulation's role in restoring market confidence.[64] Egypt's upgrades highlighted reform-driven external improvements, including a shift to flexible exchange rates in March 2024 that enhanced competitiveness and reserves. S&P Global elevated the rating to 'B' from 'B-' on October 10, 2025, while Fitch affirmed 'B' with a stable outlook the same day, both agencies crediting reduced financing needs and multilateral aid inflows.[130][121] Downgrades contrasted with these gains, underscoring fiscal vulnerabilities in advanced economies. Moody's downgraded the United States to 'Aa1' from 'Aaa' on May 16, 2025, pointing to rising deficits and interest costs eroding debt affordability.[131] For France, Moody's affirmed 'Aa3' but revised the outlook to negative on October 24, 2025, due to political gridlock hindering deficit reduction amid a debt-to-GDP ratio exceeding 110% and weakening business activity.[132] Cross-agency views diverged modestly, with S&P and Fitch maintaining stable outlooks for France post their own prior adjustments, reflecting debates over reform feasibility.[133]

Global Patterns and Future Outlook

Advanced economies have exhibited rating stability in 2025, supported by steady growth and easing inflation, while Latin America has sustained a positive credit cycle with upgrades outnumbering downgrades amid resilient economic performance and policy adjustments.[134][135] Emerging markets demonstrate resilience, with positive rating actions exceeding negative ones by a factor of 1.3 thus far in 2025, driven by capital inflows and structural reforms despite selective downgrades linked to commodity volatility and fiscal pressures.[136] This divergence reflects region-specific macro factors, including benign global financing conditions that have tempered broader deterioration. Looking to 2025-2026, major agencies project broadly supportive sovereign outlooks, with S&P Global anticipating sustained credit conditions amid steadying growth and more certain financing, though Moody's and Fitch highlight downside risks from trade policy shifts and tariffs.[137][138][139] Projections indicate moderate global GDP expansion, with Latin America at around 2.2% in 2025, bolstering rating stability in performing EMs, while advanced economies benefit from resilient labor markets and controlled debt trajectories.[134] Geopolitical tensions, including U.S. debt ceiling negotiations resolved in early 2025 without default, pose risks but have been mitigated by institutional mechanisms favoring orderly resolutions under benign monetary conditions.[140] Empirically, global sovereign default rates have declined to 0.9% in 2024 from 2.6% in 2023, with only three defaults recorded, correlating with enhanced rating stability and lower speculative-grade default probabilities as fiscal buffers strengthen.[141][125] This trend underscores causal links between improved macroeconomic fundamentals and reduced default incidence, though persistent uncertainties could elevate vulnerabilities in lower-rated sovereigns.[138]
