Czechoslovak New Wave

The Czechoslovak New Wave was a cinematic movement comprising films produced in Czechoslovakia from roughly 1963 to 1968 by a cohort of young directors who rejected dogmatic socialist realism in favor of experimental forms, ironic humanism, and subtle critiques of bureaucratic authoritarianism under communist rule.[1][2] Emerging amid de-Stalinization and a loosening of state controls on culture, the movement aligned with the political reforms of the Prague Spring, enabling works that probed everyday absurdities, personal alienation, and the stifling effects of collectivism through techniques like handheld camerawork, non-professional actors, and Dadaist whimsy.[3][4] Prominent figures included Miloš Forman, whose satirical comedies Loves of a Blonde (1965) and The Firemen's Ball (1967) exemplified the era's blend of farce and social observation, and Jiří Menzel, whose adaptation Closely Observed Trains (1966) earned the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film in 1968.[5][6] Other notables were Věra Chytilová's anarchic Daisies (1966) and Jan Němec's existential Diamonds of the Night (1964), which garnered festival praise for their formal daring and thematic depth.[7] The New Wave achieved breakthroughs in international distribution and awards, contrasting sharply with prior state-mandated propaganda cinema, but its momentum halted after the Warsaw Pact invasion of August 1968, which imposed "normalization" and prompted bans, exiles, and self-censorship among its creators.[3][2] ![A scene from The Firemen's Ball (1967), directed by Miloš Forman, exemplifying the New Wave's satirical style]center

Historical and Political Context

Post-Stalinist Thaw and Cinematic Preconditions

The 1953 monetary reform in Czechoslovakia, enacted on May 30, drastically devalued personal savings by exchanging old crowns at a 5:1 ratio for cash holdings above 3,000 crowns while limiting new account openings, rendering many citizens penniless and exacerbating economic hardships under the communist regime.[8] [9] This policy triggered the Plzeň uprising on June 1, 1953, where approximately 1,000 workers protested wage cuts and price hikes, clashing with security forces and highlighting deep public disillusionment with Stalinist economic orthodoxy.[9] The subsequent Soviet intervention in the 1956 Hungarian Revolution, which crushed reformist demands for autonomy, served as a cautionary signal to Czechoslovak leaders, prompting internal reflections on rigid Stalinism to avert analogous domestic unrest, though no immediate uprising materialized due to preemptive economic adjustments and security measures.[10] [11] These events collectively eroded faith in dogmatic socialist realism, creating societal preconditions for questioning state-mandated ideological conformity in cultural spheres. Khrushchev's February 1956 Secret Speech at the 20th Soviet Congress, which condemned Stalin's cult of personality and purges, initiated de-Stalinization across the bloc, with muted but eventual effects in Czechoslovakia where initial resistance delayed broader implementation until the late 1950s.[12] This erosion of mandatory propaganda enabled tentative deviations from socialist realism in cinema, as evidenced by director Karel Kachyňa's early post-Stalin works, which incorporated humanist portrayals of individual struggles subtly challenging regime glorification.[13] State-controlled studios like Barrandov, nationalized after the 1948 communist coup and focused on propaganda output during the early 1950s, began transitioning toward limited artistic experimentation by the late 1950s, reflecting the Thaw's gradual penetration despite persistent censorship.[14] The Film and TV School of the Academy of Performing Arts (FAMU), established in 1946, played a pivotal role by training a cohort of graduates who entered the industry between 1960 and 1963, injecting fresh, less doctrinaire approaches into production amid thawing constraints. Reforms in the late 1950s further facilitated this shift, with centralized planning yielding to selective increases in output quotas that prioritized quality over sheer propaganda volume, setting the stage for expanded creative input without yet fully dismantling state oversight.[15] This infrastructural evolution, driven by the causal interplay of economic disillusionment and ideological recalibration, provided the cinematic groundwork for subsequent innovations.

Emergence Amid 1960s Liberalization and Pre-Prague Spring Dynamics

The Czechoslovak New Wave emerged in the early 1960s as de-Stalinization policies under President Antonín Novotný gradually relaxed cultural controls, enabling filmmakers to produce works with greater artistic autonomy despite the regime's hardline communist framework.[5] Economic decentralization efforts, initiated around 1957 and intensifying in the early 1960s amid an industrial downturn, extended to the nationalized film industry, shifting production from rigid central planning toward more flexible studio operations at Barrandov and Koliba, which facilitated experimental shorts and features by FAMU graduates.[16] This structural shift, driven by pragmatic needs for efficiency rather than ideological softening, inadvertently allowed state-funded projects to explore individual experiences stifled by post-1948 bureaucratic collectivism, where centralized authority had prioritized propaganda over personal agency.[17] From 1963 to 1967, this environment yielded dozens of New Wave films, including Miloš Forman's Black Peter (1963), which critiqued workplace conformity and secured the Golden Leopard at the Locarno Film Festival, signaling international viability for domestically produced dissent.[18] State subsidies, while tying creators to regime oversight, served as a conduit for subtle anti-bureaucratic narratives, as suppressed personal initiative under decades of top-down control fostered a causal backlash in storytelling that privileged everyday absurdities over socialist realism.[2] Youth disillusionment, rooted in the 1948 communist coup's unfulfilled promises of equality and the ensuing Stalinist purges, further propelled this output, with young directors channeling generational frustration into films that humanized ordinary citizens against institutional inertia.[3] The Fourth Congress of the Union of Czechoslovak Writers in June 1967 amplified this momentum, as speakers like Pavel Kohout openly condemned Stalinist legacies and censorship, eroding Novotný's authority and spilling over into cinema by emboldening producers to greenlight riskier scripts ahead of Alexander Dubček's January 1968 ascension.[19] Festival successes, such as awards at Mannheim for early works like Jiří Menzel's adaptations, underscored the movement's growing confidence, though reliant on state apparatus that could revoke support at any perceived threat.[5] This pre-Prague Spring phase thus represented a precarious liberalization, where economic pragmatism and cultural ferment intersected to birth a cinema that, without fully escaping communist constraints, laid groundwork for broader reforms by validating individual critique within a system predicated on conformity.[20]

Stylistic and Thematic Features

Innovative Formal Techniques

The Czechoslovak New Wave marked a departure from socialist realism's emphasis on staged propaganda and heroic narratives through the adoption of cinéma vérité techniques, prioritizing unscripted authenticity in depicting everyday life. Filmmakers employed handheld camerawork, extended long takes, and non-professional actors to capture spontaneous interactions, as seen in Miloš Forman's Loves of a Blonde (1965), where factory workers and locals delivered improvised performances that revealed the banal absurdities of life under communism, eschewing didactic scripting for observational realism.[5][21] This approach contrasted sharply with the formulaic montages and composed scores of 1950s state-approved films, enabling a causal portrayal of individual agency amid systemic constraints.[5] Věra Chytilová's Daisies (1966) pushed formal experimentation further with surreal editing and color manipulations, using abrupt tint shifts, disjunctive montages, and rapid rhythmic cuts to fracture narrative continuity and evoke anarchic disruption. These techniques, including sound bridges linking disparate scenes, served as indirect responses to censorship, allowing veiled critiques through visual and auditory incoherence rather than overt exposition.[22] Such innovations reallocating resources from propagandistic elements to artistic provocation underscored the New Wave's commitment to empirical observation over ideological conformity.[5]

Recurrent Themes and Subtle Regime Critiques

Films of the Czechoslovak New Wave frequently depicted the absurdity inherent in everyday existence under communist rule, portraying mundane routines warped by ideological constraints that fostered individual alienation rather than communal harmony. This motif arose from the regime's emphasis on collectivism, which eroded personal agency and amplified petty absurdities, as seen in portrayals of routine tasks devolving into farce due to enforced conformity.[5][7] Such representations stemmed causally from the inefficiencies of centralized economic planning, which prioritized quotas over practical needs, leading to shortages and improvisational survival tactics that underscored human disconnection from authentic purpose.[23] Petty bureaucracy emerged as a recurrent indictment of administrative overreach, with depictions of officious routines and black market dealings highlighting the failures of central planning to deliver promised efficiencies. In Jiří Menzel's Closely Watched Trains (1966), the protagonist navigates a web of trivial regulations amid wartime logistics, mirroring post-war communist structures where similar bureaucratic inertia persisted, exacerbated by the 1950s Stalinist purges that instilled paranoia and stifled initiative among officials.[23][3] These purges, involving show trials and executions of thousands between 1948 and 1954, created a legacy of fear-driven compliance that perpetuated systemic rigidity, as evidenced by ongoing shortages requiring informal economies despite official rhetoric of abundance.[3] Gender roles and youth rebellion featured prominently as critiques of collectivist suppression, revealing how state-mandated equality masked persistent disparities in personal autonomy. Directors explored women's domestic burdens alongside workforce participation, countering propaganda of emancipation with realities of unequal labor division, where women in 1960s Czechoslovakia faced a gender wage gap exceeding 20% in many sectors despite high female employment rates around 70%.[24][25] Youth portrayals often depicted rebellion against paternalistic authority, attributing alienation to the regime's subsumption of individual aspirations under collective goals, which empirically failed to eradicate traditional hierarchies as promised.[23][26] While some works conveyed humanistic warmth through rural vignettes emphasizing interpersonal bonds amid simplicity, these served as counterpoints to urban regimentation, implicitly warning against utopian socialism's erosion of organic community ties. Rural depictions highlighted resilience in folk traditions, yet juxtaposed them with state intrusions that bred cynicism, prioritizing empirical observation of lived hardships over idealized collectivist narratives.[5][27] This balance underscored the New Wave's causal realism: regime policies, by overriding local incentives, amplified alienation without delivering equitable progress, as substantiated by persistent empirical gaps in social metrics.[24]

Key Figures and Regional Variations

Czech Directors and Signature Works

Miloš Forman, a graduate of the Film and TV School of the Academy of Performing Arts (FAMU) in Prague, emerged as a leading figure in the Czech New Wave with his deadpan satirical films critiquing incompetence and petty authority.[5] His debut feature Black Peter (1964) follows a young man's futile attempts at employment amid bureaucratic absurdities, employing non-professional actors and observational humor to highlight generational malaise.[28] Forman's subsequent works, including Loves of a Blonde (1965) and The Firemen's Ball (1967), continued this approach, with the latter depicting a chaotic rural festival as a microcosm of organizational failure and communal disarray, released just before the Prague Spring's end.[5] Between 1964 and 1967, Forman directed three features and several shorts, amassing a body of work that prompted his permanent emigration to the United States after the 1968 Soviet invasion, driven by political reprisals against New Wave filmmakers.[29] Jiří Menzel contributed to the Czech strain of the New Wave through literary adaptations infused with ironic detachment, focusing on wartime absurdities and personal quirks under occupation. His Closely Watched Trains (1966), adapted from Bohumil Hrabal's novella, portrays a bumbling railway apprentice's sexual awakening amid sabotage against Nazi transports, blending comedy with subtle anti-authoritarian undertones.[30] The film premiered at the 1966 Mannheim Film Festival and won the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film in 1968, marking the New Wave's early international breakthrough before suppression.[30] Menzel's style emphasized precise, understated satire on human frailty, distinguishing Czech urban-cosmopolitan critiques from Slovak tendencies toward lyrical rural introspection.[23] Věra Chytilová and Ivan Passer extended Czech New Wave absurdism into experimental urban terrains, prioritizing formal innovation and social subversion over narrative linearity. Chytilová's Daisies (1966) features two young women engaging in anarchic pranks and destruction, framed as a "philosophical farce" against consumerist norms and patriarchal order, utilizing fragmented editing, color manipulations, and collage techniques for disorienting effect.[31] Passer's Intimate Lighting (1965), his sole directorial feature before exile, observes two old friends—one a city musician, the other rural—reuniting in quiet discord, capturing the quiet absurdities of artistic ambition clashing with provincial life through long takes and ambient sound.[32] Both films underscore the Czech emphasis on cosmopolitan satire and existential irony, eschewing the poetic lyricism prevalent in Slovak New Wave productions.[5]

Slovak Directors and Distinct Approaches

Slovak filmmakers in the Czechoslovak New Wave prioritized poetic realism and introspective examinations of national identity, often rooted in rural folklore and ethnographic observation, providing a counterpoint to the Czech emphasis on urban absurdity and satire. Operating from Bratislava's Koliba studios—established in 1950 as a dedicated Slovak production hub—these directors benefited from a degree of regional autonomy within the centralized film industry, yielding a smaller but thematically cohesive body of work that grappled with Slovakia's distinct post-war experiences.[33][34] This output, while limited compared to the Czech Barrandov studios' volume, enabled critiques of ethnic marginalization and assimilation pressures under communist federalism, reflecting causal tensions from Slovakia's WWII-era puppet state legacy and subsequent reintegration into Czechoslovakia without full cultural parity.[34][35] Juraj Jakubisko exemplified this through surreal rural epics like The Deserter and the Nomads (1968), a triptych blending historical, contemporary, and futuristic war narratives to dissect cycles of violence and oppression via folk avant-gardism and ethereal imagery.[36][37] The film's stylistic experimentation—merging anarchic visuals with nomadic motifs—highlighted otherness and political alienation in Slovak contexts, diverging from state-sanctioned socialist realism by foregrounding ethnic disconnection rather than class unity.[36] Dušan Hanák's 322 (1969) adopted an ethnographic lens on working-class strife, centering a miner's confrontation with terminal cancer (coded as diagnosis "322") to allegorize both personal reckoning and broader societal decay under industrial hardship.[38] Banned shortly after premiere for its unflinching naturalism, the film critiqued the dehumanizing toll of labor in eastern Slovakia's mines, using raw, observational techniques to expose systemic failures over idealized proletarian harmony.[38] Elo Havetta infused documentary influences into poetic narratives, as in Celebration in the Botanical Garden (1969), which drew on naïve art, impressionism, and Western Slovak folk rituals for a mosaic, anarchic portrayal of communal absurdity and rural vitality.[39][36] His approach, marked by fragmented, observational framing akin to cinéma vérité, evoked post-WWII dislocations while resisting romanticized ethnic fusion narratives prevalent in official propaganda.[40] These emphases on Slovak particularity—evident in motifs of ethnic pride and autonomy amid federal constraints—drew occasional critiques of indulgent romantic nationalism, yet grounded causal analyses of historical marginalization, such as resource disparities and cultural suppression post-1945 nationalization, over narratives of seamless socialist integration.[34][35] Linguistic barriers further curtailed global reach, confining recognition largely to domestic or Eastern Bloc circuits despite artistic merits paralleling Czech innovations.[39]

Reception, Controversies, and Suppression

Initial Domestic and International Responses

The Czechoslovak New Wave films elicited positive international responses in the mid-1960s, with several entries securing awards and acclaim at major festivals. Miloš Forman's Loves of a Blonde (1965) achieved international recognition following its release, contributing to the movement's growing reputation abroad.[41] Forman's works, including early features, earned honors at the Berlin International Film Festival, underscoring the stylistic innovation's appeal beyond Eastern Europe.[42] These successes highlighted the films' export potential, generating revenue that supported the domestic industry during a period of tentative liberalization.[5] Domestically, the New Wave enjoyed initial popularity among urban youth audiences, contrasting with official preferences for socialist realism. Films drew substantial viewership, reflecting resonance with everyday absurdities under the regime rather than propagandistic narratives.[7] Attendance patterns indicated stronger embrace in cities over rural areas, where traditional values aligned more closely with state-sanctioned art.[2] Official reactions in party organs like Rudé právo revealed elite skepticism, with critiques accusing select films of excessive formalism or bourgeois tendencies, even as production proceeded under state studios.[43] Such tolerance appeared pragmatic, leveraging cultural output for prestige amid the post-Stalin thaw, without endorsing ideological deviation.[44] Debates pitted defenders of artistic freedom against hardliners decrying decadence, yet no widespread bans occurred prior to 1968.[20]

Political Backlash and 1968 Soviet Intervention

The liberalization of the Prague Spring enabled widespread uncensored public screenings of Czechoslovak New Wave films, which exposed bureaucratic absurdities and social hypocrisies through empirical depictions of everyday life under communism, challenging the regime's enforced ideological uniformity.[18] These screenings, part of broader cultural reforms under Alexander Dubček's "socialism with a human face," amplified critiques implicit in works like those probing Stalinist legacies and state paternalism, prompting Soviet concerns over ideological contamination across the Warsaw Pact.[2] Declassified Soviet documents reveal that Moscow perceived such artistic expressions as symptomatic of a broader deviation from collectivist orthodoxy, necessitating intervention to restore centralized control and suppress deviations that prioritized individual observation over party dogma.[45] On August 21, 1968, Warsaw Pact forces—primarily Soviet, with contingents from Poland, Hungary, Bulgaria, and East Germany—invaded Czechoslovakia, deploying over 500,000 troops to crush the reforms.[46] The operation resulted in 137 Czechoslovak deaths during the initial incursion, alongside hundreds wounded, as tanks rolled into Prague and other cities to enforce compliance.[47] This military action directly halted the Prague Spring's cultural thaw, framing the New Wave's truth-oriented narratives as threats to the bloc's monolithic structure, where creative liberty was subordinated to preserving the illusion of socialist harmony over verifiable causal realities of repression. In the ensuing "normalization" under Gustáv Husák, the regime imposed immediate bans on unfinished 1968 film releases and retroactively withdrew dozens of New Wave titles from circulation, purging studios like Barrandov of reformist personnel.[48] Film production plummeted, with annual output falling by approximately 70% from pre-invasion levels as creative teams were replaced by ideologically compliant ones, enforcing self-censorship to align with Soviet-prescribed realism.[49] This crackdown underscored the invasion's role in reimposing collectivist conformity, dismantling the New Wave's capacity for unvarnished empirical inquiry and revealing the fragility of liberalization attempts within a system predicated on doctrinal absolutism rather than adaptive governance.[50]

Debates on Artistic Integrity and State Complicity

The Czechoslovak New Wave filmmakers operated within a state-controlled industry, where production relied entirely on subsidies from communist-era studios such as Barrandov and the state film fund, raising persistent questions about the autonomy of their artistic output.[51] During the pre-1968 liberalization, this funding enabled works with implicit critiques of bureaucracy and socialism, but it also bound creators to party oversight, with scripts requiring approval from the Ministry of Culture and Information.[5] Post-invasion in August 1968, the normalization regime intensified scrutiny, banning dozens of New Wave films and purging dissident personnel; while leading figures like Miloš Forman and Ivan Passer opted for exile to the West—Forman departing permanently in late 1968 to pursue independent projects in Hollywood—others navigated survival by producing ideologically compliant features, such as horror or historical dramas that avoided direct confrontation.[52] Věra Chytilová, for instance, endured a seven-year ban after refusing initial demands for recantation but resumed work in 1975 under restricted conditions, highlighting varied responses from principled resistance to pragmatic adaptation.[53] Debates over whether the New Wave constituted genuine dissent or a regime-tolerated "safety valve" persist, with proponents emphasizing how state support paradoxically facilitated subtle exposures of systemic absurdities during the Prague Spring thaw, allowing films to circulate domestically and gain international acclaim without immediate reprisal.[51] Critics, however, argue that dependency on subsidies inherently compromised integrity, as filmmakers' careers originated in mandatory state apprenticeships and short films often aligned with socialist realism before evolving toward irony; this view posits the movement as a controlled outlet that vented frustrations without challenging core power structures, evidenced by the regime's selective tolerance until overt threats emerged, like Jan Němec's Report on the Party and the Guests (1966), which provoked pre-invasion backlash.[7] Revisionist analyses further contend that stylistic relativism in works—such as fragmented narratives undermining absolute truths—mirrored postmodern tendencies that diluted causal accountability toward the regime, framing the era less as a heroic pinnacle and more as an early erosion of modernist clarity under ideological pressure.[20] These controversies underscore a spectrum of complicity, from exiles who rejected normalization outright to those who, by continuing under censorship, sustained cinema's existence but at the cost of bolder expression; archival records from the Czech Film Institute reveal that while approximately half of prominent New Wave directors faced emigration or bans, a notable subset produced over 100 "normalized" features between 1969 and 1989 by self-censoring themes.[5] Such adaptations, while enabling personal continuity, fueled accusations of enabling the regime's cultural monopoly, contrasting with dissidents' stands that prioritized uncompromised vision over institutional survival.[41]

Legacy and Enduring Influence

Impact on Global Cinema and Dissident Filmmaking

The emigration of key figures like Miloš Forman to the United States after the 1968 Soviet invasion facilitated the Czechoslovak New Wave's stylistic influence on American independent cinema, particularly through Forman's adoption of naturalistic acting, improvisational techniques, and satirical portrayals of institutional absurdities in films such as One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest (1975), which won five Academy Awards including Best Director and Best Picture.[18][54] Forman's earlier Czech works, like Loves of a Blonde (1965), informed this approach, blending documentary realism with fiction to critique social conformity, elements echoed in 1970s U.S. indie productions emphasizing amateur casts and location shooting over polished narratives.[55] Internationally, the movement's films garnered measurable acclaim that boosted Central European cinema's visibility, with Jiří Menzel's Closely Watched Trains (1966) securing the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film in 1967 and Forman's The Firemen's Ball (1967) receiving a Cannes nomination, contributing to over 20 Czechoslovak entries in major Western festivals between 1963 and 1968.[5] This export success, however, often prioritized Czech directors in Western discourse, sidelining Slovak contributions such as Dušan Hanák's rural realism in The Roses of Gruňa (1970), leading to an imbalanced perception that undervalued the federal duality of the New Wave's output.[20] In Eastern Europe, the New Wave served as a template for dissident filmmaking under intensified post-1968 "normalization," inspiring subtle, allegorical critiques in underground or semi-official works across the Soviet bloc, including parallels in Poland's Cinema of Moral Concern (1976–1981), where directors like Krzysztof Kieślowski explored ethical compromises in communist society using restrained, observational styles akin to Forman and Věra Chytilová's methods.[56][57] Similar samizdat-inspired aesthetics—low-budget, non-professional performers, and encoded regime satire—emerged in Yugoslav Black Wave films post-1968 and limited Estonian productions, adapting the Czech model's evasion of censorship to local totalitarian contexts without direct institutional support.[5] This influence underscored the New Wave's role in sustaining oppositional art forms, though its pre-invasion visibility waned under bloc-wide suppression, limiting empirical cross-pollination data.[2]

Modern Reassessments and Archival Revivals

In the 2010s and 2020s, archival revivals of Czechoslovak New Wave films have sustained scholarly and public interest through high-quality restorations and festival screenings. The Criterion Collection premiered new restorations of key titles at events including the 2019 Karlovy Vary International Film Festival, highlighting ongoing efforts to preserve and exhibit works suppressed during the normalization era.[18] Similarly, the festival's 2024 edition featured screenings of classics like Joseph Kilian (1963), underscoring the movement's archival accessibility amid broader retrospectives on Czech cinema.[58] These initiatives, coupled with streaming availability on platforms like the Criterion Channel, demonstrate persistent viewership, as evidenced by dedicated programming spotlights that draw audiences to the era's experimental aesthetics and social critiques.[59] Post-1989 scholarship has reevaluated the New Wave's legacy, balancing traditional acclaim for its artistic innovations with analyses of its structural constraints under state socialism. Peter Hames's second edition of The Czechoslovak New Wave (2005), updated to incorporate perspectives from the post-communist period, examines how the movement's temporary flourishing during the 1960s thaw reflected both creative potential and inherent regime dependencies, such as state-funded production that later enabled suppression.[1] Revisionist critiques, such as a 2019 thesis by an independent researcher, challenge the "golden sixties" canon by arguing that the New Wave inadvertently presaged broader cultural declines into postmodern relativism, prioritizing stylistic experimentation over substantive resistance to statist pathologies.[20] This view contrasts with prevailing academic narratives, which often underemphasize how the Velvet Revolution of 1989 empirically validated the films' implicit prophecies of bureaucratic inefficiency and authoritarian overreach, as the regime's collapse freed filmmakers from subsidized conformity. Contemporary reassessments emphasize the New Wave's cautionary insights into censorship's long-term deterrence of innovation, particularly as parallels emerge with modern authoritarian controls on expression. While some post-1989 Czech media exhibit "ostalgia" for the thaw's perceived cultural vibrancy—evident in films romanticizing normalization-era life—causal analysis reveals the regime's net suppressive impact, where initial liberalizations masked systemic incentives against dissent that stifled sustained creativity until market-driven freedoms post-1989.[60] Archival revivals thus serve not as nostalgic endorsements of socialist experimentation but as empirical reminders that state oversight, even in "thaw" phases, correlates with artistic curtailment, a lesson resonant amid global debates on institutional biases favoring collectivist models over individual agency. Revisionist scholarship prioritizes this evidence over uncritical hagiography, noting academia's tendency to overstate the era's autonomy given funding ties to party apparatus.[20]
