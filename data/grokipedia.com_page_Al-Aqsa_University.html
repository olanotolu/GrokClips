Al-Aqsa University

Al-Aqsa University is a public Palestinian university in the Gaza Strip with campuses in Gaza City and Khan Younis, originally established in 1955 as a teachers' academy and later expanded into a full university offering degrees across multiple disciplines.[1][2] Prior to the escalation of conflict in October 2023, it enrolled around 22,000 students in ten faculties, including applied sciences, engineering, medicine, and sharia and law, positioning it as the largest university in the Gaza Strip and a leader in local scientific research output.[3][4] The institution has faced significant challenges, including infrastructure destruction from Israeli military operations in late 2023 and early 2024, which damaged or obliterated key buildings and disrupted operations, contributing to broader claims of systematic targeting of Gaza's higher education sector amid the Israel-Hamas war.[5][6] Additionally, political tensions between Hamas authorities in Gaza and the Palestinian Authority in the West Bank have led to accreditation disputes, causing enrollment declines and operational hurdles independent of external conflict.[7] Despite these adversities, the university has adapted through remote learning initiatives and international collaborations to sustain education and research efforts.[8]

History

Establishment and Early Development

Al-Aqsa University traces its origins to 1955, when it was founded as a teachers' institute in Gaza City under the administration of the Egyptian government, which controlled the Gaza Strip following the 1948 Arab-Israeli War.[9][10] The institute's primary mandate was to train and qualify educators for primary, secondary, and higher levels, addressing the acute shortage of qualified teachers in the region amid post-war displacement and educational disruptions.[9] Initial operations focused on basic pedagogical training, with classes likely held in modest facilities given the era's resource constraints under Egyptian oversight.[11] Following Israel's occupation of Gaza in 1967, the institute continued functioning, albeit under restricted conditions that limited expansion and access to advanced resources.[10] It persisted as a key provider of teacher education, graduating cohorts essential for maintaining local schooling systems despite intermittent closures and infrastructural challenges imposed by the military administration.[9] By the late 1980s, growing demand for higher education in Gaza prompted internal reforms, culminating in 1991 when the institution was restructured as the Governmental College of Education.[12] This upgrade expanded its curriculum to include broader scientific departments and educational programs, enabling the awarding of bachelor's degrees in education and initiating joint postgraduate initiatives, such as a doctorate program with Egypt's Ain Shams University.[9] These early developments positioned the college as Gaza's pioneering public higher education entity, emphasizing practical teacher preparation over theoretical research due to regional geopolitical instability and funding limitations from Egyptian and later Palestinian authorities.[9] Enrollment remained modest in the pre-1991 phase, serving primarily local Palestinians and fostering a foundation for subsequent academic growth amid the First Intifada's disruptions.[10]

Expansion Under Occupation and Post-Oslo Era

In 1991, amid the Israeli occupation of Gaza since 1967, the teachers' institute evolved into the Governmental College of Education, marking the beginning of structured expansion in academic programs, scientific departments, faculty, and student body.[10][9] This development enabled the college to offer bachelor's degrees in education and, through partnerships such as with Ain Shams University in Egypt, postgraduate programs up to the doctorate level, despite operational challenges from the occupation.[9] The Oslo Accords of 1993 and the subsequent establishment of the Palestinian Authority in 1994 provided a framework for limited autonomy in education, coinciding with relative stability in Palestinian higher education from 1994 to 1999.[13] Under this period, the college enhanced its facilities to accommodate growing demand for higher education, reflecting broader increases in student enrollment across Gaza's institutions as access expanded.[10] By 2001, the institution was officially upgraded to Al-Aqsa Universityâ€”the first public university in Gazaâ€”through a decree by Yasser Arafat on September 21, formalizing its transition and enabling further diversification of faculties and research capacities.[10] This expansion occurred under persistent occupation constraints, including movement restrictions and resource limitations, yet positioned the university as a key public higher education provider before the Second Intifada disrupted progress in 2000.[10][13]

Operations Under Hamas Governance

Since Hamas seized control of the Gaza Strip in June 2007, Al-Aqsa University has functioned under the oversight of the Hamas-led de facto government, which manages public higher education institutions through its Ministry of Education and Higher Education. This governance structure has integrated the university into Hamas's administrative framework, with the group providing funding, policy direction, and public representation on institutional matters, such as reporting damages from Israeli military actions in January 2024.[14] Operations have emphasized continuity of academic programs despite economic isolation and periodic conflicts, including virtual classes amid infrastructure disruptions as recently as July 2024.[15] Hamas has asserted dominance over campus politics and student life, mirroring patterns in other Gaza universities where the group controls student unions for ideological mobilization and recruitment. At institutions like the Islamic University of Gaza, Hamas-affiliated Islamic groups established committees for propaganda, cultural events, and distribution of materials promoting resistance and Islamist values since the 1980s, a model extended across the sector under Hamas rule.[16] In 2008, Hamas supporters raided Al-Azhar University in Gazaâ€”another public institutionâ€”to disrupt Fatah-aligned activities and enforce compliance, demonstrating the group's use of force to consolidate control over higher education environments.[17] Under this governance, the university's curriculum and activities reflect Hamas's emphasis on Islamic principles alongside professional training, though academic operations have prioritized regime-aligned narratives over unfettered inquiry. Broader Palestinian educational frameworks under Hamas influence, including online curricula implemented during conflicts, have incorporated content glorifying violence and martyrdom, consistent with critiques of systemic indoctrination in Gaza's schools that likely extends to university-level discourse.[18] Faculty and students have navigated a politicized atmosphere, with reported suppression of dissent and integration of resistance-themed events into campus life, though specific enforcement at Al-Aqsa remains less documented than at more overtly Islamist institutions.[16]

Academic Structure and Programs

Faculties and Degree Offerings

Al-Aqsa University maintains ten faculties encompassing a range of disciplines, primarily offering bachelor's degrees alongside select master's and doctoral programs.[19] The institution provides 76 bachelor's programs, 26 master's programs, and limited doctoral offerings, with a focus on fields essential to regional needs such as education, sciences, and media.[12] It is the sole university in the Gaza Strip granting bachelor's degrees in media, fine arts, physical education, and sports.[20] Key faculties and their primary degree offerings include:
  • Faculty of Education: Established as one of the core units, it delivers 18 bachelor's programs in areas like teaching methods, general education, psychology, and educational supervision, plus three master's programs and PhD options in education-related fields.[21][22][23]
  • Faculty of Applied Sciences: Founded in 1991, this faculty covers natural sciences including biology, chemistry, physics, and mathematics, with bachelor's degrees emphasizing laboratory-based training.[24]
  • Faculty of Arts and Human Sciences: Programs focus on humanities, languages (including French), history, and social sciences at the bachelor's level.[3]
  • Faculty of Media: Unique to the region, it offers bachelor's degrees in journalism, public relations, and related communication fields.[20]
  • Faculty of Fine Arts: Provides specialized bachelor's training in visual arts, design, and creative disciplines, distinguishing it among Gaza institutions.[20]
  • Faculty of Physical Education and Sports: Delivers bachelor's programs in sports science, coaching, and physical training, with no equivalents elsewhere in Gaza.[25][20]
  • Faculty of Administrative and Financial Sciences (also termed Management or Business Administration): Bachelor's degrees in accounting, business administration, and economics.[26]
  • Faculty of Computer and Information Technology: Introduced for the 2018â€“2019 academic year, it offers bachelor's programs in computer science and information systems, including a master's in cyber-physical systems.[27][28]
  • Faculty of Sharia and Islamic Studies: Focuses on Islamic law, theology, and related bachelor's programs.[19]
  • Faculty of Medical Sciences: Provides bachelor's degrees in health-related fields such as nursing and pharmacy.[25]
Graduate programs remain limited, concentrated in education and technology, reflecting resource constraints under ongoing regional challenges.[19] Additionally, the Al-Aqsa Community College offers intermediate studies as a supplementary arm.[19]

Enrollment and Student Demographics

Prior to the military conflict escalating in October 2023, Al-Aqsa University maintained an enrollment of approximately 27,000 students across its Gaza City and Khan Younis campuses, with the majority pursuing undergraduate degrees.[19][3] This figure positioned it as one of the largest higher education institutions in the Gaza Strip, serving a student body almost entirely drawn from local Palestinian Arab residents, reflecting the demographic composition of the region where over 99% of the population identifies as Sunni Muslim.[19] Student demographics mirrored regional patterns in Palestinian higher education, with females comprising roughly 60% of enrollees, a ratio driven by higher female participation rates in tertiary studies amid cultural emphases on education for both genders.[29][30] International students numbered fewer than 1% of the total, primarily from neighboring Arab countries, underscoring the university's localized focus.[3] Following the October 2023 conflict, enrollment plummeted due to campus destruction, faculty and student casualties, and displacement, with up to 90,000 Gaza Strip higher education studentsâ€”including a significant portion from Al-Aqsaâ€”losing access to in-person learning for over two years.[31][32] Limited virtual instruction resumed in mid-2024 amid ongoing hostilities and unreliable infrastructure, though precise current figures remain unavailable owing to the breakdown in administrative operations.[15]

Governance and Administration

Leadership and Internal Organization

Al-Aqsa University operates under a hierarchical administrative structure typical of Palestinian public universities, with a president at the apex overseeing academic, financial, and operational affairs, supported by vice presidents and a university council.[33] As of 2025, the president is Dr. Ayman Sobh, a professor who has emphasized the institution's role in scientific research amid ongoing challenges in Gaza.[8] [34] The university council provides advisory and decision-making input on policy, though specific membership details are not publicly detailed in recent records.[35] Vice presidents handle specialized portfolios, such as academic affairs and administrative operations; for instance, Dr. Faiq Al-Naouq served as vice president until his death in an Israeli military strike on April 25, 2025, during which he was also identified by Israeli authorities as a senior Hamas military commander involved in weapons development and recruitment.[36] Administrative deanships include those for planning, development, quality assurance, and financial affairs, which manage budgeting, staff coordination, and compliance with Palestinian higher education regulations.[37] [38] These units report to the president and support the university's approximately 1,400 employees, including 300 faculty members. Political Oversight and Funding Sources

Al-Aqsa University functions under the de facto political oversight of the Hamas administration in Gaza, which seized control of the territory in 2007 and exercises authority over educational institutions through its Ministry of Education and Higher Education. This oversight includes regulatory approval of programs, administrative appointments, and operational directives, as evidenced by Hamas officials' public statements on university activities during conflicts.[14] Concurrently, the Palestinian Authority's (PA) Ministry of Higher Education in Ramallah asserts nominal jurisdiction as the governmental body that elevated the institution to university status in 2000, leading to disputes over accreditation, salary disbursements, and compliance.[10][42] These tensions manifested acutely in 2016, when the PA attempted to seize salaries of Hamas-affiliated professors and exempt students from fees, only for the Hamas-controlled Gaza ministry to disregard the measures, exacerbating a rift that has stalled higher education coordination.[10][7] Funding sources for the university predominantly consist of student tuition fees, which form a core revenue stream amid limited alternatives, supplemented by allocations from the PA's Ministry of Higher Educationâ€”though these have faced deductions and delays since 2017 as leverage against Hamas governance.[43] The PA's policy of partial salary payments to Gaza civil servants, including academics, continues as a fiscal lifeline estimated at hundreds of millions annually across sectors, but political feuds have prompted targeted withholdings for perceived Hamas loyalists.[10] International aid, including project-specific grants from donors like Qatar and European partners, provides ad hoc support through the university's Projects and Resources Development Unit, which prioritizes developmental initiatives but remains vulnerable to blockade restrictions and conflict disruptions.[44][8]

Damage and Destruction from Military Conflicts

During the 2008â€“2009 Gaza War, Al-Aqsa University in Gaza City sustained damage from Israeli airstrikes targeting militant infrastructure in the area.[11] The 2014 Gaza conflict inflicted additional structural harm on campus buildings through similar aerial operations.[8] The 2023â€“2025 Israel-Hamas war caused far more extensive destruction. Initial Israeli bombing damaged parts of the campus in early November 2023.[45] On January 22, 2024, Israeli forces shelled the Gaza City site while it served as a shelter for displaced civilians, according to on-site reports.[5] By February 6, 2024, the Israeli military demolished two university buildings via controlled explosion, following occupation of the grounds.[46] University officials reported that Israeli troops had used the facility as a temporary military base and detention center prior to its demolition.[47] Further incidents included Israeli soldiers setting fire to remnants of the university library in Gaza City during mid-2024 operations, as documented in eyewitness accounts and media footage.[48] By mid-2024, assessments indicated near-total devastation of Al-Aqsa's infrastructure, consistent with broader patterns of higher education targeting in Gaza where Israeli forces cited militant exploitation of civilian sites, though specific justifications for Al-Aqsa strikes were not publicly detailed by the IDF.[5][49] Palestinian and international monitoring groups, including those with potential sympathies toward Gaza authorities, have described these actions as systematic attacks on educational assets, while Israeli policy emphasizes strikes on dual-use locations housing Hamas activities.[5]

Educational Impact and Achievements

Contributions to Gaza's Higher Education

Al-Aqsa University originated as a teachers' institute in 1955 under Egyptian administration in Gaza, evolving into a full university by 1991 through the expansion of its academic offerings from teacher training to broader higher education programs. This development addressed the acute shortage of local educational infrastructure in the Gaza Strip, where access to universities was historically limited by geopolitical constraints and reliance on institutions in the West Bank or abroad. By providing on-site degree programs, the university enabled thousands of Palestinians to pursue undergraduate studies without displacement, fostering a cadre of educators and professionals essential for regional self-sufficiency.[12] The institution pioneered graduate-level education in Gaza, introducing Master's and Doctorate programs to meet community demands for advanced qualifications in areas such as education management, Arabic language, psychological counseling, and chemistry. These programs, offered at costs 40% lower than comparable offerings at other Palestinian universities, included joint initiatives with institutions like Ain Shams University in Egypt to produce qualified academics. Through such efforts, Al-Aqsa University supported the professionalization of fields critical to Gaza's public sector, including teaching and counseling, while maintaining affordability amid economic pressures.[50] Prior to October 2023, Al-Aqsa University formed part of Gaza's core higher education network alongside Al-Azhar University and the Islamic University, collectively serving about 65,000 studentsâ€”or 72% of the Strip's approximately 90,000 university enrolleesâ€”and contributing to the graduation of professionals in unique disciplines like media, arts, and physical education, where it held a monopoly among local institutions. It further advanced knowledge dissemination by publishing biannual scientific journals in pure/applied sciences and humanities/education, indexed internationally via ISSN, and providing resources for researchers including funding and conference participation. These outputs enhanced Gaza's limited research capacity, enabling evidence-based contributions to local challenges despite funding constraints from Hamas-led governance and international restrictions.[51][20][50]

Research Output and Notable Alumni

Al-Aqsa University's research output has been characterized by modest productivity, with approximately 326 scholarly publications attributed to its affiliated authors, garnering around 3,658 citations as of recent assessments.[52] Contributions span fields such as computer science, chemistry, and education, including studies on potentiometric titration methods and artificial intelligence applications.[52] In chemistry specifically, the university is associated with 363 publications and 4,379 citations, positioning it as a contributor within Palestinian academic rankings, though global impact remains limited due to regional constraints like resource scarcity and recurrent conflicts.[53] The institution maintains its own journals, such as the Al-Aqsa University Journal (Humanities Series), which publish local research on topics including economics, social sciences, and applied technologies.[54] Despite these efforts, output has been hampered by the Gaza blockade and military disruptions, with recent wartime conditions severely restricting laboratory access and data collection, yet researchers have demonstrated persistence in remote and collaborative work.[6] Notable alumni include Anas al-Sharif, who earned a bachelor's degree in journalism from the Faculty of Media and later served as an Al Jazeera correspondent covering Gaza events until his death in an Israeli airstrike on August 11, 2025.[55] Many graduates contribute to regional media, education, and public service, though international prominence is rare amid the enclave's isolation and security challenges.[56]

Controversies and Criticisms

Alleged Ties to Militant Groups

Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) have alleged that senior administrative figures at Al-Aqsa University maintain operational ties to Hamas, the militant group governing Gaza since 2007. In April 2025, the IDF conducted an airstrike eliminating Dr. Faiq al-Naouq, the university's vice president and a PhD holder in mathematics, whom Israeli intelligence identified as a senior Hamas commander involved in coordinating terrorist activities.[36] Hamas's Al-Qassam Brigades, its military wing, subsequently praised al-Naouq for his "contributions to the resistance," corroborating his affiliation with the group despite university statements portraying him primarily as an academic administrator.[57] Broader allegations from Israeli sources claim Hamas exploits Gaza universities, including Al-Aqsa, for fundraising, recruitment, and logistical support, though specific evidence for Al-Aqsa beyond leadership overlaps remains limited in public records. The U.S. State Department has documented Hamas interference in Gaza's higher education sector, noting that universities operate under the de facto authority of Hamas, which restricts academic freedom and influences curricula, faculty appointments, and student activities to align with militant ideologies.[58][59] This control stems from Hamas's monopoly on governance in Gaza, enabling infiltration of public institutions without formal ownership.[58] No peer-reviewed studies or independent verifications have quantified the extent of militant embedding at Al-Aqsa specifically, but the pattern mirrors reports on other Gaza institutions like the Islamic University, where Hamas has maintained command tunnels, weapon storage, and training sites beneath campuses.[60] Palestinian sources, including university officials, have denied systematic militant use of facilities, attributing infrastructure damage to indiscriminate strikes rather than targeted counterterrorism operations.[49] These conflicting accounts highlight challenges in source credibility, with Israeli claims grounded in intelligence operations amid ongoing conflict, while Gaza-based reports occur under Hamas oversight, potentially incentivizing minimization of ties to avoid reprisals or international scrutiny.

Curriculum Bias and Indoctrination Claims

Critics have alleged that Al-Aqsa University's curriculum and teaching practices reflect ideological bias under Hamas governance, which has controlled Gaza since 2007, enforcing content that promotes antisemitic tropes, glorifies resistance against Israel, and limits academic freedom to align with Islamist narratives.[61] In a 2013 incident, faculty member Dr. Khoder Mahjez faced an administrative investigation at the university for discussing a short story titled "Love in War" in his "Literature and Modern Criticism" course, where he used terms like "Jews" rather than prescribed ideological slurs such as "sons of monkeys" or "pigs," prompting complaints from students and threats to his reputation from university officials.[62] Mahjez subsequently resigned from full-time duties, highlighting claims of censorship that prioritizes indoctrination over creative or neutral literary analysis.[61] Faculty statements broadcast on Hamas-affiliated media have fueled accusations of embedding antisemitic and jihadist indoctrination in university instruction. For instance, Al-Aqsa professor Abdul Samee' Al-'Arabeed, a specialist in Quranic studies, appeared on Al-Aqsa TV in 2010, asserting that "the Jews are behind every conspiracy" and describing them as "human garbage" based on Quranic interpretations, remarks critics interpret as integrating conspiratorial antisemitism into religious and academic discourse.[63] Similarly, Sheikh Ibrahim Madhi, who lectured on ecology and water at Al-Aqsa, delivered sermons on Palestinian television calling for the killing of Jews as part of Islamic duty, blending academic roles with calls for violence that allegedly permeate campus teachings.[64] These examples, documented by monitoring organizations, suggest a pattern where curriculum delivery enforces Hamas-aligned ideology, though defenders attribute such rhetoric to broader resistance narratives rather than systemic bias.[64] Broader analyses of Gaza's higher education claim that universities like Al-Aqsa, lacking independent oversight, replicate the incitement found in Hamas-controlled K-12 curricula, which glorify martyrdom and delegitimize Israel, potentially extending to professional training in fields like engineering and education.[65] However, specific peer-reviewed audits of Al-Aqsa's syllabi remain limited, with most evidence drawn from faculty outputs and administrative actions rather than textbook analyses, underscoring challenges in verifying curriculum content amid restricted access to Gaza institutions.[66] Critics from organizations like MEMRI argue this environment fosters radicalization, contributing to events like the October 7, 2023, attacks by indoctrinated graduates, while university statements emphasize educational resilience against external pressures.[63][67]

Role in Past and Ongoing Conflicts

Al-Aqsa University has been impacted by multiple rounds of armed conflict in the Gaza Strip, with its facilities damaged or destroyed during Israeli military operations targeting Hamas infrastructure. In the 2008â€“2009 Operation Cast Lead and the 2014 Operation Protective Edge, several university buildings sustained damage from airstrikes amid broader exchanges of fire between Israeli forces and Palestinian militants.[68] In the ongoing war triggered by Hamas's October 7, 2023, attack on Israel, the university's Gaza City campus functioned as a shelter for thousands of displaced civilians evacuated from northern areas due to intensified fighting. Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) shelled the site on January 22, 2024, destroying structures while the campus hosted internally displaced persons and limited academic activities.[5][14] Subsequently, on February 6, 2024, IDF ground troops demolished two primary buildings on the campus using controlled explosives after surrounding the area, and reports indicate troops fired on Palestinians sheltering there, causing deaths and injuries per local accounts.[46] These actions contributed to the near-total destruction of the Gaza City campus, rendering much of the infrastructure unusable.[6] The university's entanglement in hostilities stems from its location under Hamas governance since 2007, when the group assumed administrative influence over Gaza institutions, including higher education facilities, exposing them to military interference and operational risks during escalations.[10] While IDF operations in Gaza have cited Hamas's exploitation of civilian sitesâ€”including universitiesâ€”for military ends such as weapon storage and operative training, public disclosures specify such uses more explicitly for other institutions like the Islamic University of Gaza rather than Al-Aqsa.[60][69]

Recent Developments and Future Prospects

Impact of 2023-2025 War

The Al-Aqsa University, with campuses in Gaza City and Deir al-Balah, experienced severe physical damage from Israeli military operations starting in late 2023, rendering its facilities largely inoperable. In early November 2023, Israeli bombing damaged structures at the university, as evidenced by contemporaneous social media videos and reports. A subsequent airstrike in late December 2023 struck the Gaza City campus, killing at least 20 Palestinians who were sheltering there amid widespread displacement. By early February 2024, Israeli forces had destroyed two buildings on the Gaza City campus. These attacks contributed to the broader destruction of nearly all higher education institutions in Gaza, with Al-Aqsa's facilities repeatedly targeted in multiple incidents.[45] Further degradation occurred in January 2024, when the Khan Younis campus sustained severe damage during Israeli tank incursions, including reports of encouraged looting by troops. On January 22, 2024, shelling hit the Gaza City campus, which was housing displaced persons, resulting in several Palestinian deaths. In late May 2024, Israeli soldiers burned the university library, with photographic evidence circulating online. Unexploded ordnance near the Khan Younis campus in June 2024 injured six children, highlighting ongoing hazards. No specific repairs or reopenings were reported by late 2025, aligning with the total cessation of in-person higher education across Gaza since October 2023.[45][5][70] The war halted all academic activities at Al-Aqsa, affecting an estimated 20,000-30,000 students and faculty pre-conflict, with many fleeing or perishing amid Gaza-wide losses of over 5,000 university students killed by mid-2025. Universities like Al-Aqsa served as civilian shelters, complicating military targeting claims by Israel that such sites harbored militants, though independent verification of military use remains limited in available reports. UNESCO's virtual campus initiative, launched in 2025, aimed to support displaced students including those from Al-Aqsa, but physical infrastructure loss precludes near-term recovery without extensive reconstruction.[32][31]

Reconstruction Efforts and Challenges

Following extensive damage sustained during the 2023-2025 Gaza conflict, Al-Aqsa University initiated reconstruction efforts primarily through the adoption of distance learning platforms. In June 2024, the university resumed academic activities for its students, focusing on all theoretical courses delivered virtually amid persistent disruptions. This shift allowed partial continuity of education despite the absence of physical infrastructure, with faculty and students adapting to online formats even as airstrikes and unreliable internet connectivity posed ongoing risks.[8][15] Supplementary support came from international organizations, including UNESCO's Gaza Virtual Campus initiative, which aimed to enable 20,000 higher education students across Gazaâ€”including those from Al-Aqsaâ€”to access remote learning resources starting in phases from late 2024. In spring 2024, Al-Aqsa collaborated with other Gaza institutions like Al-Azhar and the Islamic University to form an emergency committee, issuing appeals for global aid to sustain higher education operations. These measures emphasized digital alternatives over physical rebuilding, given the prohibitive costs and logistical barriers in the war zone.[32][71] Reconstruction faces severe challenges, including near-total physical devastation of the campus, with administration buildings, engineering faculties, and multiple structures bombed or razed since November 2023, rendering the site largely uninhabitable and partially repurposed as a displacement shelter. Israeli military operations cited alleged Hamas usage of university facilities for militant purposes, complicating post-conflict verification and aid distribution. Broader impediments encompass Gaza's overall infrastructure collapseâ€”over 80% of university campuses destroyed, affecting nearly 100,000 studentsâ€”and the absence of a ceasefire, which hinders material imports, debris clearance, and long-term funding estimated at billions for regional recovery. Persistent hostilities, power shortages, and displacement further erode the feasibility of on-site rebuilding, forcing reliance on fragile virtual systems vulnerable to blackouts and bandwidth limitations.[45][72][73][74]
