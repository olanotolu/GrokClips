New York's 4th congressional district

![New York's 4th congressional district map since 2023](./assets/New_York's_4th_congressional_district_since2023since_2023since2â€‹023
New York's 4th congressional district is a United States House of Representatives district encompassing central and southern Nassau County on Long Island's South Shore, including suburban communities such as Baldwin, Garden City, Hempstead, and Rockville Centre.[1][2] The district, redrawn following the 2020 census to reflect population shifts, covers approximately 772,000 residents with a median household income of $137,263 and a median age of 40.6 years, characterizing it as an affluent suburban area.[3][4] Currently represented by Democrat Laura Gillen, who won the seat in the 2024 election and took office in January 2025, the district has been marked by partisan volatility in recent decades.[5][6] Prior to Gillen, Republican Anthony D'Esposito held the position from 2023 to 2025 after flipping the seat from long-term Democratic incumbent Kathleen Rice in 2022, underscoring its status as a competitive swing district amid New York's broader Democratic lean.[4] Historically part of Nassau County since the 1960s redistricting, the area has produced representatives involved in key legislative efforts, though it remains defined more by its electoral battles than singular policy legacies.[2]

Geography and Composition

Current Boundaries and Municipalities

New York's 4th congressional district encompasses central and southern portions of Nassau County on Long Island's South Shore.[7] The district lies entirely within Nassau County, excluding any areas of Queens following the 2023 implementation of the court-approved redistricting map.[8] It includes significant parts of the Town of Hempstead, such as the villages of Rockville Centre and Baldwin, along with unincorporated areas like Levittown and Garden City.[7] Additional municipalities covered are the village of Freeport in Hempstead and sections of the Town of Oyster Bay, including East Massapequa.[9] The boundaries generally follow village limits, parkways, and other natural geographic divisions, resulting in a compact suburban area spanning roughly 100 square miles.[10]

Economic and Urban Characteristics

New York's 4th congressional district encompasses predominantly residential suburbs characterized by single-family homes and low-density zoning, distinguishing it from the high-density urban core of New York City, with commercial activity concentrated in hubs like Garden City and Mineola that host office spaces, retail, and professional services.[11][12] The area's proximity to John F. Kennedy International Airport in adjacent Queens bolsters sectors such as logistics, transportation, and hospitality, contributing to a service-oriented economic base.[13] Key industries in the district include financial services, professional and business services, real estate, education, and limited manufacturing, reflecting Long Island's broader economic profile where these sectors drive employment and growth.[13] Institutions like Adelphi University in Garden City support higher education and related professional roles.[14] The median household income stood at $137,263 in 2023, underscoring an affluent profile, while the unemployment rate remained low at 3.3% in 2024.[3][15] High homeownership rates, at 84.5% in 2023, align with middle-class stability fostered by robust infrastructure, including the Long Island Rail Road, which enables daily commuting to Manhattan jobs and reinforces resistance to high-density development through preserved suburban zoning.[16] This commuter-dependent structure sustains economic resilience but ties local prosperity to regional transportation networks and New York City's employment opportunities.[17]

Demographics

Population Statistics

According to the 2020 United States Census, New York's 4th congressional district had a population of 767,277 residents.[18] The median age in the district was 41 years, reflecting a mature suburban demographic.[18] Population density within the district exhibits significant variation, with densely populated incorporated villages in central Nassau Countyâ€”such as sections of Hempstead and North Hempsteadâ€”exceeding 5,000 persons per square mile due to compact residential development, while unincorporated areas feature lower suburban sprawl densities averaging under 2,000 persons per square mile.[19] Overall, the district's terrain supports a predominantly urban-suburban character, with 99.97% of its land classified as urban.[18] Following the 2020 Census, the district has seen modest population growth, estimated at around 777,000 by 2023, fueled by net domestic migration from New York City amid the rise of remote work and preferences for larger housing amid urban density constraints.[3] Nassau County, which comprises the bulk of the district, recorded a population of 1,395,774 in 2020, with subsequent estimates showing slight increases to 1,392,000 by 2024 despite minor annual fluctuations.[20][21] County-level trends suggest continued low-single-digit growth through 2030, aligned with broader Long Island suburban migration patterns.[22]

Socioeconomic Indicators

The median household income in New York's 4th congressional district stood at $137,263 in 2023, substantially exceeding the national median of approximately $75,000, reflecting a concentration of high-earning professional households.[3] The poverty rate remained low at 5.78% that year, underscoring limited reliance on public assistance programs amid robust local employment in sectors such as finance, healthcare, and professional services. Median home values reached $625,700, supported by zoning regulations that predominantly preserve single-family housing stock in suburban Nassau County communities, thereby maintaining property appreciation and homeowner equity.[3] Educational attainment is elevated, with nearly 49% of residents aged 25 and older holding a bachelor's degree or higher as of recent estimates, fostering a workforce oriented toward knowledge-based industries rather than manufacturing or low-wage service roles.[23] This profile correlates with patterns of fiscal restraint in policy preferences, as high property values and family-oriented suburban structures incentivize priorities like tax minimization and local control over expansive redistributive measures. Health insurance coverage exceeds 95%, with only 4.29% uninsured in 2023, attributable to employer-sponsored plans prevalent among district commuters.[3] Average commute times average 36.8 minutes, often involving rail or highway travel to Manhattan employment centers, which heightens constituent emphasis on transportation infrastructure maintenance over generalized social expenditures.[3]

Political Landscape

Voter Registration and Partisan Balance

As of November 1, 2024, New York's 4th congressional district features a voter registration distribution of approximately 55% Democrats, 25% Republicans, and 20% unaffiliated or independent voters, reflecting a Democratic enrollment advantage amid the district's suburban character.[24] This partisan imbalance underscores the area's underlying lean, though high independent registration contributes to its competitiveness.[25] Registration varies significantly by municipality within the district, which encompasses much of the Town of Hempstead and portions of the Town of Oyster Bay in Nassau County. Hempstead, the district's population center, shows stronger Democratic enrollment, driven by diverse urban and working-class communities, while Oyster Bay exhibits higher Republican shares, particularly in more affluent and rural-adjacent precincts.[26] These local disparities highlight the district's mosaic of suburban preferences, with Hempstead's denser, immigrant-influenced areas bolstering Democratic numbers against Oyster Bay's conservative base. Post-2020 trends indicate modest shifts toward unaffiliated status, with blanks rising amid disillusionment with major parties, fostering greater suburban independence and narrowing effective Democratic edges in practice.[27] [28] The introduction of no-excuse absentee voting and expanded early voting options since 2020 has elevated federal election participation to 60-70%, often amplifying Republican turnout advantages through higher mobilization in key precincts.[29]

Performance in Statewide and Presidential Elections

In the 2020 presidential election, Joe Biden received approximately 54% of the vote in New York's 4th congressional district compared to Donald Trump's 46%, yielding a 10-point Democratic margin that was far narrower than Biden's 23-point statewide victory in New York.[30] This performance underscored the district's suburban character, where voters exhibited less overwhelming support for Democratic candidates than in urban New York City areas, with turnout patterns suggesting caution toward national Democratic messaging amid local concerns over taxation and law enforcement. By the 2024 presidential election, Donald Trump improved markedly in the district, capturing a plurality in Nassau Countyâ€”comprising the bulk of the districtâ€”with 52% to Kamala Harris's 47%, reversing the 2020 outcome and reflecting suburban backlash against perceived policy failures on inflation, border security, and urban crime spillover.[31] Statewide, Harris held New York by 10 points (55%-45%), but the district's shift aligned with broader Long Island trends favoring Republican gains on pocketbook and safety issues over progressive priorities.[32] Gubernatorial results further highlight the district's competitive lean. In 2022, Kathy Hochul won reelection statewide by 6.2 points, but her margin in Nassau County flipped to a 4.8-point Republican advantage for challenger Lee Zeldin, driven by voter frustration with state-level crime policies and economic pressures post-COVID restrictions.[33] U.S. Senate outcomes show similar variability: Chuck Schumer secured 56% statewide in 2018 against a weak opponent, but in 2022 against Joe Pinion, his district performance softened to around 52%, with split-ticket voting evident as Republicans prevailed in the concurrent House race despite Schumer's incumbency.[34] These patterns indicate voters in the districtâ€”predominantly middle-class suburbanitesâ€”prioritize tangible issues like property tax burdens, school safety, and commuting-related public order over ideological alignments, as evidenced by consistent cross-party support for candidates addressing local governance failures rather than national culture-war debates.[35] Empirical split-ticketing, such as Democratic Senate wins alongside Republican gubernatorial and presidential surges, counters assumptions of monolithic suburban Democratic loyalty, with causal links traceable to policy outcomes like rising retail theft and housing costs under state Democratic control.[31]

Redistricting History

Pre-2020 Boundary Evolution

New York's 4th congressional district originated as one of the state's initial six districts for the First United States Congress, formed in 1789 amid the apportionment based on the 1787 Constitutional Convention's framework for House representation. Early boundaries reflected sparse rural populations, primarily encompassing areas in Queens and Nassau counties on Long Island's western end, before significant urbanization. By the late 19th century, following the 1890 census that documented rapid population growth in urban centers, the district's boundaries shifted eastward to include emerging urbanizing sections of Long Island, incorporating parts of Suffolk County to balance representation as New York's House seats expanded to 34. These adjustments were driven by decennial reapportionments mandated by Article I, Section 2 of the U.S. Constitution, responding to empirical population data rather than fixed geographic anchors. [36] In the 20th century, post-World War II suburban expansion in Nassau County prompted further modifications. After the 1930 census, which showed New York's seats peaking at 45 before gradual declines, the district centered more firmly on Nassau's growing communities. The 1960 census revealed stark population disparities, leading to a 1967 U.S. Supreme Court ruling in Wells v. Rockefeller requiring redistricting for equal population districts ahead of the 1968 elections; the resulting maps realigned the 4th to central Nassau, excluding prior Suffolk inclusions to address suburban booms and comply with one-person, one-vote principles. [37] [36] Subsequent censuses yielded incremental changes: the 1980 census prompted contraction as New York lost seats to Sun Belt states, refining the district to southern Nassau enclaves like Hempstead and Oyster Bay towns. Post-2000 census reapportionment, with New York's delegation stabilizing at 29 seats, boundaries adjusted minimally for intra-county shifts, excluding peripheral areas to maintain compactness amid stable suburban demographics until the 2010 data implementation in 2013. These evolutions prioritized census-triggered equalization over partisan reconfiguration, tracking Long Island's transformation from rural to densely populated suburbs. [36]

2022 Redistricting Process and Legal Challenges

Following the 2020 census, New York's Independent Redistricting Commission (IRC), established by a 2014 constitutional amendment to curb partisan map-drawing, released draft congressional plans in late 2021 but failed to secure legislative approval after submitting final proposals on January 3, 2022.[8] The Democratic-majority legislature rejected the IRC's plans, citing insufficient Democratic gains despite the commission's emphasis on compactness, contiguity, and preservation of communities of interest.[8] On February 3, 2022, the legislature passed and Governor Kathy Hochul signed a congressional map (S.8172A/A.9039A) that analysts described as favoring Democrats by consolidating Republican voters in suburban areas like Nassau and Suffolk counties, including efforts to pair GOP incumbents and dilute conservative strongholds in the 4th district's region.[8] Republicans argued this constituted retaliatory gerrymandering after their 2020 gains in Long Island suburbs, while Democrats maintained the map reflected fair demographic shifts and empirical compactness metrics, though subsequent court analysis found the districts deviated significantly from state constitutional standards for shape and population equality.[38][8] The legislature's map faced immediate legal challenges from Republican voters and lawmakers in Harkenrider v. Hochul, filed February 3, 2022, alleging violations of the state constitution's redistricting criteria, including prohibitions on partisan bias and requirements for legislative deference to the IRC unless its plans were rejected by a supermajority.[8] On April 27, 2022, the New York Court of Appeals unanimously invalidated the congressional map, ruling that the legislature had unlawfully overridden the IRC process and drawn districts lacking compactnessâ€”evidenced by high polsby-popper scores indicating irregular shapes designed to maximize partisan advantageâ€”and failing to justify deviations from equal population rules.[38][39] The court rejected Democratic defenses that the map remedied prior alleged Republican advantages from the 2010 cycle, emphasizing that the constitution barred any partisan intent regardless of historical context.[8] This decision affirmed state courts' authority over redistricting, later reinforced by the U.S. Supreme Court's rejection of federal interference claims in related cases.[8] In response, Suffolk County Supreme Court Justice Steven A. Lynch appointed special master Jonathan Cervas, a Rutgers University professor, to redraw the maps adhering strictly to constitutional criteria without partisan data.[8] On May 21, 2022, the court adopted Cervas's plan for the 2022 elections, which eliminated one net Republican-leaning district statewide but preserved greater competitiveness in suburban areas, including the 4th district encompassing central Nassau and Suffolk counties.[8][40] For the 4th district, the special master map adjusted boundaries to include more balanced partisan leanâ€”shifting from approximately 55.6% support for Joe Biden in 2020 under the prior map to 56.8%â€”rejecting the legislature's configuration that packed conservative voters from Nassau into fewer GOP districts to favor Democratic incumbents elsewhere.[41] Critics from the Democratic side, including legislative leaders, contended the court overreached by imposing unelected preferences, potentially underrepresenting urban and minority interests, though empirical tests confirmed the special master's districts scored higher on neutrality metrics like the efficiency gap and partisan bias compared to the original.[8][38] The process highlighted tensions between the IRC's intended nonpartisan role and legislative ambitions, resulting in maps used through the 2024 cycle pending further litigation.[8]

Historical List of Members

Initial officeholders were divided between Federalists and Democratic-Republicans, with the latter predominant in the early 19th century. Democratic dominance emerged in the mid-19th century and solidified post-New Deal in the 20th century, exemplified by long tenures such as that of Thomas H. Cullen from 1919 to 1944. Republican control prevailed from 1947 to 1996, including extended service by Norman F. Lent from 1973 to 1993, before reverting to Democrats until a 2022 Republican gain.[42][43]

Current Representative and Legislative Record

Laura Gillen, a Democrat, assumed office as the representative for New York's 4th congressional district on January 3, 2025, after defeating incumbent Republican Anthony D'Esposito in the November 5, 2024, general election with 50.3% of the vote.[44] A Baldwin native and Rockville Centre resident, Gillen previously served as Hempstead Town Supervisor from 2018 to 2019â€”the first Democrat elected to the role in 112 yearsâ€”overseeing a nearly $500 million budget, and worked as a litigator and adjunct professor at Hofstra University.[45][46] Gillen serves on the House Committees on Transportation and Infrastructure and Science, Space, and Technology, positions aligning with district priorities in suburban Nassau County such as roadway maintenance, coastal resiliency, and technological innovation.[1] In her initial months, she has prioritized bipartisan infrastructure initiatives, including introducing the Coastal Infrastructure Improvement Act to test stabilization technologies for erosion-prone areas and the Bipartisan Traffic Safety Enhancement Act to upgrade local roads and reduce accidents.[47] She has advocated for federal funding to construct pump stations and tide valves in Freeport to mitigate nuisance flooding, contributing to state announcements of $21 million in resiliency projects along Long Island's south shore following 2024-2025 storms that exacerbated coastal vulnerabilities.[48][49] On immigration and border security, Gillen cast her first congressional vote in favor of the Laken Riley Act on January 7, 2025, which imposes stricter penalties on undocumented immigrants charged with theft or burglary to deter related crimes.[50] She led introduction of the Dignity Act of 2025 on July 17, 2025, proposing investments in border enforcement while limiting activities near sensitive sites like schools and hospitals.[51] However, Republican critics, including the National Republican Congressional Committee, have faulted her for opposing over $100 billion in ICE and border funding measures and voting against bills restricting non-citizen election influence, arguing these stances prioritize progressive priorities over enforcement amid suburban concerns about resource strain.[52] Regarding crime policy, Gillen has co-signed letters urging Democratic leadership to fund law enforcement amid rising local incidents, and supported House passage of legislation enhancing penalties for illegal entries tied to criminal acts.[53][54] Critics from conservative outlets highlight tensions, including her votes against broader GOP-led public safety packages, which they claim align with urban Democratic positions despite Nassau County's sheriff reporting increased bail-related recidivism. On fiscal issues, as a former local budget overseer, Gillen has testified for repealing the SALT deduction cap to alleviate high property tax burdens in the districtâ€”where median homes exceed $600,000â€”and opposed certain GOP tax extensions projected to raise revenues by trillions without offsetting suburban relief, though detractors from pro-Trump groups contend this sustains higher overall taxation without addressing spending drivers.[55][56] Her record reflects a moderate Democratic approach, blending district-specific advocacy with party-line votes on national spending, yielding mixed empirical outcomes such as secured infrastructure grants but ongoing debates over border and crime efficacy metrics from federal data.[57]

Election Results

2024 Congressional Election

The 2024 election for New York's 4th congressional district featured a rematch between incumbent Republican Anthony D'Esposito and Democratic challenger Laura Gillen, who had narrowly lost to him in 2022 by less than 4,000 votes. The district, encompassing parts of Nassau County on Long Island, saw Gillen secure victory on November 5, 2024, flipping the seat from Republican to Democratic control amid a national Republican gain of House seats overall.[44] Official results certified by the New York State Board of Elections showed Gillen receiving 190,602 votes (52.9% of valid ballots), while D'Esposito garnered 169,651 votes (47.1%), a margin of 20,951 votes or 5.8 percentage points.[58]

Key Historical Congressional Contests

In the 2022 election, Republican Anthony D'Esposito defeated Democrat Laura Gillen 53.0% to 47.0%, flipping the seat from Democratic control following redistricting that made the district more competitive.[62] This 6-point margin reflected broader midterm backlash against President Biden's approval ratings, which hovered around 40% amid concerns over inflation exceeding 8% annually and border crossings surpassing 2 million encounters in fiscal year 2022.[62] Earlier volatility appeared in the 1990s amid national waves and scandals. In 1992, long-serving Democratic incumbent Thomas Downey retired after revelations from the House banking scandal showed he had written 475 overdrafts, contributing to voter fatigue with congressional ethics lapses that prompted over two dozen House members to forgo re-election.[63] Republican Dan Frisa won the open seat that year by 7 points, capitalizing on the GOP's post-bank scandal momentum, only for Democrat Carolyn McCarthy to reclaim it in 1996 by 4 points following Frisa's support for welfare reform and gun control opposition, which alienated suburban voters after the 1993 Long Island Rail Road shooting. The 2010 Tea Party wave tested incumbent advantages but did not flip the seat, with McCarthy securing 57% against Republican Jack Marty amid national GOP gains driven by opposition to the Affordable Care Act and fiscal deficits exceeding $1.3 trillion.[64] Margins in these contests often ranged 4-10 points, underscoring the district's suburban sensitivity to nationalized issues like tax policyâ€”evident in voter shifts during high federal spending erasâ€”and immigration enforcement debates that have swayed Long Island independents.[64] Incumbency provided limited buffers, as empirical patterns show wave elections eroding local advantages when tied to causal factors such as economic downturns or policy overreaches.[65]
