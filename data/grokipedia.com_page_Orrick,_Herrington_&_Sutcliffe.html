Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe


Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe LLP is a global law firm headquartered in San Francisco, California, with origins tracing back to 1860 and a current roster exceeding 1,100 attorneys across offices in the Americas, Europe, and Asia.[1][2] The firm specializes in serving clients in technology and innovation, energy and infrastructure, finance, and life sciences sectors, offering expertise in transactions, litigation, and regulatory matters.[3][4] Orrick has earned recognition for its practice strengths, including top rankings in clean technology and renewable energy by Vault and high placements in Chambers USA for banking, finance, and capital markets.[5][4] The firm reported gross revenues positioning it among the top 50 global law firms in the 2025 Am Law Global 200 survey.[6] In pro bono efforts, Orrick ranked eighth for U.S. impact and fifth internationally in The American Lawyer's 2024 scorecard, reflecting sustained commitment to public interest work.[7] These achievements underscore its focus on innovation and client service in high-stakes industries.[8]

History

Founding and Early Years (1863â€“1960s)

Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe traces its origins to 1863, when it was established in San Francisco, California, initially focusing on real estate transactions and general legal practice amid the city's post-Gold Rush growth.[9] The firm's early lineage connects to attorney John R. Jarboe Jr., who began practicing law in San Francisco in 1858 after informal study, partnering with Ralph C. Harrison by 1867 to handle civil matters.[10] This partnership formalized in 1885 as Jarboe, Harrison & Goodfellow, emphasizing property and commercial law.[10] The partnership dissolved in 1891 after Harrison's election to the California Supreme Court, followed by Jarboe's death in 1893, leaving W.S. Goodfellow to reform with Charles Eells in 1901 as Goodfellow & Eells.[10] This iteration aided recovery from the 1906 San Francisco earthquake and fire, notably organizing the Fireman's Fund Insurance Company to underwrite rebuilding efforts and property claims.[2] William H. Orrick joined in 1910, initiating a 50-year association that shifted emphasis toward corporate counseling; the firm renamed to Goodfellow, Eells, Moore & Orrick in 1914 upon Stanley Moore's addition.[9][10] George Herrington became a partner in 1927 under distinctive circumstances involving prior firm ties, bolstering municipal finance expertise.[9] In the 1930s, Herrington led the successful issuance of bonds for the Golden Gate Bridge, navigating opposition from San Francisco supervisors to secure funding for the landmark infrastructure project.[10] Eric Sutcliffe joined in 1932, ascending to managing partner in 1947 and guiding operations through postwar expansion in water rights litigation and hydroelectric development disputes, including Lake Tahoe region cases.[9][10] By the 1960s, the firm had solidified as Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe, with a reputation for securities and public finance work rooted in these foundational decades.[9]

Expansion into Corporate and Finance Practices (1970sâ€“1990s)

In the 1970s, Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe maintained a conservative profile as a San Francisco-based firm, primarily centered on public finance and municipal bond work, under the long-serving managing partner Eric Sutcliffe, who led from 1947 to 1977.[10] This period saw limited diversification, with the firm's practice reflecting the stable demand for infrastructure financing in California, including ongoing involvement in projects tied to state and local government needs.[11] The firm's name was formalized as Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe in 1980, signaling a consolidation of its traditional strengths amid gradual modernization.[10] The 1980s marked the onset of strategic expansion into broader corporate and private finance practices, driven by office openings and targeted hires to access new markets. In 1983, the firm established its Sacramento office to deepen ties with California state government clients.[10] The pivotal move came in 1984 with the New York office launch, where six lawyers were recruited from the Wall Street firm Brown & Wood, bringing expertise in banking and securities; this coincided with the initiation of a dedicated tax practice under Chairman William McKee (1984â€“1986), enabling representation in complex corporate transactions and federal tax structuring.[10] A Los Angeles office followed in 1985, enhancing West Coast corporate capabilities. In 1987, the firm hired nine public finance specialists from Hawkins Delafield & Wood, bolstering its bond underwriting leadership and achieving the top ranking in bond volume by 1988 according to The Bond Buyer.[10] These steps shifted Orrick from a regional public finance specialist toward integrated corporate services, including mergers, acquisitions advisory, and private placements, amid rising national demand for legal support in deregulated financial markets. Under Chairman Ralph H. Baxter Jr., who assumed leadership in 1990, Orrick pursued aggressive diversification and growth, emphasizing corporate litigation, intellectual property, and high-yield finance to complement its public sector roots.[10] The 1995 opening of a Palo Alto office, with the recruitment of IP litigator Terrence McMahon, positioned the firm to handle emerging corporate tech deals and patent disputes in Silicon Valley.[10] By 1998, the New York office had expanded to 165 lawyersâ€”surpassing San Francisco's 155â€”and the firm entered international markets with a London office, facilitating cross-border corporate finance and structured transactions for U.S. clients.[10] This era's hires and practice builds, including leveraged finance and venture advisory, reflected adaptation to Wall Street's securitization boom and corporate restructuring waves, with attorney headcount growing from around 200 in the early 1980s to over 500 by decade's end.[12]

Global Growth and Tech Specialization (2000sâ€“Present)

In the early 2000s, Orrick pursued domestic expansion to capitalize on emerging technology sectors, opening a Seattle office in 2000 to serve clients in Internet and telecommunications industries.[10] The firm also established an Orange County office by acquiring intellectual property litigators from Lyon & Lyon, bolstering its capabilities in tech-related disputes.[10] Internationally, Orrick entered Paris in 2002 through the acquisition of Watson, Farley & Williams, adding 42 lawyers focused on structured finance and asset financing to support cross-border tech transactions.[10] That year, the firm launched its Global Operations Center in Wheeling, West Virginia, pioneering insourcing for legal support services in a law firm context.[3] Subsequent years saw accelerated global footprint growth via targeted hires and acquisitions. In 2003, Orrick opened a Milan office with 23 lawyers from Ernst & Young's Studio Legale Tributario, enhancing European operations for tech and finance clients, and integrated a team from Venture Law Group to deepen venture capital expertise.[10] The firm added a Rome office in 2004 by acquiring 16 lawyers from Studio Legale e Tributario, specializing in banking and finance, and expanded into Asia and Russia in 2005 with a Moscow office and Hong Kong office via a 25-lawyer group from Coudert Brothers, alongside Beijing and Shanghai outposts.[10] By 2008, Orrick entered Germany through a combination with HÃ¶lters & Elsing, establishing offices in DÃ¼sseldorf and Munich.[1] Orrick's technology specialization evolved concurrently, leveraging San Francisco roots to represent high-growth tech firms in venture financings, mergers, acquisitions, and initial public offerings.[13] The firm intentionally broadened its tech practice alongside international offices over the subsequent two decades, advising on IP, regulatory compliance, and strategic deals in dynamic sectors like software and hardware innovation. In the 2010s, expansions included a 2015 Geneva office for European tech financing, a 2016 Houston office targeting energy-tech intersections, and a 2019 Boston launch to access life sciences and innovation hubs.[14] Recent moves, such as 2025 openings in Miami and Charlotte, extended East Coast presence for tech and finance synergies.[15] This growth supported over 120 Financial Times Innovation awards for client solutions in tech ecosystems.[3]

venture financing, often representing emerging technology companies and established corporations in complex deals.[2] In finance, Orrick specializes in structured finance products such as collateralized loan obligations (CLOs), asset-backed securities, and banking transactions, with teams operating across New York, London, and other hubs to handle transatlantic and global matters.[18] The practice supports private equity funds, banks, and investors in debt financing and derivatives, leveraging regulatory expertise to navigate evolving financial regulations.[19] Litigation forms a Supreme Court through a dedicated appellate team.[16] Energy and infrastructure practices constitute another pillar, covering renewables, oil and gas, electric power generation, and public-private partnerships, where Orrick advises on project development, financing, and regulatory compliance across the energy transition.[22] Clients include utilities, developers, and governments in deals Organizational Profile

Global Offices and Structure

Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe LLP is headquartered in San Francisco, California, at 405 Howard Street, and maintains over 25 offices across more than 25 markets in the United States, Europe, and Asia.[38] The firm operates as a single, integrated global team, enabling lawyers to collaborate seamlessly across geographic boundaries without siloed structures.[38] In the United States, Orrick has offices in major markets including San Francisco, Menlo Park (Silicon Valley), Los Angeles, Orange County, Santa Monica, New York, Washington, D.C., Boston, Chicago, Houston, Austin, Charlotte, and Seattle.[3][5] European offices are located in London, Paris, Brussels, DÃ¼sseldorf, Munich, and Geneva.[3] In Asia, the firm maintains presence in Beijing and Tokyo.[3] These locations support the firm's focus on cross-border work in technology, finance, energy, and life sciences sectors. Organizationally, Orrick functions as a limited liability partnership (LLP) with a two-tier structure comprising equity partners, who share in profits, and non-equity (income) partners.[39] The typical timeline to partnership eligibility averages 8 to 10 years post-law school.[39] The firm is divided into six primary business units: corporate, energy and infrastructure, finance, litigation, intellectual property, and SAGE (a regulatory and government enforcement group).[17] Leadership includes a chairman and managing partners overseeing global operations, with recent partner classes expanding expertise in key sectors across multiple offices.[40] This structure promotes collaborative compensation rather than strictly "eat-what-you-kill" models, varying by practice group.[41]

Appellate and Supreme Court Work

Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe maintains a dedicated Supreme Court and Appellate practice group, recognized for handling complex appeals across federal circuits and the U.S. Supreme Court, often involving intellectual property, employment discrimination, constitutional issues, and regulatory matters.[47] The group integrates appellate strategy from the trial phase, advising on preserving issues for appeal and collaborating with trial teams to strengthen records.[63] It represents diverse clients, including corporations, governments, and pro bono parties, with a track record of arguing high-stakes cases before the Supreme Court. [64] The firm has secured multiple victories at the Supreme Court. In Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc. (2013), Orrick attorneys successfully defended the first sale doctrine, upholding the legality of reselling copyrighted works lawfully made abroad and preserving an estimated $60 billion U.S. market for such goods.[65] In Sessions v. Dimaya (2018), the firm prevailed on behalf of non-citizen Jose Dimaya, leading the Court to strike down a vague federal statute on "crimes of violence" that had mandated deportation, marking a significant immigrant rights win.[66] That same year, in a federal sentencing case, the Supreme Court sided with Orrick's client Erik Hughes, rejecting interpretations adopted by 10 circuits and clarifying guidelines for prior convictions.[67] In Unicolors, Inc. v. H&M Hennes & Mauritz, L.P. (2022), the firm restored a copyright infringement verdict by convincing the Court to narrow excusable neglect standards under the Copyright Act.[68] Orrick partners have frequently argued before the Supreme Court, demonstrating the group's depth. In October 2020, three partners presented arguments in three separate cases over six days, including matters related to Oracle.[69] In December 2023, partner Bob Loeb argued Muldrow v. City of St. Louis, a Title VII employment discrimination case on behalf of the City of St. Louis, addressing the presumption of harm from unfavorable transfers.[70] During the 2017-2018 term, the firm achieved an undefeated record in five Supreme Court cases argued by four different partners, the highest number of arguments by any firm that term.[71] Beyond the Supreme Court, Orrick's appellate team has notched wins in federal circuits on issues like intellectual property, environmental permitting, and financial services. In 2023, it defended a U.S. Army Corps of Engineers permit for a liquefied natural gas project against Sierra Club challenges in the Fifth Circuit, supporting energy infrastructure development.[72] The group has been named to the National Law Journal's Appellate Hot List 12 times as of 2024, cited for victories in IP, product liability, environmental, and financial appeals.[73] Law360 designated Orrick's appellate practice "Group of the Year" in 2019 and 2023, highlighting precedent-setting rulings and high-stakes reversals for tech, pharmaceutical, and financial clients.[74] [71] The practice emphasizes pro bono appellate work, contributing to the firm's broader commitments by handling appeals for indigent clients and public interest causes at federal courts of appeals and the Supreme Court.[75]

Controversies and Challenges

Data Security Incidents

In March 2023, Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe detected unauthorized file transfer activity on its network, confirming that cybercriminals had accessed a file share server containing sensitive client data.[88] The breach, which occurred between February and March 2023, exposed personal information of approximately 637,000 individuals, including names, addresses, dates of birth, Social Security numbers, passport numbers, health data, and medical information.[89] [90] Initially reported to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services on June 30, 2023, as impacting 40,823 individuals, the scope expanded upon further investigation to include downstream clients affected through Orrick's role as a service provider.[90] The firm responded by engaging cybersecurity experts to investigate, securing its systems, and notifying affected parties starting in January 2024, in line with data breach notification requirements.[91] No evidence emerged of ransomware demands or public extortion by the hackers, though the initial access method remained undisclosed.[92] Affected individuals reported subsequent harms such as increased spam, phishing attempts, and identity theft risks, prompting multiple class action lawsuits alleging inadequate security measures.[93] The consolidated litigation, filed in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, resulted in an $8 million settlement approved preliminarily in April 2024 and finalized on November 12, 2024.[91] [94] Under the agreement, Orrick committed to enhancing data security protocols, including multi-factor authentication, encryption, and regular audits, for a period of three years, while class members could claim up to $10,000 for documented losses or $50 without proof.[94] Approximately $3.2 million of the fund allocated to attorneys' fees and costs.[95] Orrick denied liability but settled to avoid protracted litigation, highlighting the irony given the firm's practice in representing clients on cybersecurity matters.[92]

Internal and Competitive Disputes

In 2006, former Orrick associate Anthony Hoeffner filed suit against the firm in New York state court, alleging breach of contract, fraud, promissory estoppel, and unjust enrichment after being denied partnership despite what he claimed were explicit promises from partners that he would be elevated based on his performance in the intellectual property practice.[96] Hoeffner contended that partners, including those in leadership, assured him of partnership at the end of a specified period in exchange for his continued employment and relocation efforts, but terminated him without elevation, seeking over $100 million in damages for lost future earnings.[97] The firm defended by arguing no enforceable contract existed, as partnership decisions are discretionary and at-will under standard law firm partnership agreements.[98] The court dismissed Hoeffner's fraud claims in 2008 for failing to allege actionable misrepresentations distinct from the contract claim, while allowing breach of contract to proceed initially.[99] In 2009, summary judgment was granted to Orrick on the remaining contract claim, ruling that the alleged oral promises did not obligate the firm to admit Hoeffner as partner, as no written agreement or firm policy mandated such elevation.[98] Appellate courts upheld the dismissal, finding promissory estoppel and unjust enrichment duplicative of the failed contract claim, with no evidence of independent duties owed.[100] Hoeffner lost a subsequent bid for punitive damages in 2011.[101] On the competitive front, Orrick's 2019 hire of Jones Day's Paris-based international arbitration partner, Manuel BÃ¼hler, triggered arbitration between Jones Day and BÃ¼hler over alleged breaches of his partnership agreement, including non-compete and confidentiality provisions.[102] Jones Day sought third-party discovery from Orrick via arbitral subpoena for documents related to BÃ¼hler's transition and client matters, arguing relevance to claims of improper solicitation and use of proprietary information.[103] A U.S. District Court in California initially denied enforcement of the subpoena in 2021, citing limits under the Federal Arbitration Act on third-party summonses.[104] The Ninth Circuit reversed in August 2022, holding that federal courts retain jurisdiction to enforce such summonses in international arbitrations under the New York Convention, even against non-parties like Orrick, provided the underlying dispute qualifies as foreign commerce.[105] The ruling emphasized arbitral efficiency but did not resolve the merits of Jones Day's poaching allegations against Orrick or BÃ¼hler.[103] No public resolution of the arbitration has been reported, though such lateral hires are common in Big Law amid talent competition.[106]

Innovation and Broader Impact

Legal Service Innovations
