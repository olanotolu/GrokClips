Geographical renaming

Geographical renaming is the practice of changing the official names of places, including cities, natural features, streets, and administrative divisions, typically driven by shifts in political control, cultural assertions, or efforts to revise historical associations.[1][2] These alterations serve as symbolic acts to inscribe new identities onto landscapes, often erasing prior nomenclature tied to former rulers or indigenous origins.[3] Throughout history, conquerors and colonizers have systematically renamed territories to legitimize claims, as seen in European powers replacing Native American toponyms with those reflecting metropolitan heritage during the age of exploration and settlement.[4] Post-colonial reversals, such as India's shift from Bombay to Mumbai in 1995 or South Africa's replacement of apartheid-era names, exemplify how newly empowered groups reclaim narratives through toponymy.[1] In modern contexts, geographical renaming frequently intersects with debates over heritage and identity, particularly in efforts to excise names linked to slavery, segregation, or ethnic slurs, as evidenced by the U.S. Board's initiatives to replace over 600 derogatory terms like "squaw" in geographic features since 2021.[5] Such changes, while framed as restorative justice by proponents, often provoke resistance from those viewing them as selective historical erasure that prioritizes contemporary ideologies over comprehensive factual reckoning.[6][7] Geopolitical tensions also arise, as rival namings of shared landmarksâ€”such as contested seas or mountainsâ€”exacerbate diplomatic frictions without resolving underlying territorial disputes.[8] Empirical patterns reveal renaming as a recurring mechanism of power consolidation across regimes, from ancient imperial edicts to recent municipal ordinances, underscoring its role in shaping collective memory rather than mere administrative updates.[9]

Definitions and Core Concepts

Endonyms, Exonyms, and Name Variants

An endonym refers to the name of a geographical feature in one of the official or well-established languages used within the territory where the feature is situated, employed by the local inhabitants to identify it.[10] In contrast, an exonym is a name for the same feature utilized in an official or well-established language from outside that territory, differing substantially in form from the local version.[10] These distinctions, formalized by the United Nations Group of Experts on Geographical Names (UNGEGN), underscore the linguistic boundaries between native and external naming practices, influencing how places are referenced across borders.[11] Name variants encompass alternative renderings of a geographical name, arising from transliteration differences, dialectal variations, historical evolutions, or romanization systems such as Wade-Giles versus Pinyin in Chinese contexts.[12] Standardization efforts by bodies like UNGEGN or national authorities aim to select a preferred official form from these variants to ensure consistency in mapping, documentation, and communication, often prioritizing the endonym's contemporary transliteration for international use. For instance, China's adoption of Hanyu Pinyin in 1958 standardized "Beijing" as the official romanized endonym, supplanting the older "Peking" derived from Wade-Giles, with widespread global acceptance accelerating after 1979.[13] Geographical renamings frequently involve asserting endonyms over entrenched exonyms, particularly in post-colonial or sovereignty-affirming contexts, where governments petition international organizations to adopt local forms.[14] UNGEGN resolutions advocate minimizing exonyms in favor of endonyms for standardized international nomenclature, though exonyms may persist in non-official languages due to cultural or historical entrenchment.[14] Notable cases include India's 1995 renaming of Bombay to Mumbai by the Maharashtra state government, with central approval on October 6, 1995, reflecting a shift from a Portuguese-derived exonym to the Marathi endonym honoring the goddess Mumbadevi.[15] Similarly, on June 2, 2022, the United Nations registered "TÃ¼rkiye" as the official English name following the Republic of TÃ¼rkiye's request submitted on May 26, 2022, replacing the long-standing exonym "Turkey" to align with the Turkish endonym.[16] Such changes highlight efforts to prioritize linguistic self-determination while navigating variant forms for global interoperability.[17]

Scope of Renaming: From Streets to Sovereign States

Geographical renaming extends across a vast hierarchy of spatial entities, from granular local features such as individual streets and neighborhoods to expansive sovereign states, reflecting shifts in political control, cultural identity, or administrative policy. At the smallest scale, streets serve as primary sites for commemorative or corrective renamings, often driven by grassroots activism or municipal decrees. In the United States, at least 955 streets bear the name of Martin Luther King Jr. as of recent analyses, spanning 41 states and frequently supplanting prior designations linked to Confederate leaders or colonial figures, with such changes accelerating after civil rights milestones and peaking in urban areas with histories of racial segregation.[18][19] Similar patterns emerged in Eastern Europe post-1991, where thousands of streets shed Soviet-imposed names honoring communist figures, replacing them with pre-1945 local or national heroes to dismantle ideological legacies.[8] Intermediate scales encompass cities, provinces, and regions, where renamings typically accompany decolonization, independence, or internal power transitions, altering urban identities embedded in maps, signage, and global recognition. Historical instances include the 1995 redesignation of Bombay as Mumbai by the Indian government, prioritizing the indigenous Marathi endonym over British colonial nomenclature imposed in 1668, a move formalized via state legislation to align with linguistic nationalism.[20] In the Soviet context, Petrograd reverted to Leningrad in 1924 following Vladimir Lenin's death, only to revert again to Saint Petersburg in 1991 amid the USSR's collapse, illustrating how urban names pivot with regime stability.[21] Other examples, such as New Amsterdam's 1667 transformation to New York under British conquest, underscore how conquest-driven renamings consolidate territorial claims at the metropolitan level.[22] At the sovereign state level, renamings assert national self-determination on the international stage, often requiring diplomatic notifications and updates to treaties, passports, and UN records, with implications for legal continuity and foreign relations. Ceylon adopted the name Sri Lanka on May 22, 1972, via constitutional amendment to evoke ancient Sinhalese heritage over colonial-era terms derived from Portuguese explorers.[23] Burma's 1989 shift to Myanmar, decreed by the military junta, aimed to romanize the Burman ethnic endonym while sidelining British transliterations, though it sparked debate over junta legitimacy and ethnic inclusivity.[24] More recently, Swaziland became Eswatini on April 19, 2018, under King Mswati III, restoring a siSwati-language name predating colonial partitions to symbolize monarchical revival and anti-colonial sentiment.[25] Such state-level changes, numbering over a dozen in the post-1945 era, demand broad consensus or authoritative fiat, contrasting with localized street alterations that proceed via petitions or ordinances.[26]

Colonial Impositions and 19th-Century Shifts

During the height of European colonialism, particularly from the 15th to 19th centuries, imperial powers systematically imposed exonyms on indigenous territories to legitimize territorial claims, facilitate administration, and inscribe cultural dominance over conquered landscapes. This practice often involved replacing native toponyms with names derived from European monarchs, explorers, saints, or hometowns, effectively erasing local histories and knowledge systems embedded in original names. In North America, for example, the Dutch renamed the indigenous Mahicannituck River as the Hudson River in 1609 following Henry Hudson's exploration, a pattern replicated across the continent as British and French settlers anglicized or francized hundreds of features during territorial expansions.[31] Such impositions served causal purposes beyond symbolism, enabling mapmakers and administrators to project control and standardize navigation for colonial enterprises.[3] The 19th century marked an acceleration of these practices amid the "Scramble for Africa" and expanded imperialism in Asia and the Pacific, where rapid partition of uncharted territories necessitated wholesale renaming to align with European geopolitical frameworks. Between 1880 and 1914, European powers delineated and named vast African regions, often honoring imperial figures: the British established Southern Rhodesia in 1895, named after Cecil Rhodes, who spearheaded mining and settlement ventures; similarly, Victoria Falls was designated in 1855 by David Livingstone to commemorate Queen Victoria.[32] In the Congo Basin, King Leopold II of Belgium proclaimed the Congo Free State in 1885, a personal domain spanning 2.3 million square kilometers renamed to reflect his authority rather than local Bantu-derived terms like Kongo.[33] These shifts were not merely administrative but instrumental in formalizing "informal imperialism" into direct rule, as economic extractionâ€”via rubber and mineralsâ€”required mapped boundaries that privileged colonizer perspectives over indigenous cartographic traditions.[32] In Asia, 19th-century conflicts amplified renaming amid territorial gains; after the Opium Wars, Britain formalized Hong Kong as a crown colony in 1842, imposing English nomenclature on features previously known in Cantonese, while France coined "Indochine" in 1887 for its Vietnamese protectorates to evoke classical European geography.[3] Such changes often disregarded phonetic or semantic indigenous variants, prioritizing Latin-script romanization for telegraphic and trade efficiency, though this introduced inconsistencies across empiresâ€”British "Burma" for Myanmar persisted from annexations in 1824â€“1885. These impositions reflected a broader causal dynamic: renaming facilitated resource mobilization and settler migration, with over 50 million Europeans emigrating between 1820 and 1930, many to rechristened frontiers that symbolically detached lands from prior inhabitants.[34] Empirical patterns show that while some names adapted hybrid forms, the dominant trend erased endonyms, a legacy critiqued in postcolonial scholarship for perpetuating epistemic hierarchies without equivalent reversals during colonial rule.[35]

20th-Century Transformations Amid Decolonization and Conflicts

The mid-20th century decolonization wave prompted numerous sovereign states to abandon colonial-era exonyms in favor of indigenous or pre-colonial endonyms, reflecting assertions of national identity amid the dissolution of European empires. Between 1945 and 1975, over 30 African territories gained independence, many accompanied by name changes; for instance, the Gold Coast, named for its resource extraction under British rule, became Ghana on March 6, 1957, drawing from an ancient Ghana Empire to evoke historical continuity.[23] Similarly, French Sudan transitioned to Mali in September 1960, reclaiming a name linked to the medieval Mali Empire, while Dahomey adopted Benin in 1975 to honor the Kingdom of Benin.[23] These shifts often prioritized linguistic and cultural reclamation over administrative continuity, though implementation varied by local political dynamics.[32] In Asia, decolonization similarly fueled renamings, with Dutch East Indies formally becoming Indonesia upon proclaimed independence in 1945, later ratified in 1949, to consolidate diverse archipelagic identities under a unified national term derived from ancient Indian linguistic roots.[36] Ceylon, a colonial designation from Portuguese via Sinhalese "Selan," changed to Sri Lanka in May 1972, emphasizing the island's Sinhalese heritage and distancing from European cartographic impositions.[21] Post-partition India saw systematic decolonization of toponyms after 1947, with over 100 cities and towns reverting to local-language names by century's end; early examples included the shift from princely state descriptors, though major urban changes like Calcutta's 2001 respelling to Kolkata built on independence-era momentum to erase British orthographic influences.[37] Territorial conflicts and post-war redrawings amplified renamings as tools for demographic and ideological consolidation. In Europe, Poland's 1945 acquisition of former German territories under the Potsdam Agreement led to the Polonization of approximately 30,000 place names, replacing Prussian and German designations with Slavic equivalents to integrate the regions and efface prior occupancy.[38] Soviet annexations, including East Prussia as Kaliningrad Oblast in 1946, involved Russifying German toponyms across hundreds of settlements to align with ideological Russification policies.[39] In the Middle East, Israel's 1948 independence prompted the Governmental Names Committee to Hebraize Arabic village and feature names, reviving biblical or historical Hebrew terms for over 300 sites by 1960 as part of state-building efforts to establish linguistic dominance in contested landscapes.[40] These conflict-driven changes often prioritized strategic erasure of adversary associations over etymological fidelity, contrasting with decolonization's focus on revival.

Motivations Driving Renamings

Assertion of National Sovereignty and Independence

Geographical renamings frequently function as symbolic assertions of national sovereignty, particularly when newly independent states replace exonyms imposed by colonial rulers with endonyms derived from indigenous languages or historical self-identifications, thereby rejecting foreign linguistic dominance and reaffirming territorial control. This practice underscores a causal link between nomenclature and state legitimacy, where adopting a self-chosen name signals autonomy from imperial legacies and fosters national unity. Such changes proliferated during the decolonization era following World War II, as former colonies sought to erase markers of subjugation and project independent identities on the global stage.[41][42] In Africa, several nations exemplified this motivation upon achieving independence. The Gold Coast transitioned to Ghana on March 6, 1957, adopting a name referencing ancient West African empires to evoke pre-colonial heritage and distance itself from British colonial nomenclature. Similarly, Northern Rhodesia became Zambia on October 24, 1964, and Southern Rhodesia was renamed Zimbabwe on April 18, 1980, both changes explicitly rejecting the "Rhodesia" label tied to British imperial figure Cecil Rhodes and emphasizing indigenous ethnic identities. Upper Volta's renaming to Burkina Faso in 1984 under Thomas Sankara further illustrated this, with "Burkina Faso" meaning "land of upright people" in local languages, symbolizing self-reliance and liberation from French colonial naming conventions.[43][41][44] In Asia, Ceylon's official change to Sri Lanka via the 1972 constitution marked a post-independence reclamation of a Sinhalese-derived name meaning "resplendent island," superseding the colonial exonym originating from Portuguese explorers and affirming the island nation's self-governance after British rule ended in 1948. Siam's adoption of Thailand in 1939, prior to widespread decolonization but amid rising nationalism, highlighted ongoing sovereignty assertions by invoking "land of the free" to underscore the kingdom's evasion of European colonization and internal modernization efforts. More recently, Swaziland's renaming to Eswatini in 2018 by King Mswati III reverted to the siSwati term for "land of the Swazi," explicitly framed as correcting colonial distortions and reinforcing monarchical and cultural independence from British-imposed nomenclature dating to 1968.[45][46][47] These renamings often extend beyond countries to subnational entities, such as cities or regions, where local authorities invoke national independence narratives to purge colonial traces. For instance, in India, post-1947 independence saw systematic shifts like Calcutta to Kolkata in 2001, justified partly as restoring Bengali linguistic sovereignty against British anglicizations, though intertwined with cultural motivations. Internationally, such changes can provoke disputes over recognition, as seen with the Republic of Macedonia's 2018 agreement to become North Macedonia, resolving a naming conflict with Greece while asserting distinct national identity amid EU accession pressures. Empirical patterns indicate these acts correlate with political transitions, with over a dozen sovereign states altering names since 1950 primarily to signal breaks from colonial or contested histories, though success depends on domestic consensus and diplomatic acceptance.[48][8][24]

Controversies and Critical Perspectives

Critics of geographical renaming contend that such changes often constitute historical revisionism by prioritizing contemporary political narratives over layered historical records embedded in toponyms, which serve as "condensed narratives" linking communities to their past, as recognized by UNESCO conventions on intangible cultural heritage.[116] In cases driven by ideological campaigns, renamings have systematically erased pre-existing cultural identities;
