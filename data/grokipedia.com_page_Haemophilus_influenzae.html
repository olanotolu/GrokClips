Haemophilus influenzae

Haemophilus influenzae (formerly called Pfeiffer's bacillus or Bacillus influenzae) is a Gram-negative, non-motile, coccobacillary, facultatively anaerobic, capnophilic pathogenic bacterium of the family Pasteurellaceae. The bacteria are mesophilic and grow best at temperatures between 35 and 37 °C.[1] H. influenzae was first described in 1893[2] by Richard Pfeiffer during an influenza pandemic when he incorrectly identified it as the causative microbe, which is why the bacteria was given the name "influenzae". H. influenzae is responsible for a wide range of localized and invasive infections, typically in infants and children,[3] including pneumonia, meningitis, or bloodstream infections.[4] Treatment consists of antibiotics; however, H. influenzae is often resistant to the penicillin family, but amoxicillin/clavulanic acid can be used in mild cases.[5] Serotype B H. influenzae have been a major cause of meningitis in infants and small children, frequently causing deafness and mental degradation. However, the development in the 1980s of a vaccine effective in this age group (the Hib vaccine) has almost eliminated this in developed countries.[3] Since the introduction of the Hib vaccine, invasive H. influenzae disease in vaccinated populations is now mostly caused by nontypeable strains.[3] This species was the first organism to have its entire genome sequenced.[6][7]

Physiology and metabolism

Structure

H. influenzae is a small Gram-negative bacterium, approximately 0.3 micrometer to 1 micrometer.[5] Like other Gram-negative bacteria, H. influenzae has a thin peptidoglycan layer surrounded by an outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharide.[8] Some types of H. influenzae contain a polysaccharide capsule around the outer membrane to aid in protection and colonization.[9] The bacteria are pleomorphic, meaning the shape of the bacterium is variable, however it is typically coccobacillus or rod-shaped.[10] H. influenzae contains pili, which are specialized to adhere to the human nasopharynx. The H. influenzae pili, unlike those of E. coli, resist unwinding, allowing for stronger adhesion to resist expulsion when coughing or sneezing.[11] A minority of non-typeable, or unencapsulated, H. influenzae employ a variety of attachment techniques, such as pili, adhesins, or Hia and Hap proteins.[12] Though the bacteria possess pili, they are not used for traditional movement or motility, and the bacterium is still considered to be non-motile.[1] The cell wall of H. influenzae bacterium contains various proteins, referred to as autotransporters, for adherence and colony formation. H. influenzae prefers to bind to mucus linings or non-ciliated epithelial cells, which is facilitated by HapS autotransporters in the cell wall binding with unknown receptors within the epithelium.[13] The HapS autotransporters also facilitate the formation of microcolonies of the bacteria. These microcolonies are likely responsible for the formation of various biofilms within the body, such as those responsible for middle ear or lung infections.[13]

Penicillin binding proteins

Penicillin binding proteins (PBPs) catalyze steps in peptidoglycan metabolism. They carry out essential processes needed to build and modify the cell wall.[14] These proteins are the targets blocked by penicillin and other beta-lactam antibiotics that bind to PBPs, hence their name.[14] Antibiotic resistance in H. influenzae occurs via two main mechanisms: production of beta-lactamases, which degrade beta-lactam antibiotics, or modifications in PBPs that reduce antibiotic binding affinity.[15] Key mutations in penicillin binding protein 3 (PBP3) include N526K or R517H (often in conjunction with other changes, as R517H alone does not confer resistance).[15] Beta-lactamase emergence in the 1970s caused the therapy for severe cases of H. influenzae to be changed from ampicillin to cephalosporins, however further resistance to cephalosporins has occurred due to changes in the transpeptidase domain of penicillin binding protein 3 (PBP3).[15]

Serotypes

H. influenzae isolates were initially characterized as either encapsulated (having an extracellular polysaccharide layer, the bacterial capsule) or unencapsulated. Encapsulated strains were further classified on the basis of the immune response to the type of polysaccharides in their capsule. The six generally recognized types of encapsulated H. influenzae are: a, b, c, d, e, and f.[16] Historically, H. influenzae type b (Hib), recognizable by its polyribosyl ribitol phosphate (PRP) capsule, was the most virulent and common encapsulated form causing invasive disease mostly in children.[17] Due to widespread Hib vaccination since the 1990s, its prevalence has decreased dramatically in vaccinated populations, with nontypeable strains now predominant overall.[17] As of 2022-2024 surveillance in Europe and North America, serotype f is the most common encapsulated type in some regions (∼10% of invasive cases), followed by slight increases in Hib and emerging serotype a in certain populations (e.g., Indigenous communities); types e, d, and c remain infrequent.[16][17][18][19] Unencapsulated strains are more genetically diverse than the encapsulated group.[20] Unencapsulated strains are termed nontypable (NTHi) because they lack capsular serotypes; however, all H. influenzae isolates can now be classified by multilocus sequence typing and other molecular methods. Most NTHi strains are considered to be part of the normal human flora in the upper and lower respiratory tract, genitals, and conjunctivae (mucous membranes of the eye).[17]

Metabolism

H. influenzae uses the Embden–Meyerhof–Parnas (EMP) pathway for glycolysis and the pentose phosphate pathway, which is anabolic rather than catabolic. The citric acid cycle is incomplete and lacks several enzymes that are found in a fully functioning cycle. The enzymes missing from the TCA cycle are citrate synthase, aconitate hydratase, and isocitrate dehydrogenase.[21] H. influenzae has been found in both aerobic and anaerobic environments, as well as environments with different pH's.[22]

Genome and genetics

H. influenzae was the first free-living organism to have its entire genome sequenced. The sequencing was completed by Craig Venter and his team at the Institute for Genomic Research, now part of the J. Craig Venter Institute. Haemophilus was chosen because one of the project leaders, Nobel laureate Hamilton Smith, had been working on it for decades and was able to provide high-quality DNA libraries. The sequencing method used was whole-genome shotgun, which was completed and published in Science in 1995.[6] The genome of strain Rd KW20 consists of 1,830,138 base pairs of DNA in a single circular chromosome that contains 1,743 predicted ORFs.[6] Current annotations identify 1,709 protein-coding genes, 58 tRNA genes, 9 rRNA genes, and additional non-coding RNAs.[23] About 90% of the genes have homologs in E. coli, another gamma-proteobacterium. In fact, the similarity between genes of the two species ranges from 18% to 98% protein sequence identity, with the majority sharing 40–80% of their amino acids (with an average of 59%).[24] Conjugative plasmids (DNA molecules that are capable of horizontal transfer between different species of bacteria) can frequently be found in H. influenzae. It is common that the F+ plasmid of a competent Escherichia coli bacterium conjugates into the H. influenzae bacterium, which then allows the plasmid to transfer among H. influenzae strains via conjugation.[25] Since the initial sequencing, genomes of numerous H. influenzae strains have been sequenced, revealing high genetic diversity, especially in nontypeable strains, with phase-variable loci contributing to host adaptation and immune evasion.[26]

Role of transformation

H. influenzae mutants defective in their rec1 gene (a homolog of recA) are very susceptible to being killed by the oxidizing agent hydrogen peroxide.[27] This finding suggests that rec1 expression is important for H. influenzae survival under conditions of oxidative stress. Since it is a homolog of recA , rec1 likely plays a key role in recombinational repair of DNA damage. Thus, H. influenzae may protect its genome against the reactive oxygen species produced by the host's phagocytic cells through recombinational repair of oxidative DNA damages.[28] Recombinational repair of a damaged site of a chromosome requires, in addition to rec1 , a second homologous undamaged DNA molecule. Individual H. influenzae cells are capable of taking up homologous DNA from other cells by the process of transformation. Transformation in H. influenzae involves at least 15 gene products,[6] and is likely an adaptation for repairing DNA damage in the resident chromosome.

Culture methods and diagnosis of infections

Culture

Bacterial culture of H. influenzae is performed on agar plates. The strongest growth is seen on chocolate agar at 37 °C in a CO2-enriched incubator.[30] The ideal CO2 concentration for the culture is ~5%.[31] However adequate growth is often seen on brain-heart infusion agar supplemented with hemin and nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD) Colonies of H. influenzae appear as convex, smooth, pale, grey, or transparent colonies with a mild odor.[31] H. influenzae will only grow on blood agar if other bacteria are present to release these factors from the red blood cells, forming 'satellite' colonies around these bacteria. For example, H. influenzae will grow in the hemolytic zone of Staphylococcus aureus on blood agar plates; the hemolysis of cells by S. aureus releases NAD which is needed for its growth. H. influenzae will not grow outside the hemolytic zone of S. aureus due to the lack of nutrients in these areas.[32]

Diagnosis of infections

Symptoms of noninvasive H. influenzae respiratory tract infections, such as pneumonia or bronchitis, may include fever, chills, cough (with or without mucus), shortness of breath, chest pain, and excessive tiredness. These can mimic viral upper respiratory infections initially, with low-grade fever, and progress to lower respiratory involvement resembling wheezy bronchitis. Sputum is often purulent and difficult to expectorate. The cough may persist for weeks without appropriate treatment. Cases may be diagnosed when chest infections do not respond to penicillins or first-generation cephalosporins. A chest X-ray can identify alveolar consolidation.[33][5] For invasive H. influenzae infections, symptoms vary by site. Meningitis typically presents with sudden high fever, headache, stiff neck, nausea, vomiting, and confusion; in infants, irritability, poor feeding, and bulging fontanelle. Bloodstream infections may cause fever, chills, rapid breathing, and lethargy. Epiglottitis involves severe sore throat, drooling, and difficulty breathing.[33] Clinical diagnosis of invasive H. influenzae infection (infection that has spread to the bloodstream and internal tissues) is typically confirmed by bacterial culture, latex particle agglutination tests, or polymerase chain reaction tests on clinical samples obtained from an otherwise sterile body site. In this respect, H. influenzae cultured from the nasopharyngeal cavity or throat would not indicate H. influenzae disease, because these sites are colonized in disease-free individuals.[35] However, H. influenzae isolated from cerebrospinal fluid or blood or joint fluid would indicate invasive H. influenzae infection. Microscopic observation of a Gram stained specimen of H. influenzae will show Gram-negative coccobacillus. The cultured organism can be further characterized using catalase and oxidase tests, both of which should be positive. Further serological testing is necessary to distinguish the capsular polysaccharide and differentiate between H. influenzae b and nonencapsulated strains. Although highly specific, bacterial culture of H. influenzae lacks sensitivity, with yields around 50% or less in pneumonia cases. Use of antibiotics prior to sample collection greatly reduces the isolation rate by killing the bacteria before identification is possible.[36] Recent work has shown that H. influenzae uses a highly specialized spectrum of nutrients where lactate is a preferred carbon source.[37]

Latex particle agglutination

The latex particle agglutination test (LAT) is a more sensitive method to detect H. influenzae than is culture.[38] Because the method relies on antigen rather than viable bacteria, the results are not disrupted by prior antibiotic use. It also has the added benefit of being quicker than culture methods. However, antibiotic sensitivity testing is not possible with LAT alone, so a parallel culture is necessary.[39]

Molecular methods

Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) assays have been proven to be more sensitive than either LAT or culture tests and are highly specific.[40] These PCR tests can be used for capsular typing of encapsulated H. influenzae strains.[41]

Pathogenicity

Host colonization

Many microbes colonize within a host organism. Colonization occurs when a microorganism continues to multiply within the host, without interaction, causing no visible signs of illness or infection. H. influenzae colonizes differently in adults than it does young children. Because this bacterium colonizes more rapidly in young children, they are capable of carrying more than one strain of the same bacterium. Once in the adult stage of life, a human is likely to only be carrying one strain as this bacterium does not colonize as aggressively in adults. Approximately 20% of infants are colonized with H. influenzae within their first year of life.[12] H. influenzae is generally found within and upon the human body. It does not survive long on dry inanimate surfaces (typically less than 1 day) and transmission occurs primarily person-to-person via respiratory droplets.[42] Most strains of H. influenzae are opportunistic pathogens; that is, they usually live in their host without causing disease, but cause problems only when other factors (such as a viral infection, reduced immune function or chronically inflamed tissues, e.g. from allergies) create an opportunity. They infect the host by sticking to the host cell using trimeric autotransporter adhesins.[13] The pathogenesis of H. influenzae infections is not completely understood, although the presence of the polyribosyl ribitol phosphate (PRP) capsule in encapsulated type b (Hib), a serotype causing conditions such as epiglottitis, is known to be a major factor in virulence.[43] Their capsule allows them to resist phagocytosis and complement-mediated lysis in the nonimmune host. The unencapsulated strains are almost always less invasive; however, they can produce an inflammatory response in humans, which can lead to many symptoms. Vaccination with Hib conjugate vaccine is effective in preventing Hib infection but does not prevent infection with NTHi strains.[44] H. influenzae can cause respiratory tract infections including pneumonia, otitis media, epiglottitis (swelling in the throat), eye infections and bloodstream infection, meningitis. It can also cause cellulitis (skin infection) and infectious arthritis (inflammation of the joint).[45]

Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) infection

Naturally acquired disease caused by H. influenzae seems to occur in humans only. In healthy children under the age of 5, H. influenzae type b was responsible for more than 80% of aggressive infections, before the introduction of the [Hib] vaccine.[46] In infants and young children, H. influenzae type b (Hib) causes bacteremia, pneumonia, epiglottitis and acute bacterial meningitis.[47] On occasion, it causes cellulitis, osteomyelitis, and infectious arthritis. It is one cause of neonatal infection.[48] Due to routine use of the Hib vaccine in the U.S. since 1990, the incidence of invasive Hib disease has decreased by over 99%, to approximately 0.02 per 100,000 in children under 5 years as of 2023.[49] However, Hib remains a major cause of lower respiratory tract infections in infants and children in developing countries where the vaccine is not widely used. Unencapsulated H. influenzae strains are unaffected by the Hib vaccine and cause ear infections (otitis media), eye infections (conjunctivitis), and sinusitis in children, and are associated with pneumonia.[47] In vaccinated populations, invasive disease due to non-type b serotypes (e.g., type a) has emerged in some groups, such as indigenous communities, highlighting the need for ongoing surveillance.[49]

Treatment

Some strains of H. influenzae produce beta-lactamases, and are also able to modify their penicillin-binding proteins, so the bacteria have gained resistance to the penicillin family of antibiotics.[5] In severe cases, such as meningitis or bacteremia, cefotaxime or ceftriaxone administered intravenously are the preferred antibiotics.[50][51] For Hib meningitis in children over 2 months, adjunctive dexamethasone (0.6 mg/kg/day for 4 days) is recommended to reduce neurologic complications.[50] Treatment duration is typically 10-14 days for meningitis and 7-10 days for pneumonia.[51] For less severe cases, such as otitis media or sinusitis in children, high-dose oral amoxicillin (80-90 mg/kg/day) is first-line if beta-lactamase production is unlikely; otherwise, amoxicillin-clavulanate is preferred empirically due to high prevalence of beta-lactamase-producing strains.[50][5] Second- or third-generation oral cephalosporins (e.g., cefuroxime) or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole may be used in cases of penicillin allergy. Ampicillin-sulbactam can be used for hospitalized moderate cases.[50] Fluoroquinolones (e.g., levofloxacin) are reserved for adults or resistant cases in children when no alternatives exist, due to risks of musculoskeletal adverse effects; fluoroquinolone-resistant strains have been observed, particularly in long-term care settings.[50][52] Macrolides (e.g., azithromycin) have activity against nontypeable H. influenzae and may be considered for beta-lactam-allergic patients, but resistance rates exceed 30-50% in many regions as of 2025, limiting their use.[50][5]

Serious and chronic complications

Invasive disease caused by Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) can lead to serious complications including brain damage, hearing loss, and death. Meningitis due to Hib has a fatality rate of 3-6% in children, with 15-30% of survivors experiencing long-term neurological sequelae such as hearing impairment or developmental delays.[53] Nontypeable H. influenzae (NTHi) strains rarely cause serious invasive disease but are associated with chronic respiratory infections, facilitated by phase variation that alters surface antigens to evade the immune system. These chronic infections are generally less severe than acute invasive ones.[54] H. influenzae commonly colonizes the nasopharynx, and nontypeable strains can spread to the ear canal or lungs, causing infections such as otitis media or pneumonia. Hib was historically a major cause of epiglottitis, a life-threatening airway obstruction. In individuals with chronic lung conditions, NTHi colonization can exacerbate chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) through recurrent exacerbations and biofilm formation, and contribute to airway inflammation in asthma.[5][55][56]

Vaccination

Effective vaccines for Haemophilus influenzae serotype b have been available since the early 1990s, and are recommended for children under age 5 and asplenic patients. The World Health Organization recommends a pentavalent vaccine, combining vaccines against diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, hepatitis B and Hib. There is not yet sufficient evidence on how effective this pentavalent vaccine is in relation to the individual vaccines.[57] Consequently, whereas approximately 93% of the population in the WHO European Region was vaccinated against Hib, vaccination coverage was 78% globally, and 34% in the WHO Western Pacific Region (as of 2023 data revised in 2024).[58] The Hib vaccines do not provide cross-protection to any other H. influenzae serotypes like Hia, Hic, Hid, Hie or Hif.[59] An oral vaccination has been developed for non-typeable H. influenzae (NTHi) for patients with chronic bronchitis, but it has not shown to be effective in reducing the number and severity of COPD exacerbations.[60] However, there is no effective vaccine for the other types of capsulated H. influenzae or NTHi. Vaccines that target unencapsulated H. influenzae serotypes are in development, including candidates like GSK's NTHi-Mcat vaccine, which has demonstrated safety and immunogenicity in phase II trials for adults with COPD, though efficacy in preventing exacerbations remains unproven as of 2025.[61][62]
