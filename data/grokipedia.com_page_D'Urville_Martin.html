D'Urville Martin

D'Urville Martin (February 11, 1939 – May 28, 1984) was an American actor and film director recognized for his character roles in 1960s and 1970s cinema, spanning mainstream productions and blaxploitation genres.[1][2] Born in New York City, he entered the industry in the mid-1960s with early appearances in films like Black Like Me (1964) and gained visibility through supporting parts in notable titles such as Guess Who's Coming to Dinner (1967) and Rosemary's Baby (1968).[3][1] Throughout the 1970s, Martin frequently portrayed tough or comedic figures in blaxploitation films, including collaborations with Fred Williamson in The Legend of Nigger Charley (1972), Hammer (1972), and Black Caesar (1973), as well as roles in Watermelon Man (1970) and Sheba, Baby (1975).[4] His directorial debut came with Dolemite (1975), a low-budget comedy starring Rudy Ray Moore, where Martin also played the antagonist Willie Green; the film later achieved cult status despite its initial rough production.[5][4] Martin's career reflected the era's independent filmmaking scene, marked by prolific output in genre pictures amid limited opportunities for Black actors in Hollywood. He died of a heart attack in Los Angeles at age 45, linked to a lifestyle involving heavy partying and substance use.[3][2]

Early Life

Birth and Upbringing

D'Urville Martin was born on February 11, 1939, in New York City.[1] [6] He was raised in Harlem.[4]

Entry into Acting

Martin entered the acting profession in the mid-1960s, beginning with minor film roles amid the era's emerging civil rights-themed cinema.[7] His screen debut came as a speaking-line extra in the 1964 adaptation of Black Like Me, portraying a Black man in a narrative exploring racial identity through a white journalist's temporary pigmentation change.[4] This uncredited but dialogue-inclusive part marked his initial foray into professional acting, leveraging his New York City upbringing in Harlem for authenticity in such productions.[3] Subsequent early roles built on this start, including appearances in high-profile films like Guess Who's Coming to Dinner (1967) and the elevator operator in Rosemary's Baby (1968).[7] He also secured a television credit as Lionel Jefferson in the unaired 1968 pilot episode "Those Were the Days" for what became All in the Family, predating Sherman Hemsley's casting in the role.[3] These bit parts, often without billing, reflected the limited opportunities for Black actors at the time, positioning Martin as a versatile supporting player in both mainstream and genre work before his prominence in blaxploitation films.[1]

Professional Career

Initial Roles and Breakthroughs

Martin made his film debut with an uncredited small role in the drama Black Like Me (1964), adapted from John Howard Griffin's book about racial passing.[3] Subsequent minor roles followed in major studio pictures, including Frankie—a street hustler who propositions the white protagonist's daughter—in Stanley Kramer's Guess Who's Coming to Dinner (1967), a film addressing interracial marriage starring Sidney Poitier, Katharine Hepburn, and Spencer Tracy.[8][1] In 1968, he played Diego, the building's elevator attendant, in Roman Polanski's horror classic Rosemary's Baby, which grossed over $33 million against a $2.3 million budget and received critical acclaim for its psychological tension.[9] Martin's early television exposure came through casting as Lionel Jefferson, the son of the Bunkers' neighbors, in the two unaired pilots for Norman Lear's sitcom All in the Family, shot in 1968 and 1969 before the role was recast with Mike Evans for the 1971 series premiere.[10][3] These appearances in culturally significant projects provided initial visibility, positioning him as a versatile supporting player amid Hollywood's gradual integration of Black actors in non-stereotypical capacities during the late 1960s.[1] By 1970, Martin secured a credited role as a bus driver in Godfrey Cambridge's comedy Watermelon Man, directed by Melvin Van Peebles, which satirized racial identity and earned praise for its bold premise despite mixed reviews. This sequence of roles in commercially successful and discussion-generating films represented his breakthrough from obscurity, facilitating transitions into more substantial character work in the ensuing decade's independent cinema.[1]

Blaxploitation Period Roles

During the blaxploitation era of the early 1970s, D'Urville Martin frequently appeared in supporting roles in low-budget action films featuring black leads navigating crime, revenge, and empowerment themes. He collaborated multiple times with actor and producer Fred Williamson, contributing to the genre's emphasis on charismatic anti-heroes and gritty urban narratives. Martin's portrayals often involved morally ambiguous characters, adding depth to ensemble casts in productions that capitalized on the era's demand for black-centered cinema.[7] In Black Caesar (1973), directed by Larry Cohen, Martin played Reverend Rufus, a sleazy pastor who shelters the injured protagonist Tommy Gibbs (Fred Williamson) in his church, highlighting corruption within institutions. He reprised the role as Rufus in the sequel Hell Up in Harlem (1973), where the character continues to intersect with Gibbs' criminal empire-building. These performances underscored Martin's ability to embody duplicitous figures in blaxploitation's exploration of power dynamics.[11][12] Martin portrayed Amos in Boss Nigger (1975), a blaxploitation Western directed by Jack Arnold, serving as the reluctant partner and sidekick to Williamson's bounty hunter Boss. The film, also known as Boss, follows the duo imposing law on a lawless town, blending spaghetti Western tropes with racial commentary. Martin's role emphasized comic relief and loyalty amid action sequences.[13][1] In Dolemite (1975), Martin directed and starred as Willie Green, the scheming pimp who frames the titular character (Rudy Ray Moore) for drug possession, sparking a revenge plot involving martial arts and exploitation elements. This dual role marked a significant venture into the genre's independent filmmaking scene, though the production faced challenges with choreography and effects. Martin's performance as the antagonist contributed to the film's cult status for its over-the-top style.[5]

Directing and Producing Ventures

Martin's directorial debut came with Dolemite (1975), a blaxploitation comedy produced by and starring Rudy Ray Moore as the titular pimp-turned-avenger character.[5] In the film, Martin also acted as the scheming villain Willie Green, who frames Dolemite for drug possession and fur theft, leading to a revenge plot involving prostitutes trained in martial arts.[5] The production, shot in Los Angeles over approximately 16 days on a modest budget, emphasized Moore's stand-up persona and featured improvised elements, including explicit dialogue and fight scenes coordinated without professional stunt expertise.[5] Despite its technical shortcomings, such as inconsistent editing and amateurish cinematography, Dolemite achieved cult status through underground distribution and drive-in screenings, grossing modestly but influencing later homage films.[14] Following Dolemite, Martin directed Disco 9000 (1976), alternatively titled The Soul Sounder or Fass Black, a musical drama centered on a singer's rise in the disco scene amid personal and criminal entanglements.[15] He also took on producing duties for this project, which starred himself alongside singers like Brook Johnson and aimed to capitalize on the era's disco trend but received limited release and critical dismissal for its formulaic plot and uneven pacing. These two films represent the extent of Martin's credited directorial work, both aligned with independent, low-budget urban cinema of the mid-1970s. In producing, Martin's credits include The Final Comedown (1972), a blaxploitation drama about a black militant group's confrontation with white supremacists, directed by Oscar Williams and starring Billy Dee Williams. He also produced Disco 9000, handling aspects of financing and oversight in its completion. These ventures reflect Martin's shift toward behind-the-camera roles in the blaxploitation genre, often leveraging his acting connections for collaborative, self-financed projects amid the era's independent film boom.[1]

Personal Life and Challenges

Family and Relationships

Martin was married twice. His first marriage was to Frances L. Johnson, with whom he fathered a daughter named Debra; the couple divorced prior to 1966.[7] On March 21, 1966, he married Lillian Ferguson, and the union produced two children; they remained wed until Martin's death on May 28, 1984.[7] No public records detail additional relationships or separations beyond these marriages.

Lifestyle and Health Issues

D'Urville Martin pursued a lifestyle marked by intense socializing and partying, common among actors in the 1970s blaxploitation and independent film scenes. This pattern of heavy indulgence reportedly strained his physical well-being over time.[7][3] No public records detail specific chronic health conditions or medical diagnoses during his active years, though contemporaries noted the toll of such habits on performers of that era. Martin's regimen, involving late nights and excess, aligned with broader industry anecdotes of substance-fueled creativity and networking, but lacked formal interventions or rehabilitations as documented in available accounts.[7][4]

Death

Circumstances of Death

D'Urville Martin died on May 28, 1984, in Los Angeles, California, from a heart attack at the age of 45.[7][16] The event occurred amid reports of Martin's long-term hard-partying lifestyle, characterized by heavy substance use and irregular health habits during his years in New York and Los Angeles, which multiple accounts link as a contributing factor to his cardiovascular failure.[7][3] No autopsy details or further medical investigations have been publicly disclosed, and the death was classified as due to natural causes, specifically myocardial infarction. At the time, Martin was serving as artistic director for a theater production, though no evidence indicates work-related stress as a direct precipitant.[4]

Immediate Aftermath

Martin's death prompted limited immediate media coverage, consistent with his status as a supporting actor in independent and blaxploitation films rather than mainstream cinema. An obituary in the June 11, 1984, issue of Jet magazine reported the cause as a heart attack and noted that, at the time of his passing, he served as artistic director of the Afro-American Total Theatre in Los Angeles. He was interred on June 1, 1984, at Inglewood Park Cemetery in Inglewood, California. No public statements from family members or colleagues appear in contemporaneous reports, and his survivors included his wife, Lillian Martin, and two children.[17]

Legacy

Posthumous Recognition

Martin's contributions to blaxploitation cinema, particularly his direction of Dolemite (1975), garnered cult following in the years after his death, with the film's amateurish charm and Rudy Ray Moore's performance sustaining interest among enthusiasts of independent Black filmmaking.[16] This status was amplified by the 2019 Netflix biographical comedy Dolemite Is My Name, directed by Craig Brewer, which depicted Martin—played by Wesley Snipes—as the reluctant director recruited for the project despite his established acting career and disdain for its low production values.[18] The portrayal emphasized Martin's professional credentials, including roles in films like Guess Who's Coming to Dinner (1967), and introduced his story to broader audiences, though family members expressed concerns over the depiction's accuracy and compensation.[19] Efforts to document Martin's overlooked career have included biographical work by his nephew, David Damas, who published The Actor Hollywood Forgot: Pioneer D'Urville Martin, a book chronicling his uncle's roles in over 50 films and his pioneering status in Black-directed productions during the 1970s.[20] No major awards or institutional honors have been conferred posthumously, reflecting the niche reverence for his work amid the era's underfunded and often dismissed genre films.[4]

Critical Assessments and Controversies

Martin's performances in blaxploitation films were frequently praised for their charismatic energy and comic timing, with critic Roger Ebert noting in his 1973 review of Book of Numbers that Martin delivered a "genial and funny" turn as a supporting character.[21] Similarly, a 1972 New York Times review of The Legend of Nigger Charley highlighted Martin among the cast for his effective presence, amid the film's broader commercial appeal despite its provocative title drawing public complaints for racial insensitivity.[22] These roles positioned him as a reliable ensemble player in low-budget productions, though the genre itself later faced retrospective critique for perpetuating stereotypes of black criminality and hyper-masculinity, elements Martin's characters often embodied without deeper subversion.[23] As a director, Martin helmed Dolemite (1975), a blaxploitation comedy starring Rudy Ray Moore, which earned dismal initial reviews for its amateurish execution, including choppy editing, wooden acting, and incoherent plotting; a 2019 New York Times assessment of the film characterized it as "bad, very bad," underscoring its technical deficiencies despite its exploitation appeal.[24] The picture's IMDb user rating stands at 5.7/10 from over 7,000 votes, reflecting mixed cult appreciation for its campy dialogue and action sequences over any artistic merit, with Martin's direction faulted for failing to elevate the material beyond its shoestring origins.[5] His follow-up, The Human Tornado (1979), repeated these patterns, prioritizing sensationalism—kung fu fights, nudity, and profanity—over narrative coherence, contributing to a directorial legacy viewed as emblematic of 1970s grindhouse excess rather than innovation.[25] Controversies surrounding Martin were sparse and largely tied to the exploitative nature of his projects rather than personal scandals. His work on Dolemite, for instance, involved overseeing content heavy in sexual innuendo and violence, which aligned with blaxploitation's boundary-pushing but drew no unique backlash beyond genre-wide debates over glorifying pimps and hustlers as antiheroes.[23] Posthumously, his depiction in the 2019 biopic Dolemite Is My Name—portrayed by Wesley Snipes as an arrogant, substance-addled figure—prompted some criticism for insensitivity, particularly in exaggerating stereotypes of flamboyance and unreliability, though this reflected anecdotal accounts from collaborators rather than verified biography.[26] Overall, Martin's career evaded major public disputes, with assessments centering on his contributions to a commercially viable but aesthetically uneven cinematic niche.

Filmography and Credits

Feature Films

D'Urville Martin began his feature film career in the late 1960s with minor roles in prominent Hollywood productions. His early appearances included playing Frankie, a pedestrian whose car is damaged in a parking lot scene, in Stanley Kramer's Guess Who's Coming to Dinner (1967), and Dr. Emilio Deogracias, a consulting doctor, in Roman Polanski's Rosemary's Baby (1968).[1] During the 1970s, Martin transitioned to supporting roles in blaxploitation and action films, frequently collaborating with Fred Williamson in pictures produced by the actor's Po' Boy Productions. Notable credits include Toby in The Legend of Nigger Charley (1972), Sonny in Hammer (1972), Joe in Black Caesar (1973), Reverend Rufus in Hell Up in Harlem (1973), Amos in Boss Nigger (1975), and Willie Green in Dolemite (1975), where he also served as director.[1] Martin's later film work encompassed diverse genres, such as the pilot in Pam Grier's Sheba, Baby (1975), Detective Don in Death Journey (1976), Willie Black in Blind Rage (1978), Easy in The Big Score (1983), and Billy in The Bear (1984). These roles often portrayed streetwise or authoritative Black characters, reflecting the era's cinematic trends in independent and genre filmmaking.[1][27]

Television Work

Martin's television career consisted primarily of guest roles on episodic series during the late 1960s and 1970s, often portraying supporting characters in drama, adventure, and anthology formats.[1] His debut television appearance was as Gage, a street youth, in the Dr. Kildare episode "A Cry from the Street," broadcast on June 2, 1966.[30] In 1967, he appeared as Joseph, a settler, in the Daniel Boone episode "The Wolf Man."[31] That same year, Martin played the boxer known as The Champ in The Monkees episode "Monkees in the Ring," where the protagonists enter a boxing match to raise funds. He also portrayed Gardner Brown in The Man from U.N.C.L.E.'s "The Apple a Day Affair," involving a scheme with mind-altering drugs.[32] Martin guest-starred in Daktari across two episodes: as Ngaio in "The Test" (season 2, episode 6, aired May 10, 1967), centered on a boy's challenge with wildlife, and as Abdul in "License to Kill" (season 3, episode 20, 1968), dealing with poachers.[33][34] Later roles included Cpl. Lester Howe, a soldier accused of murder, in the Ironside episode "Good Samaritan" (season 5, episode 11, aired November 23, 1971).[35] He made multiple appearances on the religious anthology Insight, such as Eddie in "Look Back to the Garden" (1968), Al in "Snow in Summer" (1969), Mr. Anders in "The Immigrant" (1971), and Jelly in "Hellhound Blues" (1975).[36][37][38][39] These roles highlighted Martin's versatility in character parts, though his television work remained secondary to his film appearances.[1]

Directorial Works

D'Urville Martin directed two feature films, both within the blaxploitation genre during the mid-1970s. His directorial debut, Dolemite (1975), starred Rudy Ray Moore as the eponymous pimp convicted on framed drug charges and subsequently paroled to dismantle a criminal network. Martin portrayed Willie Green, the film's chief antagonist and Dolemite's betrayer, while overseeing production that spanned over a year from January 1974, characterized by minimal budget constraints, non-professional actors, and rudimentary action choreography including martial arts fights.[5][14][40] The film emphasized Moore's stand-up routine-derived humor, profane dialogue, and exaggerated bravado, aligning with the era's independent urban cinema trends but earning mixed reception for its technical shortcomings.[23] Martin's follow-up, Disco 9000 (also released as Fass Black, 1976), depicted Fass Black (John Poole), a prosperous Black disco club owner and music producer in Los Angeles, fending off extortion from organized crime figures demanding he playlist inferior records over his preferred grooves. Featuring a soundtrack with contributions from Johnnie Taylor and appearances by Jeannie Bell and dancer Harold Nicholas, the narrative explored entrepreneurial resilience against mob interference, interspersed with disco sequences and chase elements.[41][42][43] Critics noted the film's plodding pace and formulaic plotting, though it captured the disco era's cultural fusion with blaxploitation motifs.[44]
