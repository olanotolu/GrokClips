Al-Quds University

Al-Quds University is a public collegiate research university in the Palestinian territories, officially established in 1995 through the amalgamation of several pre-existing colleges dating back to the late 1970s, with its primary campus situated in Abu Dis, east of Jerusalem, and additional facilities across the West Bank including Jerusalem, Bethlehem, and Nablus.[1][2] It enrolls approximately 12,700 students and operates 15 faculties offering over 120 undergraduate and postgraduate programs in fields ranging from medicine and engineering to arts and Islamic studies.[2][3] The university holds the top ranking among Palestinian institutions and places 52nd in the Arab region according to QS assessments, reflecting its prominence in regional higher education despite infrastructural challenges posed by the Israeli separation barrier adjacent to its main campus.[4] Notable international collaborations include dual-degree programs with institutions like Bard College, though such partnerships have been strained by campus events.[5] Al-Quds University has drawn international scrutiny for permitting student-organized rallies by factions affiliated with Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad, featuring militaristic displays, glorification of suicide bombers as martyrs, and gestures resembling Nazi salutes, which have led to the suspension of ties by partners such as Brandeis University.[6][7][8] These incidents underscore a campus culture where Islamist militant ideologies influence student activities, contrasting with the institution's academic aspirations and highlighting tensions between educational objectives and political militancy in the Palestinian context.[9]

History

Founding and Early Institutions

Al-Quds University originated from several independent higher education institutions established in Jerusalem and its suburbs during the late 1970s and early 1980s, amid growing demand for local Palestinian academic programs. The earliest of these was the College of Da'wah and Religious Principles, founded in 1978 in Beit Hanina, which focused on Islamic studies and principles.[1] This was followed by the College of Science and Technology in 1979, emphasizing technical and scientific education. Additional colleges, including those for arts and medical professions, emerged in the early 1980s to address specialized needs in the region.[10] In 1984, these institutionsâ€”primarily four specialized collegesâ€”were unified to form Al-Quds University, marking the establishment of a confederated Palestinian academic entity in Jerusalem aimed at providing comprehensive higher education.[1] [11] This merger created the only Arabic-language university in Jerusalem at the time, with initial operations centered on coordinating programs across the precursor campuses.[12] The early structure retained the autonomy of the founding colleges while introducing unified administrative oversight, laying the groundwork for expanded faculties. Official amalgamation of all components, including newer additions like the Faculty of Medicine established in 1994, occurred in 1995, solidifying the university's institutional framework.[13] These foundational steps reflected efforts to build indigenous educational capacity independent of external influences.[1]

Unification and Expansion in the 1990s

In the early 1990s, Al-Quds University advanced toward formal unification by consolidating its disparate colleges, which had originated as independent institutions in Jerusalem and surrounding areas during the late 1970s and 1980s. These included specialized entities such as the College of Science and Technology and others focused on arts and professional training, initially operating semi-autonomously under shared aspirations for a unified Palestinian higher education framework in the region.[5][13] The process culminated in 1995 with the official amalgamation of all colleges and faculties into a single administrative entity, marking the full realization of the university's structure. This step involved integrating campuses like Abu Dis and Beit Hanina, along with the endorsement of a foundational "basic law" that defined governance, academic standards, and operational protocols.[1][13] Following unification, the university pursued expansion through infrastructure development and program diversification, including the establishment of the Faculty of Arts at the Beit Hanina campus to broaden humanities offerings. These efforts aligned with post-Oslo Accords opportunities for institutional growth amid emerging Palestinian administrative autonomy, enabling enhanced enrollment capacity and facility upgrades, though constrained by regional political and resource limitations.[14][1]

Challenges During Conflicts (2000sâ€“2010s)

During the Second Intifada (2000â€“2005), Al-Quds University encountered frequent operational disruptions from Israeli military-imposed closures on Palestinian higher education institutions, intended to mitigate security threats amid widespread unrest including suicide bombings.[15][16] A notable incident occurred on July 26, 2002, when Israeli troops raided the university's East Jerusalem administrative offices, detaining several officials for questioning, confiscating files, documents, and computers, and sealing the premises. Israeli authorities justified the action by accusing university president Sari Nusseibeh of undermining sovereignty through unauthorized Palestinian Authority operations in territory claimed as Israel's.[17] The early 2000s construction of Israel's separation barrier exacerbated access and infrastructural challenges for the Abu Dis main campus, which straddled the planned route. Requisition orders issued around 2003 targeted university land, potentially isolating sections of the grounds and restricting entry for roughly 6,000 students via checkpoints and gates, thereby fragmenting campus unity and daily operations.[18][19] These measures, part of broader security protocols following Intifada violence, prompted university-led campaigns and legal appeals against the barrier's path, though implementation proceeded with ongoing permit and mobility hurdles for staff and students.[20] In the 2010s, coinciding with Gaza operations like Pillar of Defense (2012) and Protective Edge (2014), Al-Quds University faced intensified military interventions on its main campus, totaling 31 incidents from 2012 to 2014. These operations involved Israeli forces deploying tear gas, rubber bullets, and live ammunition, injuring 2,473 people, firing 5,121 projectiles, and interrogating 276 individuals, frequently amid student-led protests against occupation policies.[21] The disruptions led to 640 class cancellations in 2013â€“2014 alone, three mass evacuations affecting 12,000 students, and arrests of 60 students on political affiliation grounds, compounding enrollment declines and academic delays.[21] In response to accumulating pressures, the university severed academic ties with Israeli institutions in February 2009, citing irreconcilable conflict dynamics.[22]

Campuses and Infrastructure

Abu Dis Main Campus

The Abu Dis Main Campus serves as the central and largest facility of Al-Quds University, situated in the town of Abu Dis, east of Jerusalem in the West Bank. Established in 1978, it hosts the majority of the university's academic departments and administrative functions, drawing students from across the West Bank and Jerusalem area.[32] [5] The campus encompasses 15 faculties, including those of medicine, dentistry, engineering, law, arts, and Qurâ€™an and Islamic studies, supporting 117 degree programs at undergraduate and postgraduate levels. It employs 1,012 faculty members and accommodates a substantial share of the university's roughly 12,000 full-time students.[32] [33] Key infrastructure includes student housing, a sports center, dining facilities, and offices for the Deanship of Student Affairs, fostering both educational and extracurricular activities. Positioned adjacent to the Israeli separation barrier, the campus layout was preserved after negotiations averted plans to divide its grounds, with the barrier ultimately encircling the site.[32] [10]

Jerusalem-Area Campuses

The Jerusalem-area campuses of Al-Quds University consist of the Beit Hanina, Sheikh Jarrah, and Old City facilities, located within Jerusalem to sustain Palestinian higher education amid restrictions imposed by the separation barrier.[34] These campuses, established primarily in the late 1970s and 1990s, host specialized programs and centers focused on arts, culture, and Jerusalem studies, serving students from Jerusalem and surrounding West Bank districts.[35] Unlike the larger Abu Dis main campus, these sites emphasize accessibility within the city and partnerships with international institutions.[36] The Beit Hanina campus, established in 1994, is situated in northern Jerusalem and houses Al-Quds Bard College, a collaborative liberal arts program with Bard College offering undergraduate degrees in humanities and social sciences.[36] In February 2025, the campus underwent a redevelopment project funded by the Kingdom of Morocco, transforming 1,830 square meters of outdoor spaces to enhance student facilities and learning environments.[37] The Sheikh Jarrah campus, founded in 1978, operates in the Sheikh Jarrah neighborhood and supports eight academic programs, contributing to the university's emphasis on cultural and community-oriented education.[34] It facilitates international student exchanges, such as programs with the University of Cagliari in Italy and the University of Huelva in Spain.[34] The Old City campus, also established in 1978, occupies a three-story building in Islamic architectural style within Souq Al-Qattanin, near Tareeq Al-Wad and Via Dolorosa, hosting two programs and the Center for Jerusalem Studies dedicated to preserving Palestinian heritage through research on the city's historic sites.[35][38] This campus underscores Al-Quds University's role as the sole Palestinian institution maintaining operations in Jerusalem despite ongoing pressures to relocate.[10]

Additional Facilities

Al-Quds University operates a network of libraries distributed across its campuses, including the central Munib Rashid Al-Masri Library at the Abu Dis main campus, Al-Babtain Library at Beit Hanina, Hind Al-Husseini College Library at Sheikh Jarrah, Al-Quds Public Library in the Old City, and Media College Library at Al-Bireh.[39] These facilities house over 113,000 print titles and provide access to seven global electronic databases covering full-text books and periodicals in fields such as medicine, engineering, sciences, law, and education.[39] The university maintains specialized museums focused on education and historical documentation. The Abu Jihad Center for Palestinian Captives Movement Affairs, located at the Abu Dis campus, serves as a three-story museum, library, archive, and office space dedicated to preserving the history and struggles of Palestinian prisoners through artifacts, stories, and exhibits.[40][41] The Science Museum, established in 2007 and renovated in 2014, offers interactive educational exhibits on scientific phenomena.[42] Additionally, the Meet Math Museum provides advanced tools for school students, featuring interactive displays to explain mathematical and natural concepts.[43] Research and training centers form a key part of the university's infrastructure. The Said Khoury Information Technology Center of Excellence, inaugurated as the India-Palestine Centre for Excellence in ICT on October 13, 2015, by Indian President Pranab Mukherjee, delivers training programs in information technology to students and serves national needs in the field.[44][45] Other facilities include the Al-Quds Botanic Garden, which maintains a herbarium with over 1,000 botanical accessions and a seed bank focused on native medicinal plants.[46] Sports and student housing support campus life. The Sports Center manages indoor and outdoor courts for basketball, football, volleyball, and tennis, alongside a fitness center and gym facilities.[47][48] Student accommodations include dormitories such as Dormitory 2, known as Turkish Housing, providing residential spaces for students.[49]

Student Enrollment and Demographics

Al-Quds University enrolls approximately 12,000 full-time students across its undergraduate and graduate programs.[33][62] This figure encompasses students pursuing degrees in its 15 faculties, with instruction delivered by over 700 faculty members, yielding a student-to-faculty ratio of roughly 17:1.[63] Undergraduate enrollment stands at about 10,623 students, while postgraduate enrollment includes around 1,705 students.[33] Female students form the majority, comprising more than 55% of the total enrollment.[63][62] This gender distribution aligns with patterns observed in sample cohorts from university studies, where females slightly outnumber males (approximately 53-55%).[64] The student body is overwhelmingly composed of Palestinian Arabs, primarily from the West Bank, Gaza Strip, and East Jerusalem regions, reflecting the university's location and role as the only Palestinian institution in the Jerusalem area.[62] International student representation remains minimal, consistent with the university's focus on local higher education amid regional constraints.[5] Enrollment data for the 2024â€“2025 academic year indicate sustained participation across genders and faculties, though specific breakdowns by program or origin are not publicly detailed in official reports.[65] Approximately 47% of undergraduates receive financial aid, totaling $9.8 million annually, supporting access for students from varied socioeconomic backgrounds.[33]

Partnerships and Collaborations

International Academic Ties

Al-Quds University maintains an Academic International Relations Office responsible for coordinating international academic programs, including student exchanges where students can study abroad for Regional and Local Initiatives

Al-Quds University conducts community services extending to Jerusalem, surrounding towns, villages, and refugee camps across the West Bank, including areas such as Bethlehem, Hebron, Jenin, Jericho, Nablus, Ramallah, Tulkarem, and Qalqilya.[2] These efforts focus on empowering local Palestinians through education, health support, and economic development programs tailored to regional challenges. The Community Action Center, founded in 1999 as a semi-autonomous entity under the university, delivers pro bono legal aid to thousands of East Jerusalem residents, facilitating access to rights and entitlements amid bureaucratic obstacles imposed by Israeli authorities.[75][76] This initiative emphasizes rights-based advocacy for disadvantaged communities, including negotiation with administrative systems affecting Palestinian daily life.[13] University-led volunteerism drives local engagement, with students and staff participating in projects addressing immediate community needs, such as educational outreach and health campaigns; cumulative contributions exceed 206,710 volunteer hours.[77] Complementary service-learning programs share institutional resources with the broader Palestinian society, fostering social responsibility and practical skill application in West Bank contexts.[78] In economic resilience efforts, the university released a study on October 21, 2025, promoting cooperatives as a model for Jerusalem youth to counter labor market instability and sustain livelihoods amid territorial constraints.[79] A related workshop on October 8, 2025, advanced resource efficiency and cleaner production techniques for Palestinian industries, involving stakeholder discussions to broaden adoption locally.[80] On February 6, 2025, Al-Quds University signed a memorandum with the International Labour Organization to equip Palestinian youthâ€”primarily in the West Bank and Gazaâ€”with vocational skills, entrepreneurial training, and employment resources, targeting structural unemployment in the territories.[27] Additional local ties include a cooperation agreement with the Palestinian Police Force on October 22, 2025, to enhance security and institutional linkages within the West Bank.[81] Regionally, the university positions itself as a hub for Arab Jerusalem's cultural and intellectual advancement, supporting initiatives that integrate Palestinian needs with broader Arab educational networks, though specific cross-border projects remain secondary to domestic priorities.[13]

Governance and Leadership

Administrative Structure

Al-Quds University operates as a private, non-profit institution under the oversight of a Board of Trustees, which exercises ultimate authority in governance, strategic planning, and key appointments, including the selection and removal of vice presidents upon the president's recommendation. The board's role is formalized in the university's by-laws, emphasizing financial sustainability, policy approval, and evaluation of administrative systems to ensure alignment with institutional goals.[82][26] The president serves as the chief executive, directing daily operations, academic programs, and international relations. Prof. Dr. Imad Abu Kishek, a professor of public policy with a PhD from Northeastern University, has held the position since his election in 2014 as the university's fourth president; prior roles included vice president for administrative and financial affairs (1999â€“2011) and executive vice president (2011â€“2014).[62][83] Reporting to the president are multiple vice presidents managing specialized domains, such as academic affairs, administrative and financial matters, development, planning, and quality assurance, and medical and health affairs. For instance, the vice president for administrative and financial affairs oversees budgeting, procurement, and resource allocation, with appointments requiring board approval. Deans of faculties, student affairs, and admissions operate within this hierarchy, coordinating implementation of policies across the university's campuses and over 15 faculties.[82][62][84] This structure supports the university's operations amid regional challenges, with the Board of Trustees convening periodicallyâ€”most recently in Amman, Jordan, on December 22, 2024â€”to review governance efficacy and competitive positioning.[26]

Key Presidents and Controversial Figures

Sari Nusseibeh served as president of Al-Quds University from January 1995 to March 2014, during which he worked to consolidate the institution from disparate colleges into a unified university emphasizing academic excellence and interfaith dialogue. A philosopher educated at Oxford and Harvard, Nusseibeh advocated non-violent resistance and co-authored peace proposals, such as the 2002 Nusseibeh-Ayalon plan calling for mutual recognition of national rights.[85][86] His leadership promoted partnerships with Western institutions, including Bard College, but drew sharp criticism from Palestinian hardliners who accused him of normalizing relations with Israel, culminating in a 2005 demand for his dismissal by the Palestinian teachers' union.[87] Nusseibeh's tenure ended amid campus unrest, including a March 2014 rally by supporters of Palestinian Islamic Jihad featuring masked militants displaying rifles and glorifying "martyrs," which he publicly condemned as contrary to university values but which led to insufficient institutional response in the view of critics, prompting Brandeis University to suspend its partnership.[88][89] Nusseibeh cited his age of 65 and 20-year tenure in his resignation statement, though the timingâ€”three days after the rallyâ€”fueled speculation of pressure from both Palestinian factions and international partners.[90] Imad Abu Kishek, a professor of public policy and long-time administrator at the university, succeeded Nusseibeh as the fourth president in 2014.[62] Under Kishek, the university has pursued expanded international ties, including cooperation agreements with Russian institutions in 2025 and Malaysian research centers in 2023, alongside anti-corruption initiatives with Palestinian authorities in 2016.[74][91][92] No major public controversies have been associated with Kishek's leadership, which has emphasized resilience amid regional challenges.[93]

Controversies

Incitement to Violence and Terrorism Glorification

Al-Quds University has hosted rallies and events featuring the glorification of Palestinian militants and acts classified as terrorism by Israel and Western governments. On November 24, 2013, Islamic Jihad-affiliated gatherings at the university included participants performing Nazi-style salutes while chanting in praise of martyrdom and violent resistance against Israel. These displays, documented through video footage, portrayed "martyrs" as heroic figures, a common trope in Palestinian militant rhetoric that critics argue normalizes suicide bombings and armed attacks. In February 2016, the university organized a "cultural event" to honor Baha Alyan, a Palestinian who, along with another assailant, carried out a stabbing and shooting attack on a Jerusalem bus on October 12, 2015, killing three Israeli civilians and wounding others before being killed by security forces.[9] The event, held on February 13, 2016, featured speeches and activities framing Alyan as a martyr, consistent with patterns of rapid commemoration following attacks.[9] Similarly, on November 9, 2014, just four days after Hamas operative Ibrahim Al-Akari's vehicular and stabbing attack in Jerusalem that killed two Israeli Border Police officers, Al-Quds University held an event lauding him as a hero of the Palestinian cause.[94] The university operates the Abu Jihad Center for Palestinian Captives Movement Affairs, which includes a museum dedicated to documenting the experiences of Palestinian prisoners held by Israel. Named after Khalil al-Wazir, known as Abu Jihadâ€”a co-founder of Fatah and PLO military wing leader responsible for planning attacks including the 1978 Coastal Road massacre that killed 38 Israeli civiliansâ€”the facility preserves artifacts and narratives of the "prisoner movement" intertwined with armed resistance.[40] [95] Opened around 2013 on the Abu Dis campus, the museum exhibits stories of detainees, many convicted of terrorism-related offenses, framing their incarceration as part of a legitimate struggle while critics contend it sanitizes violence by emphasizing prisoner hardships over victims of attacks.[96] University-affiliated activities have also involved praise for other figures tied to terrorism. Participants from Al-Quds University at women's rights NGO events have cited Dalal Mughrabiâ€”a Fatah operative behind the 1978 Coastal Road bus hijacking and massacreâ€”as a "role model" and "heroic female martyr," reflecting broader institutional endorsement of violent icons.[97] Such commemorations, often integrated into student days or seminars, align with Palestinian Authority practices of honoring "martyrs" but occur within an academic setting, raising concerns among watchdogs like NGO Monitor and MEMRI about the normalization of incitement on campus.[97] These sources, while advocacy-oriented against perceived anti-Israel bias, rely on primary materials such as event footage and official statements to substantiate claims of terrorism glorification.

Antisemitism Allegations and Campus Events

On November 5, 2013, supporters of Palestinian Islamic Jihad held a rally on the main campus of Al-Quds University in Abu Dis, attended by approximately 1,000 students and lasting nearly three hours.[98] Participants wore black paramilitary uniforms and masks, carried mock assault rifles and swords, performed stiff-arm salutes resembling the Nazi salute, and marched in formation while chanting slogans against Israel.[99] [100] Banners depicting suicide bombers as martyrs adorned the campus square, and demonstrators stepped on Israeli flags during the event.[99] [101] Critics, including observers from pro-Israel advocacy groups and media outlets, characterized the rally as antisemitic due to the explicit use of Nazi-like imagery and gestures, which evoked historical symbols of Jewish extermination, combined with the glorification of attackers targeting Israeli civilians.[98] [101] The Zionist Organization of America described the event as promoting antisemitism and calls for Jewish murder, urging partner institutions to sever ties.[102] In response, Brandeis University suspended its decade-long academic partnership with Al-Quds on November 18, 2013, citing the rally's failure to align with shared values against violence and intolerance.[100] [103] Syracuse University followed suit in December 2013, ending collaboration after deeming the demonstration incompatible with academic principles.[104] Al-Quds University President Sari Nusseibeh condemned the rally on November 20, 2013, stating it violated institutional policies against violent displays and did not represent the university's commitment to peace.[105] However, an earlier university statement attributed media coverage to "vilification campaigns by Jewish extremists" aiming to misrepresent Al-Quds as promoting inhumane or antisemitic ideologies.[106] Nusseibeh emphasized that such events were not university-sanctioned and urged students to reject extremism, though critics argued the administration's initial reluctance to swiftly denounce the rally enabled a campus culture tolerant of antisemitic rhetoric.[98] [105] Subsequent reports highlighted recurring concerns, such as a 2016 monument on campus inscribed with text encouraging martyrdom operations against Israelis, interpreted by some as perpetuating anti-Jewish hostility through veneration of violence.[107] No major partnerships were restored following the 2013 incident, reflecting ongoing scrutiny of Al-Quds's campus environment regarding antisemitic elements.[102]

Interactions with Israeli Authorities and Sanctions

Al-Quds University, situated in Abu Dis east of Jerusalem and partially encircled by the Israeli separation barrier, operates under constraints imposed by Israeli military oversight in areas designated as Area C under the Oslo Accords, where Israel retains full civil and security control.[108] These include routine student commutes through military checkpoints, which impose bureaucratic hurdles and delays, as well as periodic campus incursions by Israeli forces responding to or coinciding with student demonstrations.[10] Between 2012 and 2014 alone, Israeli military actions targeted the main campus 31 times, resulting in injuries to 2,473 individuals, often during clashes involving protests against occupation policies.[109] Israeli authorities have enacted targeted sanctions affecting the university's academic standing. On December 5, 2018, Israel's Council for Higher Education announced it would cease recognizing degrees issued by Al-Quds University, citing unspecified security concerns related to the institution's operations in a contested area under Israeli administrative oversight.[108] This decision reversed prior informal recognitions and has limited graduates' eligibility for certain professional certifications or employment in Israel, though the university maintains that its credentials remain valid internationally. Earlier commitments by Israeli officials to retroactively validate degrees were not fulfilled, according to university records.[1] Recent military orders have included temporary campus closures and restrictions on access. On April 8, 2025, Israeli forces raided the Abu Dis campus ahead of a pro-Gaza demonstration, deploying tear gas and stun grenades, injuring approximately 30 students and prompting a one-week closure order.[31][30] Similar operations occurred on July 28, 2025, with forces firing tear gas at assembled students.[110] In June 2023, military orders temporarily barred two West Bank students from attending classes.[111] During such raids, Israeli troops have confiscated equipment, damaged property, and seized materials from student organizations and faculty offices, actions framed by authorities as security measures but criticized by academic freedom advocates as disproportionate interference.[112][113]

Notable Individuals

Alumni in Politics and Militancy

Hassan Yousef, a co-founder of Hamas and its longtime leader in the West Bank, earned a bachelor's degree from the Islamic College of Al-Quds University.[114] Yousef has been imprisoned multiple times by Israeli authorities for involvement in militant activities, including orchestrating attacks during the Second Intifada, and remains a key figure in Hamas's political and military operations in the region.[114] Fadwa Barghouti, a lawyer and member of Fatah's Revolutionary Council, obtained a master's degree in civil law from Al-Quds University in 2003.[115] As the wife of Fatah leader Marwan Barghouti, who is serving multiple life sentences in Israel for directing attacks on civilians, she has been active in Palestinian politics, including unsuccessful bids for the Palestinian Legislative Council in 2006 and 2021.[115] Saeed Abu Ali, a Fatah politician who served as Minister of Local Government in the Palestinian Authority's 2013 cabinet, holds a bachelor's degree in law from Al-Quds University.[116] Abu Ali has held various roles within Fatah, including positions in the Palestinian Liberation Organization, and advocates for diplomatic engagement with Arab states on Jerusalem issues.[116] Laila Ghannam, appointed Governor of Ramallah and al-Bireh by the Palestinian Authority in 2014, earned a master's degree in psychological and educational counseling from Al-Quds University.[117] Previously a lecturer at the university and chair of the Wattan Center for Women, Ghannam focuses on local governance, women's rights, and community development under Fatah-aligned administration.[118] In the realm of militancy, a campus monument commemorates Al-Quds University alumni killed while carrying out terrorist attacks against Israeli targets, underscoring connections between some graduates and armed resistance groups. Wafa al-Bass, affiliated with Fatah's Al-Aqsa Martyrs' Brigades, was enrolled as a student at the university when she attempted a suicide bombing at an Israeli checkpoint on June 12, 2005, carrying 11 kilograms of explosives; the attack failed due to a malfunction, leading to her arrest and confession to planning to target a mall in Tel Aviv.[119]

Faculty and Researchers

Al-Quds University maintains a faculty comprising professors, associate professors, and assistant professors across disciplines such as medicine, pharmacy, engineering, public health, and sciences, with a focus on undergraduate and graduate instruction alongside research activities.[50] The Deanship of Scientific Research coordinates faculty-led initiatives, including student engagement in knowledge creation and the operation of specialized labs like the Biophysics Research Laboratory established in 2005 and the Anticancer Drugs Research Lab.[120] Faculty members contribute to peer-reviewed outputs, with the university reporting inclusions in global rankings; for instance, in 2025, Professor Rafiq Qarman from the Faculty of Pharmacy appeared on Stanford University's long-term list of top 2% scientists worldwide based on citation impact, while Professor Maher Khaddour was noted in the short-term list.[60] In public health, Associate Professor Nuha A. El Sharif, holding a doctorate in medical sciences/epidemiology from KU Leuven, conducts research on epidemiological trends and health policy in Palestinian contexts.[121] The Faculty of Public Health, established in 1997 as the first of its kind among Palestinian universities, emphasizes study plans, training, and awareness programs led by its staff.[122] Similarly, in information technology, Assistant Professor Salah in the Faculty of Dual Studies researches computational applications relevant to regional needs.[123] Engineering faculty includes professors like Mahmoud Eljafari, who serves as Professor of Economics but has directed economic research units, and staff in the Najjad Zeenni Faculty of Engineering such as Abdel Rahman Kaloti and Ahmad Qutob, focusing on applied technologies.[124][125] Historical and social sciences feature Associate Professor Mutasem Alnaser in the Department of History, teaching regional Arab history courses.[126] These researchers often collaborate with international partners, though outputs are constrained by regional geopolitical factors affecting funding and access.[127]
