Photorefractive keratectomy

Photorefractive keratectomy (PRK) is a laser eye surgery procedure intended to correct a person's vision by treating refractive errors such as myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism, thereby reducing dependency on glasses or contact lenses. Laser-assisted subepithelial keratectomy (LASEK), a variant of PRK also known as laser epithelial keratomileusis[1], similarly reshapes the anterior central cornea using an excimer laser to ablate a small amount of tissue from the corneal stroma just under the corneal epithelium. In PRK, the outer corneal epithelium layer is removed prior to ablation and discarded, allowing regeneration afterward; in LASEK, the epithelium is loosened and preserved as a hinged flap that is repositioned after the procedure. A computer system tracks the patient's eye position 60 to 4,000 times per second, depending on the specifications of the laser that is used. The computer system redirects laser pulses for precise laser placement. Most modern lasers will automatically center on the patient's visual axis and will pause if the eye moves out of range and then resume ablating at that point after the patient's eye is re-centered. The outer layer of the cornea, or epithelium, is a soft, rapidly regrowing layer in contact with the tear film that can completely replace itself from limbal stem cells within a few days with no loss of clarity. The deeper layers of the cornea, as opposed to the outer epithelium, are laid down early in life and have very limited regenerative capacity. The deeper layers, if reshaped by a laser or cut by a microtome, will remain that way permanently with only limited healing or remodelling. The procedure is distinct from LASIK (laser-assisted in-situ keratomileusis), a form of laser eye surgery where a permanent flap is created in the deeper layers of the cornea. PRK takes longer to heal and can, initially, cause more discomfort.

LASEK

LASEK and PRK are two different procedures. While both procedures interact with the epithelium atop the cornea, the PRK procedure removes this entirely, while in LASEK, the epithelium is loosened using a diluted alcohol solution and lifted as a thin flap, which is repositioned after the laser ablation.[2] A diluted alcohol solution is applied for about 30 seconds to loosen the epithelium, after which a circular incision is made with a trephine, leaving a small hinge; the flap is then lifted, the excimer laser reshapes the underlying cornea, and the flap is repositioned. A bandage contact lens is placed over the eye to aid healing.[2] The procedure can be used to treat astigmatism, nearsightedness, and farsightedness.[3] During the procedure, the epithelium is displaced using a diluted alcohol solution.[4] LASEK has advantages over LASIK in that it avoids added complications associated with the flap created during surgery. The procedure may also reduce the chances of dry eye symptoms after surgery.[5] Due to the LASEK procedure not requiring a surgical flap, athletes or individuals concerned with trauma introduced by the flap may see benefits to LASEK. Patients that wear contact lenses will typically need to stop wearing these for a specified time before the procedure.[5] LASEK disadvantages include a longer recovery time for vision in contrast to LASIK which may be between five days and two weeks for blurred vision to properly clear.[4][6] Another disadvantage is that patients may be required to apply steroid eye drops for a few weeks longer than that of a LASIK procedure.[3] When LASEK is compared to LASIK, LASIK can have better outcomes with corneal haze while LASEK has a lower rate of flap complications than LASIK.[7]

Eligibility

There are a number of basic criteria which a person should satisfy:
  • Normal ocular health, including sufficient corneal thickness (typically at least 250 microns) and absence of active eye infections
  • Age 18 years or older
  • Stable refraction error (no noticeable change in the last year) correctable to 20/40 or better
  • Refractive error of myopia up to âˆ’12.00 diopters, hyperopia up to +6.00 diopters, and astigmatism up to 6.00 diopters (ranges vary by laser system)
  • Not pregnant or nursing at the time of surgery
  • Realistic expectations of the final results (with a complete understanding of the benefits, as well as the possible risks)
  • Pupil size 6 mm or less in a dark room is ideal (but some newer lasers may be acceptable for larger pupils)
  • Assessment of allergies, (e.g., pollen) where allergy may complicate the eyelid margins following surgery leading to dry eye
  • No history of recurrent herpes simplex keratitis
There are also some pre-existing conditions that may complicate or preclude the treatment.[8]
  • Collagen vascular disease (e.g., corneal ulceration or melting)
  • Ocular disease (e.g., dry eye, keratoconus, glaucoma)
  • Systemic disorders (e.g., diabetes, [rheumatoid arthritis](Rheumatoid arthritis "Rheumatoid arthritis"))
  • History of side effects from steroids
  • Granular corneal dystrophy type II

Possible complications

Some complications that can be temporary or permanent include:
  • Dry eyes[9]
  • Recurrent erosions during sleep
  • Long healing period
  • Pain
  • Glare, halos, or starburst aberrations
  • Increased ocular straylight
  • Under- or overcorrection
  • Recurrence of myopia
  • Corneal haze
  • Scarring
  • Reduced best corrected visual acuity
  • Reduced acuity in low light
  • Increased sensitivity
  • Neuropathic pain
  • Infectious keratitis (rare)[11]

Dry eyes

As with other forms of refractive surgery, keratoconjunctivitis sicca, colloquially referred to as 'dry eye,' is the most common complication of PRK, and can be permanent. In more advanced cases, recurrent erosions occur during sleeping from adherence of the corneal epithelium to the upper eyelid with rapid eye movement. Adjuvant polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) with high Omega-3 content before and after surgery improves sicca, possibly due to their anti-inflammatory effects. Foods containing PUFAs include flax and fish oil.[12] Brush PRK to denude the epithelium, instead of alcohol based techniques, also result in quantitatively lower ocular dryness after surgery. The amount of corneal hazing after surgery is also decreased with brush technique.[13] The platelet activating factor blocker LAU-0901 has shown effect in mitigating dry eye in mouse models.[14] Rabbit models have also shown improvement with topical nerve growth factor (NGF) in combination with docosahexaenoic acid (DHA).[15] Mitomycin C worsens post-surgical dry eye.[16] PRK may be performed on one eye at a time to assess the results of the procedure and ensure adequate vision during the healing process. Activities requiring good binocular vision may have to be suspended between surgeries and during the sometimes extended healing periods.

Halos, starbursts and refractive errors

PRK can be associated with glare, halos, and starburst aberrations, which can occur with postoperative corneal haze during the healing process. Night halos are seen more often in revisions with small ablation zone size.[17] Advances in laser technology as of the 2020s have reduced the incidence of these symptoms, which typically resolve within 6 months, though they can persist longer in some cases.[18] A dilute concentration of the chemotherapeutic agent, Mitomycin-C, can be applied briefly at the completion of surgery to reduce risk of hazing, although with increased risk of sicca.[16] Postoperative topical steroids can also help reduce haze and regression.[18] Predictability of the resulting refractive correction after healing is not totally exact, particularly for those with more severe myopia. This can lead to under/overcorrection of the refractive error. In the case of the overcorrection, premature consequences of presbyopia is a possibility. Experienced surgeons employ a custom-profile algorithm to further enhance predictability in their results. Loss of best corrected visual acuity (BCVA) is rare in modern procedures, occurring in less than 1% of cases, often due to decentered ablative zones or other surgical complications. Many patients experience stable or improved BCVA. Decentration is becoming less and less of a problem with more modern lasers using sophisticated eye centering and tracking methods.

Comparison to LASIK

A 2013 systematic review that compared PRK and LASIK concluded that LASIK has shorter recovery time and less pain.[19] The two techniques after a period of one year have similar results.[19] A 2016 systematic review found that it was unclear whether there were any differences in efficacy, accuracy, and adverse effects when comparing PRK and LASEK procedures among people with low to moderate myopia.[20] The review stated that no trials have been conducted comparing the two procedures on people with high myopia.[20] A 2017 systematic review found uncertainty in visual acuity, but in one study with very low-certainty evidence, fewer eyes receiving LASEK achieved a postoperative refraction within 0.5 diopters of the target, while no over-correction occurred in the LASEK group compared to 12.8% in the LASIK group.[21] A 2025 meta-analysis confirmed similar long-term clinical outcomes between LASIK and PRK for myopia correction, with LASIK showing less corneal haze at 1 month postoperatively.[22]

Types

  • Alcohol assisted PRK
  • Transepithelial PRK (TransPRK)
  • ASA (Advanced Surface Ablation), a PRK variant that uses Amoils brush for epithelial removal and gas cooling to reduce pain
  • Epi-LASIK, which creates a thin epithelial flap using a mechanical separator
  • M-LASEK, which uses mitomycin in an attempt to reduce post-operative haze.[23] Possible long-term side effects are unknown.[24]

US Military

As of 2025, history of refractive surgery including photorefractive keratectomy (PRK) and laser-assisted in situ keratomileusis (LASIK) is not disqualifying for U.S. Army special operations programs such as Special Forces Qualification, Combat Diving Qualification, Military Free Fall, Airborne, Air Assault, Ranger, or United States Army Special Operations Command (USASOC) schools (e.g., HALO, SCUBA, SERE), provided post-operative visual standards are met and the patient is fully healed (typically >3 months post-procedure), per Army Regulation 40-501 (2019). Waivers for recent procedures are routinely granted without requiring observational studies.[25][26] The U.S. Federal Aviation Administration considers applicants who have undergone PRK or LASIK if the procedure was performed at least 3 months prior, with stable refraction, no complications or adverse effects (e.g., glare, halos), and all other visual standards met, based on an Aviation Medical Examiner evaluation. However, some commercial airlines and private employers may have policies disqualifying candidates with refractive surgery history. Civilians seeking to fly military aircraft face additional restrictions. The Army permits flight applicants who have undergone uncomplicated PRK or LASIK, noting it as information only without requiring a waiver if standards are met.[27] The U.S. Navy and Marine Corps routinely grant waivers for pilots, student naval aviators, naval flight officers, unmanned aerial system (UAS) operators, and aircrew to return to flight after PRK or LASIK, provided preoperative refractive standards are met, no healing complications occurred, the individual is asymptomatic for significant halos, glare, or dry eyes, off all medications, and passes standard vision tests. Per the Navy Aeromedical Reference and Waiver Guide (2025), return-to-flight timelines are 3-6 months for PRK and 2-8 weeks for LASIK, depending on the correction level; implantable collamer lenses (ICLs) may be waived for non-pilot classes. An early study found 967 of 968 naval aviators returned to flying duty after PRK. The Warfighter Refractive Eye Surgery Program (WRESP) at Walter Reed National Military Medical Center offers free PRK and LASIK to eligible active duty personnel, including Naval Academy midshipmen pursuing paths like flight school or special operations (1,800 surgeries in 2024, majority for midshipmen). Note: LASIK remains disqualifying without waiver for Navy SEALs and divers; PRK is allowed.[28][29][30] The U.S. Air Force authorizes PRK and LASIK for eligible active duty and reserve airmen, including aviators, under the Warfighter Refractive Eye Surgery Program at Department of Defense (DOD) Refractive Surgery Centers. PRK is preferred for unrestricted aviation duty, particularly high-performance aircraft, but LASIK is approved with waivers if post-operative standards are met and no complications occur. Applicants seeking pilot slots must demonstrate compliance during medical flight screening.[31][32] The Warfighter Refractive Eye Surgery Program coordinates PRK and LASIK across U.S. military branches to improve vision readiness for service members.

History

The first PRK procedure was performed in 1987 by Dr. Theo Seiler, then at the Free University Medical Center in Berlin, Germany.[33] The first procedure similar to LASEK was performed at Massachusetts Eye and Ear Infirmary in 1996 by ophthalmologist and refractive surgeon Dimitri Azar.[34] Dr. Massimo Camellin, an Italian surgeon, was the first to write a scientific publication about the new surgical technique in 1998, coining the term LASEK for laser epithelial keratomileusis.
