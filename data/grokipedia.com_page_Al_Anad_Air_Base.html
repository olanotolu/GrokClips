Al Anad Air Base

Al Anad Air Base is Yemen's largest military air base, located in Lahij Governorate approximately 60 kilometers north of Aden.[1] Originally developed with Soviet assistance in the 1970s, the facility has hosted various air operations, including Soviet staging in 1976.[1] From around 2012, it served as a hub for U.S. Reaper drones conducting strikes against al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula operatives until American forces evacuated the site in March 2015 amid advancing Houthi rebels.[1][2] In the Yemeni civil war, Al Anad has held strategic value due to its proximity to southern front lines, functioning as a base for government-aligned and southern separatist forces while facing repeated assaults from Houthi militants.[3] Houthi forces overran the base in early 2015 before pro-government coalitions recaptured it later that year.[4] Notable incidents include a 2021 drone strike killing around 40 soldiers and injuring 60, attributed to Houthi operations from Taiz, underscoring the base's symbolic role for southern Yemeni factions.[3] As of July 2025, explosions were reported at the installation, highlighting its ongoing military prominence amid persistent conflict.[5]

Location and Facilities

Geographical and Strategic Position

Al Anad Air Base is located in the Tuban District of Lahij Governorate in southern Yemen, at coordinates 13Â°10â€² N latitude and 44Â°46â€² E longitude, with an elevation of approximately 900 feet (274 meters) above sea level.[6][7] The base lies in a hot desert climate region, approximately 60 kilometers north of the port city of Aden and north of Lahij's provincial capital, Al Houta.[3] It features a single runway oriented 07/25, supporting medium-sized aircraft operations as Yemen's largest military air base.[7][8] Strategically, the base's position in southern Yemen provides control over key airspace routes proximate to the Gulf of Aden and the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, facilitating rapid aerial responses to threats in the Arabian Peninsula.[9] Its inland location, roughly 100 kilometers from the Saudi Arabian border to the north, positions it as a logistical hub for counterterrorism operations targeting al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP), which has historically operated in Yemen's southern provinces.[9] The facility's size and infrastructure have made it a focal point for international military support, including U.S. drone operations and Saudi-led coalition air assets, underscoring its role in regional stability efforts amid Yemen's internal conflicts.[10][9] Proximity to contested southern territories enhances its utility for projecting power against Houthi incursions from the north, though vulnerability to long-range drone attacks has been demonstrated in repeated strikes.[11]

Infrastructure and Capacity

Al Anad Air Base possesses a single asphalt runway, designated 07/25, with a length of 2,819 meters and a width of 43 meters, situated at an elevation of approximately 900 feet above mean sea level.[7] The runway's orientation supports operations for fixed-wing aircraft, including fighters and transport planes capable of utilizing such dimensions.[7] The base's ground facilities encompass armories for munitions storage, residential annexes for pilot housing, a dedicated training camp, and maintenance workshops equipped for aircraft servicing.[3] These elements enable sustained operational tempo, including repair and logistical support for air assets.[3] Following its use in U.S. counterterrorism efforts, which involved basing MQ-9 Reaper drones for targeted strikes against al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula, the facility underwent expansions under the Saudi-led coalition starting in 2015.[1] Enhancements included the construction of additional hangars, barracks for troop accommodation, and command centers, transforming it into a major forward operating base capable of supporting close air support missions, drone operations, fighter jet deployments, and personnel training for Yemeni and coalition forces.[12] These upgrades increased the base's overall capacity to handle multiple aircraft types and hundreds of personnel simultaneously, though exact figures remain classified or unpublicized in open sources.[12]

Historical Development

Soviet-Era Construction and Early Operations

Al Anad Air Base was constructed by the Soviet Union in 1976 to bolster the military infrastructure of the People's Democratic Republic of Yemen (South Yemen), a Soviet-aligned Marxist state established after British withdrawal from Aden in 1967. This development occurred amid intensified Cold War competition in the Horn of Africa and Arabian Peninsula, where South Yemen served as a forward outpost for Soviet influence near key maritime chokepoints like the Bab el-Mandeb Strait.[13] The base's establishment reflected Moscow's strategy of providing comprehensive military aid, including airfield construction, to client states in order to project power and counter Western naval presence in the Indian Ocean.[1] Significant expansion of the airfield took place in 1986, enabling operations with heavier Soviet aircraft and improving logistical support for regional deployments.[1] This upgrade was part of broader Soviet efforts to integrate South Yemen into their reconnaissance network, with the base facilitating staging for Tu-95 Bear-D strategic bombers conducting maritime surveillance flights over the Arabian Sea.[1] These missions aimed to track U.S. carrier battle groups and other naval assets, leveraging al-Anad's proximity to contested waters while minimizing reliance on more distant bases like those in Somalia.[14] In its early years, the base primarily supported the South Yemeni Air Force, which operated Soviet-supplied fighter and transport aircraft under the guidance of embedded Soviet advisors.[13] Operations focused on pilot training, air defense patrols, and logistical sustainment for the People's Democratic Republic's armed forces, amid internal factional struggles and border tensions with North Yemen.[14] Soviet personnel stationed aircraft at al-Anad to aid naval operations, underscoring the base's dual role in Yemeni national defense and Moscow's extended force projection.[14] By the late 1980s, these activities had solidified al-Anad as Yemen's largest air facility, capable of accommodating squadrons for both routine patrols and potential escalation scenarios.[1]

Post-Unification Yemeni Use (1990sâ€“2000s)

Following Yemen's unification on May 22, 1990, Al Anad Air Base, previously a key Soviet-constructed facility in the former People's Democratic Republic of Yemen, was incorporated into the newly formed Republic of Yemen's military structure, serving as a primary hub for the unified Yemeni Air Force (YAF). The base, located in Lahij Governorate approximately 50 kilometers north of Aden, retained its strategic value due to its expansive infrastructure, including runways capable of supporting fighter jets and transport aircraft inherited from South Yemen's Soviet-era arsenal, such as MiG-21s and Su-22s. However, underlying north-south tensions persisted, with southern military units, including those at Al Anad, maintaining loyalty to former southern leadership amid disputes over power-sharing and integration of forces. Tensions escalated into the 1994 Yemeni Civil War, triggered by the Democratic Republic of Yemen's declaration of independence on May 21, 1994, positioning Al Anad as a critical southern stronghold. Northern forces, loyal to President Ali Abdullah Saleh, launched offensives toward Aden, encountering prolonged resistance at the base, where fighting bogged down for several days in mid-May due to southern defenses leveraging the site's fortifications and air assets.[15] By May 20, northern troops overran Al Anad, marking a pivotal advance that facilitated their push into Aden; the base became a site for holding captured southern soldiers, with reports of at least 3,000 prisoners detained there by early July.[16] Human Rights Watch documented major battles at and south of Al Anad during the 70-day conflict, highlighting its role in air and ground operations that contributed to the northern victory by July 7, 1994.[17] In the aftermath of the civil war, Al Anad continued as Yemen's largest air base, hosting YAF squadrons for routine training, maintenance, and patrols amid the country's internal security challenges. The unified YAF, formalized in 1990 but strained by the war's destruction of southern assets, relied on Al Anad for operational continuity in the south, supporting sporadic counterinsurgency efforts against tribal unrest and early Islamist threats through the late 1990s and 2000s. By the early 2000s, the base facilitated air support missions, including strikes in northern Yemen against Houthi insurgents starting in 2004, though the YAF's aging fleet limited effectiveness. No major expansions or foreign deployments occurred at Al Anad during this period, as Yemen prioritized domestic stabilization over external engagements, with the base remaining under central government control until heightened foreign involvement post-2001.

US Military Presence Post-9/11

In the aftermath of the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks, the United States expanded counterterrorism partnerships with Yemen to target al-Qaeda affiliates, including through access to air bases for intelligence and strike operations. Al Anad Air Base emerged as a focal point for U.S. activities starting in 2012, when it began hosting MQ-9 Reaper unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) for surveillance and precision strikes against Al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP). This presence was facilitated by Yemeni President Abdrabbuh Mansur Hadi's administration, which permitted U.S. Special Operations Forces to establish a forward operating site at the facility to enhance operational tempo against AQAP targets in southern Yemen.[1][18] U.S. personnel at Al Anad, numbering around 100 by early 2015, primarily conducted drone missions, joint training with Yemeni forces, and intelligence support, contributing to dozens of strikes that degraded AQAP leadership and infrastructure in the preceding years. The base's strategic location near AQAP strongholds in Abyan and Shabwa provinces enabled rapid response capabilities, with Reaper drones providing persistent overhead coverage and Hellfire missile engagements. Operations remained low-profile to minimize political backlash in Yemen, where public opposition to foreign military footprints was widespread, though U.S. officials emphasized the strikes' role in preventing attacks on American interests.[19][1] The U.S. presence ended abruptly on March 22, 2015, when remaining Special Operations Forces evacuated the base amid Houthi rebel advances toward Aden and concurrent AQAP seizures of nearby territory, which heightened risks to personnel. This withdrawal, prompted by the collapse of central government control and embassy closure in Sanaa the prior month, shifted U.S. drone operations to offshore platforms and regional allies, effectively halting land-based activities at Al Anad.[20][21][2]

Capture and Recapture During Houthi Conflicts (2015)

In March 2015, as Houthi rebels advanced southward amid Yemen's escalating civil conflict, their forces, supported by allied army units loyal to former President Ali Abdullah Saleh, launched an assault on Al Anad Air Base in Lahij Governorate, approximately 60 kilometers north of Aden.[22][23] The base, Yemen's largest military air facility and recently vacated by U.S. counterterrorism personnel who had withdrawn on March 21 amid rising instability, fell to the Houthis by the morning of March 25 following intense fighting that left the installation looted and two senior pro-government officers captured.[2][24][23] This seizure facilitated the rebels' push toward Aden, aligning with their broader consolidation of power after ousting President Abdrabbuh Mansur Hadi earlier that month.[24] The Saudi-led coalition, intervening on March 26 to support Hadi's government, immediately targeted the base with airstrikes as part of Operation Decisive Storm, aiming to dislodge Houthi control and degrade their military capabilities. Houthi forces retained possession through mid-2015, utilizing the facility amid ongoing clashes, while coalition efforts shifted focus to retaking Aden in July.[4] By early August, pro-government forces, including Yemeni loyalists trained and equipped by the United Arab Emirates, laid siege to the base, supported by coalition air support and ground advances.[25][26] On August 4, 2015, these forces recaptured Al Anad after fierce multi-day battles that killed dozens on both sides, marking a significant strategic gain following the coalition's reclamation of Aden two weeks prior.[27][4] The operation, dubbed part of broader "Golden Arrow" efforts, involved approximately 1,500 Yemeni troops backed by UAE armor and artillery, expelling Houthi and Saleh-aligned elements from the site.[25][28] Yemen's government hailed the victory, crediting coalition airstrikes for neutralizing rebel defenses, though Houthi sources disputed the completeness of their defeat.[29][4]

Military Significance and Operations

US Counterterrorism Efforts

The United States established a counterterrorism presence at Al Anad Air Base in southern Yemen during the early 2010s to target al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP), which had expanded control in Abyan and Shabwa provinces following Yemen's 2011 political upheaval. In coordination with the Yemeni government under President Abd Rabbu Mansour Hadi, U.S. special operations forces deployed to the base starting around April 2012, providing training and advisory support to approximately 100-150 Yemeni counterterrorism personnel aimed at disrupting AQAP networks responsible for attacks like the 2009 Fort Hood shooting and the 2010 Christmas Day airline bombing attempt.[30][31] The base served as a forward hub for intelligence collection, joint operations planning, and logistical support, enabling more precise targeting of AQAP leaders and facilitators amid a surge in U.S. drone strikes that killed over 100 AQAP operatives between 2011 and 2014.[32] Al Anad's role extended to hosting U.S. unmanned aerial vehicles, including MQ-9 Reaper drones, for surveillance and potential strike missions against AQAP strongholds, enhancing real-time intelligence on militant movements in Yemen's rugged terrain. This presence facilitated operations that degraded AQAP's operational capacity, such as the June 2011 strike killing AQAP propagandist Anwar al-Awlaki and the 2012 elimination of multiple mid-level commanders, though AQAP retaliated with plots against the base itself. On October 5, 2012, Yemeni troops thwarted a suicide vehicle-borne improvised explosive device (VBIED) attack by AQAP targeting U.S. and Yemeni personnel at Al Anad, killing two assailants and preventing significant casualties.[33][34] U.S. efforts emphasized capacity-building over direct combat, with special forces focusing on mentoring Yemeni units to conduct raids and secure liberated areas from AQAP, reflecting a strategy to minimize American footprint while leveraging local forces against a resilient insurgency. By 2015, however, escalating Houthi advances forced the withdrawal of all U.S. personnel from Al Anad on March 21, amid concerns over base security and the risk of capture. Post-withdrawal, U.S. counterterrorism shifted to remote drone and airstrikes from regional bases, sustaining pressure on AQAPâ€”evidenced by strikes killing over 200 militants since 2015â€”but without the on-ground advisory role Al Anad had enabled.[20][30][32]

Yemeni Government and Coalition Utilization

Following its recapture from Houthi forces on August 4, 2015, by Yemeni pro-government troops supported by Saudi-led coalition airstrikes, Al Anad Air Base served as a central hub for loyalist military logistics and staging operations in southern Yemen.[4][29] The base, Yemen's largest airfield, enabled rapid deployment of government-aligned ground units toward Lahij and Taiz provinces, facilitating advances that linked pro-Hadi forces with Southern Resistance militias after the liberation of Aden.[25] The Saudi-led coalition transformed Al Anad into a fortified forward operating base by mid-October 2015, hosting the Saudi 6th Airborne Brigade, 64th Special Forces Brigade, and United Arab Emirates ground contingents for joint close air support and ground offensives against Houthi-held territories.[12] Coalition engineers expanded runway capacity and defensive perimeters, allowing sustained F-15 and Typhoon fighter sorties alongside Yemeni Air Force remnants for precision strikes on rebel supply lines and command nodes.[35] This utilization underscored the base's strategic value in denying Houthis aerial dominance while bolstering government control over key coastal approaches to Aden. Through 2018, Al Anad supported coalition amphibious and heliborne insertions, including operations to secure Lahij Governorate, though intermittent Houthi counterattacks strained its defensive posture.[12] Yemeni government forces maintained a rotational presence for training and reconnaissance, leveraging the base's Soviet-era hangars for Mirage F1 maintenance despite coalition primacy in operational tempo.[36] By the early 2020s, as coalition ground commitments tapered amid a UN-brokered truce, the facility shifted toward lighter Yemeni-led patrols and intelligence gathering, retaining its role as a deterrent against Houthi incursions in the Aden hinterland.[37]

Adversary Attacks and Defenses

Houthi militants have conducted several drone and missile strikes against Al Anad Air Base, exploiting its strategic value to Yemeni government, Southern Transitional Council (STC), and Saudi-led coalition forces through low-cost, asymmetric aerial tactics that bypass traditional ground defenses. These attacks, often involving Iranian-supplied or modified UAVs such as the Qasef series, have inflicted significant casualties and highlighted vulnerabilities in the base's air defense posture.[38][39] A notable early incident occurred on January 10, 2019, when a Houthi drone targeted a military parade at the base, detonating amid assembled troops and killing six soldiers while wounding senior officers, including Yemen's chief of staff.[40][41] The strike, executed with a modified Qasef-2K UAV, underscored the Houthis' growing precision capabilities against fixed targets during vulnerable moments like gatherings.[38] The most lethal assault took place on August 29, 2021, involving a barrage of drones and ballistic missiles that killed at least 30 pro-government fighters and wounded over 60 others at the base, which houses southern forces aligned against the Houthis.[42][40] Houthi spokesmen claimed the operation used multiple explosive-laden drones to overwhelm defenses, though no immediate interceptions were reported at the site.[43] A smaller-scale Houthi attack near the base in Lahj province on June 5, 2024, killed six government soldiers, demonstrating persistent threats despite coalition control.[44] Al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP) has also targeted Yemeni air bases, including Al Anad, with ground-based raids and vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices (VBIEDs) as early as 2013, aiming to disrupt counterterrorism operations amid the base's prior U.S. usage for drone launches.[45] These assaults capitalized on AQAP's infiltration tactics during periods of instability, such as the 2015 Houthi capture of the facility. Defensive measures at Al Anad have relied primarily on ground fortifications, troop deployments from Yemeni National Army, STC militias, and Saudi-led coalition units, with limited success against standoff aerial weapons due to the challenges of detecting low-altitude, slow-speed drones.[46] Post-attack responses have included vows by southern separatists for retaliation and improved perimeter security, alongside regional Saudi intercepts of Houthi projectiles, but the base remains exposed to saturation attacks that exceed available radar and interceptor capacities.[46][47] U.S. forces, which maintained a presence until their 2015 evacuation amid Houthi advances, did not deploy permanent air defense systems there, leaving reliance on ad hoc coalition assets.[2]

Controversies and Impacts

Drone Strikes: Effectiveness and Collateral Damage Claims

The United States conducted drone strikes against al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP) from Al Anad Air Base, where CIA-operated unmanned aerial vehicles were stationed alongside American special forces, as part of broader counterterrorism operations authorized by the Yemeni government prior to the Houthi advance in 2015.[48][49] These strikes targeted AQAP militants and infrastructure, contributing to the elimination of key figures such as Anwar al-Awlaki in 2011, though not directly from Al Anad, and subsequent operations that reportedly killed over 100 AQAP members in Yemen between 2002 and 2015.[50] US assessments, including those from the Bureau of Investigative Journalism and New America Foundation tallies, indicate that drone campaigns disrupted AQAP's operational tempo, preventing attacks on the US homeland and degrading leadership structures, with strikes peaking in 2012 under Yemeni cooperation.[32] However, AQAP demonstrated resilience, expanding territorial control in southern Yemen during periods of reduced strikes, such as after the 2015 base evacuation, suggesting that while tactically effective in targeting individuals, the program failed to eradicate the group's underlying capabilities or local recruitment driven by governance vacuums.[50] Claims of collateral damage from these Al Anad-linked operations remain contested, with US government sources asserting high precision and minimal civilian harmâ€”estimating fewer than 10% of casualties as non-combatants based on intelligence reviewsâ€”due to signature strikes on militant patterns and Hellfire missile accuracy.[32] Independent investigations, however, document instances of civilian deaths, such as a 2012 strike near Jaar killing at least 12 non-militants in a convoy misidentified as AQAP, and a 2013 attack in al-Majalah that Human Rights Watch verified killed 21 civilians including women and children alongside targeted fighters, attributing errors to faulty intelligence rather than deliberate policy.[51] Yemeni officials and local accounts reported additional unintended casualties near Al Anad's operational radius, fueling anti-US sentiment and AQAP propaganda that portrayed strikes as indiscriminate, though empirical audits like those from the Long War Journal found verified civilian deaths in Yemen strikes averaging under one per operation from 2010-2014.[52] These discrepancies highlight systemic challenges in signature-based targeting, where civilian proximity to militantsâ€”often in shared tribal areasâ€”inevitably risks non-combatant harm, irrespective of technological precision.[50]

Houthi Assaults and Iranian Proxy Dynamics

The Al Anad Air Base has been targeted by Houthi forces in multiple drone and missile strikes since its recapture from rebel control in 2015, reflecting the group's strategy to undermine Saudi-led coalition and affiliated southern Yemeni forces. On January 10, 2019, a Houthi-operated Qasef-2K drone struck a military parade at the base in Lahij province, killing at least six Yemeni officers, including the deputy chief of staff of the Yemeni army and the governor of Lahij, while injuring dozens more.[41] The attack demonstrated the Houthis' growing precision strike capabilities, with the drone originating from Houthi-held territory in northern Yemen. A subsequent major assault occurred on August 29, 2021, when Houthi missiles and drones hit the base, then controlled by the UAE-backed Southern Transitional Council (STC), resulting in at least 30-40 soldiers killed and over 60 wounded.[53][40] Houthi spokesmen claimed responsibility for both operations, framing them as retaliation against coalition airstrikes and southern separatist activities.[54] These assaults underscore the Houthis' reliance on Iranian-supplied weaponry and technical expertise, positioning the group as a de facto proxy in Iran's broader regional confrontation with Saudi Arabia and its allies. United Nations reports and U.S. assessments have documented Iran's transfer of drone components, ballistic missile technology, and training to the Houthis, enabling attacks with ranges exceeding 1,000 kilometers into southern Yemen and Saudi territory.[47] The Qasef-2K model used in the 2019 strike, for instance, derives from Iran's Ababil-2 design, with modifications for loitering munitions, as confirmed by Saudi and U.S. intelligence analyses.[55] Iran has denied direct involvement, attributing Houthi successes to indigenous development, though ballistic missile debris recovered from similar attacks bears hallmarks of Iranian manufacturing, including shared propulsion systems with models like the Qiam-1.[41] Regional actors, including the Arab Parliament, have explicitly held Iran accountable for escalating the conflict through such proxy enablement, arguing it violates UN Security Council Resolution 2216 by arming the Houthis despite an embargo.[56] Causal links between Iranian support and Houthi operational reach are evident in the evolution of these attacks: pre-2015 Houthi capabilities were limited to short-range rocketry, but post-Iranian aid inflowsâ€”estimated by U.S. officials at hundreds of millions in funding and expertiseâ€”the group achieved sustained asymmetric warfare against hardened targets like Al Anad.[57] This proxy dynamic allows Iran to project power without direct confrontation, pressuring Saudi Arabia economically through disrupted logistics and deterring foreign military footprints in Yemen, while the Houthis gain legitimacy as anti-coalition resistors. Saudi-led responses, including intercepts and counterstrikes, have mitigated but not eliminated the threat, with Houthi attacks persisting amid intermittent ceasefires.[58] Empirical data from conflict trackers indicate over 20 documented Houthi drone incursions on southern bases like Al Anad between 2019 and 2021, correlating with spikes in Iranian arms smuggling via dhows from Bandar Abbas.[59]

Strategic Withdrawal and Regional Security Implications

The United States completed the withdrawal of its approximately 100 Special Operations forces from Al Anad Air Base on March 22, 2015, evacuating the personnel amid heightened threats from al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP) militants and Houthi rebels.[20] [60] The move followed an AQAP assault on the nearby city of al-Houta and the broader collapse of Yemeni government control under President Abdrabbuh Mansur Hadi, rendering the base untenable for sustained US operations.[19] This action ended the on-ground US military footprint at the facility, which had served since the early 2010s as a hub for training Yemeni special forces and facilitating intelligence for drone strikes against AQAP targets.[2] The strategic retreat shifted US counterterrorism efforts in Yemen toward remote aerial operations launched from bases in Djibouti and naval assets in the Arabian Sea, reducing direct exposure to ground threats but complicating logistics and real-time intelligence gathering.[61] Post-withdrawal, Houthi forces seized the base in late March 2015, using it to stage attacks until pro-government coalition forces, backed by Saudi Arabia, recaptured it on August 4, 2015.[4] This turnover highlighted vulnerabilities in southern Yemen, enabling Iran-backed Houthis to consolidate logistics routes for smuggled weapons, including ballistic missiles and drones, which later targeted Saudi infrastructure starting in 2015.[62] Regionally, the US disengagement contributed to a perceived power vacuum that prompted Saudi Arabia's military intervention on March 26, 2015, aimed at preventing Houthi encirclement of key southern ports like Aden and curbing Iranian influence along the Saudi border.[63] The absence of US ground support at Al Anad strained Yemeni government defenses against both Houthis and AQAP, fostering conditions for AQAP's temporary territorial gains in 2015â€“2016 before coalition airstrikes degraded them.[64] Over the longer term, Houthi entrenchment post-recaptureâ€”despite repeated assaults like the August 2021 barrage killing over 30 pro-government troopsâ€”has sustained threats to the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, disrupting 12% of global trade and prompting US-UK strikes in 2024â€“2025 to counter Houthi maritime attacks linked to the Israel-Hamas conflict.[65] [66] The withdrawal underscored trade-offs in US policy: prioritizing personnel safety and avoiding entanglement in Yemen's civil war preserved resources for higher-priority threats like ISIS in Iraq and Syria, yet analysts note it emboldened Tehran to escalate proxy support, with over 200 Iranian-supplied missile strikes on Saudi targets by 2019.[59] Saudi and Emirati forces, lacking US forward basing, faced higher operational costs, contributing to war fatigue and a 2023 truce that left Houthis controlling northern Yemen and key terrain near Al Anad.[67] Persistent instability has enabled AQAP resurgence attempts, with the group claiming responsibility for attacks into the 2020s, while Houthi drone innovationsâ€”tested at bases like Al Anadâ€”have extended threats to regional energy infrastructure and international shipping lanes.[38]

Recent Developments

Post-2020 Attacks and Resilience

On August 29, 2021, Houthi militants executed a coordinated assault on Al Anad Air Base using a ballistic missile followed by two explosive-laden drones targeting a barracks with around 50 pro-government troops in Lahij province.[68][40] The strike killed at least 30 soldiers and wounded over 60 others, primarily members of forces aligned with the Southern Transitional Council and the Yemeni government.[69][70] Houthi spokespersons claimed responsibility, framing the operation as retaliation against coalition-backed positions, while Yemeni military officials attributed the precision to Iranian-supplied weaponry enhancements.[68][71] No major verified Houthi attacks on the base have been publicly documented between 2022 and mid-2025, though the facility remains within range of Houthi drone and missile systems amid ongoing regional hostilities.[38] Al Anad, Yemen's largest air base, has exhibited operational resilience by sustaining its role as a forward operating hub for pro-government forces despite recurrent threats, including the 2021 incident that caused significant but non-crippling damage.[3] In April 2025, a Yemeni Defense Ministry delegation inspected frontline positions and evaluated active operations at Al Anad, confirming its continued functionality for logistics, training, and deployment support in southern Yemen.[72] This persistence reflects adaptations such as dispersed assets and coalition air cover, which have mitigated Houthi asymmetric tactics without fully neutralizing the base's strategic value to anti-Houthi efforts.[73]

2025 Munitions Detonation

On July 24, 2025, multiple explosions rocked a weapons depot at Al Anad Air Base in Lahj Governorate, southern Yemen, due to the unintended detonation of stored munitions.[66] A spokesman for Yemen's Defence Ministry confirmed the cause as munitions ignition within the depot, emphasizing that the base, under control of the internationally recognized Yemeni government, sustained no casualties or significant structural damage.[66] [74] Eyewitness accounts described hearing a series of intense blasts around evening hours, followed by thick smoke plumes visible from surrounding areas approximately 60 kilometers north of Aden.[75] Local residents reported observing secondary effects, including rockets and artillery shells ejecting erratically from the site amid the chain reaction.[5] Initial probes by base authorities suggested an electrical short circuit in the depot's storage area as the likely trigger, though no official final determination was released by late July.[76] The incident prompted temporary evacuations in nearby villages but did not escalate into broader conflict, with no claims of responsibility from Houthi forces or other adversaries despite the base's history of prior attacks.[66] [77] Yemen's military leadership downplayed long-term operational impacts, attributing the event to routine storage risks in a conflict zone rather than sabotage.[74]
