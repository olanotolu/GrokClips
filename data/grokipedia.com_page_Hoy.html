Hoy

Hoy is the second-largest island in the Orkney archipelago of Scotland, covering approximately 57 square miles and distinguished by its rugged, mountainous terrain that earned it the Old Norse name Háey, meaning "high island".[1][2] Its northern moorlands rise to Ward Hill, the highest point in Orkney at 481 metres, while the western cliffs, sculpted by relentless Atlantic gales, include some of Britain's tallest coastal formations.[3][4] The island's most iconic feature is the Old Man of Hoy, a 137-metre sea stack of red sandstone formed by erosion and first scaled in 1966, symbolizing Hoy's dramatic geology and appeal to climbers and naturalists.[5] With a small resident population sustained by farming and tourism, Hoy preserves Neolithic tombs like the Dwarfie Stane alongside World War II naval remnants from nearby Scapa Flow, underscoring its layered human history amid unspoiled wilderness.[2][6]

Linguistic and nautical meanings

Interjection and exclamation

"Hoy" serves as an interjection to attract attention, hail a person or vessel, or urge animals forward, with roots in nautical and rural calls.[7][8] Its usage appears in Middle English texts from the 14th century, as in driving livestock or signaling from afar.[7] The term derives from Middle Dutch hoey, a cry of similar purpose, reflecting early trade and maritime influences on English.[8] Historically, "hoy" functioned as a precursor to "ahoy," which emerged in the 18th century by prefixing a- to the base form for emphasis in seafaring greetings.[9] Records from 1393, such as in William Langland's Piers Plowman, illustrate its exclamatory role in everyday or directive speech.[10] By the 15th century, it extended to shouts between ships or crew members, underscoring its practical utility in noisy or distant environments.[11] Though archaic today, "hoy" persists in dialectal or literary echoes, distinct from modern casual interjections like "hey."[12]

Vessel type

A hoy refers to a small, single-masted coastal sailing vessel or heavy barge employed for freight transport, commonly displacing up to 60 tons and rigged as a sloop with a gaff mainsail that could be fitted with or without a boom.[13] These vessels were prevalent in European short-sea trade routes, particularly from the 15th century onward, with designs evolving from Dutch origins featuring a spritsail mainsail, short bowsprit, jib, and foresail for maneuverability in shallow or confined waters.[14] Hoys served utilitarian roles, including harbor work such as carrying anchors or materiel, and were adaptable for oar propulsion when sails were insufficient.[15] In British contexts, hoys operated extensively along the Thames River and Kent coastline for coasting trade, functioning as fore-and-aft-rigged craft akin to sloops but optimized for heavy loads in estuarine and nearshore environments.[16] Their flat-bottomed or barge-like hulls facilitated beaching for loading/unloading, though this limited seaworthiness in open waters compared to larger merchantmen.[17] By the 18th and 19th centuries, hoys had largely been supplanted by steam-powered barges and improved canal infrastructure, rendering the type obsolete for commercial use.[14]

Geographical locations

Hoy, Orkney Islands

Hoy is the second-largest island in the Orkney archipelago, located off the north coast of Scotland in the North Atlantic Ocean.[18] Its name derives from the Old Norse term Háey, meaning "high island," reflecting its rugged, elevated terrain compared to the flatter Orkney Mainland.[1] The island covers approximately 143 square kilometers and had a population of 419 in recent estimates covering Hoy and adjacent Walls.[18] Hoy lies south of the Mainland, separated by the Pentland Firth, and forms part of the southern Orkney group alongside smaller islands like Graemsay and Flotta. Geographically, Hoy features dramatic sea cliffs and moorland, with Ward Hill as its highest point at 481 meters (1,578 feet) above sea level, marking the summit of Orkney.[3] The western coast includes the iconic Old Man of Hoy, a 137-meter (449-foot) sea stack of red sandstone atop a basalt plinth, formed by marine erosion and recognized as one of Britain's tallest such formations.[5] First scaled in 1966 by climbers Chris Bonington, Rusty MacAlpine, Tom Patey, and Ian Clough, the stack draws adventurers despite erosion risks that reduced nearby stacks in the 1950s.[19] The eastern side borders Scapa Flow, a sheltered natural harbor historically vital for naval operations. Hoy's history traces to Norse settlement from the 8th century, with archaeological evidence of Viking longhouses and integration into the Earldom of Orkney under Norwegian rule until 1468.[20] During the World Wars, Lyness on the southeast coast became a key Royal Navy base overlooking Scapa Flow, where the fleet anchored; the population tripled to over 20,000 island-wide due to military influx, with Lyness hosting oil storage and a cemetery for over 1,300 war dead.[21] The Scapa Flow Visitor Centre at Lyness, housed in a former pumphouse, preserves engines, artifacts, and exhibits on the base's role, including the 1919 scuttling of the interned German High Seas Fleet.[20] Today, settlements like Lyness (ferry terminal to Houton on Mainland) and Rackwick (near the Old Man trailhead) support a sparse community reliant on agriculture, particularly sheep farming on moorlands.[22] Tourism centers on natural sites and heritage, with walking paths to Ward Hill and the sea stack attracting visitors via ferries from Stromness or Kirkwall.[5] A proposed community wind farm south of Wee Fea includes six 149-meter turbines with 28.8 MW capacity, aimed at generating local revenue for services upon completion around 2027.[23] The Orkney Islands Council leads this project to leverage wind resources, supplementing traditional sectors amid Orkney's broader economic shift from fishing.[24]

Other places named Hoy

Hoy is the name of an uninhabited island in Lake Constance (Bodensee), Germany, located about 400 meters east of Lindau Island in the Bay of Reutin and 100 meters south of the mainland shore. Covering roughly 53 square meters, it functions as a small artificial bathing island, created in the 1920s by depositing landfill material, and is reachable primarily by boat or swimming from nearby areas.[25][26] Minor localities named Hoy are recorded in the United States, specifically in West Virginia and Mississippi, as well as in the Nord region of Cameroon. These appear to be small, sparsely populated or historical communities with limited documentation beyond geographic databases.[27]

Personal names

As a given name

Hoy is an uncommon masculine given name with roots in Old Norse, deriving from "hoyr," signifying "high" or "hill," often linked to geographical features like elevated terrain.[28] In Scottish contexts, particularly associated with the Orkney Islands, it stems from "Háey," translating to "high island."[29] Danish influences contribute a connotation of "tall" via "høj," reflecting a nickname for stature.[30] These etymologies underscore its topographic origins rather than occupational or patronymic ones prevalent in its more common use as a surname. Historical records indicate sporadic use as a first name, predominantly in English-speaking regions with Scandinavian or Celtic heritage. U.S. Social Security Administration data from the late 19th to 20th centuries show it as exclusively male, though ranking outside the top 1,000 names, with peak occurrences in the early 1900s numbering fewer than 50 annually.[31] Its rarity persists into modern times, absent from contemporary top baby name lists compiled by agencies like the SSA.[32] The most prominent bearer is William Ellsworth Hoy (1862–1961), a pioneering deaf American professional baseball player who competed in Major League Baseball from 1888 to 1902, amassing 1,792 hits, 438 stolen bases, and a .287 batting average across teams including the Washington Nationals and Cincinnati Reds. Despite being born hearing but losing it to meningitis at age three, Hoy adapted innovative signals for umpires, influencing modern hand gestures in the sport, and holds the MLB record for most career putouts by a center fielder (2,044).[33] No other individuals with the given name Hoy have achieved comparable historical or cultural prominence in verified records.

As a surname

Hoy is an Irish surname derived from the Gaelic Ó hEochaidh, translating to "descendant of Eochaidh," a personal name meaning "horseman" or linked to Old German roots for "horse rider."[34][35] Early records trace Irish Hoy families to Tipperary and Ulster, with migrations during the 19th century, including to Pennsylvania amid events like the Tithe War.[34][36] In Scotland, the name appears as a locational surname from the Orkney island of Hoy, recorded as "Haey" in the 13th century and denoting a "high island."[37] English variants, especially in Essex, emerged as an occupational nickname for sailors, from Middle Dutch hoey referring to a "cargo ship."[38] A Danish form, Høy, served as a nickname for a tall individual, from høj meaning "high."[39] Globally, Hoy bearers number approximately 50,000, with 49% in the Americas—primarily North America (47%) and Anglo-North America (44%)—and significant concentrations in the United States, where 85.3% of Hoys identify as White and 47.4% trace British or Irish ancestry.[40][41] U.S. census data from 1880 show the highest Hoy populations there, following 1840 immigration waves from the UK, Canada, and Scotland.[38] Notable individuals include Sir Christopher Hoy (born 1976), a Scottish track cyclist who won six Olympic gold medals between 2004 and 2012, and pioneered the use of the Sir Chris Hoy Velodrome in Glasgow.[42] William Ellsworth "Dummy" Hoy (1862–1961), an American Major League Baseball outfielder, was the first deaf player in the majors, compiling 1,946 hits over 17 seasons from 1888 to 1903 despite communication barriers.[43] Other bearers encompass Charles Sew Hoy (1838–1902), a New Zealand merchant and gold-dredging pioneer of Chinese descent who advanced mechanical dredging techniques in Otago.[44]

Media and entertainment

Newspapers and publications

In the United States, Hoy was a chain of Spanish-language daily newspapers published by the Tribune Company (later Tribune Publishing). The New York edition launched on November 16, 1998, and rapidly grew to lead the Spanish-language market in the New York designated market area by March 2001, with subsequent editions in Chicago and Los Angeles.[45][46] By January 2004, the combined editions reported a daily circulation of 94,000 copies, positioning Hoy as the second-largest Spanish-language newspaper network in the country after La Opinión.[47] The publications encountered legal issues in December 2007, when Hoy Publications, LLC, and Newsday, Inc., agreed to forfeit $15 million to the U.S. Department of Justice to resolve a criminal investigation into a scheme that allegedly inflated circulation figures through fictitious subscribers and kickbacks.[48] The New York edition ceased operations in 2010 amid Tribune's financial difficulties, and the remaining editions, including Chicago, were shuttered by November 2019.[46] El Diario de Hoy is a longstanding morning daily newspaper in El Salvador, first published in San Salvador on June 1, 1936. It circulates nationwide and has maintained a prominent position among the country's print media, alongside competitors like La Prensa Gráfica.[49][50] The outlet has faced operational challenges, including the dismissal of over 25 journalists and staff in January 2017 amid cost-cutting measures.[51] Diario Hoy (full title Diario Hoy en la Noticia) operates as a regional newspaper in La Plata, Buenos Aires Province, Argentina, focusing on local, provincial, and national coverage. It continues to publish daily content online and in print, emphasizing unfiltered reporting on current events.[52] In Cuba, Noticias de Hoy functioned as a Spanish-language newspaper from its establishment in 1938 until its cessation on October 2, 1965, after which copies were reportedly destroyed within the country. It was affiliated with leftist political groups during its run.[53]

Television programs

Hoy is a Mexican morning variety television program produced by Televisa and aired on the Las Estrellas network. It debuted on August 3, 1998, initially as a two-hour show expanding over time to cover lifestyle topics, current events, cooking demonstrations, and live performances.[54] The format emphasizes interactive segments, audience participation, and guest appearances by celebrities, musicians, and experts, positioning it as a daily wake-up staple for Mexican viewers.[55] The program's hosting roster has evolved, with long-term figures like Andrea Legarreta anchoring since the early years, joined by Galilea Montijo, Raúl Araiza, Arath de la Torre, Andrea Escalona, Paul Stanley, and Tania Rincón in recent seasons.[54] These hosts facilitate a blend of light-hearted banter, contests such as dance competitions, and informational content on health, fashion, and pop culture, often drawing high ratings in its time slot due to its accessible, family-oriented appeal.[55] Episodes typically run for three to four hours, incorporating real-time viewer calls and social media integration for broader engagement.[56] Internationally, Hoy content has been syndicated or adapted for networks like Univision in the United States, where it retains a focus on Spanish-language entertainment from Mexico, including gossip, music previews, and variety acts.[57] Despite criticisms of sensationalism in some segments, the show maintains a consistent broadcast schedule, adapting to digital platforms like YouTube for clips and full episodes to extend its reach.[56] Another program bearing the name is Hoy mismo, a Spanish talk show that aired from 1971 to 1987, featuring discussions on politics, society, and culture with notable guests, though it garnered less enduring prominence compared to its Mexican counterpart.

Music titles and works

Hoy-Hoy! is a compilation album by the American rock band Little Feat, released in August 1981 by Warner Bros. Records. It features a mix of previously unreleased live recordings, studio outtakes, and earlier tracks spanning the band's career up to their 1979 disbandment following the death of founder Lowell George.[58] "Hoy" is a song composed by Peruvian singer-songwriter Gian Marco Zignago and first recorded in Spanish by Gloria Estefan for her 2003 album Unwrapped. The track, emphasizing themes of living in the present, was released as a single with an accompanying music video filmed in Peru's Machu Picchu and has accumulated over 97 million views on YouTube as of November 2024.[59][60] "Hoy" is also the title of a 2024 single by Argentine artist Valentino Merlo featuring The La Planta, which debuted at number one on the Billboard Argentina Hot 100 chart on September 12, 2024, and held the top position for 11 consecutive weeks.[61] Additional musical works include the 2019 album Hoy by Puerto Rican singer MYA, comprising reggaeton and Latin pop tracks, and various other songs titled "Hoy" by artists such as Colombian singer Maluma from his 2012 self-titled album.[62][63]

Other uses

Spanish language term

"Hoy" is the Spanish adverb denoting "today," referring to the current day or the present time in a general sense.[64] It functions as a demonstrative adverb, commonly used in sentences such as "Hoy es lunes" (Today is Monday) to specify the temporal context of the day in question.[64] When preceded by prepositions, it indicates recency, as in "pan de hoy" (bread from today).[64] The term originates from the Latin adverb hodie, a contraction of the phrase hoc die ("on this day"), combining hoc (this, ablative of hic) and die (ablative of dies, meaning day).[65] This evolution reflects phonetic simplification in Vulgar Latin, where the intervocalic /d/ weakened and the final /e/ was lost, yielding the modern Spanish form through regular sound changes in the Iberian Romance languages.[65] Cognates appear in other Romance languages, such as Italian oggi and Portuguese hoje, both tracing to the same Latin root, underscoring a shared Indo-European heritage for temporal adverbs.[66] In contemporary usage, "hoy" appears in idiomatic expressions like "hoy por hoy" (for the time being or currently), emphasizing provisional states that may change.[64] The Real Academia Española recognizes its role in both literal and figurative temporal references, without significant regional variations in core meaning across Spanish-speaking territories.[64]

Acronyms and abbreviations

HOY serves as an initialism for various organizations, roles, and technical terms. In education, particularly within British secondary schools, HOY denotes Head of Year (often stylized as HoY), a pastoral leadership position overseeing the welfare, discipline, and academic progress of students in a specific year group.[67] In equestrian sports, HOY refers to Horse of the Year, the name of New Zealand's premier annual show jumping and equestrian event, held since 1951 and attracting international competitors.[68] HOY is the abbreviation for House of Yahweh, a religious assembly established in 1980 by Yisrayl Hawkins in Eula, Texas, which emphasizes strict observance of Old Testament laws and has faced allegations of cult-like practices from critics.[69] In public health, HOY stands for Helping Our Youth, a curriculum-based program by Doña Ana County Health & Human Services in New Mexico, focusing on alcohol and drug prevention education for middle and high school students through interactive sessions and health fairs.[70] Less commonly, HOY designates High Oriented Yarn, a type of nylon filament yarn with enhanced tensile strength due to molecular alignment during manufacturing, used in industrial applications like tires and ropes.[71]
