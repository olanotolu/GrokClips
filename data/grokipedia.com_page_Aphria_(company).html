Aphria Inc.


Aphria Inc. was a Canadian cannabis company founded in 2013 and headquartered in Leamington, Ontario, that focused on producing and distributing medical and recreational cannabis products following Canada's legalization of recreational marijuana in 2018.[1][2]
The company secured its initial production license for its Leamington facility in November 2014 and pursued aggressive expansion through domestic cultivation sites and international acquisitions in markets such as Latin America and Europe.[3]
Aphria achieved notable scale as one of Canada's largest cannabis producers by revenue prior to its merger, but it faced substantial controversies, including short-seller allegations in 2018 of inflated acquisition values involving purportedly connected parties in Argentina and Colombia, which triggered regulatory probes, shareholder lawsuits, and a CAD 30 million class-action settlement approved in 2025 over claims of misleading disclosures.[4][5][6]
In December 2020, Aphria announced a merger with Tilray Inc., which closed in May 2021, forming Tilray Brands, Inc.â€”the world's largest cannabis company by pro forma revenue of approximately C$874 million at the timeâ€”with Aphria's CEO Irwin Simon leading the combined entity.[7][8]

Founding and Early Development

Inception and Initial Licensing (2013â€“2015)

Aphria Inc. was founded in 2013 in Leamington, Ontario, by greenhouse operators Cole Cacciavillani and John Cervini, who brought expertise from their prior ventures in flower and produce cultivation, including CF Flowers and Lakeside Produce, respectively.[9][10] The company established its initial operations at a facility in Leamington, leveraging the region's established greenhouse infrastructure to pursue opportunities in medical cannabis production amid Canada's evolving regulatory framework under the Marihuana Medical Access Regulations, which preceded the Marihuana for Medical Purposes Regulations (MMPR) introduced in 2014.[3] Vic Neufeld, a former CEO of Jamieson Laboratories, served as Aphria's CEO during its early development, guiding the transition from traditional horticulture to licensed cannabis cultivation.[4] In preparation for MMPR compliance, Aphria applied for producer licensing from Health Canada, which required stringent security, quality control, and operational standards for medical cannabis. The company operated under a partial license at its Leamington facility while awaiting full approval, focusing on facility construction and process validation to meet federal requirements for cultivation, processing, and distribution.[11] On November 26, 2014, Health Canada granted Aphria its initial full license to produce and sell medical cannabis under the MMPR, positioning it among Canada's early licensed producers and enabling greenhouse-based cultivation advantages over indoor methods used by competitors.[11][3] Aphria commenced wholesale shipments of medical cannabis in April 2015, marking the operationalization of its licensed production and entry into the Canadian medical market.[12] This period solidified the company's focus on cost-efficient, large-scale greenhouse growing, with initial output directed toward patients via Health Canada's authorized framework, though sales volumes remained modest as the producer network expanded.[9] By mid-2015, Aphria had validated its model as one of the lowest-cost producers, attributing efficiencies to its founders' horticultural background and the Leamington site's natural light and climate advantages.[9]

Expansion in Canadian Medical Cannabis Market (2016â€“2017)

In 2016, Aphria advanced its production capabilities under the newly implemented Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes Regulations (ACMPR), which replaced the Marihuana for Medical Purposes Regulations in August 2016 and emphasized licensed producers supplying medical cannabis directly to patients. On June 2, 2016, the company announced a capital project to more than double its annual growing capacity from 2,500 kilograms to 5,500 kilograms.[13] Later that year, on September 16, 2016, Aphria initiated its Part III Expansion, which tripled its licensed production space from 100,000 square feet to 300,000 square feet to accommodate rising medical demand.[14] These efforts were supported by a November 2016 bought deal financing of C$35 million, the largest capital raise by a Canadian cannabis company at the time, earmarked for facility upgrades and capacity scaling.[15] Regulatory approvals continued to drive growth into 2017. In February 2016, Health Canada had already greenlit the company's Part I Expansion, enhancing initial infrastructure, while on January 16, 2017, Aphria's board approved the $137 million Part IV Expansion project, targeting an increase in greenhouse space to 1 million square feet for long-term medical supply.[3][16] On December 14, 2016, Aphria secured 200 acres of serviced land for $6.24 million to support these initiatives.[3] By May 15, 2017, Health Canada approved the greenhouse component of the Part II Expansion, adding 57,000 square feet and boosting annualized production capacity to approximately 8,000 kilograms.[11] This expansion was bolstered by a $100 million financing round in April 2017, intended to fund production enhancements projected to yield over 75,000 kilograms annually by 2018 at low costs.[17] These developments enabled Aphria to capture a larger share of Canada's medical cannabis market, where demand was surging ahead of anticipated recreational legalization, with the company's fiscal 2017 revenue reaching $20 million amid operational scaling.[18] The focus on efficient, low-cost greenhouse cultivation differentiated Aphria from competitors, positioning it as a key supplier under strict Health Canada oversight.[11]

Business Operations and Growth

Domestic Production and Distribution Facilities

Aphria Inc. operated primary production facilities in Leamington, Ontario, leveraging the region's status as Canada's greenhouse capital for low-cost, sunlight-powered cannabis cultivation. The company's flagship Aphria One facility, located in Leamington, underwent expansion with Health Canada approving full production capacity on March 4, 2019, enabling scalable output of medical and recreational cannabis. [19] This site supported Aphria's emphasis on efficient, large-scale greenhouse operations without prior crop history on adjacent lands. [3] Complementing Aphria One, the Aphria Diamond facility on a 100-acre campus in Leamington featured a 1.3 million square foot greenhouse retrofitted for cannabis production, securing a cultivation license from Health Canada on November 4, 2019, which doubled Aphria's overall production capacity. [20] Designed to yield up to 120,000 kilograms annually upon full operation, Aphria Diamond integrated advanced extraction capabilities planned via a dedicated Centre of Excellence announced in June 2018. [21] [22] Aphria also maintained Broken Coast, an indoor production site on Vancouver Island, British Columbia, specializing in premium strains like "B.C. Bud," with expansions increasing its footprint to support diversified output. [23] [3] For distribution, Aphria relied on strategic partnerships rather than proprietary centers, selecting Great North Distributorsâ€”a Canadian subsidiary of Southern Glazer's Wine & Spiritsâ€”as its exclusive nationwide representative for adult-use cannabis on May 17, 2018, achieving 100% provincial coverage. [24] This network facilitated supply agreements with provincial boards post-legalization, enabling coast-to-coast retail reach for Aphria's portfolio without direct ownership of logistics hubs. [25] Such arrangements aligned with Aphria's focus on production efficiency over vertical integration in distribution.

International Expansion Strategies

Aphria Inc. pursued international expansion primarily through strategic acquisitions to secure footholds in established medical cannabis markets and low-cost production regions, emphasizing vertical integration from cultivation to distribution. The company targeted jurisdictions with progressive regulatory frameworks for medical use, such as Germany and Italy in Europe, and emerging opportunities in Latin America for export-oriented growth. This approach aimed to leverage Canada's expertise in high-quality production while mitigating domestic market saturation risks ahead of recreational legalization.[26][27] In January 2018, Aphria announced the acquisition of Nuuvera Inc., a global medical cannabis firm, for approximately CA$826 million in cash and stock, which closed on March 23, 2018. Nuuvera provided entry into European markets, including a cultivation and distribution license in Italy and partnerships for import and sales in Germany, enabling Aphria to build an international supply chain for medical products. The deal, valued at CA$8.50 per Nuuvera share (including CA$1.00 cash and 0.3546 Aphria shares), positioned Aphria Internationalâ€”a new subsidiaryâ€”as a vehicle for global branding and off-take agreements, with Nuuvera contributing existing export contracts from Aphria's Canadian facilities.[26][28][29] Further expansion into Latin America occurred on July 17, 2018, when Aphria agreed to acquire assets from Scythian Biosciences Corp. for about US$194 million, closing in September 2018. These included Colcanna S.A.S. in Colombia for cultivation and processing, ABP S.A. in Argentina for distribution in a market with over 100,000 medical patients, and Marigold in Jamaica for R&D and production. The moves established low-cost outdoor growing capabilities in Colombiaâ€”South America's most advanced cannabis marketâ€”and positioned Aphria for exports to Europe and beyond, with on-site due diligence confirming operational viability in Colombia, Argentina, Jamaica, and Brazil.[30][31][32] In Europe, Aphria strengthened its German presence on November 8, 2018, by acquiring CC Pharma GmbH, a major pharmaceutical wholesaler distributing to over 13,000 pharmacies, for up to â‚¬48 million (â‚¬18.92 million cash plus earn-out based on future EBITDA). The transaction closed on January 9, 2019, integrating CC Pharma's import and logistics infrastructure to handle medical cannabis supply from Aphria's facilities, including those in Canada and Colombia. Additionally, Aphria secured one of three German federal licenses for domestic medical cannabis cultivation, receiving five cultivation slots to support localized production amid growing demand. This acquisition enhanced end-to-end control in Germany's â‚¬400 million-plus medical cannabis import market.[33][34][35]

Product Portfolio and Innovation

Aphria's medical cannabis portfolio centered on dried flower produced via large-scale greenhouse cultivation and cannabis-derived extracts such as oils, emphasizing consistency and quality for therapeutic use.[36] These products were distributed through Health Canada's Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes program, with offerings including various strains selected for cannabinoid profiles suited to conditions like pain management and nausea relief.[37] Following the October 2018 legalization of recreational cannabis in Canada, Aphria diversified into adult-use products under a portfolio of targeted brands launched in September 2018.[38] Solei focused on balanced cannabinoid ratios for everyday wellness; RIFF offered flavored, accessible options for social consumption; Good Supply provided value-oriented core products; Goodfields targeted active lifestyles with functional edibles and beverages; and Broken Coast delivered premium, craft-grown flower from British Columbia.[38] By late 2020, the company expanded formats across these brands to include 510-thread vaporizers, enhancing portability and discretion.[39] In product innovation, Aphria prioritized scalable greenhouse methods over energy-intensive indoor grows, enabling sun-exposed cultivation that reduced costs while maintaining potency through strain-specific optimizations.[40] A key advancement came in June 2018 with an $85 million investment in an extraction facility equipped for CO2, butane, and ethanol processes to produce high-purity concentrates and support derivative innovations like oils and topicals.[41] This infrastructure facilitated launches such as Solei's high-potency topicals in January 2021, including CBD-dominant balms for relaxation, and balanced oils in April 2021 targeting diverse consumer profiles.[42][43] Earlier efforts included harvesting new strains and introducing oils by March 2016 to address growing medical demand.[44]

Financial Performance

Revenue Growth and Key Milestones (2018â€“2020)

Aphria Inc. experienced explosive revenue growth during the 2018â€“2020 period, primarily driven by Canada's federal legalization of recreational cannabis on October 17, 2018, which enabled the company to pivot from a medical-focused producer to a broader consumer packaged goods entity. Net revenue for the fiscal year ended May 31, 2018 (pre-legalization) stood at CAD 36.9 million, reflecting primarily medical cannabis sales.[45] This surged to CAD 237.1 million in fiscal 2019â€”a 542% increaseâ€”fueled by adult-use market entry, initial recreational product launches, and distribution agreements with provincial boards.[45] By fiscal 2020 (ended May 31, 2020), net revenue reached CAD 543.3 million, a 129% year-over-year rise, supported by scaled production, expanded adult-use sales volumes, and early international medical exports despite competitive pricing pressures and excise taxes.[46]

Key milestones underscored this trajectory. In early 2018, Aphria formed a joint venture with Double Diamond Farms (branded Aphria Diamond) to develop over 700,000 square feet of greenhouse capacity in Leamington, Ontario, enhancing domestic production scalability ahead of recreational launch.[47] The company also acquired Broken Coast Cannabis in June 2018 for CAD 55 million, securing premium medical-grade supply and cultivation expertise.[48] Post-legalization, Aphria rapidly rolled out adult-use brands like Solei and Good Supply, achieving record quarterly adult-use gross revenue of CAD 56.7 million in Q4 fiscal 2020.[49] Internationally, the December 2018 acquisition of Nuuvera Holdings for CAD 425 million provided entry into Germany and other markets via medicinal imports, while the February 2019 purchase of CC Pharma bolstered European distribution infrastructure.[47] Further expansions included Health Canada approvals for Aphria One facility upgrades in 2019, increasing licensed production to over 255,000 kg annually by 2020.[50] In fiscal 2020, Aphria secured medicinal cannabis supply deals in Latin America, including investments in Colombia and Argentina, diversifying beyond Canada amid domestic market saturation.[46] These steps, coupled with four consecutive quarters of positive adjusted EBITDA starting in Q3 fiscal 2019, marked Aphria's shift toward operational efficiency, though growth was tempered by industry-wide oversupply and regulatory hurdles.[51]

Profitability Challenges and Market Volatility

Despite achieving substantial revenue growthâ€”rising from $237.1 million in fiscal year 2019 to $543.3 million in fiscal year 2020, a 129% increaseâ€”Aphria Inc. grappled with persistent net losses driven by aggressive expansion investments and operational inefficiencies common in the nascent Canadian cannabis market.[52] For fiscal 2019, the company reported a net loss of $16.5 million, reversing a $29.4 million net income from the prior year, amid heightened selling, general, and administrative expenses tied to scaling production capacity ahead of recreational demand.[53] These challenges were amplified by industry-wide factors, including slower-than-expected consumer adoption, persistent black market competition, and an oversupply of cannabis flower that depressed wholesale prices and strained margins.[54][40] Significant one-time impairments further eroded profitability, particularly from international ventures. In the third quarter of fiscal 2019 (ended February 28, 2019), Aphria recorded a $108.2 million net loss, primarily attributable to non-cash asset impairments on Latin American operations following revised financial forecasts and higher discount rates.[55][56] Similarly, the fourth quarter of fiscal 2020 (ended May 31, 2020) saw a $98.8 million net loss, including a $64 million impairment linked to COVID-19 disruptions in international assets.[49][57] While select quarters, such as the first of fiscal 2020, delivered net income of $16.4 million, these were outliers amid broader cost pressures from biological asset fair value adjustments, share-based compensation, and facility buildouts that outpaced revenue realization.[58] Aphria's stock price exhibited extreme volatility, mirroring the cannabis sector's boom-and-bust cycles fueled by legalization hype, regulatory uncertainties, and profitability doubts. Shares peaked near $25 CAD in October 2018 amid pre-legalization optimism but plummeted over 70% by early 2019 following a December 2018 short-seller report alleging inflated acquisition values, exacerbating investor concerns over cash burn and execution risks.[59][4] By December 2019, the stock had fallen to approximately $4.85 CAD, reflecting a 69% drop in three months amid Cannabis 2.0 rollout disappointments and broader sector oversupply.[60] The company's annual disclosures highlighted such price swings as inherent risks, influenced by speculative trading, U.S. reform speculation, and revelations of underwhelming recreational sales volumes that undermined growth narratives.[23]

Controversies and Criticisms

Short-Seller Report and Acquisition Scrutiny (2018)

On December 3, 2018, short-selling firm Hindenburg Research, in collaboration with Quintessential Capital Management, published a report titled "Aphria: A Shell Game with a Cannabis Business on the Side," accusing Aphria Inc. of engaging in a scheme involving overvalued acquisitions in Latin America.[4] The report focused on Aphria's September 27, 2018, closure of a transaction acquiring assets through LATAM Holdings Inc., including cultivation facilities and licenses in Colombia, Argentina, and Jamaica, for which Aphria paid over C$280 million in cash and shares.[61] Hindenburg alleged that these assets were nearly worthless, with evidence of insider self-dealing involving connected parties, estimating that at least 50% of Aphria's C$1.46 billion in net assets had been diverted to grossly inflated "investments."[4] The report triggered immediate market scrutiny, causing Aphria's shares to plummet nearly 28% on the Toronto Stock Exchange, closing at around C$10.50 from a prior level above C$14.[62] Aphria responded the same day, describing the allegations as "false and defamatory" and affirming that its board had conducted due diligence, including site visits to Colombia, Argentina, Jamaica, and Brazil, while obtaining a fairness opinion from independent financial advisors.[63] The company emphasized the strategic value of the LATAM assets for future medical cannabis exports, particularly from Colombia, and rejected claims of undisclosed conflicts, stating that all transactions were at arm's length.[64] In response to the controversy, Aphria's board formed a special committee on December 6, 2018, comprising independent directors to investigate the LATAM acquisitions and related-party allegations.[65] The scrutiny extended to proposed class-action lawsuits filed by December 18, 2018, alleging that Aphria made false and misleading statements about the LATAM Holdings deal, including inadequate disclosures on asset valuations and potential conflicts.[66] These events highlighted broader concerns in the cannabis sector regarding opaque international expansions amid rapid growth pressures ahead of Canada's recreational legalization.[65]

Securities Class Actions and Regulatory Investigations

In response to short-seller reports questioning the legitimacy and valuation of Aphria's acquisitions of Nuuvera Inc. (announced January 29, 2018) and LATAM assets (announced July 17, 2018), investors filed securities class actions alleging that Aphria made false and misleading statements in its public disclosures about these transactions.[67] The class period for the primary Canadian action spanned January 29 to December 3, 2018, during which Aphria's share price declined significantly on March 22 and December 3, 2018, following disclosures that purportedly corrected prior misrepresentations regarding acquisition values, related-party conflicts, and asset quality.[67] A global class action consolidated claims from shareholders who acquired Aphria securities (TSX: APHA; NYSE: APHA) during this period, asserting violations of secondary market liability under Ontario securities law.[68] Aphria's board formed an independent special committee in late 2018 to review the contested acquisitions, engaging external counsel and forensic accountants.[69] The committee's February 15, 2019, report concluded that the LATAM acquisition prices were within an acceptable range compared to peer transactions, though it identified elevated costs and undisclosed conflicts of interest involving certain directors and insiders connected to the sellers.[69][70] Specifically, one director failed to disclose conflicting interests, and the review noted that while no fraud was found, the deals enriched related parties at potentially unfavorable terms for shareholders.[70] This internal probe partially addressed short-seller allegations from firms like Hindenburg Research but did not fully resolve investor concerns, contributing to ongoing litigation.[4] The class actions culminated in a CAD $30 million settlement approved by the Ontario Superior Court on March 27, 2025, to compensate eligible class members without any admission of liability by Aphria or its former officers and directors, who denied all wrongdoing.[71][67] U.S.-based claims were pursued separately as federal securities fraud actions but aligned with the global resolution framework.[72] No major enforcement actions resulted from regulatory probes by bodies like the Ontario Securities Commission (OSC) or U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) directly targeting Aphria's disclosures, though the OSC monitored the matter amid broader cannabis sector scrutiny.[73] Separate SEC charges in 2022 involved individuals for insider trading ahead of Aphria-related announcements, but these did not implicate the company itself.[74] Health Canada issued citations to Aphria in 2018 and 2020 for operational noncompliance in production and distribution, distinct from securities issues.[75]

Responses and Internal Reviews

In response to the December 3, 2018, short-seller report by Citron Research alleging fraudulent elements in Aphria's Latin American acquisitions, the company issued statements describing the claims as "false and defamatory" and affirmed its reliance on independent fairness opinions from financial advisors during the transaction approvals.[76][77] Aphria emphasized that its board had received comprehensive financial advice and proceeded with due diligence.[76] On December 6, 2018, Aphria established a Special Committee comprising independent directors to conduct an internal review of the governance processes surrounding the LATAM Holdings Inc. acquisition, engaging external legal counsel to assist.[78] The committee's investigation, completed by February 15, 2019, determined that while Aphria paid a premium for the assetsâ€”deemed higher than some comparable dealsâ€”the transactions fell within an acceptable valuation range relative to competitors' acquisitions in emerging markets.[69] It found no substantiation for allegations of fraud, self-dealing, or material undisclosed conflicts of interest, though it noted that certain directors had prior indirect connections to the sellers, which had been disclosed to the board.[69][79] Regarding subsequent securities class action lawsuits filed in Canada and the United States alleging misrepresentations in public disclosures tied to the 2018 acquisitions, Aphria and its officers consistently denied liability, asserting that all statements were accurate and supported by due diligence.[67][80] The company defended the actions through certification proceedings and expert reports, covering defense costs for underwriters initially while contesting the claims' merits.[81] In 2025, Aphria (by then merged into Tilray Brands Inc.) reached a CAD 30 million settlement to resolve the consolidated Canadian proceedings, explicitly without any admission of wrongdoing or liability, ahead of a scheduled trial.[82][68] Similar U.S. actions were addressed through parallel defenses, with no regulatory enforcement actions resulting in findings against the company.[67]

Merger with Tilray

Negotiation and Announcement (2020)

On February 17, 2020, Aphria Inc. and Tilray, Inc. entered into an updated mutual non-disclosure agreement, facilitating preliminary discussions on potential strategic combinations amid industry consolidation pressures in the cannabis sector.[83] These talks, which had been paused earlier due to the COVID-19 pandemic, resumed and intensified in the fall of 2020, with the companies exchanging drafts of a potential merger agreement between October 9 and October 12, 2020.[84] Negotiations focused on creating a scaled entity with complementary assets, including Aphria's strong Canadian and international distribution networks and Tilray's established medical cannabis operations in Europe and the U.S., aiming to enhance global market position and operational efficiencies.[85] By December 15, 2020, the parties finalized and executed a definitive arrangement agreement, structured as a reverse takeover where Tilray would nominally acquire Aphria, but Aphria shareholders would receive 0.8381 shares of the new Tilray common stock for each Aphria share, resulting in Aphria holders owning approximately 62% of the combined company while Tilray shareholders retained 38%.[8][83] The all-stock transaction implied a 23% premium to Tilray's closing price of $7.87 on December 15, 2020, valuing the deal at roughly C$4.8 billion in equity.[86] The agreement included standard conditions such as shareholder and regulatory approvals, with the combined entity projected to generate pro forma trailing twelve-month revenue of C$874 million (US$685 million) as of September 30, 2020, positioning it as the world's largest cannabis company by revenue.[8] The merger was publicly announced on December 16, 2020, via a joint press release emphasizing synergies in production, R&D, and international expansion to capitalize on emerging markets and regulatory tailwinds.[8][87] Market reaction was positive for Tilray, with its shares surging over 25% in pre-market trading on the announcement day, reflecting investor optimism about the deal's potential to consolidate fragmented competition and improve bargaining power with strategic partners.[88] Leadership of the new Tilray would feature Irwin D. Simon, Aphria's CEO, as executive chairman and CEO, with Brendan Kennedy, Tilray's CEO, transitioning to a board role.[8]

Completion and Integration (2021)

The merger between Aphria Inc. and Tilray Inc. was completed on April 30, 2021, after obtaining necessary shareholder approvals from both companies' meetings held in April and requisite regulatory clearances.[7][89] Under the terms of the arrangement, each Aphria common share was exchanged for 0.8381 shares of Tilray common stock, granting former Aphria shareholders approximately 62% ownership of the combined entity on a fully diluted basis.[7][90] The transaction created a global cannabis company with pro forma trailing twelve-month revenue of US$685 million (C$874 million) as of September 30, 2020, and a market capitalization of approximately US$8.2 billion based on closing stock prices on April 30, 2021.[7][8] Post-closing, the integrated company retained the Tilray name and NASDAQ ticker (TLRY), with Aphria's CEO, Irwin D. Simon, assuming the role of chief executive officer of the combined entity, supported by a unified board of directors.[7][90] Integration efforts commenced immediately, emphasizing operational synergies projected to yield US$78 million (C$100 million) in annual pre-tax cost savings within 24 months through supply chain optimization, administrative efficiencies, and facility rationalization across the companies' complementary portfolios in medical and adult-use cannabis, as well as international and beverage alcohol segments.[83][91] As of the fiscal year-end May 31, 2021, these initiatives remained in early stages, with management prioritizing the consolidation of production, distribution, and R&D capabilities to enhance global market presence in regions including Canada, Europe, Australia, and Latin America.[91] No significant disruptions to ongoing operations were reported during the initial integration phase.[7]

Post-Merger Outcomes and Ongoing Operations

The merger between Tilray, Inc. and Aphria Inc. was completed on May 3, 2021, forming Tilray Brands, Inc., which positioned the combined entity as the world's largest cannabis company by pro forma revenue at the time.[7] Post-merger integration efforts focused on realizing approximately US$78 million in annual pre-tax cost synergies through operational efficiencies, supply chain optimization, and facility consolidations, though challenges such as system integration and management of combined global operations persisted as noted in regulatory filings.[92][93] Financial outcomes reflected industry volatility and strategic shifts, with fiscal year 2025 (ended May 31, 2025) net revenue reaching $821 million, equivalent to $834 million in constant currency, though impacted by $35 million from deliberate divestitures and portfolio adjustments.[94] Gross margins improved across segments, including a cannabis gross margin of 44% in the fourth quarter, driven by higher-margin international sales and cost controls, while adjusted EBITDA rose to $10.2 million in the first quarter of fiscal 2026.[95][96] However, a $1.27 billion net loss in the fourth quarter of fiscal 2025 stemmed largely from non-cash impairment charges tied to merger-related assets.[97] Ongoing operations emphasize diversification beyond cannabis into beverages, wellness, and entertainment, with acquisitions such as four craft beer brands from Molson Coors in fiscal 2025 enhancing the beverage segment.[98] In Canada, key production facilities including Aphria One and Aphria Diamond were fully utilized by early 2025 to support domestic and export growth, bolstering a global supply chain amid regulatory progress in markets like Germany.[99] The company continued M&A activity and debt reduction, prioritizing margin protection and capital structure improvements, as evidenced by 9% year-over-year revenue growth and 29% gross profit increase to $61 million in the second quarter of fiscal 2025.[100][101]

Leadership and Governance

Key Executives and Board Changes

Vic Neufeld, co-founder and CEO of Aphria Inc. since its inception in 2013, and co-founder Cole Cacciavillani, who served in executive roles including director of cultivation, faced increased scrutiny following a December 2018 short-seller report by Hindenburg Research alleging improper related-party acquisitions.[102] In response, Aphria's board underwent refreshment; on November 5, 2018, shareholders elected four new independent directors, including Renah Persofsky, Shawn Dym, and Michael Serruya, with Persofsky chairing the Nominating and Governance Committee.[103] On December 27, 2018, Irwin D. Simon, founder and former CEO of The Hain Celestial Group, Inc., was appointed independent Chair of the Board, succeeding Neufeld who retained his CEO position.[104] This followed an internal review initiated amid the short-seller allegations. On January 11, 2019, Neufeld and Cacciavillani announced their transition out of executive roles upon identification of successors, citing the completion of their foundational contributions after five years; both remained on the board during the handover.[105][106] Simon assumed the role of interim CEO in March 2019, becoming permanent later that year as Aphria pursued global expansion.[107] On May 14, 2019, the company appointed Jim Meiers as Chief Operating Officer, promoted Tim Banerjee to Senior Vice President of Corporate Development, and accepted the resignation of President Jakob Ripshtein, who stayed in role temporarily to ensure continuity.[108] These moves aimed to bolster operational expertise amid regulatory investigations. On November 15, 2019, shareholders elected all seven board nominees, including Simon as Chair, to support strategic growth initiatives.[109] Neufeld and Cacciavillani eventually departed the board post-merger preparations with Tilray in 2020, with Simon leading the combined entity's leadership as announced in December 2020.[110]

Corporate Governance Reforms Amid Scrutiny

In response to the December 3, 2018, short-seller report by Hindenburg Research alleging undisclosed conflicts of interest and self-dealing in Aphria's Latin American acquisitions, the company's board formed a special committee of independent directors on December 6, 2018, to review governance processes surrounding the LATAM Holdings Inc. transaction, which had closed on September 27, 2018.[111][4] The committee's mandate included assessing compliance with company policies, the fairness of the deal terms, and any potential conflicts among directors or management.[111] The special committee's review, completed and announced on February 15, 2019, determined that the $130 million purchase price for the LATAM assets fell within an acceptable range relative to comparable acquisitions by competitors, though it identified conflicting interests held by certain directors that had not been fully disclosed to the full board prior to approval.[69][112] In light of these findings, three directors with ties to the conflicted parties resigned, including co-founder John Cervini, who stepped down effective March 1, 2019; the company pledged to strengthen its governance framework by enhancing conflict-of-interest disclosures and board oversight protocols for future transactions.[112][79] These measures built on prior governance adjustments implemented earlier in 2018 following scrutiny over related-party investments in Nuuvera Holdings, where Aphria had admitted to undisclosed insider stakes; responses included appointing a chief compliance officer and revising investment approval policies to mandate greater independent review.[4] Despite these steps, ongoing securities class actions and regulatory probes into Aphria's disclosures highlighted persistent investor concerns about the efficacy of the reforms in mitigating risks of insider favoritism.[113][67]
