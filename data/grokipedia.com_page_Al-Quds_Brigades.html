Al-Quds Brigades


Saraya al-Quds, commonly referred to as the Al-Quds Brigades, is the paramilitary armed wing of the Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), a Sunni Islamist militant organization dedicated to the destruction of Israel and the creation of an Islamic state encompassing historic Palestine through violent jihad.[1][2] Established in 1992 during the aftermath of the First Intifada, the Brigades emerged as PIJ's formalized military apparatus, evolving from earlier informal units like the Saif al-Islam Brigades to conduct organized operations including suicide bombings, rocket launches, and ambushes targeting Israeli military personnel and civilians.[3][2] As the second-largest armed faction in Gaza after Hamas's Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades, it maintains a presence in the Gaza Strip and northern West Bank areas such as Jenin and Nablus, often coordinating with allied groups in joint assaults.[2] The Brigades has been designated a terrorist entity by the United States, Australia, the European Union, and others, owing to its history of deliberate attacks on non-combatants, including participation in the October 7, 2023, incursion into southern Israel alongside Hamas and other factions, as well as recurrent involvement in Gaza-Israel conflicts involving indiscriminate rocket fire.[4][5][2] Funded primarily by Iran and Hezbollah, which provide weapons, training, and financial aid, the group eschews political governance in favor of unrelenting militancy, rejecting negotiations like the Oslo Accords and prioritizing ideological purity over pragmatic alliances.[1][3] Its operations have resulted in hundreds of Israeli casualties over decades, while sustaining heavy losses from Israeli counterstrikes that have repeatedly decimated its leadership and infrastructure.[2]

Origins and Ideology

Founding and Early Development

The Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) was founded in 1981 in the Gaza Strip by Fathi Shiqaqi, a Gaza-born physician who had studied in Egypt and been influenced by the Muslim Brotherhood, along with Abdel-Aziz Odeh and other Islamists seeking to establish an armed resistance movement distinct from the secular Palestine Liberation Organization.[6] [7] The Al-Quds Brigades (Saraya al-Quds), PIJ's military wing, developed as its operational armed component in the early 1980s, reflecting the group's emphasis on immediate jihadist action inspired by the 1979 Iranian Revolution's model of Islamist mobilization against perceived oppressors.[1] This formation occurred amid rising Islamist sentiments among Palestinians, positioning the Brigades to conduct paramilitary activities separate from PIJ's political efforts. Early recruitment targeted Gaza's youth, including students and individuals from refugee communities frustrated with existing factions, with training emphasizing guerrilla tactics conducted in Syriaâ€”where PIJ relocated its leadership after Israeli deportationsâ€”and to a lesser extent in Lebanon.[3] The Brigades' inaugural documented attacks took place in the mid- to late 1980s, involving small-scale shootings, stabbings, and improvised bombings aimed primarily at Israeli soldiers and security personnel, predating and then aligning with the broader unrest of the First Intifada that erupted in December 1987.[1] [8] These initial operations were limited in scope and resources, relying on rudimentary weapons and hit-and-run methods rather than sustained campaigns.[3]

Ideological Foundations and Goals

The Al-Quds Brigades, as the military wing of Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), derives its ideological foundations from radical Islamist thought emphasizing armed jihad as the sole path to liberating Palestine. Influenced by the Muslim Brotherhood and thinkers like Sayyid Qutb, PIJ rejects secular nationalism in favor of an Islamic framework where struggle against Israel is a religious obligation aimed at establishing an Islamist state across historic Palestine, from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea.[9][10] This worldview frames the land as an inalienable Islamic endowment, incompatible with any territorial concessions or recognition of Israel.[1][11] Central to its goals is the complete destruction of Israel through relentless military action, viewing political negotiations or compromise as apostasy. PIJ explicitly opposed the Oslo Accords, dismissing them as a betrayal that legitimizes occupation rather than eradicating it, in contrast to groups willing to entertain pragmatic arrangements.[3] The organization prioritizes violent resistance, including suicide bombings and rocket attacks, as legitimate expressions of jihad, with martyrdom exalted as the highest aspiration for fighters.[12][13] While primarily focused on Palestine, PIJ's ideology aligns with broader jihadist principles, fostering tactical alliances with entities like Hamas and external supporters despite sectarian differences, and endorsing attacks on Israeli civilians as part of defensive jihad against perceived aggressors.[11] This stance underscores a rejection of two-state solutions, insisting on Islamic governance over the entire territory without coexistence.[14][1]

Organizational Structure

Internal Hierarchy and Units

The Al-Quds Brigades maintains a decentralized paramilitary structure optimized for asymmetric warfare, comprising autonomous cells that prioritize operational secrecy, rapid deployment, and evasion of superior conventional forces. This framework aligns closely with the Palestinian Islamic Jihad's (PIJ) political apparatus, separating military functions into compartmentalized units to minimize disruption from targeted strikes, while enabling coordinated actions across Gaza and limited West Bank networks.[2][15] In Gaza, the organization divides operations into regional commands covering northern, central, and southern districts, allowing localized adaptation to terrain and Israeli incursions. These commands oversee infantry squads for close-quarters combat, sniper teams for precision targeting, and diver units for potential coastal activities, with recent developments including a nascent naval commando element for maritime sabotage. West Bank activities rely on smaller, clandestine cells focused on hit-and-run tactics rather than sustained brigade-level formations.[16][15][17] Specialized subunits handle technical domains, including rocket manufacturing and firing crews capable of producing short- and medium-range projectiles, anti-tank teams equipped with shoulder-fired guided missiles for armored vehicle engagements, and mortar detachments for indirect fire support. Intelligence elements conduct surveillance and reconnaissance to support ambushes, while historical capabilities encompassed suicide operations, though emphasis has shifted toward enduring guerrilla tactics. The Brigades' estimated 8,000â€“11,000 fighters depend on Gaza's smuggling tunnel network for intra-regional mobility, weapon concealment, and operational continuity amid blockades.[15][16][17]

Leadership and Commanders

The Al-Quds Brigades, as the military arm of Palestinian Islamic Jihad, operate under the overarching command of PIJ's secretary-general, Ziyad al-Nakhalah, who assumed leadership in 2018 and directs operations from Damascus, Syria, while coordinating with a leadership council that includes figures like deputy secretary-general Muhammad al-Hindi.[1] Local commanders in Gaza manage tactical units, but the structure emphasizes decentralized cells to mitigate losses from targeted killings, with rapid promotions from mid-level operatives filling vacancies.[2] Early commanders faced initial Israeli eliminations, setting a pattern of attrition; for example, Baha Abu al-Ata, who led Gaza Strip operations, was killed in an Israeli airstrike on November 12, 2019, after overseeing rocket launches and tunnel networks.[1] Similarly, Hussam Abu Harbid, head of the northern Gaza division responsible for attacks on Israeli border communities, was struck down on May 17, 2021, in an airstrike that also wounded other militants.[18] Since 2000, Israeli forces have eliminated at least dozens of senior Al-Quds commanders through precision operations, including Taysir al-Jabari in August 2022, forcing frequent leadership rotations that temporarily hinder coordination but enable the group to draw from a deep pool of ideologically committed replacements.[19] Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps-Quds Force maintains substantial sway over Brigades leadership by funding, arming, and training commanders, often conditioning multimillion-dollar annual supportâ€”such as $70 million pledged in 2012â€”on the appointment of Iran-approved figures to key military roles.[20] This vetting process, coordinated via Tehran proxies in Syria and Lebanon, ensures alignment with broader Iranian strategic objectives, including proxy warfare against Israel, while providing specialized instruction in asymmetric tactics to successors amid ongoing Israeli targeting.[21]

Military Operations

Initial Activities and Tactics (1980s-1990s)

The Al-Quds Brigades, as the nascent military wing of Palestinian Islamic Jihad, initiated operations in the late 1980s with low-intensity guerrilla tactics, including stabbings, shootings, and small-scale ambushes targeting Israeli civilians and security forces primarily in the Gaza Strip. These early efforts were constrained by the group's rudimentary organization, reliance on local recruits, and scarcity of advanced weaponry, resulting in sporadic hit-and-run attacks rather than sustained offensives.[1][22] Among the debut incidents, Palestinian Islamic Jihad claimed responsibility for the July 6, 1989, attack on Egged bus route 405 between Tel Aviv and Jerusalem, in which a Gaza-based operative seized control of the vehicle, causing it to crash and killing 16 Israeli civilians while injuring dozens more.[11] Similar bus shootings occurred in Gaza during 1989 and 1990, such as gunmen firing on civilian transports, which resulted in targeted killings of passengers and established a pattern of exploiting soft targets for maximum psychological impact with minimal resources.[23] Following the 1991 Gulf War, enhanced coordination emerged as Iranian backing provided training and explosives, enabling a tactical evolution toward vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices (VBIEDs) and suicide bombings modeled on Hezbollah methods. This shift was exemplified by the January 22, 1995, double suicide bombing at Beit Lid junction near Netanya, where two Al-Quds Brigades operatives detonated explosives-laden vehicles among soldiers and civilians awaiting buses, killing 22 people (predominantly Israeli troops) and wounding over 60.[24][25] Despite these escalations, operations remained limited in scope, emphasizing asymmetric ambushes over conventional engagements due to persistent logistical constraints and Israeli counterintelligence pressures.[26]

Second Intifada Era (2000-2005)

During the Second Intifada, which erupted in September 2000 following the breakdown of peace negotiations, the Al-Quds Brigades, as the military arm of Palestinian Islamic Jihad, intensified its campaign against Israeli targets, primarily through suicide bombings aimed at civilians.[2] The group claimed responsibility for numerous such operations, often coordinating with Hamas but issuing independent statements to assert its role in the violence.[27] Notable examples include the August 9, 2001, Sbarro pizzeria bombing in Jerusalem, a joint operation with Hamas that killed 15 civilians and injured over 130, and the October 28, 2002, attack at a Hadera market stall, where a suicide bomber killed 4 Israelis.[27] [28] These tactics marked a strategic emphasis on high-casualty strikes to maximize psychological impact and disrupt daily life in Israeli cities.[29] The Brigades expanded operations beyond Gaza into the West Bank, adapting to urban environments with improvised explosive devices (IEDs), roadside ambushes, and small arms fire, including occasional sniper attacks against Israeli security forces and settlers.[30] Activity peaked in 2002-2003, with the group conducting attacks at a high frequencyâ€”often weekly during intense phasesâ€”amid broader Palestinian militant efforts that included over 130 suicide bombings overall in Israel proper from 2000 to 2005.[29] This escalation correlated directly with spikes in Israeli fatalities, prompting military responses such as Operation Defensive Shield in March-April 2002, which targeted militant infrastructure in West Bank cities like Jenin and Nablus after a series of bombings killed dozens.[31] The Brigades' rejection of the Oslo Accords framework, coupled with its persistent attacks, played a causal role in undermining remaining peace efforts by eroding Israeli public support for concessions and justifying heightened security measures, including the construction of barriers and expanded checkpoints.[3] Empirical data from the period shows that waves of suicide bombings, including those by Islamic Jihad, preceded collapses in negotiations, such as after the 2001 attacks that followed Camp David, reinforcing a cycle of retaliation that prolonged the Intifada and inflicted heavy civilian tolls on both sides.[31] By 2005, Israeli counteroperations had degraded the group's operational capacity in the West Bank, shifting its focus toward Gaza-based rocket launches in subsequent years.[2]

Gaza Conflicts and Rocket Campaigns (2006-2022)

During the Gaza conflicts following Israel's 2005 disengagement, the Al-Quds Brigades shifted focus to asymmetric warfare, emphasizing rocket barrages from Gaza into southern Israel and rudimentary tunnel networks for infiltration and smuggling. In coordination with Hamas's Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades, the group launched salvos designed to saturate Israeli defenses, including the Iron Dome system deployed from 2011 onward, though interception rates often exceeded 85% for targeted threats.[32] These operations relied on short-range Qassam and Grad rockets, later supplemented by Iranian-designed variants like the Badr-3 with extended range up to 160 kilometers, components of which were smuggled through Sudanese factories and Egyptian Sinai routes before assembly in Gaza.[33][34] Tunnel warfare enabled cross-border raids and weapon caching, with Al-Quds fighters using subterranean paths for ambushes during ground incursions, though such tactics were less extensive than Hamas's network.[35] In the 2008-2009 Operation Cast Lead, triggered by escalating rocket fire, Al-Quds Brigades participated alongside Hamas in launching over 1,500 projectiles into Israel in 2008 alone, including Qassam rockets that struck civilian areas like Ashkelon, causing property damage and prompting Israeli counterstrikes that killed several PIJ operatives.[36] The group claimed attacks on military targets but fired indiscriminately from populated zones, contributing to the cycle of escalation despite a prior six-month tahdiya (calming) period.[36] The 2012 Operation Pillar of Defense saw Al-Quds Brigades fire dozens of longer-range Fajr-5 rockets toward Tel Aviv and Jerusalem, marking a tactical evolution in joint barrages with Hamas to test Iron Dome limits; the group claimed most early salvos in preceding border clashes.[37][38] Over eight days, Palestinian groups including PIJ launched around 1,500 rockets total, with Al-Quds emphasizing anti-tank ambushes from tunnels during brief ground engagements.[39] The 2014 Operation Protective Edge represented peak rocket intensity, with Al-Quds Brigades and allies firing over 4,500 projectiles, including Grad and Badr variants, in coordinated volleys overwhelming defenses and reaching as far as Haifa; PIJ alone claimed responsibility for hundreds, framing launches as retaliation for border killings. Tunnels facilitated smuggling and raids, such as attempted infiltrations near Kerem Shalom, though IDF operations neutralized many shafts.[35] Post-ceasefire, sporadic Al-Quds fire persisted, sustaining low-level provocations. In the 2021 Guardian of the Walls escalation, Al-Quds Brigades joined Hamas in barrages exceeding 4,000 rockets, including anti-personnel types, with PIJ volleys retaliating for targeted strikes on its commanders.[40] Tunnel-based logistics supported sustained fire, though misfires caused Gaza civilian casualties.[41] Throughout 2022, post-ceasefire incidents escalated, with Al-Quds launching over 1,100 rockets in August alone after Israeli strikes on PIJ leaders, killing border civilians and prompting retaliatory operations; such patterns empirically fueled recurrent cycles rather than deterring incursions, as groups violated tacit understandings via border fire.[42][43]

October 7, 2023 Attack and Subsequent Engagements

The Al-Quds Brigades participated in the October 7, 2023, cross-border attacks initiated by Hamas, with its militants supporting the assault that killed approximately 1,200 Israelis and foreigners and resulted in the abduction of over 250 hostages.[1] [44] Palestinian Islamic Jihad claimed its forces took part in the infiltrations targeting military bases, kibbutzim such as Nir Oz and Be'eri, and the Nova music festival site near Re'im, where hundreds were killed.[45] The group's involvement was coordinated with Hamas under the "Al-Aqsa Flood" operation, though PIJ's contingent was smaller than Hamas's estimated 3,000 fighters.[46] In the ensuing Gaza war, Al-Quds Brigades sustained rocket barrages against Israeli population centers, including claims of launches during escalations involving Iranian proxies in 2024.[47] Alongside Hamas and other factions, PIJ contributed to over 29,000 projectiles fired at Israel in the first 600 days of the conflict, many intercepted by defenses like Iron Dome but causing disruptions and occasional impacts.[48] These attacks continued sporadically into 2025, with PIJ claiming responsibility for specific salvos, such as two projectiles from Gaza in September 2025.[49] Amid Israeli Defense Forces campaigns in Gaza, Al-Quds Brigades conducted ground engagements, including ambushes on IDF troops and anti-tank missile strikes, while facing heavy attrition. The IDF targeted and eliminated multiple senior commanders, degrading the group's command structure, though spokesmen like Abu Hamza asserted ongoing resilience and commitment to resistance operations as of October 2025.[50] These engagements have prolonged the conflict, with PIJ maintaining a role in firing thousands of rockets and holding some hostages captured on October 7.[1]

Funding and External Support

Iranian Sponsorship and Armaments

The Al-Quds Brigades, military wing of Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), relies heavily on Iran for funding and armaments, channeled primarily through the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps' Quds Force (IRGC-QF) since PIJ's establishment in the early 1980s.[51] Iran has provided PIJ with annual financial support estimated in the tens of millions of U.S. dollars, contributing to a broader allocation of over $100 million yearly to Palestinian militant groups including PIJ and Hamas.[52][51] This sponsorship enables PIJ's operational capacity, with funding restored after temporary withholdings tied to geopolitical alignments, such as support for Iranian proxies like the Houthis.[53] Iranian training programs for Al-Quds Brigades operatives occur in camps located in Iran, Syria, and Lebanon via Hezbollah intermediaries, focusing on rocket manufacturing, improvised explosive device (IED) construction, and advanced combat tactics.[53][51] These sessions, ongoing since the late 1980s, have equipped PIJ fighters with technical expertise to produce and deploy munitions indigenously in Gaza.[51] Armaments supplied by Iran include rocket systems like the Badr-3, derived from Iranian designs, with technology transfers facilitating local adaptations of longer-range variants.[54] Following disruptions from the 2011 Syrian civil war, deliveries shifted through Hezbollah smuggling networks into Gaza, including components for Fajr-series and other unguided rockets previously associated with Iranian exports.[53] PIJ's arsenal, bolstered by these inputs, featured prominently in coordinated barrages, such as those during the October 7, 2023, attacks.[53] PIJ activities exhibit alignment with Iranian strategic directives within the "Axis of Resistance," with spikes in rocket fire and engagements correlating to broader mobilizations from 2023 to 2025, facilitated by joint operations rooms linking Tehran, Hezbollah, Hamas, and PIJ.[53] This proxy dynamic underscores Iran's role in directing escalations against Israel, as evidenced by unified responses to regional flashpoints.[53]

Other International Ties and Resources

Prior to the outbreak of the Syrian civil war in 2011, the regime of Bashar al-Assad hosted Palestinian Islamic Jihad's (PIJ) political leadership and Al-Quds Brigades operatives in Damascus, providing safe havens, office space for coordination, and facilitation of arms smuggling routes through Syrian territory.[55] Syrian authorities permitted PIJ training activities and material support, including transit of weapons from Iran, as part of broader backing for anti-Israel militant groups until PIJ's refusal to align with Assad's forces led to expulsion of its Damascus headquarters by 2012.[56] The Al-Quds Brigades supplements external state aid through Gaza's extensive cross-border tunnel network with Egypt's Sinai Peninsula, where it engages in direct smuggling of small arms, explosives, and rocket components, as documented in operations uncovered during the 2014 Gaza conflict.[57] Revenue streams derive from extortion rackets imposing fees on commercial smugglers using the tunnels for goods like fuel and construction materials, with PIJ enforcing compliance through threats and seizures, contributing to operational sustainment amid blockades.[58] Opportunistic partnerships with Sinai-based Bedouin smuggling clans and criminal syndicates enable procurement of materiel, including anti-tank weapons and ammunition, exchanged for protection fees or shares in illicit trade, bypassing formal state controls.[3] U.S. intelligence evaluations assess PIJ's assertions of domestic arms production as exaggerated, revealing heavy dependence on smuggled foreign imports for advanced munitions like Grad and Fajr rockets, with Gaza-based workshops limited to rudimentary assembly of imported kits rather than full self-reliance.[59]

Designations and Controversies

Terrorist Designations by Governments

The United States designated Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), whose military wing is the Al-Quds Brigades, as a Foreign Terrorist Organization (FTO) on October 8, 1997, under section 219 of the Immigration and Nationality Act, citing its involvement in planning, preparing, or committing acts of terrorism that threaten the security of U.S. nationals or the national security of the United States, including indiscriminate attacks on civilians via bombings and shootings.[4] This designation was reaffirmed in periodic reviews, such as in 2021, based on continued patterns of terrorist activity threatening civilian populations.[60] The European Union added PIJ to its list of persons, groups, and entities involved in terrorist acts on December 27, 2001, under Common Position 2001/931/CFSP, which imposes asset freezes and travel bans for organizations committing, attempting, or facilitating terrorist acts, including those targeting civilians indiscriminately; the listing was extended and reviewed annually thereafter.[61] The United Kingdom proscribed PIJ under the Terrorism Act 2000 on March 16, 2001, classifying it as an organization concerned in terrorism through its use of violence for political aims, including suicide bombings and rocket attacks on non-combatants.[62]

Major Attacks, Casualties, and Human Impact

The Al-Quds Brigades, as the armed wing of Palestinian Islamic Jihad, has executed high-profile operations including suicide bombings and rocket salvos targeting Israeli civilian areas, often resulting in limited direct fatalities due to defensive measures but significant psychological and infrastructural disruption. During the Second Intifada (2000-2005), the group claimed responsibility for multiple suicide attacks, such as bus bombings that killed Israeli civilians and security personnel, contributing to heightened border security protocols and long-term vigilance in urban centers.[64][27] In more recent years, rocket campaigns from Gazaâ€”frequently coordinated with other groupsâ€”have numbered in the thousands, with Al-Quds firing volleys in conflicts like 2021 and 2022, prompting mass evacuations in southern Israel and economic losses exceeding hundreds of millions in shekels from disrupted commerce and property damage.[65][66] Casualties inflicted on Israelis by Al-Quds operations remain empirically low in aggregate compared to peer groups, with rocket impacts rarely lethal post-2010 due to interception systems, though isolated fatalities occurred, such as one civilian death from a Gaza-launched projectile in May 2023 amid escalated barrages.[67] Participation in the October 7, 2023, cross-border assault alongside Hamas involved Al-Quds fighters in ground incursions, aiding the overall toll of approximately 1,200 Israeli deaths, predominantly civilians, through direct combat and abductions that exacerbated national trauma and military mobilization.[1] On the Palestinian side, misfires from Al-Quds rockets have caused self-inflicted losses, with Israeli assessments indicating 10-20% of launches fail and strike Gaza territory; for instance, in August 2022, up to 12 children were among those killed by such errant projectiles during a single exchange.[68][66] The human toll extends beyond immediate deaths to enduring security repercussions, including fortified barriers and aerial defenses in Israel that curtail mobility and foster societal anxiety in border communities. In Gaza, recurrent Al-Quds-initiated escalations trigger retaliatory operations, perpetuating infrastructure devastation and economic contractionâ€”evident in unemployment spikes and GDP per capita halving amid conflict cyclesâ€”while diverting resources from development to armament, entrenching dependency and stagnation without advancing territorial or political objectives.[69][70] These patterns reflect tactics emphasizing volume over precision, yielding retaliatory asymmetries that amplify civilian exposure on both sides without resolving underlying stalemates.[71]

Critiques of Ideology and Methods

The Palestinian Islamic Jihad's ideology, which mandates the complete destruction of Israel through perpetual armed jihad without compromise, eschews pragmatic political engagement, resulting in the group's marginalization within Palestinian politics compared to Hamas, which maintains a governance apparatus in Gaza.[10] Unlike Hamas, PIJ lacks a political wing and rejects participation in electoral processes or interim governance, framing any negotiation as apostasy and prioritizing eternal confrontation over territorial or administrative gains.[1] This absolutist stance, rooted in Iranian-influenced Shia-inspired militancy adapted to Sunni contexts, renders PIJ heavily dependent on external patronage, undermining its claims of autonomous Palestinian resistance as operations and funding are dictated by Tehran's strategic priorities rather than local agency.[72] Al-Quds Brigades' methods, including suicide bombings and indiscriminate rocket barrages, frequently target or endanger civilians, contravening even traditional Islamist interpretations of just war that prohibit harming non-combatants.[73] Such tactics, exemplified by attacks during the Second Intifada where PIJ claimed responsibility for bus bombings killing dozens of Israeli civilians, prioritize symbolic escalation over precision, inviting accusations of moral and strategic bankruptcy within jihadist doctrine itself.[74] Empirically, PIJ's campaigns have provoked disproportionate Israeli military responses, exacerbating Palestinian civilian hardship without yielding verifiable territorial or political advancements; for instance, rocket salvos from Gaza since 2006 have correlated with operations like Cast Lead (2008-2009) and Protective Edge (2014), which inflicted thousands of Palestinian casualties and infrastructure damage far exceeding attack impacts.[75] In contrast, the negotiation phase post-Oslo Accords (1993-2000) facilitated Palestinian Authority establishment, partial Israeli withdrawals from West Bank cities, and economic aid inflows, concessions halted by the Second Intifada's violence, which PIJ amplified through suicide operations, leading to over 1,000 Israeli and 3,000 Palestinian deaths by 2005 with no net Palestinian gains.[76] This pattern demonstrates a causal link wherein escalation entrenches Israeli security measures, such as the West Bank barrier reducing infiltrations by over 90%, while diplomacy periods yielded incremental autonomies absent in pure confrontation eras.[77]

Relations with Other Groups

Alignment and Rivalries with Hamas

The Al-Quds Brigades, as the military arm of Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), maintains a relationship with Hamas characterized by tactical cooperation against Israel alongside underlying rivalries rooted in differing ideologies and funding dependencies. PIJ adheres to a strict Islamist doctrine emphasizing perpetual jihad without political compromise, eschewing participation in elections or governance, which contrasts with Hamas's pragmatic engagement in Palestinian politics, including its 2006 electoral victory and administration of social services in Gaza.[3][78] This ideological purity allows PIJ greater operational agility but limits its popular base compared to Hamas's broader sociopolitical infrastructure.[2] Despite these differences, the groups frequently coordinate military actions, such as synchronized rocket launches during escalations and joint participation in the October 7, 2023, attacks on Israel, where Al-Quds Brigades fighters joined Hamas-led incursions into southern Israeli communities, contributing to the assault's scale.[74] However, competition persists over resource allocation, recruit loyalty, and attribution of successful operations, with PIJ leveraging exclusive Iranian fundingâ€”estimated at tens of millions annually for weapons and trainingâ€”to maintain autonomy from Hamas's reliance on Qatari and Turkish support.[72][79] This financial independence enables PIJ to pursue riskier, Iran-aligned strategies, often sidelining Hamas's efforts to consolidate control in Gaza.[53] Tensions have surfaced in instances of divergent adherence to ceasefires, where Al-Quds Brigades have launched rockets independently, undermining truces negotiated primarily by Hamas. For example, in August 2022, PIJ initiated over 1,100 rocket salvos toward Israel following targeted strikes on its leaders, prompting a four-day conflict without Hamas's direct involvement, which strained intra-Gaza dynamics as Hamas sought to preserve calm for governance.[3] Similarly, PIJ's isolated escalations in May 2023 highlighted its willingness to prioritize militant action over Hamas's stabilizing role, fostering rivalry for influence among Gaza's armed factions.[3] These frictions underscore PIJ's niche as a nimble, ideologically uncompromising force, reliant on external patronage rather than domestic popularity.[2]

Interactions with Fatah and Broader Palestinian Factions

The Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), through its military wing Al-Quds Brigades, has historically opposed the secular nationalist framework of Fatah and the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO), viewing negotiations like the 1993 Oslo Accords as a betrayal of armed jihad and recognition of Israel.[80][81] This ideological rift led PIJ to reject participation in Palestinian Authority (PA) institutions, unlike Hamas which has engaged in elections, positioning Al-Quds Brigades in direct antagonism to Fatah's governance model that includes security coordination with Israel.[81][82] In the 1990s and 2000s, Al-Quds Brigades conducted assassinations of Palestinians accused of collaborating with Israel, often targeting those perceived as aligned with PA or Fatah elements facilitating such cooperation, framing it as apostasy and treason against Islamist principles. For instance, on November 5, 2006, the group claimed responsibility for kidnapping and murdering four Palestinians who provided intelligence to Israeli forces during operations in Gaza. Similarly, in August 2006, Al-Quds Brigades publicly executed a 26-year-old man in Gaza who confessed to aiding Israel, underscoring their enforcement of intra-Palestinian purges independent of PA authority.[83] These actions exacerbated tensions, as Fatah viewed them as undermining unified resistance while PIJ saw PA coordination as complicity in occupation. Al-Quds Brigades maintains a dominant operational presence in Gaza, where PIJ rivals Hamas as the second-largest faction, but only marginal activity in the West Bank, limited to pockets like Jenin and Nablus under joint brigades with other militants.[2][1] In the West Bank, PA security forcesâ€”predominantly Fatah-ledâ€”have repeatedly clashed with PIJ cells, including arrests and raids, as seen in Jenin's 2024 operations where PA targeted Jenin Brigades incorporating Al-Quds elements, resulting in violent confrontations and mutual accusations of betrayal.[84] Opportunistic coordination occurred during uprisings like the Second Intifada (2000â€“2005), where Al-Quds joined Fatah-linked groups in attacks on Israeli targets, but underlying hostility persisted, with PIJ refusing integration into the PLO structure.[2] This rigidity has fueled intra-Palestinian violence, such as PIJ-affiliated killings in Jenin and PA crackdowns arresting PIJ members, empirically fragmenting factions and diminishing collective negotiating power against Israel by diverting resources inward.[85][86] Deep mistrust, including PIJ's non-compliance with PA directives, perpetuates cycles of arrests and ambushes, as evidenced by 2024â€“2025 escalations in Jenin that killed dozens and stalled unity efforts.[87][86]
