Barbie (disambiguation)

Barbie most commonly refers to the fashion doll and associated toy line manufactured by the American company Mattel.[1] The term also denotes a 2023 fantasy comedy film directed by Greta Gerwig, based on the Mattel dolls and starring Margot Robbie as the titular character.[2] Additionally, Barbie serves as a diminutive of the given name Barbara, derived from the Greek "barbaros" meaning foreign or strange.[3] In Australian English, "barbie" is slang for a barbecue, reflecting a cultural abbreviation common in informal speech.[4] Other uses include:
  • Specific individuals bearing the name or nickname Barbie, such as singer-songwriter Barbie Almalbis.
  • Slang extensions in American English for a stereotypically attractive but superficial young woman, akin to a "doll."[5]

People

Given name

Barbie is a feminine given name, serving as a diminutive of Barbara, which originates from the Greek term barbaros ("foreign" or "strange"), referring to non-Greek speakers and later denoting barbarity or otherness in Latin adaptations.[6][7] Notable individuals known by the given name Barbie include:
  • Barbie Hsu (born October 6, 1976 â€“ February 2, 2025), Taiwanese actress, singer, and television host recognized for roles in dramas such as Meteor Garden (2001).[8]
  • Barbie Forteza (born July 31, 1997), Filipino actress and commercial model, prominent in GMA Network productions including Anna Isabelle (2014) and The Half-Sisters (2014).[9]

Nickname

Barbie serves as a common diminutive or nickname for the given name Barbara, derived from the Greek root "barbaros" meaning "foreign" or "stranger," with usage predating modern cultural associations.[3][10] Notable individuals known professionally by this nickname include actress and model Barbara "Barbie" Ferreira, whose full name is Barbara Linhares Ferreira.[11] Similarly, professional wrestler Barbara Jean "Barbie" Blank adopted the ring name Kelly Kelly while using Barbie as her personal moniker.[12][13] These examples illustrate informal or stage adoption of the nickname in entertainment contexts, distinct from formal legal naming.

Surname

Barbie is an uncommon surname, primarily of occupational origin derived from the Anglo-Norman French term "barbier," denoting a barber or one who worked with beards, tracing back to the Late Latin "barba."[14] Genealogical records indicate its presence in the United States, Canada, and Scotland from the mid-19th century onward, with limited distribution globally, concentrated in regions including North Africa.[15][16] The most notable individual bearing the surname is Niklaus Barbie (1913â€“1991), a German Schutzstaffel (SS) officer who headed the Gestapo in Lyon, Vichy France, during World War II.[17] Known as the "Butcher of Lyon" for his role in torturing and deporting over 7,500 Jews and French Resistance members to concentration camps, Barbie directly oversaw the 1944 raid on the Izieu orphanage, resulting in the murder of 44 Jewish children at Auschwitz.[18][17] After the war, he evaded capture until 1983, when he was extradited from Bolivia to France, convicted of crimes against humanity in 1987, and sentenced to life imprisonment.[17] No other prominently documented figures with the surname Barbie appear in historical or contemporary records of significant public note.

Arts and entertainment

Films and television

Barbie (2023) is a live-action fantasy comedy film directed by Greta Gerwig, starring Margot Robbie as Barbie and Ryan Gosling as Ken, with production by Warner Bros. Pictures and Mattel Films.[2][19] The film premiered on July 9, 2023, in Los Angeles and was released theatrically in the United States on July 21, 2023.[2] Mattel produced a series of direct-to-video computer-animated films featuring Barbie as the protagonist starting in 2001, with the first being Barbie in the Nutcracker, released on October 2, 2001, which adapts E.T.A. Hoffmann's story.[20] Early entries include Barbie as Rapunzel (October 1, 2002), an adaptation of the fairy tale, and Barbie of Swan Lake (September 30, 2003), based on Tchaikovsky's ballet.[20] These films, often distributed by Universal Pictures or Lionsgate, continued with titles like Barbie as the Princess and the Pauper (September 28, 2004) and Barbie: Fairytopia (March 8, 2005), blending fairy tale retellings and original fantasy narratives voiced primarily by Kelly Sheridan as Barbie.[20] By mid-2023, Mattel had released at least 42 such animated features.[21] Television productions include Barbie: Life in the Dreamhouse, a CGI-animated web series of comedic shorts produced by Mattel and Arc Productions, released on YouTube from May 14, 2012, to November 17, 2015, across 7 seasons and 134 episodes satirizing the doll's lifestyle and accessories.[22] Additional series feature Barbie Dreamhouse Adventures, an animated show streaming on Netflix and YouTube starting June 4, 2018, focusing on Barbie's family life in episodes up to 2020.[23] Mattel has also released Barbie-themed specials, such as holiday episodes integrated into these series.[24]

Music

"Barbie Girl" is a song by the Danish-Norwegian eurodance group Aqua, released as a single on April 7, 1997, from their debut studio album Aquarius.[25] The track, featuring satirical lyrics portraying idealized doll-like personas, topped charts in the United Kingdom, Australia, and numerous European countries, while reaching number seven on the US Billboard Hot 100.[26] It has sold over 8 million copies worldwide and was certified triple platinum by the RIAA in the United States in 2023, reflecting its enduring popularity as a bubblegum pop anthem.[27] Mattel sued the band and their label for trademark infringement and defamation in 1997, alleging the song commercialized and sexualized the Barbie doll; the US Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals ruled in Aqua's favor in 2002, affirming the song's status as protected parody.[28] Barbie the Album, released on July 21, 2023, is a soundtrack compilation featuring 17 original tracks by artists such as Dua Lipa, Nicki Minaj, Ice Spice, Lizzo, Charli XCX, and Ryan Gosling, spanning pop, hip-hop, and electronic genres.[29] Notable singles include "Dance the Night" by Dua Lipa, which debuted at number one on the Billboard Dance/Electronic Songs chart, and "Barbie World" by Nicki Minaj and Ice Spice featuring Aqua, sampling the 1997 hit and peaking at number 48 on the Billboard Hot 100.[30] The album generated over 1 billion global streams within its first week, driven by promotional tie-ins.[31] Other recordings with "Barbie" in the title include "Not Your Barbie Girl" by Ava Max, a 2018 pop track critiquing superficial beauty standards, released as a promotional single ahead of her debut album.[32] Brazilian singer Kelly Key covered "Barbie Girl" in 2005, adapting it into Portuguese for her pop repertoire.[33]

Other media

The Barbie franchise encompasses a extensive lineup of video games, initiated with early titles like Barbie for the ColecoVision in 1984, developed by APh Technology Consultants and published under Mattel's license. These games predominantly feature fashion simulation, adventure, and puzzle elements, with notable entries including Barbie Fashion Designer (1996) for Windows and Macintosh, which sold over 2.5 million copies and introduced virtual dress-up mechanics. Later releases expanded to consoles and handhelds, such as Barbie as the Island Princess (2007) for Nintendo DS, Wii, and PlayStation 2, often adapting doll-related storylines with gameplay focused on exploration and customization; the series totals over 100 titles across platforms up to mobile apps in the 2020s.[34] In print media, Barbie comics debuted with Dell Comics' Barbie and Ken series (1962â€“1963), comprising five issues that depicted lighthearted adventures of the characters.[35] Marvel Comics later produced a 63-issue run of Barbie from 1991 to 1996, illustrated by artists like Win Mortimer, emphasizing fashion, friendship, and mild conflicts targeted at young readers, alongside special editions like the Barbie Halloween Special.[36] Additional comic publications appeared in the UK via IPC Magazines' fortnightly Barbie in the late 1980s, featuring serialized stories with photographic doll integrations.[37] Tie-in literature includes numerous children's books under the Barbie banner, such as activity and storybooks from Golden Books and Random House since the 1960s, often accompanying doll playsets with narratives of career aspirations and social scenarios; for instance, the Barbie Comic Collection (2024) compiles illustrated tales from Mattel properties.[38] Web-based animations like Barbie: Life in the Dreamhouse (2012â€“2015), a 75-episode series initially released on Mattel's YouTube channel, offered satirical glimpses into Barbie's exaggerated lifestyle, garnering over 1 billion views before transitioning to streaming platforms.[39]

Slang

Barbecue in Australian and New Zealand English

In Australian and New Zealand English, "barbie" serves as an informal abbreviation for "barbecue," denoting both the grill used for outdoor cooking and the associated social gathering centered on grilling meats, seafood, and vegetables.[40][41] This usage aligns with a linguistic pattern in Australasian varieties of English, where the diminutive suffix "-ie" is routinely appended to nouns for brevity and familiarity, as seen in terms like "barbie" derived directly from "barbecue."[42] The term gained international prominence through the 1984 Australian Tourism Commission advertisement campaign featuring actor Paul Hogan, which coined the phrase "throw another shrimp on the barbie" to evoke casual Australian hospitality and attract U.S. tourists; however, native speakers more commonly refer to the shellfish as "prawns" rather than "shrimp," reflecting regional lexical preferences.[43] Everyday applications emphasize communal outdoor cooking, such as "fire up the barbie" for summer weekend events involving friends and family.[44] In New Zealand, "barbie" similarly describes barbecues as a core social ritual, often phrased as "throwing some snags on the barbie," with "snags" denoting sausages, underscoring the prevalence of such gatherings in mild climates conducive to al fresco dining.[45] This culinary slang remains embedded in daily vernacular without connotations beyond relaxed, food-focused leisure.[46]

Derogatory term for a woman

"Barbie" serves as a pejorative slang term for a woman viewed as conventionally attractive yet superficial, materialistic, and intellectually shallow, evoking the Mattel doll's archetype of exaggerated femininity and artificial perfection.[5] This connotation stems directly from the Barbie doll's design since its 1959 debut, which features disproportionate physical idealsâ€”such as a 5'9" height, 18-inch waist, and 39-inch bust on a 6-inch figureâ€”prioritizing aesthetic appeal and consumerist fashion over substantive traits, fostering perceptions of shallowness in female role models.[47] Dictionaries define it explicitly as "a conventionally attractive young woman typically perceived as lacking substance, character, or intelligence" or "a person, especially a young woman, perceived as blandly attractive and vacuous."[5][47] Similarly, "Barbie doll" denotes an offensive reference to a woman deemed sexually attractive in an unnatural, uninteresting manner.[48] The term's derogatory application emerged in cultural critiques tying the doll to unrealistic beauty standards that encourage women to value appearance and consumerism above depth or achievement, with usage documented in literary and social commentary from the late 20th century onward.[5] For instance, it critiques embodiments of hyper-feminine stereotypes like the "tall, blonde, thin young lady" focused on vanity rather than intellect, reinforcing objectification under the male gaze.[49] Empirical observations in media and discourse highlight its role in labeling women who prioritize physical allure and materialism, often blonde and fashion-obsessed, as emblematic of societal pressures that diminish personal substance.[50] Critics argue this slang perpetuates harm by normalizing judgments that equate female attractiveness with intellectual deficiency, mirroring the doll's own history of feminist backlash for promoting unattainable proportionsâ€”equivalent to a human woman with measurements unfeasible for health or functionalityâ€”and consumerism as fulfillment.[51] Such usage underscores causal links between media icons and real-world expectations, where the term dismisses women as plastic-like figures devoid of agency or complexity, without evidence of inherent traits but based on observed correlations in popular stereotypes.[52]

Other uses

Places and locations

Barbie's Village is a tiny home development in Portland, Oregon, United States, dedicated to providing housing and support services for Native American families facing homelessness. Located at the site of a former Presbyterian church at 935 Northeast 33rd Avenue, the project was initiated in 2022 by the Future Generations Collaborative, a nonprofit focused on Indigenous community needs. Construction and land transfer occurred in late 2023, with the initiative aiming to accommodate 6 to 10 families through culturally sensitive tiny homes, early learning programs, and natural helper services.[53][54][55]
