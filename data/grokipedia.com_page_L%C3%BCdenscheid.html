Lüdenscheid

Lüdenscheid (German pronunciation: [ˈly:dn̩ʃaɪt] ⓘ) is a city in the Märkischer Kreis district, in North Rhine-Westphalia, Germany. It is located in the Sauerland region.

Geography

Lüdenscheid is located on the saddle of the watershed between the Lenne and Volme rivers which both empty into the Ruhr river (which subsequently flows into the Rhine), with three smaller valleys leading to them. The saddle has a height of 420 meters, higher elevations on the watershed are an unnamed hilltop of 505 meters in the north, and the 663 meters high Nordhelle in the Ebbe Mountains range. In the surrounding mountainous area, six dams created reservoirs to regulate the water flow in the Ruhr and supply drinking water. The mountainous nature of the city's territory gave rise to the nickname "Bergstadt" (mountain town). The original settlement circles around the church built on a ledge of the slope above the saddle.

History

While first settlement in the Lüdenscheid area is confirmed for the 9th century, the first mention of the place as a village was made in 1067 and as a city in 1268. In the 15th and 16th centuries, Lüdenscheid had close connections to the Hanseatic League, including trade in metal products across Europe.[3] Since 1609 the town belonged to the Electorate of Brandenburg, the later Prussia; during this period, it suffered impacts from the Thirty Years' War and a major fire in 1723 that destroyed much of the city. In 1815 it became part of the new Prussian Province of Westphalia, and 1975, with the creation of the Märkischer Kreis, became seat of its administration. In 1898 the aluminium framework of the first Zeppelin airship was built in the factory of Carl Berg in Lüdenscheid.

Economy

Lüdenscheid is a predominantly industrial city with small and medium-sized firms in the metal and plastics industry. As of around 2023, the largest company, Leopold Kostal GmbH & Co. KG, employed more than 2,000 people in Lüdenscheid, with other firms employing 800 people or less.[4] The economic importance came to the fore in the Middle Ages, when the city became a centre for metal ore mining in the surrounding area, through the "osemund/osmond iron. The ore was processed by the hammers and wire drawers of Lüdenscheid, serviced by water power in the surrounding valleys. A number of artisanal smitheries in Lüdenscheid made finished products out of those materials. In order to market these products to a wider area, Lüdenscheid joined the Hanseatic League. With the emergence of the industrial age, the production of buttons and buckles played a large role, reflected in the Button Museum. Later, new materials like aluminium, bakelite, and plastics were adopted. A supporting industry producing the forms for the stamping, blanking, pressing and casting emerged and still plays an important part in Lüdenscheid's industry. Production of parts of the auto industry and of fittings for electrical installations play an important role as well as continuous casting of aluminium profiles. Products notable to end consumers are lamps and lighting systems and toys. In recent years, the automotive sector has faced challenges; for instance, autoparts manufacturer Gerhardi Kunststofftechnik GmbH filed for bankruptcy in November 2024 but had its assets acquired by Hannover Finanz in August 2025, preserving operations at its Lüdenscheid site. Additionally, in October 2025, Kostal announced layoffs affecting 164 positions in Lüdenscheid due to ongoing industry pressures.[5][6][7]

Education

Lüdenscheid has 13 primary schools (Grundschulen), all offering full-day educational and care programs (Offene Ganztagsschule).[8] The city also features various secondary schools, including gymnasiums (academic high schools), comprehensive schools (Gesamtschulen), and special needs schools (Förderschulen). Notable secondary institutions include the Zeppelin Gymnasium and the Adolf-Reichwein-Gesamtschule. The South Westphalia University of Applied Sciences (German: Fachhochschule Südwestfalen, FH SWF) has one of its five campuses in Lüdenscheid, offering bachelor's and master's programs primarily in engineering fields such as plastics engineering, medical engineering, mechanical engineering, and sustainable building.[9]

Places of interest

  • Church of the Redeemer in the center of the circular layout of the old city. The protestant church is the oldest in the city. Its tower dates from the 11th century, while the parish nave was rebuilt during the classicistic period in the early 19th century.
  • Water castle Neuenhof in the Elspe valley behind the Nurre mountain. The present building with an early baroque style was erected in the end of the 17th century.
  • Homert tower Observation tower on top of the Homert mountain (539 m above sea level), the highest location in Lüdenscheid
  • Verse River reservoir
  • City Museum including among others the Button Museum and other exhibits showing the evolution of Lüdenscheid's industry

Coat of arms

The coat of arms shows Saint Medardus as the patron of the city. Below it has the red-and-white checked fess from the arms of the Counts of the Mark. The city wall at the bottom denotes the city rights that Lüdenscheid received in 1268.[10]

Lüdenscheid-Nord

Sports

"Lüdenscheid-Nord" is a demeaning and satirical name, most commonly used by the fans of FC Schalke 04 to mock the football club Borussia Dortmund. The real football club Rot-Weiß Lüdenscheid plays in the Kreisliga A, the eighth tier of the German football league system, but played in the 2. Bundesliga Nord for four seasons from 1977/78 to 1980/81. The Highlander Lüdenscheid are a skaterhockey team playing in the 2. Bundesliga Nord.

Culture

Lüdenscheid has a vibrant cultural scene, supported by various public and private institutions. The Kulturhaus Lüdenscheid serves as the main venue for professional theater, concerts, and events.[11] The city operates several museums, including the Geschichtsmuseum Lüdenscheid, which covers local history, and the Städtische Galerie, focusing on contemporary art.[12] Music education is provided through the Musikschule Lüdenscheid, offering instrumental and vocal training.[13] Notable events include the annual Nacht der Kultur, featuring performances, exhibitions, and workshops across the city, and the Lichtrouten festival, part of the "Stadt des Lichts" initiative since 2000, showcasing light art installations.[14][15] Amateur theater is represented by the Lüdenscheider Altstadtbühne, an e.V. association based at Luisenstraße 21, with Christel Gabler as chairwoman (as of 2025).[16]

Media

The Lüdenscheider Nachrichten is the primary local daily newspaper for Lüdenscheid and the surrounding area. It publishes the Süderländer Volksfreund for the nearby town of Werdohl. Local radio is provided by Radio MK, broadcasting on 100.2 FM in Lüdenscheid.[20] The online news portal come-on.de covers news from the Märkischer Kreis, including Lüdenscheid.[21]
