Anzavur Ahmed Pasha

Anzavur Ahmed Pasha (c. 1885 – 15 April 1921) was an Ottoman military officer and pasha of Circassian origin, who served as a gendarme lieutenant and major before becoming a provincial governor and leading monarchist rebellions against the Turkish nationalist forces during the Turkish War of Independence.[1][2][3] Anzavur participated in the Italo-Turkish War of 1911, the Balkan Wars of 1912–1913, and World War I, advancing through combat experience without formal military academy training.[1] In the post-war chaos following the Ottoman defeat and Allied occupation, he was appointed governor of Izmit by the Istanbul-based Sultanate government in 1920, reflecting his alignment with officials opposed to the Ankara nationalists.[1][4] From late 1919 to 1920, Anzavur organized three major uprisings in western Anatolia, mobilizing Circassian militias and invoking religious and caliphal authority to rally support against the Kuva-yi Milliye irregulars, with backing from Sultanate grand viziers like Damad Ferid Pasha and journalists such as Ali Kemal who favored negotiation with the Allies over revolutionary resistance.[1][4] His forces aimed to restore centralized Ottoman control amid fears of imperial partition under the Treaty of Sèvres, but were successively defeated by nationalist troops.[1] Captured after the failure of his final revolt, Anzavur was tried and executed by the emerging Republican authorities, cementing his portrayal in official Turkish historiography as a traitor, though recent scholarship reevaluates him as a loyalist officer responding to legitimate uncertainties in the empire's survival rather than mere opportunism.[1][4][2]

Early Life and Background

Circassian Origins and Family

Anzavur Ahmed Pasha, born around 1885, hailed from a family of Circassian ethnicity whose ancestors were displaced from the North Caucasus during the Russian Empire's conquests in the 1860s.[5][1] These Circassians, primarily Adyghe Muslims, were resettled by the Ottoman authorities in Anatolian provinces, including the Biga district near the Sea of Marmara, where Anzavur grew up in the village of Cihadiye.[1] His family's migration was part of the broader Muhacir influx, comprising over a million North Caucasians fleeing Russian expansion and seeking refuge under Ottoman suzerainty, often integrating into agricultural roles or military service to bolster the empire's defenses.[1] Circassians like Anzavur's kin contributed to Ottoman elite circles, including the Janissary remnants and later gendarmerie, reflecting their warrior traditions from the Caucasus highlands. Historical records provide scant details on his immediate family, such as parents or siblings, though his lineage aligned with the diaspora's pattern of assimilation into Ottoman loyalist structures amid post-migration hardships.[1] This background shaped his early identification with imperial authority rather than emerging nationalist movements.

Entry into Ottoman Service

Anzavur Ahmed was born around 1885 into a family of the Abzakh subgroup of Circassians, who had been forcibly exiled to the Ottoman Empire following the Russian conquest of the North Caucasus in the 1860s.[6] These Circassian migrants, valued for their martial traditions, often integrated into Ottoman society through military roles, providing a pathway for families like Anzavur's to seek advancement amid the empire's reliance on irregular and volunteer forces.[6] Lacking formal training from a military academy such as the Harbiye, Anzavur entered Ottoman military service directly as a low-ranking soldier or volunteer, a common route for Circassian diaspora members without elite education.[6] His recorded service began by 1911, during the Italo-Turkish War in Libya, where he participated in combat operations against Italian forces invading Ottoman North Africa.[6] This early involvement demonstrated the empire's practice of mobilizing ethnic militias and volunteers from peripheral groups to supplement regular troops amid resource shortages. Following the Libyan campaign, Anzavur continued in service through the Balkan Wars of 1912–1913, fighting Ottoman defensive efforts against Balkan League invasions that resulted in significant territorial losses.[6] By World War I (1914–1918), he had risen to the rank of major, serving in gendarmerie roles that aligned with Circassian strengths in irregular warfare and local security.[7] These experiences solidified his position within the Ottoman military structure, which favored loyal ethnic officers for counterinsurgency and frontier duties despite the absence of standardized officer training.[6]

Military and Administrative Career

Service as Gendarme Officer

Anzavur Ahmed, lacking formal military academy training, entered Ottoman military service and participated in the Italo-Turkish War of 1911 in Libya, followed by engagements in the Balkan Wars of 1912–1913.[1] His role as a gendarme officer involved duties in the Ottoman Gendarmerie, a paramilitary force tasked with internal security, law enforcement in rural areas, and suppressing banditry amid the empire's declining territorial control.[3] During World War I (1914–1918), Anzavur served as a major in the gendarmerie, contributing to Ottoman efforts in maintaining order and supporting regular army operations in Anatolia and surrounding regions strained by wartime mobilization and Allied occupations.[7] This period marked a progression in his career, leveraging his Circassian tribal networks for recruitment and local intelligence, though specific gendarmerie postings remain sparsely documented beyond general involvement in counterinsurgency tasks.[4] Post-armistice in 1918, Anzavur continued gendarme duties amid the power vacuum following the Ottoman defeat, focusing on quelling local disorders in western Anatolia, where ethnic tensions and disarmament mandates exacerbated instability.[8] His experience as an "aging gendarmerie captain" positioned him to mobilize irregular forces loyal to the Sultanate, bridging formal service with emerging militia leadership.[8]

Governorship of Izmit and Promotions

Anzavur Ahmed, a Circassian Ottoman gendarme officer who had retired with the rank of lieutenant after service in the Italo-Turkish War of 1911, the Balkan Wars of 1912–1913, and World War I, received the honorary title of pasha from the late Ottoman government in recognition of his military contributions and loyalty.[9] This elevation aligned with Ottoman practices of granting the pasha rank to experienced officers for administrative or militia roles during periods of instability. His career trajectory included progression to major within the gendarmerie, reflecting competence in internal security operations prior to the empire's collapse.[3] In 1920, amid post-World War I chaos and opposition to emerging nationalist forces, Anzavur was appointed governor of Izmit (modern Kocaeli) by the Ottoman administration in Istanbul.[9] This role, supported by high-ranking officials such as Grand Vizier Damad Ferid Pasha and journalist Ali Kemal—who viewed him as a counterweight to the Kuva-yi Milliye irregulars—entailed maintaining order in a strategically vital area near the capital, leveraging local Circassian networks and imperial authority.[4] The appointment underscored the Ottoman government's reliance on provincial loyalists like Anzavur to preserve sultanic control against regional autonomy movements, though it coincided with his mobilization of militias in the vicinity.[9]

Context of Opposition to Kemalism

Broader Turkish War of Independence Dynamics

The Turkish War of Independence encompassed not only military campaigns against Allied occupation forces and their proxies, such as the Greek advance into western Anatolia starting in May 1919, but also acute internal schisms between the nationalist provisional government in Ankara and the Ottoman Sultanate's administration in Istanbul. The latter, under Sultan Mehmed VI and Grand Vizier Damad Ferid Pasha, operated amid British occupation of the capital since March 16, 1920, and prioritized compliance with Allied demands, including the impending Treaty of Sèvres, over unified resistance to partition. This stance framed the Kemalists as insurgents challenging the sovereign's authority, a view reinforced by Istanbul's issuance of fatwas from religious leaders declaring participation in the nationalist struggle as apostasy.[10] Loyalist forces, including irregular militias and later formalized units, mobilized to restore central control and suppress Kuva-yi Milliye bands—decentralized nationalist guerrillas formed in response to the Armistice of Mudros on October 30, 1918—which were seen as exacerbating anarchy and banditry in Anatolia.[11] In April 1920, the Istanbul regime established the Kuva-yi İnzibâtiye ("Forces of Order"), numbering around 10,000-12,000 troops under officers like Süleyman Şefik Pasha, explicitly to dismantle Kemalist organizations and secure supply lines for Allied operations. British authorities provided logistical aid, arms, and intelligence to these units, aiming to prevent a cohesive Turkish front that could derail the Sevres negotiations and maintain a pliable Ottoman remnant amid post-World War I realignments.[12] This support reflected Allied strategy to exploit Ottoman fractures, as evidenced by London's pressure on Damad Ferid to confront nationalists, despite risks of alienating moderate Muslim opinion. The resulting clashes, concentrated in regions like the Marmara and Black Sea areas, highlighted causal tensions: war exhaustion from 1912-1918 conflicts, economic collapse with hyperinflation exceeding 1000% annually by 1920, and fears of further territorial losses under Sevres, which allocated over 80% of Ottoman lands to mandates or minorities.[13] These dynamics underscored a civil war-like dimension within the independence struggle, where pro-Sultan factions appealed to traditional Islamic legitimacy and caliphal unity against what they portrayed as Kemalist secular radicalism and authoritarian centralization. While Kemalist sources later emphasized foreign puppetry in these oppositions, contemporary loyalist rhetoric stressed defense of the dynasty against rebellion, drawing adherents from conservative ulema, demobilized soldiers (over 2 million Ottoman troops lost in World War I), and ethnic militias wary of Ankara's homogenization efforts. Anzavur Ahmed Pasha's activities in Izmit-Adapazarı emerged within this matrix, leveraging Circassian loyalties and British-encouraged anti-Kemalist networks to contest nationalist expansion before the loyalist cause waned post-Sevres rejection on August 10, 1920.[1][11]

Factors Fueling Local Resistance

Local resistance to the Kemalist nationalists in the Izmit and Gönen regions during 1919–1920 stemmed primarily from unwavering loyalty to the Ottoman Sultanate and Caliphate, which Anzavur Ahmed Pasha invoked through religious rhetoric to rally supporters against the revolutionary aims of the Turkish National Movement. Anzavur positioned his forces as defenders of Islamic authority, framing the Kemalists as threats to the sultan's legitimacy and promising restoration of Ottoman governance, a narrative bolstered by endorsements from Istanbul officials like Grand Vizier Damat Ferid Pasha.[1] This appeal resonated amid perceptions that the nationalists' push for centralized control undermined traditional caliphal structures, drawing backing from pro-Sultan factions in southern Marmara who viewed the resistance as a bulwark against secular republicanism.[1] Ethnic tensions exacerbated opposition, particularly among Circassian communities—immigrants from the North Caucasus since the 1860s—who feared marginalization under emerging Turkish nationalist dominance. Anzavur, himself Circassian, mobilized these groups by exploiting historical grievances against the Committee of Union and Progress (CUP), seen as exploitative, and by highlighting the Kemalists' ethnic favoritism toward Turks, which alienated non-Turkic Muslims like Circassians numbering around 108,000 in the Hüdavendigar region by 1922.[14] Local power struggles intensified this, as rival ethnic networks—such as conflicts between Circassians, Albanians, and Georgian families like the Yetimoğlus—erupted into violence against nationalist administrators, with disputes over local governance roles dating back to World War I and escalating by July 1919.[15] These dynamics reflected broader Circassian distrust of the National Movement's Turkification efforts, prompting appeals for Western protection and autonomy by 1921.[15] Economic burdens imposed by the Kuva-yı Milliye further fueled discontent, including heavy exactions such as one ox from every seven households or one sheep per household in Biga by early 1920, alongside documented looting of civilian property that bred widespread resentment.[14] Anzavur capitalized on these grievances by promising plunder from nationalist targets and securing ammunition and funds from Istanbul, which sustained his insurgents and attracted opportunistic local adherents amid the disruptions of post-World War I instability.[14] Turkish official narratives have since dismissed such resistances as mere banditry or treason, yet archival evidence underscores their roots in tangible ideological, ethnic, and material conflicts that fragmented loyalties in the region.[1]

The Series of Revolts

First Revolt: October-November 1919

Anzavur Ahmed Pasha launched the initial phase of his armed opposition to the Turkish nationalist Kuva-yi Milliye forces in October 1919, centered in the southern Marmara region around Manyas and Biga.[6] Drawing on his Circassian heritage, he mobilized local immigrant communities wary of nationalist policies that threatened their settlements and autonomy, framing the conflict as a defense of the Ottoman Sultanate and Caliphate against perceived republican subversion.[6] Ottoman administrators, including Grand Vizier Damad Ferid Pasha and Interior Minister Ali Kemal, provided ideological and logistical encouragement, viewing Anzavur's efforts as a bulwark against the Ankara-based nationalists.[6] By early November, Anzavur's irregular forces, numbering in the hundreds and primarily Circassian irregulars, clashed with Kuva-yi Milliye units in skirmishes across the Manyas-Susurluk area.[16] A pivotal engagement occurred on 15 November north of Balıkesir, where Anzavur's troops inflicted initial disruptions but ultimately retreated after sustaining heavy casualties against reinforced nationalist defenders.[10] His appeals emphasized restoring Ottoman governance, abolishing irregular taxes imposed by nationalists, and combating local corruption, resonating with conservative Muslim villagers opposed to the secularizing tendencies of the Kemalist movement.[6] Nationalist countermeasures, led by figures like Çerkes Ethem's detachments, encircled and dismantled Anzavur's positions by late November, culminating in the destruction of his main forces on 30 November 1919.[16] Anzavur evaded capture and dispersed into the countryside, preserving a core of loyalists for subsequent actions. While Kemalist historiography depicts this episode as a treasonous rebellion instigated by external influences, reevaluations highlight it as a manifestation of fidelity to the legitimate Istanbul government amid the succession crisis fracturing Ottoman loyalties post-World War I.[6][10]

Second Revolt: February-March 1920

Following the suppression of his initial uprising in late 1919, Anzavur Ahmed Pasha regrouped during the harsh winter of 1919-1920, recruiting among Circassian communities in the Southern Marmara region to challenge the Turkish nationalist Kuva-yi Milliye forces. His efforts were bolstered by support from Ottoman government figures in Istanbul, including Grand Vizier Damat Ferid Pasha and Interior Minister Ali Kemal, who opposed the nationalists' autonomy and favored accommodation with Allied powers. Anzavur framed the revolt as a religious defense of the Sultan-Caliph against perceived threats to Islamic order, mobilizing irregular bands equipped with official Ottoman military uniforms and supplies.[1][6] Hostilities resumed on 16 February 1920, when Anzavur's vanguard forces initiated attacks in the Marmara countryside, targeting nationalist outposts and disrupting supply lines. These better-organized Circassian-led bands, numbering in the hundreds and leveraging local loyalties to the Sultanate, clashed repeatedly with Kuva-yi Milliye units, achieving initial gains through guerrilla tactics amid the region's ethnic and political fractures. The uprising drew on fears among conservative Muslim villagers of Kemalist policies, including tax impositions and conscription, which were portrayed by Anzavur's propagandists as rebellious innovations against legitimate Ottoman authority.[17][14] In March 1920, fighting escalated as nationalist commander Çerkes Ethem deployed his Kuva-yi Seyyare irregulars to counter Anzavur's advances, leading to intense engagements that strained both sides' resources. Anzavur's forces looted and occupied villages sympathetic to nationalists, while facing retaliatory raids that exacerbated local civilian hardships, including displacements and reprisals. Official Turkish historiography later depicted these actions as treasonous banditry, but contemporary Ottoman records indicate coordination with Istanbul's anti-nationalist faction, underscoring the revolt's basis in loyalty to the Sultan rather than mere opportunism. By late March, mounting nationalist pressure began eroding Anzavur's momentum, though he evaded decisive defeat and escaped to regroup.[1][6][14]

Third Revolt: Spring 1920 and Final Defeat

In early 1920, following the suppression of his second uprising, Anzavur Ahmed Pasha received appointment as mutasarrıf (governor) of Izmit from the Ottoman government in Istanbul, which sought to counter the Turkish National Movement through local loyalist forces.[4] This position enabled him to reorganize Circassian militias and irregulars, drawing on religious appeals to the Sultan-caliph and opposition to Kemalist taxation and conscription policies.[1] By April, clashes intensified in the southern Marmara region, culminating in a nationalist assault on Anzavur's positions at Yahyaköy village near Bandırma, where overwhelming Kuva-yi Milliye forces under Çerkes Ethem trapped and dislodged his troops on April 16 after bitter fighting; Anzavur escaped by sea to Istanbul.[18] The defeat at Yahyaköy triggered a broader uprising in the Adapazarı area, marking the third phase of Anzavur's resistance from approximately May 10 to May 22, 1920.[4] Anzavur's forces, numbering several thousand irregulars supplemented by Ottoman-supplied uniforms and arms, briefly controlled villages and disrupted nationalist supply lines toward the western front.[1] However, Ethem's mobile cavalry units swiftly counterattacked, recapturing Sapanca and Adapazarı by late May through rapid maneuvers that exploited the rebels' lack of cohesion and artillery.[19] Local support for Anzavur eroded amid reports of plunder by his Circassian bands, further isolating his command.[20] This spring campaign represented the final coordinated defeat of Anzavur's revolt, as his "Army of Muhammad" disintegrated, with remnants scattering or defecting to nationalists by month's end.[21] Although Anzavur evaded capture and briefly returned to Istanbul under protection from figures like Damad Ferid Pasha, the loss severed organized loyalist threats in the Marmara, allowing Kemalists to consolidate control ahead of Greek advances. He faced execution by nationalist tribunal on April 15, 1921, in Adliye village near Karabiga.[22] Official Turkish accounts emphasize the revolt's role in aiding enemy occupations, though reevaluations highlight its roots in genuine Ottoman loyalism amid partitioned governance.[4]

Ideology, Motivations, and Support Base

Loyalty to Sultanate and Caliphate

Anzavur Ahmed Pasha's commitment to the Ottoman Sultanate and Caliphate was central to his opposition against the Kemalist nationalists, whom he portrayed as insurgents eroding the authority of Sultan Mehmed VI, who also held the position of Caliph. His mobilization efforts emphasized fidelity to the Sultan-Caliph, leveraging religious appeals to portray the conflict as a defense of Islamic sovereignty against perceived threats to traditional order.[1][4] This loyalty manifested in direct support from the Istanbul government, including his appointment as governor of Izmit in early 1920 and the provision of official Ottoman military uniforms to his forces on June 5, 1920, as documented in Ottoman archives. On April 11, 1920, Sultan Mehmed VI personally conferred the title of pasha upon Anzavur in recognition of his campaigns against nationalist irregulars, aligning him with the Sultan's broader strategy to quell the Anatolian resistance through loyalist militias.[1][10] Anzavur received backing from key Ottoman figures such as Grand Vizier Damad Ferid Pasha and journalist Ali Kemal, who viewed the nationalists as antithetical to imperial restoration and sought Allied cooperation to sustain the Sultanate post-World War I. His rhetoric framed the nationalists as akin to the discredited Unionist regime, invoking the Caliph's spiritual authority to recruit Circassians and other groups fearful of Kemalist policies, including potential ethnic displacements.[1][4] The Istanbul-aligned campaign, including Anzavur's revolts, drew on religious legitimization, such as fetvas from Şeyh-ül-İslâm declaring armed opposition to Kemalists a religious obligation, thereby casting the struggle in terms of jihad to protect the Caliphate from secular republican encroachments. This ideological stance prioritized the indivisibility of political and religious authority under the Sultan-Caliph over the nationalists' emerging territorial nationalism.[10]

Appeals Against Kemalist Policies

Anzavur Ahmed Pasha framed his opposition to the Kemalist nationalists primarily as a defense of the Ottoman Sultanate and Caliphate, portraying Mustafa Kemal's movement as an illegitimate rebellion against the legitimate sovereign authority in Istanbul. In proclamations and mobilizations during his revolts from October 1919 onward, he invoked religious loyalty to Sultan Mehmed VI, who had issued fatwas in April 1920 declaring the nationalists infidels and calling for their elimination, positioning Anzavur's forces as enforcers of this decree.[23][14] He appealed to local populations in regions like Manyas and Biga by vowing to capture or eliminate nationalist commanders such as Hacim Muhittin Çarıklı, whom he accused of subverting imperial order and imposing burdensome taxes linked to the nationalists' resistance efforts.[1] Anzavur's rhetoric targeted Kemalist policies that bypassed the sultan's government, such as the establishment of the Grand National Assembly in Ankara on April 23, 1920, which he and his Ottoman backers, including Grand Vizier Damad Ferid Pasha, viewed as usurpation of caliphal authority and a continuation of Young Turk-era chaos that had led to Ottoman defeat in World War I. He likened the nationalists to the discredited Committee of Union and Progress regime, exploiting widespread discontent over wartime conscription, famine, and perceived corruption to rally support from conservative Muslims, Circassians, and other groups fearing marginalization under emerging Turkish nationalism.[8][1] Promises to abolish irregular taxes levied by Kuva-yi Milliye forces and restore pre-war Ottoman stability further fueled his appeals, drawing backing from Istanbul administrators who anticipated British-mediated partition under the Treaty of Sèvres would preserve sultanate remnants.[24][25] To ethnic minorities like Circassians in the Marmara region, Anzavur warned of existential threats from Kemalist centralization, which prioritized ethnic Turkish dominance and risked their exile or assimilation, urging unification under sultanic protection as a bulwark against such policies. Religious appeals emphasized jihad against the "rebel" nationalists, aligning with Istanbul's counter-narrative that rejected Kemalist claims to Islamic legitimacy, especially after Ankara's mufti issued a rival fatwa on April 16, 1920, affirming the nationalists' orthodoxy.[1][14] These efforts, supported by British arms and logistics, temporarily garnered hundreds of fighters but faltered against coordinated nationalist reprisals by April 1920.[20][26]

Death, Immediate Aftermath, and Controversies

Circumstances of Death

Anzavur Ahmed Pasha evaded capture following the suppression of his final revolt in spring 1920, during which his forces were decisively defeated by Kuva-yi Milliye units under Çerkes Ethem. He reportedly disguised himself as a peasant and sought refuge or escape routes in the Biga-Karabiga region of Balıkesir Province, amid ongoing nationalist efforts to eliminate pro-Sultan loyalist holdouts.[27][28] On April 15, 1921, Anzavur was killed in an ambush near Karabiga, specifically in the vicinity of Adliye village, by a group of Albanian irregulars (Arnavut çetesi). Historical accounts indicate these bandits, potentially acting on suggestions from nationalist commanders like Kazım Özalp to employ local ethnic militias against rebels, surprised and eliminated him during his movements in the area.[27][29] His death marked the end of organized pro-Sultan resistance in the northwest, though some contemporary reports framed it as a formal execution tied to a death sentence, reflecting nationalist historiography's portrayal of rebels as traitors warranting summary justice.[3] Anzavur's body was interred in Buzağlık Cemetery, Biga, without public ceremony, underscoring the marginalization of his loyalist cause in emerging republican narratives.[27]

Accusations of Treason vs. Legitimate Loyalism

Anzavur Ahmed Pasha faced accusations of treason primarily from the Kemalist nationalists and subsequent Republican Turkish historiography, which portrayed his revolts as betrayals of the Turkish independence struggle against Allied occupation. In Mustafa Kemal's 1927 Nutuk speech, Anzavur's actions were described as "treasonous and monstrous," framing his militias as tools undermining the Kuva-yi Milliye (National Forces) by attacking Anatolian resistance groups and facilitating British influence in the region.[8] These claims were bolstered by evidence of British logistical and financial support for Caliphate-aligned forces, including Anzavur's Kuva-yı Muhammediye ("Forces of Muhammad"), which operated under the banner of defending the Sultan-Caliph against perceived Kemalist rebellion. Official narratives emphasized his Circassian origins and irregular warfare tactics—such as raids on nationalist depots—as acts of collaboration with occupiers, culminating in his posthumous vilification as a reactionary traitor who prolonged foreign domination.[1] In defense of Anzavur's legitimacy as a loyalist, contemporaries and later reevaluations highlight his adherence to the Ottoman Sultanate as the recognized sovereign authority until its abolition in 1922. As a former gendarme officer and provincial governor appointed by Istanbul, Anzavur organized his forces explicitly in loyalty to Sultan Mehmed VI and the Caliphate, receiving endorsements from Grand Vizier Damat Ferid Pasha and pro-Sultan administrators like Ali Kemal, who viewed the Kemalists as insurgents defying central authority.[4] His revolts, spanning October 1919 to spring 1920, invoked religious and monarchical appeals—promising tax relief, anti-corruption measures, and restoration of Ottoman order—mobilizing local support against Kemalist policies seen as republican threats to the Sultan-Caliph's rule.[1] While British backing existed for anti-Kemalist elements, Anzavur's primary allegiance was to the Istanbul government, which legally commissioned the Hilafet Ordusu (Caliphate Army) to suppress provincial disorders, positioning his efforts as enforcement of legitimate imperial directives rather than foreign puppetry.[14] Historiographical debates underscore systemic biases in Kemalist accounts, which, as victors, retroactively delegitimized Sultan loyalists to consolidate republican ideology. Official Turkish narratives, dominant since the 1920s, branded Anzavur a rebel and traitor to erase monarchical resistance and justify the National Struggle's singularity, often overlooking the Istanbul regime's de jure status and Allied pressures that compelled its anti-nationalist stance.[1] Recent reassessments, drawing on Ottoman archival records, argue that labeling him a traitor ignores causal realities: the Kemalists' initial oaths of loyalty to the Sultan masked their separatist ambitions, while Anzavur's opposition stemmed from principled anti-Committee of Union and Progress sentiments and defense of sultanic authority, rendering his actions defensible as counter-rebellion within a fractured empire.[4] [17] This perspective challenges the treason narrative by privileging empirical evidence of his gubernatorial role and palace ties over post-hoc nationalist myth-making.[1]

Legacy and Historiographical Debates

Portrayal in Official Turkish Narratives

In the historiography promoted by the Republic of Turkey's foundational institutions, such as the Turkish Historical Society (Türk Tarih Kurumu) and military academies, Anzavur Ahmed Pasha is consistently depicted as the leader of counter-revolutionary uprisings that undermined the Turkish National Movement during the War of Independence (1919–1923). His revolts, spanning October 1919 to May 1920, are framed as extensions of the Ottoman government's Istanbul-based efforts to obstruct Mustafa Kemal's Ankara-led resistance against Allied occupation, often portraying Anzavur as a tool of Sultan Mehmed VI Vahideddin's administration, which collaborated with British authorities.[30][31] This narrative emphasizes Anzavur's Circassian militia as disruptive forces that exploited local grievances—such as tax burdens and disarmament policies—to rally support for restoring caliphal authority, thereby threatening national unity at a critical juncture when external invasions loomed.[32] State-endorsed accounts, including those in Atatürk's Nutuk (1927) and subsequent military histories, label Anzavur's actions as "gerici" (reactionary) rebellions akin to other internal threats like the Düzce and Alaşehir uprisings, accusing him of treasonous alignment with imperial powers seeking to partition Anatolia under the Treaty of Sèvres (1920). Anzavur is characterized not as a principled loyalist but as a opportunistic bandit-chief whose brief successes, such as capturing Gönen and Bandırma in November 1919, were swiftly crushed by nationalist forces under Çerkes Ethem, underscoring the movement's resilience.[32][31] These portrayals serve to reinforce the teleological view of the republican founding as an inevitable triumph over monarchical decay and foreign intrigue, with Anzavur's defeat on 22 May 1920 near Susurluk symbolizing the rejection of Ottoman revivalism.[30] This official framing, dominant in Turkish education and public commemorations, attributes Anzavur's motivations to personal ambition and religious fanaticism rather than genuine ideological commitment to the sultanate, often citing his prior Ottoman service as ironic evidence of defection from patriotic duty.[4] While empirical records confirm British logistical support for his bands—estimated at 2,000–3,000 fighters in peak phases—the narrative minimizes any legitimate grievances against Kemalist centralization, prioritizing causal emphasis on external orchestration to justify repressive measures like summary executions of rebels.[31][4]

Modern Reassessments and Empirical Re-evaluations

In recent scholarship, Anzavur Ahmed Pasha's legacy has undergone reevaluation, shifting from the Republican-era depiction of him as a mere reactionary rebel to a multifaceted Ottoman loyalist navigating the empire's collapse. Historian Caner Yelbaşı's 2023 analysis in Archiv Orientální utilizes Ottoman archival documents to demonstrate that Anzavur's uprisings in 1919–1920 were not isolated banditry but extensions of official policy aimed at countering the Kuva-yi Milliye's separatist tendencies and restoring centralized imperial authority.[6] Yelbaşı contends that Anzavur, a Circassian officer with service in the Italo-Turkish War (1911), Balkan Wars (1912–1913), and World War I (1914–1918), received explicit backing from Sultan Mehmed VI and Grand Vizier Damad Ferid Pasha, who appointed him to mobilize forces against nationalist disruptions in regions like Biga and Izmit.[6] This support stemmed from Anzavur's alignment with the Sultan's ferman-ı âli (imperial decree) invoking religious loyalty to the Caliphate, as evidenced by telegrams in the Başbakanlık Osmanlı Arşivi (BOA), including one dated December 24, 1919 (BEO 4603 345166), authorizing his recruitment of militias.[6] Empirical re-evaluations emphasize the post-World War I context of administrative vacuum and ethnic fragmentation, where Anzavur's governorship of Izmit in early 1920 positioned him as a de facto imperial agent rather than a rogue actor. Primary sources reveal his appeals leveraged Circassian grievances over land expropriations and fears of Kemalist purges, framing the revolts as defensive restorations of Ottoman sovereignty against what officials perceived as Bolshevik-influenced anarchy.[6] Yelbaşı critiques Kemalist historiography—rooted in sources like the Atatürk Research Center—for systematically downplaying these legitimacies to consolidate Republican narratives, arguing that archival evidence exposes a causal chain: imperial directives preceded Anzavur's mobilizations, not vice versa, underscoring loyalties divided by dual power centers in Istanbul and Ankara.[6] Such reassessments highlight broader historiographical shifts toward multinodal analyses of the Turkish War of Independence, incorporating non-Turkish ethnic dynamics and Sultanate perspectives without endorsing failure as treason. While official Turkish accounts persist in portraying Anzavur's defeats—culminating in his death on April 15, 1921—as vindication of nationalist unity, archival-driven works like Yelbaşı's reveal operational complexities, including endorsements from figures like Ali Kemal, suggesting Anzavur's efforts embodied a viable, if quixotic, imperial continuity amid causal pressures of partition treaties like Sèvres (August 10, 1920).[6] This approach privileges documentary verification over ideological framing, fostering recognition of Anzavur as a product of late Ottoman military professionalism rather than perfidy.
