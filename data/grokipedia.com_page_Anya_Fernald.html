Anya Fernald

Anya Fernald is an American entrepreneur specializing in sustainable food production and direct-to-consumer protein products.[1] She co-founded Belcampo Meat Co. in 2012 with the aim of creating a vertically integrated supply chain for regeneratively farmed meat, raising over $50 million in funding and expanding to multiple retail and restaurant locations across California.[2] However, in 2021, Belcampo faced a major scandal when a whistleblower revealed that much of its marketed "organic" and "regeneratively raised" meat was sourced from conventional suppliers and mislabeled, prompting the company to admit errors in labeling some products and leading to the abrupt closure of its retail and food-service operations later that year.[3][4] The fallout included a federal investigation by the U.S. Department of Agriculture into its practices.[5] Prior to Belcampo, Fernald worked with the Slow Food movement in Italy and founded Live Culture, a consulting firm focused on artisanal food businesses.[6] She has received accolades such as Food & Wine's 40 under 40 and The New York Times' Nifty Fifty, and authored the cookbook Home Made: 101 Easy-as-Pie Recipes for Relaxed Entertaining.[1] More recently, Fernald co-founded Shakewell Fresh Protein, a brand offering whey-based shakes marketed for their regenerative sourcing and metabolic benefits.[7]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Anya Fernald was born on a raw-milk dairy farm in Bavaria, Germany, outside Munich, while her parents, American academics, were teaching and conducting research abroad.[8][6] Her father, Russell Fernald, is a biologist and the Benjamin Scott Crocker Professor in Human Biology at Stanford University, and her mother, Anne Fernald, is a developmental psychologist and professor emerita of psychology at Stanford.[8][6] The family resided above a cow shed during their time in Bavaria, reflecting the unconventional lifestyle of her professor parents, who emphasized direct engagement with agricultural environments.[6] Fernald's early childhood involved consuming unpasteurized milk and foods such as liverwurst sandwiches, shaped by the farm setting and her parents' maintenance of a large home garden upon returning to the United States.[8][9] The family moved back to the U.S. when she was three years old and later settled in Palo Alto, California, where Fernald completed high school.[10]

Academic pursuits

Fernald attended Wesleyan University in Middletown, Connecticut, enrolling in the College of Social Studies, an interdisciplinary program emphasizing history, government, economics, and philosophy.[11][10] In 1995, midway through her undergraduate studies, she took a year-long leave from the university.[12] She graduated in 1998 and received the Thomas J. Watson Fellowship, a prestigious one-year grant awarded to graduating seniors for independent international research projects free from formal academic affiliations.[11][13] The fellowship supported Fernald's self-directed inquiry into traditional cheesemaking and food production techniques, involving travel and hands-on work in Europe and North Africa.[14] At Wesleyan, Fernald cultivated an intense interest in cooking, dedicating substantial time to it and briefly considering withdrawal to focus on culinary pursuits instead.[15]

Professional career

Early roles in Europe (1999â€“2005)

Following her graduation from Wesleyan University in 1999, Fernald undertook a Watson Fellowship focused on the study of artisan cheesemaking, which enabled her to work as an itinerant cheesemaker across Europe and North America.[1] This fellowship, awarded in 1998, supported independent academic exploration of regional food traditions.[16] From 1999 to 2001, Fernald concentrated her efforts in Sicily, Italy, where she collaborated with small-scale cheesemakers to develop and implement business and marketing plans aimed at rural economic development.[1] Her work involved supporting producers associated with the Consorzio Pecorino Siciliano DOP, a protected designation of origin for Sicilian sheep's milk cheese, through initiatives that included European Union-funded programs to enhance local artisan operations and market access.[16] These activities emphasized sustainable practices for traditional products facing competition from industrialized alternatives.[17] Subsequently, Fernald relocated to Bra, Piedmont, to direct the International Presidia program at Slow Food headquarters, a role she held through much of the early 2000s until approximately 2005.[1] The Presidia initiative sought to preserve endangered food varieties and support small producers worldwide by documenting and promoting biodiversity in agriculture, with Fernald overseeing global coordination and funding efforts through the Slow Food Foundation for Biodiversity.[15] In this capacity, she co-authored A World of Presidia: Food, Culture & Community (2004), a publication detailing over 100 Presidia projects and their role in cultural preservation and economic viability for traditional foods.[18] Her contributions included fieldwork in Italy and advocacy for policies linking local identity to food production, drawing from her cheesemaking experiences to prioritize producer cooperatives and direct marketing strategies.[15]

Initial U.S.-based ventures (2006â€“2013)

In 2006, upon returning to California after years in Europe, Fernald directed a statewide farm-to-school initiative that integrated fresh, local produce into meals for over 100 low-income schools and spearheaded the launch of the California Buy Fresh, Buy Local campaign to promote regional sourcing by institutions and consumers.[1] By the summer of 2007, she had assumed the role of executive director for Slow Food Nation, coordinating a landmark event in San Francisco under the guidance of Alice Waters to elevate awareness of sustainable, high-quality food production and consumption, drawing tens of thousands of attendees and featuring panels, tastings, and policy discussions on reforming industrial agriculture.[1][15] Following this, Fernald initiated an informal community-supported agriculture (CSA) venture in San Francisco, distributing nose-to-tail shares of whole cows from a rented van to promote full-animal utilization and challenge prevailing low-fat dietary trends skeptical of pasture-raised meats.[6] In 2009, Fernald established Live Culture Co., a marketing and business consulting firm based in Oakland that advised more than two dozen values-aligned food enterprises on scaling operations while maintaining sustainability commitments, operating actively until around 2010.[1][15] That same year, she founded the Eat Real Festival Company, which hosted its inaugural Eat Real Festival in Oakland's Jack London Square, an annual event emphasizing unprocessed, regionally sourced foods through chef demonstrations, vendor markets, and educational workshops attended by thousands, with subsequent editions expanding to sites like Los Angeles by 2011.[1] These efforts positioned Fernald as a bridge between European artisanal traditions and American scalable food systems; by 2010, her consulting through Live Culture Co. included engagements with agricultural investors like Todd Robinson, laying groundwork for vertically integrated meat production models amid growing demand for traceable, regenerative sourcing.[15] During this period, she also served as a judge on Food Network's Iron Chef America starting in 2009, appearing in multiple episodes through 2011 to evaluate culinary innovations.[1]

Founding and leadership of Belcampo Meat Company (2013â€“2021)

Anya Fernald co-founded Belcampo Meat Co. in 2012 with Todd Robinson, who provided $50 million in initial financing to establish a vertically integrated meat production system focused on regenerative agriculture and sustainable practices.[6][2] The company aimed to control the entire supply chain, from owning and managing farmland to slaughter, processing, and retail distribution of grass-fed, antibiotic- and hormone-free beef, lamb, pork, and poultry raised on pasture.[8] Belcampo began operations with a processing facility and farm in Northern California, emphasizing transparency and ethical animal husbandry to differentiate from conventional industrial meat production.[19] As CEO from 2012 to August 2020, Fernald directed the expansion of Belcampo's land holdings to approximately 20,000 acres of certified organic farmland by 2017, enabling the company to scale production while maintaining claims of humane and environmentally restorative farming methods.[20][21] Under her leadership, Belcampo opened multiple retail butcher shops in urban centers including San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Santa Monica, alongside developing an e-commerce platform for nationwide shipping of fresh and frozen meats.[19] The company projected $20 million in revenue for 2016, driven by premium pricing for products marketed as superior in taste and nutrition due to grass-fed diets and stress-reduced slaughter processes.[6] Fernald's strategy involved investing heavily in infrastructure, such as a state-of-the-art slaughterhouse designed to minimize animal stress, and promoting Belcampo's model through public advocacy for regenerative grazing to sequester carbon and improve soil health.[8][19] She transitioned to co-CEO in August 2020 alongside Garry Embleton, continuing oversight until the company's challenges intensified in 2021, while maintaining focus on operational efficiency amid the slower maturation cycles of grass-fed livestock.[20][21]

Media and public appearances

Fernald has frequently appeared on podcasts to discuss regenerative agriculture, meat production, and her entrepreneurial ventures. On the Lex Fridman Podcast episode #203, released July 23, 2021, she addressed topics including carbon-positive farming methods and culinary techniques for meat, drawing from her experience at Belcampo.[22] She also guested on The Ready State podcast, where she explored the construction of Belcampo operations and adaptability in industrial meat alternatives.[23] Additional podcast features include episode #388 of the TSC HIM & HER SHOW, focusing on her role as Belcampo co-founder and CEO, and episode #49 of The Melanie Avalon Podcast on July 9, 2020, covering sustainable eating and red meat truths.[24][25] In television and video media, Fernald demonstrated recipes on CBS This Morning Saturday on August 24, 2019, featuring Belcampo bratwurst and sausage preparation.[26] She appeared in a collaborative video with Traeger Grills on September 29, 2019, emphasizing the superior taste of naturally raised meats.[27] The PBS program Food Forward profiled her as a "Food Rebel," showcasing her emphasis on paying premiums for high-quality, sustainable meat.[28] Print and online interviews have covered her daily routines and business expansions. The Wall Street Journal detailed a typical day in her life on April 20, 2017, including plans for a Belcampo distillery in Belize.[21] Food GPS conducted an interview on June 10, 2016, discussing Belcampo alongside her cookbook Home Cooked and the Eat Real Festival.[29] Post-Belcampo, she featured in a YouTube discussion on August 15, 2024, critiquing conventional farming practices while promoting her newer initiatives like Shakewell.[30]

Belcampo controversies and downfall

Sourcing and labeling discrepancies

In May 2021, a former butcher at Belcampo's Santa Monica store posted a video alleging that the company was sourcing meat from conventional distributors and mislabeling it as organic, grass-fed, and originating from Belcampo's own regenerative farms in California.[31][32] The employee claimed that products labeled as Belcampo-branded, sustainable beef were actually factory-farmed cuts from industrial suppliers, including grain-finished animals not raised on pasture their entire lives, violating grass-fed standards.[31][33] Belcampo co-founder and CEO Anya Fernald acknowledged in a statement that the Santa Monica location had failed to adhere to the company's "strict protocols" for sourcing and labeling, resulting in misrepresented product origins for a "small percentage" of inventory.[34][2] The company admitted to mislabeling some meats as organic and grass-fed that did not meet those certifications, attributing the issue to lapses in verification processes rather than intentional fraud across operations.[35][36] Fernald emphasized that the majority of products complied with claims of being raised on Belcampo-controlled lands using regenerative practices, but halted sales at the implicated store and initiated an internal investigation.[34][37] Subsequent reports from current and former employees indicated the mislabeling was not isolated to Santa Monica, alleging it occurred systematically at multiple locations for months, involving substitution of cheaper, non-pasture-raised beef to meet demand amid supply constraints from Belcampo's limited farm output.[38] Belcampo's marketing positioned nearly all products as vertically integrated from its 20,000-acre California ranches, with cattle grass-fed year-round and processed in-house, but records showed reliance on external Midwest suppliers for volume, where animals often received grain supplements, undermining "100% grass-fed" labels under USDA guidelines.[3][39] These discrepancies eroded consumer trust in the premium pricingâ€”often 20-50% above conventional meatâ€”tied to unverified sustainability narratives.[2][3]

Regulatory investigations and legal fallout

In May 2021, allegations surfaced from a former employee at Belcampo's Santa Monica store claiming that commodity beef and poultry were being repackaged and mislabeled as organic, grass-fed, and farm-raised from Belcampo's own facilities, prompting the company to acknowledge isolated instances of mislabeling following an internal review.[36][3] This led to a class-action lawsuit filed by customer Justine Freedman in June 2021 in Los Angeles Superior Court, alleging false advertising and seeking damages for purchasers of the misrepresented products on behalf of affected consumers.[5] Regulatory scrutiny intensified after Belcampo's abrupt closure of all operations in October 2021, with the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) launching an investigation into the company's practices, as reported in December 2022.[5][40] The probe focused on unsanitary conditions at Belcampo's Colusa, California processing facility, including mold on hanging meat carcasses, improper storage of meat in non-refrigerated areas, and violations of federal standards for processing, maintenance, and labeling.[41][42] Documents obtained by the San Francisco Chronicle indicated that these issues contributed to broader non-compliance with USDA oversight, though the agency has not publicly detailed outcomes or penalties as of the latest reports.[5] No criminal charges have been filed against Belcampo executives, including CEO Anya Fernald, and the class-action suit's resolution remains pending without publicized settlements tied directly to regulatory findings.[5] The fallout underscored gaps in federal verification of premium meat claims, with critics noting that USDA inspections failed to detect ongoing discrepancies despite prior warnings about imported meat sourcing.[39]

Company closure and financial implications

Belcampo Meat Co. ceased all retail, e-commerce, and restaurant operations on October 18, 2021, abruptly shuttering its butcher shops in cities including San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Santa Monica, as well as its meatpacking and farm assets in subsequent months.[43][4][41] The closure followed months of fallout from a May 2021 scandal involving admitted mislabeling of meat sources, which eroded consumer trust in the company's claims of sustainable, pasture-raised production.[5][44] While the company stated it was exploring options for its supply chain assets, no revival of public-facing operations occurred, and the entity effectively dissolved without reported formal bankruptcy filings.[43] Financially, Belcampo had launched in 2013 with $50 million in seed financing from investor Todd Robinson, intended to fund vertical integration from regenerative farms to direct-to-consumer sales at premium prices.[45][2] However, scaling this model amid high operational costs for land, labor, and certificationâ€”coupled with supply disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemicâ€”strained cash flows, as acknowledged by co-founder Anya Fernald in post-scandal statements.[45] Indicators of distress included a job listing for its Siskiyou County processing plant offering $18 per hour starting pay, below the state average for meat processing roles, suggesting efforts to cut expenses.[46] The mislabeling revelations amplified these pressures by prompting customer refunds, lost sales, and heightened scrutiny, ultimately rendering the business model untenable.[3][47] The shutdown's implications extended beyond Belcampo, rippling through the sustainable meat sector by disrupting supplier networks and underscoring risks in premium, ethics-branded supply chains.[44][47] Employees faced immediate job losses without public notice of severance or transition support, while creditors and ranching partners in Northern California reported unpaid obligations in industry accounts, though specific debt totals remain undisclosed.[47] For Fernald, who had withdrawn from day-to-day management months earlier but retained a board seat, the closure marked the end of her primary entrepreneurial venture, with no reported personal financial liability from lawsuits tied directly to insolvency at the time.[4][5] The episode highlighted causal vulnerabilities in ventures reliant on consumer premiums for unverified sustainability claims, contributing to broader skepticism in the regenerative agriculture movement.[44]

Fernald's accountability and public response

Fernald addressed the May 2021 mislabeling allegations at the Santa Monica store by issuing a statement describing the issue as an "isolated incident" affecting a small percentage of products, while announcing an internal investigation and retraining protocols.[34] [33] In an Instagram video apology, she acknowledged the errors, stressed the company's commitment to a "culture of accountability and integrity," and vowed to rebuild customer trust, though she did not confirm whether mislabeling extended beyond that location.[3] The response drew criticism for minimizing the scope, with commenters questioning the adequacy of the measures.[48] Following a viral video in summer 2021 exposing non-Belcampo meat being prepared and labeled in-store, Fernald publicly apologized on behalf of the company, positioning herself as a key voice in damage control efforts.[4] However, she refrained from commenting on allegations that she or executives were aware of ongoing sourcing discrepancies, such as the use of imported grains contradicting grass-fed claims.[49] Amid Belcampo's October 2021 closure, Fernald had withdrawn from operational involvement months earlier, retaining only a board seat, with the company's statement citing pandemic-related financial strains alongside reputational damage from the scandals.[4] She subsequently removed Belcampo affiliations from her Instagram profile and, in a podcast appearance, described her role as limited to chair of the board, thereby lacking day-to-day operational accountability.[43] [23] Facing 2022 reports of USDA and state investigations into false labeling and whistleblower claims of retaliatory firings, Fernald offered no public response despite multiple interview requests directed through legal and press channels.[5] This silence contrasted with her earlier proactive statements, leaving unresolved questions about personal oversight during the company's supply chain practices.[50]

Post-Belcampo activities

New business ventures

Following the closure of Belcampo Meat Company in October 2021, Fernald co-founded Shakewell Fresh Protein in 2025, a direct-to-consumer company specializing in fresh, cold-shipped protein shakes formulated with organic whey isolate.[51] Each serving provides 25 grams of protein from whey isolate, totaling 175 calories, and incorporates minimal ingredients such as fresh liquid whey, organic allulose for sweetness, and lemon juice, without preservatives, gums, or artificial additives.[52] The shakes are positioned as a tool for muscle preservation and fat loss, drawing on Fernald's prior emphasis on high-quality, minimally processed animal-derived proteins.[53] Shakewell began shipping products in June 2025, with Fernald promoting DIY replication recipes for consumers lacking access to fresh whey, underscoring the company's reliance on non-shelf-stable components for freshness and efficacy.[54] The venture reflects a pivot from large-scale meat production to portable, nutrition-focused supplements, maintaining Fernald's focus on regenerative and clean sourcing, though independent verification of supply chain claims remains limited to her public statements.[55] No additional capital raises or partnerships have been publicly detailed as of October 2025.

Ongoing advocacy and content creation

Following the closure of Belcampo Meat Company in 2021, Fernald has maintained advocacy for regenerative agriculture practices, emphasizing their role in soil health, carbon sequestration, and sustainable livestock production. In October 2024, she publicly critiqued major food corporations such as Cargill, Bayer, and Unilever for adopting the "regenerative agriculture" label without implementing verifiable changes in farming methods, arguing this constitutes greenwashing that undermines genuine efforts in the sector.[56] She has highlighted policy challenges, including the decline in U.S. dairy farms from 6 million to under 26,000 by 2023, attributing it to regulatory and economic pressures that disadvantage small-scale, regenerative operations while underscoring the essential contributions of animal agriculture to food security and environmental restoration.[57] Fernald's content creation centers on social media platforms, where she shares practical recipes focused on whole foods, open-fire cooking techniques, and nutrient-dense ingredients like fresh whey protein. With over 256,000 Instagram followers as of 2025, her posts demonstrate methods for preparing meats, fish, and homemade protein shakes using minimal, high-quality components, such as combining organic whey isolate with allulose and lemon juice for DIY shakes.[58][54] She extends this through podcast appearances, including an August 2024 episode where she discussed undisclosed practices in industrial farming, such as sourcing inconsistencies and the nutritional superiority of regeneratively raised meats over conventional alternatives.[59] Her advocacy intersects with content by promoting consumer education on food labeling and production realities, often drawing from her experience to advocate for transparency in supply chains. For instance, in 2025 social media updates, she addressed the erosion of trust in meat labeling post-Belcampo scandals, urging scrutiny of claims around sustainability and animal welfare.[51] This work positions her as a vocal proponent of shifting toward decentralized, soil-focused farming models amid broader industry critiques of factory farming's environmental and health impacts.

Publications and intellectual contributions

Authored books

Anya Fernald co-authored Home Cooked: Essential Recipes for a New Way to Cook, published by Ten Speed Press in April 2016, with recipe developer Jessica Battilana. [60] The 304-page cookbook emphasizes foundational cooking techniques, such as preparing stocks, sauces, and braises, to enable efficient weeknight meals using high-quality ingredients like pasture-raised meats and seasonal produce.[61] [62] It reflects Fernald's advocacy for sustainable, flavor-forward home cooking, drawing from her experience at Belcampo Meat Co. and Slow Food USA.[1] Fernald has also contributed to Slow Food publications, including co-authorship on titles issued by the organization's international publishing house, though these are not solo-authored works.[1] No additional primary-authored books by Fernald appear in major publisher catalogs as of 2025.[63][64]

Other media and writings

Fernald served as a judge and sustainable food expert on Iron Chef America during the 2009, 2010, and 2011 seasons, evaluating contestants' dishes with an emphasis on quality ingredients and ethical sourcing.[1] She also appeared as a judge on related Food Network programs, including Iron Chef Gauntlet and The Next Iron Chef.[24] In podcast media, Fernald has contributed discussions on regenerative agriculture, ethical meat production, and culinary techniques. Notable appearances include the Lex Fridman Podcast episode #203 in July 2021, where she detailed carbon sequestration through farming practices and optimal meat preparation methods.[65] On The Ready State podcast in June 2021, she addressed Belcampo's shift to carbon-positive operations amid industrial meat challenges.[23] Additional episodes feature her on Wellness Mama in February 2020, advocating meat's role in human and environmental health,[66] and Roots of Change in January 2019, covering her transition from Slow Food initiatives to meat entrepreneurship.[67] Fernald's writings outside authored books are sparse in public record, with contributions primarily through co-authored Slow Food publications predating her major books; however, she shares practical recipes and farming insights via Instagram, such as a 2021 post on searing ground beef with chili powder for quick protein preparation.[68] No major op-eds or guest articles in outlets like The New York Times or Bon AppÃ©tit were identified as penned by her.[69]

Recognition and critiques

Awards received

In 2010, Food & Wine magazine named Fernald one of its "40 Big Food Thinkers 40 and Under" for her early work in sustainable food systems and fermentation through Slow Food and Live Culture Productions.[70][1] In 2016, she received the University of California Global Food Initiative 30 Under 30 Award, recognizing emerging leaders addressing global food challenges, particularly through Belcampo's vertically integrated regenerative meat production model.[71] Fernald was also designated a "Nifty Fifty" by The New York Times as part of its STEM initiative highlighting influential young professionals in science, technology, engineering, and math-related fields, with her inclusion tied to innovations in ethical livestock farming.[1] In 2018, Inc. magazine included her among its "100 Female Founders," honoring women building high-impact companies, in recognition of her leadership at Belcampo Meat Co.[72][23]

Broader industry evaluations

The 2021 mislabeling scandal at Belcampo's Santa Monica location, where commodity meat from external suppliers was sold as the company's own organic and grass-fed products at premiums up to $47.99 per pound, drew industry scrutiny to verification challenges in regenerative supply chains.[32][34] Sustainable butchers, such as Bryan Mayer of Golden Gate Meat Company, argued the incident eroded trust in the "good meat" movement, making it harder for ethical producers to differentiate from commodity systems reliant on lax oversight.[2] Fernald's vertical integration approachâ€”combining ranching, processing, and retail to promote regenerative practices like rotational grazing and soil healthâ€”was lauded early for transparency but critiqued for scalability limits, as farm output failed to match retail expansion, prompting unverified sourcing.[38] Industry observers noted that Belcampo's model exemplified broader hurdles in premium meat, including high costs and supply inconsistencies that pressure operators to cut corners without robust auditing.[73] Post-scandal evaluations highlighted regenerative agriculture's promise for environmental benefits, such as carbon sequestration, but emphasized economic viability issues, with Belcampo's store closures in October 2021 underscoring the need for scale to reduce per-unit costsâ€” a challenge Fernald's framework did not overcome despite raising over $100 million in funding.[45][19] Experts in the sector, including those from Pasturebird, urged caution against generalizing the failure but acknowledged it as a cautionary tale for verifying "farm-to-table" claims amid growth pressures.[32]

Personal life

Family and relationships

Fernald married Renato Sardo, an Italian cheesemaker and former executive with Slow Food International, around 2004 after meeting him during her time in Italy.[74] The couple resided in Oakland, California, and had two children: a daughter, Viola, born circa 2012, and a son, Theo.[8][75] Fernald and Sardo separated by late 2014.[8] In subsequent years, Fernald entered a relationship with neuroscientist Andrew Huberman, during which the pair pursued in vitro fertilization to conceive a child, though no offspring resulted. The relationship concluded amid allegations of infidelity and mismatched commitments, as detailed in a 2024 New York magazine profile. No further marriages or partnerships have been publicly confirmed as of 2025.

Lifestyle and public persona

Fernald resides in the San Francisco Bay Area in a 1920s-style home featuring a renovated, maximalist kitchen with DIY elements such as antique elevator cage cabinet doors, tin ceiling tile backsplash, and custom Moroccan tiles, creating a whimsical space intended to induce smiles in her children, aged 7 and 4 as of the article's publication.[76] She cooks nearly all meals outdoors on two wood-fired grills accessible via French doors during warm weather, managing multiple projects in a lively, rustic environment with vintage collections like mortars and pestles and nicked cast-iron sinks.[76] Her personal habits emphasize artisanal food practices, including avid production and consumption of fermented items, alongside simple, hearty preparations like butter and salami sandwiches or steaks cooked in a screaming-hot cast-iron skillet.[1] [6] Entertaining centers on no-fuss, Italian peasant-style dishes such as caramelized green beans and tallow-fried potatoes, informed by her travels and internships in Europe and Africa.[6] In her public persona, Fernald embodies a forthright advocate for sustainable, nutrient-rich foods, famously stating she has "never been overly concerned about whether people like me or not."[6] Dubbed America's foremost female butcher, she promotes hands-on skills through women-only "meat camps" focused on butchering and grilling, while emphasizing intuitive food preferences from infancyâ€”such as her daughter's enjoyment of bone-teething and fatsâ€”to foster generational appreciation for quality animal products.[6]
