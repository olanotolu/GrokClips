List of current NFL stadiums

The list of current NFL stadiums enumerates the 30 venues primarily used as home fields by the National Football League's 32 franchises during regular season and playoff games, with the New York Jets and New York Giants sharing MetLife Stadium and the Los Angeles Rams and Los Angeles Chargers sharing SoFi Stadium.[1][2] These facilities, distributed across the United States, range from the league's oldest continuously operating stadium, Lambeau Field in Green Bay, Wisconsin, which opened in 1957, to the newest, SoFi Stadium in Inglewood, California, which debuted in 2020.[3][4] Capacities vary from approximately 61,000 at Dignity Health Sports Park to over 100,000 at events in AT&T Stadium, reflecting differences in construction eras, urban settings, and design priorities such as natural grass surfaces, artificial turf, fixed roofs, or retractable coverings.[1] Many have undergone significant renovations to meet modern safety, comfort, and revenue standards, often involving substantial public subsidies that have sparked debates over economic returns for host communities.[5] The stadiums also host non-NFL events like college bowl games, concerts, and international NFL matchups, underscoring their role as multi-purpose arenas in American sports infrastructure.[6]

Stadium Characteristics

Capacity and Seating

NFL stadiums feature fixed seating capacities typically ranging from 61,500 at Soldier Field to 82,500 at MetLife Stadium, accommodating an average of approximately 70,000 spectators across the league's 30 venues.[7][8] This range reflects permanent bowl configurations optimized for regular-season games, where lower capacities in older or urban-constrained sites like Soldier Field limit baseline attendance compared to expansive modern facilities.[9][8] Many stadiums incorporate expandable designs, adding temporary stands or standing-room areas to increase capacity for postseason playoffs, Super Bowls, or mega-events, often exceeding 100,000 total attendees. For instance, AT&T Stadium maintains a base of 80,000 fixed seats but expands to nearly 100,000 through end-zone additions and standing options, enabling higher attendance peaks without permanent overbuild.[10] Similarly, SoFi Stadium reaches up to 100,240 in expanded mode, illustrating how such flexibility supports event-specific demand while preserving regular-game intimacy.[11] These configurations distinguish core permanent seatingâ€”prioritizing sightlines and comfortâ€”from auxiliary spaces used sporadically to boost per-game revenue potential via additional tickets and concessions.[11] Seating allocations emphasize premium club and suite areas, which comprise 10-12% of total capacity in most venues and generate disproportionate team profits through elevated pricing and amenities like lounges and exclusive access.[12] This tiering contrasts with general admission sections, fostering revenue diversification as clubs and suites attract high-value patrons, while broader seating ensures mass attendance for sustained fan engagement. Post-2000 renovations and constructions have amplified this trend, with over half of current stadiums built or overhauled since then to enlarge overall capacities and integrate more premium inventory, aligning with rising league popularity and monetization strategies.[13][13]

Field Surfaces and Environmental Controls

As of the 2025 NFL season, NFL stadiums utilize a mix of artificial turf and natural grass surfaces, with approximately 15 stadiums featuring artificial turf and 15 employing natural grass or hybrid variants.[14] Artificial turf, such as FieldTurf, predominates in indoor and multi-purpose venues due to its durability and lower maintenance requirements, serving as the primary surface for eight teams including the New England Patriots at Gillette Stadium and the Seattle Seahawks at Lumen Field.[15] Natural grass, often Bermuda or Kentucky bluegrass varieties, is common in open-air stadiums like Hard Rock Stadium (Miami Dolphins), where it supports high-traffic play but demands intensive upkeep including frequent resodding.[16] Hybrid systems, blending natural grass roots with synthetic fibers for enhanced stability, are used in select venues such as Lambeau Field (Green Bay Packers) and Lincoln Financial Field (Philadelphia Eagles), where SIS Grass technology injects plastic fibers into the turf to reduce wear while preserving a grass-like footing.[17] These hybrids aim to mitigate the divot formation and uneven play associated with pure natural grass under NFL's demanding schedule of up to 10 home games plus playoffs. Empirical data from NFL player injury tracking indicates higher rates of non-contact lower extremity injuries on artificial turf compared to natural grass; for instance, a 2012-2016 analysis found elevated lower body injury incidence on synthetic surfaces, with non-contact injuries occurring at a 28% higher rate per NFL Players Association (NFLPA) assessments.[18][19] Similarly, anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) sprains show a 67% increase on FieldTurf versus grass in NFL games, underscoring turf's firmer, less forgiving impact absorption that correlates with greater joint stress during cuts and decelerations.[20] Environmental controls in NFL stadiums primarily distinguish open-air configurations from enclosed ones, with 10 venues featuring fixed domes or retractable roofs as of 2025, including U.S. Bank Stadium (Minnesota Vikings, fixed translucent dome) and Mercedes-Benz Stadium (Atlanta Falcons, retractable ETFE panels).[1] These structures enable year-round climate regulation via HVAC systems, eliminating weather-induced postponementsâ€”such as snow or extreme heat delays that have historically affected open-air gamesâ€”and maintaining consistent footing temperatures to prevent surface hardening or softening.[21] However, enclosed stadiums incur elevated operational costs for heating, ventilation, and air conditioning, with energy demands amplified in larger domes like AT&T Stadium (Dallas Cowboys), where retractable roofs allow selective openness but default to closed modes for fan comfort and turf preservation. The remaining 20 open-air stadiums, such as Arrowhead Stadium (Kansas City Chiefs), expose fields to ambient conditions, fostering variable game dynamics like wind-influenced kicking accuracy or rain-slicked traction but heightening risks of playable surface degradation from precipitation or frost.[22] Overall, surface and enclosure choices balance player safetyâ€”evidenced by turf's injury profileâ€”with logistical reliability, though the NFL has not mandated uniform grass adoption despite union advocacy citing biomechanical data favoring natural substrates for shock dissipation.[23]

Architectural and Technological Features

Several current NFL stadiums incorporate advanced video display technologies to enhance spectator immersion. AT&T Stadium features two high-definition video boards, each measuring 160 feet wide by 72 feet high, suspended above the field to provide near end-zone views for all seats and generate significant advertising revenue through dynamic content.[24] SoFi Stadium employs a 360-degree dual-screen video board encircling the playing field, integrated into open-air concourses for unobstructed viewing across its 3.1 million square feet.[25] Allegiant Stadium includes over 2,550 displays, comprising 41 LED videoboards and three 4K HDR-capable boards within the bowl, supporting high-resolution replays and fan engagement features.[26] Sustainability integrations vary, with some stadiums prioritizing on-site renewable energy. MetLife Stadium's solar ring consists of 1,350 building-integrated photovoltaic panels encircling the roof, generating electricity to power its LED lighting ringâ€”producing 25 times the energy required for nighttime illuminationâ€”while reducing reliance on grid power during events.[27] Allegiant Stadium utilizes energy-efficient LED lighting and event-specific controls that consume 49% less energy than code allowances, complemented by a translucent ETFE roof allowing natural light penetration.[28] Accessibility enhancements comply with the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), mandating features like wheelchair-accessible seating, companion seats, and ramps at all major entrances. Post-ADA enforcement, stadiums such as SoFi incorporate universal design principles, including sensory rooms for neurodiverse fans and automated checkout-free stores aiding mobility-impaired visitors.[29] [30] Operational technologies, including 1,700 Wi-Fi 6 access points at Allegiant Stadium backed by 40-gigabit connections, ensure reliable connectivity for assistive apps and real-time accommodations.[31]

Geographical Distribution and Accessibility

The NFL's 32 stadiums are geographically concentrated in 22 states, with clustering around major metropolitan areas such as the New York-New Jersey region, Greater Los Angeles, the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex, and Chicago, facilitating access for dense urban populations. Sun Belt states exhibit higher team density relative to their share of the U.S. population, exemplified by Texas (Dallas Cowboys at AT&T Stadium in Arlington and Houston Texans at NRG Stadium) and Florida (Miami Dolphins at Hard Rock Stadium and Tampa Bay Buccaneers at Raymond James Stadium), driven by southward migration and economic expansion since the league's post-merger expansions in the 1970s and 1990s.[33][34] In contrast, the Midwest preserves legacy venues in smaller or mid-sized markets like Green Bay's Lambeau Field and Detroit's Ford Field, reflecting historical franchise stability rather than recent relocations or expansions.[33] Accessibility to these stadiums predominantly relies on automobile travel via interstate highways, with most facilities designed around expansive parking lots accommodating 10,000 to 20,000 vehicles, as suburban or edge-city placements allow for such infrastructure without urban congestion constraints. For example, NRG Stadium's location in Houston's NRG Park complex, approximately 10 miles southwest of downtown, emphasizes highway access (Interstate 610) but has faced criticism for insufficient integration with broader public transit networks, necessitating drives for many fans despite adjacent METRORail service.[35] Proximity to major airports enhances regional draw, with venues like SoFi Stadium near Los Angeles International Airport (about 10 miles away) and MetLife Stadium near Newark Liberty International (roughly 15 miles), though ground transport post-flight often defaults to rideshares or shuttles due to variable public options. Public transit viability differs markedly: urban-adjacent sites like M&T Bank Stadium in Baltimore benefit from MARC train and light rail connections, while more isolated suburban setups, such as Empower Field at Mile High in Denver's outskirts, prioritize I-25 access over rail.[35][36] Regional attendance patterns in the 2024 season, with a league-wide average of 69,555 per home game, reveal influences from local demographics and climate, as Sun Belt markets leverage milder weather for consistent outdoor events and larger, growing populations in metros like Dallas (averaging over 93,000) and Miami.[37][38] Midwest venues, facing colder temperatures that can deter casual attendees in late-season games, nonetheless achieve high utilization through entrenched fan loyalty, as evidenced by Green Bay's near-capacity averages of 78,000 despite smaller metro size and variable winter conditions.[38] These variations underscore how locational factorsâ€”population density in Sun Belt hubs versus cultural entrenchment in Midwest outpostsâ€”correlate with turnout, independent of stadium capacity alone.[37]

Upcoming Stadium Projects

Under Construction and Planned Replacements

The Buffalo Bills' New Highmark Stadium in Orchard Park, New York, is under construction as a direct replacement for the existing open-air facility, with completion targeted for July 2026 ahead of the 2026 NFL season. Groundbreaking occurred in June 2023, and by October 2025, crews had advanced to installing the natural grass field and initial seating sections, reflecting steady progress despite broader industry challenges from post-pandemic supply chain disruptions.[39][40] The Tennessee Titans' New Nissan Stadium, an enclosed domed structure on the East Bank of the Cumberland River in Nashville, Tennessee, reached its halfway construction mark in September 2025, on pace for a February 2027 opening. Site work began in early 2024, with recent focus on integrating mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems to support year-round event hosting.[41][42] Jacksonville Jaguars' Stadium of the Future entails a $1.4 billion renovation of EverBank Stadium in Jacksonville, Florida, incorporating a fixed roof and enhanced amenities, with full completion scheduled for August 2028. As of October 2025, visible milestones include the opening of the initial grand staircase, allowing continued home games at near-full capacity through the 2025 season before phased reductions in 2026 and 2027 to accommodate structural upgrades.[43][44] The Chicago Bears' proposed replacement for Soldier Field centers on a new facility in Arlington Heights, Illinois, with detailed renderings unveiled in September 2025 envisioning a modern venue potentially opening by 2028. The team has pledged over $2 billion toward development, including site infrastructure, though as of October 2025, no groundbreaking has occurred amid ongoing negotiations over property taxes and community approvals.[45][46]

Financing and Economic Realities

Public Funding Mechanisms and Subsidies

A significant portion of NFL stadium financing relies on public subsidies, with only three of the league's current venues constructed primarily through private funding, leaving the remainder involving taxpayer contributions exceeding $10.6 billion in total.[58] These subsidies often manifest through mechanisms such as tax-exempt municipal bonds, dedicated sales or hotel occupancy taxes, and infrastructure improvements adjacent to stadium sites, enabling local governments to issue debt backed by future tax revenues without directly raising general property taxes.[58] For instance, the new Nissan Stadium for the Tennessee Titans in Nashville incorporates $1.26 billion in public fundsâ€”comprising $760 million in city bonds and $500 million in state bondsâ€”out of a total projected cost of $2.1 billion, marking the largest such subsidy for any U.S. sports stadium.[59] Lease agreements between teams and public entities further structure these subsidies by allowing franchise owners to retain substantial revenue streams, including naming rights, premium seating sales, and concessions, while municipalities assume ongoing maintenance and operational expenses.[60] In the case of the Buffalo Bills' new Highmark Stadium, public contributions total $850 million ($600 million from New York State and $250 million from Erie County) toward a project now exceeding $2.1 billion, with the team covering the balance but benefiting from lease terms that limit its share of non-NFL event revenues and shift upkeep costs to taxpayers.[61] Such arrangements persist despite NFL franchises averaging $7.1 billion in valuation as of 2025, with owners frequently citing anticipated economic developmentâ€”such as job creation and tourism boostsâ€”to justify public involvement, though these rationales focus on projected local multipliers rather than direct fiscal returns.[62]

Empirical Assessments of Economic Impact

Empirical research, including meta-analyses of over 100 studies, demonstrates that NFL stadiums generate negligible net positive economic impacts on host cities, with public subsidies rarely, if ever, recouping costs through increased GDP, employment, or tax revenues. Economists Brad R. Humphreys, Dennis Coates, and J.C. Bradbury's 2023 policy retrospective synthesizes evidence showing zero to negative effects on local per capita income and overall economic activity, attributing this to substitutionâ€”where consumer spending shifts from other sectors without net additionâ€”and leakage of revenues to non-local team owners and leagues.[63][64] Peer-reviewed analyses consistently refute claims of substantial job creation or tourism multipliers, finding that stadium-related employment is temporary and low-wage, often offset by displacement in retail and hospitality; for example, a comprehensive review by Coates and Humphreys documents adverse or insignificant long-term employment effects across U.S. metropolitan areas with new venues. Event-day spending, while generating gross figures touted by team-commissioned reports, fails to exceed baseline economic activity, as fans substitute stadium visits for alternative local expenditures, yielding no causal boost to broader fiscal health.[65][66] No NFL stadium has empirically returned public investments via tax recoupment, with studies showing subsidiesâ€”averaging hundreds of millions per projectâ€”exceed any attributable revenue gains by factors of 2 to 10, after adjusting for time horizons of 30+ years. In Atlanta's Mercedes-Benz Stadium case, Georgia World Congress Center Authority reports claim 15,392 supported jobs and $127.5 million in state/local taxes for FY2025, yet independent causal assessments align with broader findings of minimal net addition, as construction peaks fade without sustained multipliers and opportunity costs mount for alternative public uses like schools yielding 1.5-2x higher returns per dollar invested.[67][68][69] This consensus holds against proponent arguments, as rigorous difference-in-differences and instrumental variable approaches in recent papers confirm that funds diverted to stadiums represent inefficient transfers benefiting private entities over public welfare, with no evidence of positive spillovers justifying the fiscal burden on taxpayers.[70]

Additional Uses and Neutral Sites

Hosting Non-Regular Season Events

Current NFL stadiums frequently host postseason playoff games, including wild-card, divisional, and conference championship contests, when their teams secure home-field advantage via regular-season seeding. These events draw intensified crowds and require enhanced security, field conditioning, and fan amenities beyond regular games, with higher-seeded teams benefiting from familiar environments and supporter energy. For instance, in the 2024-25 playoffs, venues such as GEHA Field at Arrowhead Stadium, Raymond James Stadium, NRG Stadium, M&T Bank Stadium, and State Farm Stadium accommodated multiple rounds, underscoring the role of team performance in venue utilization.[71] The Super Bowl, the NFL's neutral-site championship, is awarded to select current stadiums meeting stringent criteria: a base capacity of at least 70,000 seats (expandable for larger attendance), advanced media facilities, electrical infrastructure for broadcasts, and luxury features like premium suites and climate control to attract visitors. Allegiant Stadium hosted Super Bowl LVIII on February 11, 2024, accommodating 62,000 fixed seats plus expansions. Caesars Superdome hosted Super Bowl LIX on February 9, 2025, leveraging its indoor design and 73,000-seat capacity. Levi's Stadium is scheduled for Super Bowl LX on February 8, 2026, with its 68,500 seats expandable to over 75,000 via temporary stands.[72][73][74] Pro Bowl events, restructured as flag football showcases since 2023, have utilized NFL stadiums intermittently, such as Allegiant Stadium for the 2022 edition before shifting to Orlando's Camping World Stadium in subsequent years. Recent NFL announcements integrate Pro Bowl Games into Super Bowl week at the host city, potentially returning to primary NFL venues like those in Levi's Stadium's vicinity for 2026, emphasizing broadcast-friendly formats over traditional tackle play. Host cities for these marquee events shoulder substantial costs for policing, transportation upgrades, and maintenanceâ€”often exceeding $100 million for Super Bowlsâ€”while the NFL allocates revenue shares from tickets, sponsorships, and media rights, though empirical analyses indicate net economic benefits to locales remain debated due to displacement of regular tourism.[75]

International and Temporary Venues

The National Football League has conducted regular-season games at international venues since 2007, utilizing soccer-specific stadiums adapted temporarily for American football through the installation of synthetic turf fields over existing pitches, goalpost additions, and sideline adjustments to meet league specifications.[76] These setups typically require 48-72 hours for field preparation and removal, incurring additional costs estimated at $500,000-$1 million per event for logistics and conversions.[77] In 2025, the NFL scheduled seven international games across five countries, marking the league's most extensive overseas slate to date, with three contests in London at Tottenham Hotspur Stadium and Wembley Stadium.[76] The October 5 matchup between the Minnesota Vikings and Cleveland Browns at Tottenham Hotspur Stadium, which holds 62,850 spectators, drew 61,247 attendees and resulted in a 21-17 Vikings victory.[78] [79] Subsequent London games included the New York Jets versus Denver Broncos on October 12 at Wembley Stadium (capacity adapted to approximately 83,000 for NFL configurations) and the Jacksonville Jaguars versus Los Angeles Rams on October 19 at Wembley.[78] [80] Additional 2025 venues encompassed Aviva Stadium in Dublin, Ireland (capacity 51,700), Olympiastadion in Berlin, Germany (capacity 74,475), and Santiago BernabÃ©u Stadium in Madrid, Spain (capacity 81,044), each requiring similar field adaptations.[81] Logistical challenges for these games include transatlantic travel disrupting player recovery, with jet lag contributing to elevated injury risks and performance dips; teams mitigate this by arriving 7-10 days early for acclimation, inverting sleep schedules pre-flight, and employing cryotherapy, as documented in NFL player health protocols.[77] While international games have boosted global viewershipâ€”averaging 1.5-2 million U.S. TV viewers per London contest since 2013â€”they generate limited per-game profits after accounting for $3-5 million in travel and setup expenses, often offset by long-term marketing gains rather than immediate revenue.[77] Ad-hoc temporary venues within international series, such as neutral-site backups for weather or scheduling conflicts, remain rare but exemplify the league's contingency planning, as seen in prior European events rerouted due to pitch conditions.[82]
