2023 Gaza war ceasefire

The 2023 Gaza war ceasefire was a temporary seven-day humanitarian pause in the Israel-Hamas conflict, effective from November 24 to December 1, 2023, following Hamas's October 7 terrorist attacks on Israel that killed approximately 1,200 people and resulted in the abduction of 251 hostages.[1] Mediated by Qatar, the United States, and Egypt, the agreement facilitated the phased release of 105 civilian hostages held by Hamas—including 81 Israeli women, children, and elderly—in exchange for Israel's release of 240 Palestinian prisoners, predominantly women and minors convicted of non-lethal offenses.[2][3] The truce also enabled a significant increase in humanitarian aid deliveries to Gaza, with up to 200 aid trucks permitted daily compared to prior restrictions, alongside a partial Israeli withdrawal from certain positions to allow southern Gaza access for displaced civilians.[3] Despite initial extensions from four to seven days, the ceasefire collapsed on December 1 when Hamas failed to release the final group of promised hostages, conditioning further exchanges on Israeli commitments to a permanent end to hostilities, which Israel rejected in favor of continued military operations to secure remaining captives and dismantle Hamas infrastructure.[4][3] Israel accused Hamas of violating the truce through projectiles fired toward Israeli territory and failure to adhere to release terms, prompting resumption of airstrikes and ground operations that intensified the war's toll, with Gaza health authorities later reporting over 44,000 Palestinian deaths by mid-2025.[4] Hamas, in turn, alleged Israeli breaches via troop movements and aid obstructions, though independent verification highlighted Hamas's tactical delays in hostage handovers as a primary breakdown factor.[3] The pause achieved partial hostage recovery and brief relief for Gaza's 2.3 million residents but underscored deep asymmetries: Israel's focus on eradicating Hamas's military capacity post-October 7 versus Hamas's demands for full prisoner releases and war termination without demilitarization concessions. Controversies included domestic Israeli criticism of prisoner swaps potentially incentivizing future abductions and Hamas's use of the interlude for regrouping, as evidenced by subsequent rocket fire and tunnel repairs. No permanent resolution emerged, with fighting persisting until later truces in 2025, reflecting the ceasefire's role as a limited tactical respite rather than a strategic pivot toward peace.[1][5]

Historical Context

October 7, 2023, Hamas Attack

On October 7, 2023, Hamas launched a multi-pronged assault on Israel, commencing at approximately 6:30 a.m. with a barrage of over 3,000 rockets fired from Gaza toward southern and central Israeli population centers, overwhelming air defenses and causing widespread disruption.[6] Concurrently, around 3,000 Hamas militants and members of allied groups, including Palestinian Islamic Jihad, breached the Gaza-Israel border fence at over 100 points using bulldozers, explosives, and motorized paragliders, enabling ground incursions into military outposts and adjacent civilian communities.[6] This operation, dubbed "Al-Aqsa Flood" by Hamas, marked the largest single-day terrorist attack in Israel's history.[6] The incursions targeted both military bases, such as Nahal Oz and Re'im, and civilian areas, including multiple kibbutzim (e.g., Be'eri, Kfar Aza, Nir Oz) and the Nova music festival near Kibbutz Re'im, where festivalgoers were hunted and massacred en masse.[7] In Be'eri alone, over 100 residents were killed, many in their homes, with militants engaging in close-quarters combat and executions.[8] The assault resulted in approximately 1,200 deaths, including over 800 civilians, with the remainder comprising security personnel; an additional 250 individuals, mostly civilians, were taken hostage and transported into Gaza.[8] Tactics employed included indiscriminate shootings, arson of structures, and deliberate killings of non-combatants, evidenced by forensic analysis of attack sites.[9] Documented patterns of violence included systematic sexual assaults, such as rape, gang rape, and genital mutilation, primarily against women but also affecting male victims, as corroborated by eyewitness testimonies, forensic evidence, and survivor accounts reviewed by international bodies.[9] [10] These acts, occurring amid broader mutilations and desecrations of bodies, align with Hamas' operational directives prioritizing terror against non-combatants.[11] Hamas' motivations stem from its foundational ideology, as outlined in the 1988 Covenant of the Islamic Resistance Movement, which explicitly rejects any Jewish sovereignty over historic Palestine and mandates jihad to obliterate Israel as a prerequisite for Islamic governance in the territory.[12] The document frames the conflict in religious terms, portraying Jews as eternal enemies and denying legitimacy to negotiated peace, a stance that persists despite a 2017 policy addendum softening tactical rhetoric without altering core rejectionism.[13] [14]

Israel's Declaration of War and Initial Operations

On October 8, 2023, Israel's security cabinet formally declared a state of war in response to the Hamas attack launched the previous day, invoking Article 40 of the Basic Law: The Government to authorize expanded military operations against Hamas targets.[15] [16] This declaration enabled the government under Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu to mobilize national resources for self-defense, framing the response as necessary to neutralize the immediate threat posed by Hamas's invasion and ongoing rocket barrages from Gaza.[17] Concurrently, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) initiated the largest reservist call-up in the nation's history, summoning approximately 360,000 reservists to bolster active forces and prepare for potential ground engagements.[18] [19] This mobilization, approved by the cabinet on October 8, aimed to restore deterrence and secure borders, with units rapidly deploying to southern Israel to counter infiltration attempts and repel further incursions.[20] From October 7 onward, the IDF conducted intensive airstrikes targeting Hamas's military infrastructure, including rocket launch sites, command centers, and weapon storage facilities across Gaza, with operations escalating on October 8 and 9 to degrade the group's capacity for sustained attacks.[21] These precision strikes, guided by real-time intelligence, eliminated hundreds of Hamas operatives in the initial days, as reported by IDF assessments, while prioritizing the destruction of tunnels and production sites used for manufacturing projectiles fired at Israeli population centers.[22] Ground operations commenced with limited raids in mid-October, transitioning to a broader incursion on October 27, 2023, focused on dismantling Hamas command structures in northern Gaza, particularly Gaza City.[23] [24] IDF armored and infantry units advanced to neutralize entrenched positions, supported by ongoing air cover, with objectives centered on high-value targets such as leadership bunkers verified through signals intelligence. During these early phases, the IDF rescued its first hostage, surveillance soldier Ori Megidish, on October 30 in a targeted operation within Gaza, confirming the feasibility of special forces extractions amid combat.[25] [26]

Escalation and Humanitarian Concerns Prior to Ceasefire

As Israeli forces initiated a ground offensive in Gaza on October 27, 2023, advancing into densely populated northern areas including Gaza City, they encountered and exposed elements of Hamas's extensive underground infrastructure. By mid-November, IDF units operating near Al-Shifa Hospital, Gaza's largest medical facility, uncovered a tunnel shaft and associated militant weaponry hidden beneath the compound, confirming Hamas's use of civilian sites for military purposes. Similar discoveries included weapons caches and tunnel entrances under schools and UNRWA facilities, where Hamas had positioned rocket launchers amid civilian populations. These findings aligned with prior intelligence on Hamas's tactic of embedding command centers and arms in protected sites to deter attacks and maximize civilian exposure during responses.[27][28][29] Hamas sustained offensive operations throughout the escalation, launching barrages of thousands of rockets toward Israeli population centers despite IDF evacuation warnings and precision strikes on launch sites. From October 7 to early November 2023, over 9,000 projectiles were fired from Gaza, with interceptions by Israel's Iron Dome system preventing most impacts but straining defenses and necessitating repeated civilian sheltering in Israel. In Gaza, the group's reliance on urban densities—firing from residential zones and refusing dispersal—amplified risks to non-combatants under first-principles of warfare, where combatants integrated into civilian fabrics elevate collateral potential compared to separated forces. Empirical comparisons to operations like the 2016-2017 Battle of Mosul, where U.S.-led coalitions faced ISIS in comparable urban settings, show Gaza's reported ratios as relatively restrained given the adversary's tactics.[30][31] Casualty figures, primarily sourced from Gaza's Hamas-controlled Health Ministry, reached approximately 11,000 deaths by late November 2023, though these aggregates do not distinguish combatants from civilians and have been critiqued for lacking verification amid institutional biases favoring underreported militant losses. IDF assessments indicated around 5,000 Hamas fighters eliminated by early December, yielding a civilian-to-combatant ratio of roughly 1:1 to 2:1—unusually low for high-density urban combat against an enemy employing human shields, per analyses from military experts evaluating precision targeting and warnings. Humanitarian pressures mounted from displacement of over 1 million Gazans and infrastructure strain, exacerbated by Hamas's refusal to permit civilian corridors or surrender terms that could halt operations, as articulated by leader Yahya Sinwar in defying unconditional capitulation.[32][33] The abduction of 251 hostages on October 7 intensified domestic and international urgency, with families reporting psychological torment and released captives later describing confinement in tunnels with minimal food, medical care, and exposure to abuse—conditions contributing to at least several deaths in captivity by November. Hamas's tactical holding of civilians as leverage, rather than releasing them amid safe passage offers, prolonged exposure to crossfire and starvation risks, underscoring causal links between militant strategies and civilian endangerment over broader famine narratives lacking empirical starvation metrics at that stage. This dynamic, coupled with ongoing rocket fire, forestalled earlier de-escalation despite mediator entreaties, prioritizing Hamas's military continuity.[34][33]

Negotiations

Mediators: Qatar, Egypt, and United States

Qatar, Egypt, and the United States served as primary external mediators in the indirect negotiations leading to the November 2023 ceasefire between Israel and Hamas. These countries facilitated shuttles between the parties, with talks alternating between Doha and Cairo, as direct communication was absent. The framework for the truce was finalized on November 22, 2023, following intensified efforts in early November.[35] Qatar's mediation was enabled by its hosting of Hamas's political leadership in Doha since 2012, allowing Qatari officials to relay proposals directly to Hamas figures like Ismail Haniyeh. However, Qatar's provision of over $1.8 billion in funding to Gaza since 2012—much of it supporting Hamas's governance through salary payments, fuel imports, and infrastructure—has drawn scrutiny for undermining its neutrality, as the funds effectively bolstered the group's control and military capabilities despite Israeli approvals for transfers aimed at maintaining calm. This financial ties, often portrayed in mainstream outlets as benign humanitarian aid, reflect a potential bias favoring Hamas, given Doha's annual pledges of around $360 million that sustained the enclave's de facto rulers.[36][37] Egypt leveraged its geographical proximity and control over the Rafah crossing—the sole non-Israeli conduit for aid into Gaza—to influence terms related to humanitarian access and border security. Cairo hosted multiple rounds of indirect talks and proposed verification mechanisms for aid and prisoner exchanges, driven by concerns over potential refugee inflows and Sinai militancy spillover. Egyptian intelligence chief Abbas Kamel played a central role in bridging gaps, though Egypt's mediation was complicated by its own restrictions on aid flows pre-ceasefire.[38] The United States, under the Biden administration, coordinated with allies to prioritize hostage releases, dispatching CIA Director William Burns to Doha on November 9, 2023, for high-level meetings with Qatari Prime Minister Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani and Israel's Mossad chief David Barnea to refine the deal's text amid sticking points like hostage lists. U.S. envoy Brett McGurk also shuttled between capitals, emphasizing phased releases to build momentum. This involvement marked a shift from Washington's initial reluctance for a full pause, focusing instead on tactical pauses for hostages.[39][35]

Israeli Positions and Red Lines

Israel maintained that any agreement must constitute a temporary truce limited to facilitating hostage releases, explicitly rejecting a permanent ceasefire that would halt operations against Hamas until its military and governance capabilities were dismantled. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu articulated this stance on November 22, 2023, framing the deal as a "pause in the fighting" to retrieve captives while preserving Israel's right to resume full-scale military action immediately thereafter, underscoring that the war's objectives—eliminating Hamas's threat—remained unmet.[40][41] Central red lines centered on the verification and phased release of 50 specified hostages—primarily women, children, and elderly civilians—deemed alive through independent medical checks prior to transfer, with daily increments starting November 24, 2023, to minimize risks of deception or non-compliance. Israel conditioned continuation of the truce on Hamas's adherence, including no rearmament or attacks, and reserved the authority to terminate the pause and recommence offensives if violations occurred, as evidenced by the resumption of strikes on December 1, 2023, following Hamas's alleged failure to release additional captives.[42][40] The Israeli security establishment, including IDF assessments, supported these parameters by highlighting incomplete mission accomplishment: Hamas retained operational battalions, extensive tunnel networks, and leadership structures capable of reconstitution, necessitating sustained pressure without territorial concessions or withdrawal from captured Gaza positions. Netanyahu reinforced this on November 21, 2023, vowing "absolute victory" over Hamas post-pause, prioritizing long-term deterrence over immediate de-escalation.[41][43]

Hamas Demands and Tactical Maneuvering

Hamas articulated core demands in the ceasefire negotiations that extended beyond hostage releases, insisting on a permanent end to Israel's military operations in Gaza, a complete withdrawal of Israeli forces from the territory, and the release of hundreds of Palestinian prisoners serving long sentences for involvement in terrorist attacks, including figures like Marwan Barghouti, who was convicted in Israeli courts for directing suicide bombings during the Second Intifada that killed multiple civilians.[44][45] These conditions reflected Hamas' broader objective of ensuring the group's political and military continuity rather than pursuing disarmament or long-term demilitarization, as evidenced by the organization's charter and repeated public statements rejecting Israel's existence.[46] The abduction of over 250 individuals on October 7, 2023, served as Hamas' primary leverage, with captives deliberately held in Gaza's densely populated urban areas and tunnel networks to complicate Israeli rescue efforts and maximize their utility as human shields in bargaining.[44][45] Hamas leadership, including Gaza commander Yahya Sinwar, calculated that prolonging the hostage crisis would erode Israeli public resolve and international support for the war, while exchanging select captives for prisoners would replenish its ranks with experienced militants.[46] This approach aligned with historical precedents, such as the 2011 Gilad Shalit exchange where Hamas secured over 1,000 prisoners, including Sinwar himself, for one soldier, demonstrating the tactic's effectiveness in sustaining the group's operational capacity.[47] By embedding military infrastructure amid civilian populations, Hamas exacerbated Gaza's humanitarian crisis during the conflict's early phases, using the resulting casualties and displacement—estimated at over 1.5 million people by late November 2023—to amplify global condemnation of Israel and bolster its negotiating stance without conceding core demands.[48] Internal communications intercepted by Israeli intelligence revealed Hamas prioritizing force preservation over civilian evacuation, viewing suffering as a strategic multiplier to compel concessions, consistent with doctrinal statements framing Palestinian losses as martyrdom advancing the cause.[49] Hamas' acceptance of the temporary truce framework on November 22, 2023, mediated by Qatar, Egypt, and the United States, yielded a phased release of 105 hostages in exchange for 240 Palestinian prisoners and increased aid inflows, but served primarily as a tactical respite.[40] The seven-day pause enabled Hamas to reposition fighters, salvage weapons from debris, and import materiel via relaxed border controls, as later confirmed by Israeli assessments of renewed rocket salvos post-truce on December 1, 2023.[4] Hamas officials subsequently described the interval as vital for "rebuilding strength," underscoring the decision's alignment with survival imperatives over genuine de-escalation.[49]

Domestic Pressures in Israel

Families of Israeli hostages held by Hamas mounted significant protests in November 2023 to pressure Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's government toward a ceasefire and prisoner exchange deal. On November 14, relatives initiated a five-day march from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem demanding negotiations for their loved ones' release.[50] Thousands gathered in Jerusalem on November 18, criticizing Netanyahu's handling of the hostage crisis and calling for concessions to secure freedoms.[51] These actions, alongside persistent advocacy, contributed to shifting government reluctance toward pausing military operations in Gaza.[52] Opposition figures, including National Unity leader Benny Gantz, amplified calls for prioritizing hostage returns, even at high costs, framing it as a moral imperative over continued warfare. Gantz welcomed the eventual deal, urging its use to recover all abductees while applying pressure to dismantle Hamas governance.[53] In contrast, right-wing cabinet members like National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir opposed aspects of the agreement, arguing that releasing Palestinian prisoners rewards the October 7 atrocities and bolsters Hamas capabilities. Ben-Gvir and allies contended such exchanges historically empower terrorist groups, citing risks of future attacks.[54] Public opinion polls in November 2023 reflected broad but cautious support for a prisoner swap, with nearly two-thirds of Israelis favoring an exchange despite security concerns.[55] Knesset discussions highlighted recidivism dangers, drawing on the 2011 Gilad Shalit deal where hundreds of released prisoners later resumed terrorism at elevated rates, as noted by IDF prosecutors who documented instances exceeding typical offender patterns.[56] Critics invoked these precedents to warn against empowering Hamas through the 2023 releases, balancing familial pleas against long-term national security imperatives.[57]

Agreement Terms

Hostage-Prisoner Exchange Protocol

The hostage-prisoner exchange protocol, finalized on November 21, 2023, and commencing on November 24, mandated the release by Hamas of 50 Israeli women and children hostages—captured during the October 7 attack—in exchange for Israel freeing 150 Palestinian women and children prisoners serving terms for security offenses, primarily low-level infractions like stone-throwing, membership in banned groups, and public disturbances, while excluding those convicted of murder or major terrorist acts.[40][58] The deal was extended beyond the initial four days, culminating in Hamas releasing 81 Israeli civilians (including additional women and children) and 24 foreign nationals (23 Thai and 1 Filipino), over seven days, for a total of 240 Palestinian prisoners released by Israel, consisting of 133 minors and 107 women.[58][2] This ratio—approximately three Palestinian prisoners per hostage—underscored a structural asymmetry, enabling Hamas to regain personnel potentially capable of resuming militant activities, in return for civilians whose captivity served primarily as leverage.[40][59] Exchanges occurred in daily phases, with Hamas handing over groups of 6 to 12 hostages to International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) representatives at undisclosed Gaza locations, followed by transport via ambulances to the Rafah crossing for medical verification of vitality and identity before transfer to Israel.[40][60] Concurrently, Israel released corresponding prisoner batches from facilities like Ofer Prison, processed through West Bank checkpoints with ICRC oversight to confirm numbers and identities, emphasizing non-violent offenders to minimize immediate security risks.[2][40] The ICRC's role extended to pre-exchange welfare checks on hostages where access was granted, though limitations persisted due to Hamas control, ensuring procedural integrity amid mutual distrust.[60]

Humanitarian Aid and Truce Conditions

The ceasefire agreement stipulated the entry of at least 200 trucks of humanitarian aid daily into Gaza, comprising food, water, medical supplies, and fuel, primarily via the Rafah crossing controlled by Egypt and the Kerem Shalom crossing under Israeli oversight.[40] This mechanism aimed to alleviate acute shortages amid the ongoing conflict, with provisions for safe passage and distribution coordinated by international mediators including Qatar and Egypt. A temporary suspension of Israeli military operations in northern Gaza was also mandated to enable aid delivery to that region without interference.[40] Truce conditions enforced a mutual halt to hostilities, barring Hamas and affiliated groups from firing rockets, mortars, or conducting incursions, while prohibiting Israeli airstrikes, artillery barrages, or troop advances beyond pre-truce positions. Israel committed to suspending aerial operations over northern Gaza daily from 10:00 a.m. to 4:00 p.m. local time, facilitating ground-level monitoring and reducing risks to civilians during aid movements.[61] Technical committees involving Qatari, Egyptian, and U.S. representatives were tasked with oversight to verify compliance, including restrictions on any movements that could enable rearmament or repositioning of forces. These provisions incorporated explicit prohibitions on Hamas exploiting aid for military ends or rearming, driven by documented patterns of diversion where humanitarian materials have been redirected toward tunnel networks, weapon caches, and fighter sustenance—issues longstanding despite denials from Gaza authorities and some UN assessments. Such safeguards countered attributions of crises solely to border restrictions, emphasizing causal factors like governance failures under Hamas control since 2007, where empirical inflows (often exceeding 500 trucks daily pre-October 2023) have not precluded engineered scarcities for propaganda or operational advantage.[62]

Phased Structure and Extension Clauses

The November 2023 ceasefire agreement outlined an initial four-day truce effective from November 24, 2023, focused on the phased release of up to 50 Israeli women and children hostages held by Hamas, in exchange for the release of 150 Palestinian women and children prisoners by Israel, coupled with a temporary halt in military operations and an increase in humanitarian aid deliveries to 200 trucks daily.[40][63] Extension provisions were structured as conditional and non-binding, requiring Hamas to submit lists of additional verifiable hostages—prioritizing civilians—for release, with Israel agreeing to extend the truce by one day for every 10 such captives freed, potentially allowing for further negotiations on military personnel and displaced Palestinians but without any guaranteed progression to subsequent phases.[64][65] Israel explicitly rejected any commitment to a permanent end to hostilities, framing the arrangement as a limited humanitarian pause within the ongoing war to dismantle Hamas's military capabilities, with clauses permitting resumption of operations if Hamas failed to comply with release obligations or committed breaches such as rocket fire, thereby preserving operational flexibility.[66] Hamas, in contrast, offered only vague assurances regarding the remaining hostages, including adult males and soldiers, conditioning fuller cooperation on broader concessions like a full Israeli withdrawal, which were not incorporated into the framework.[64]

Implementation Phase

Initial Hostage Releases and Verifications

The initial phase of hostage releases commenced on November 24, 2023, with Hamas transferring 13 Israeli hostages—primarily women and minors—along with 10 Thai nationals to the International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) for handover to Israeli authorities following a several-hour delay.[2][67] The ICRC verified the identities and living status of the captives during the transfer process out of Gaza. Medical assessments upon arrival in Israel revealed widespread evidence of mistreatment by Hamas captors, including malnourishment, dehydration, untreated injuries, and severe psychological trauma among the released individuals.[34] For instance, an 84-year-old hostage was hospitalized in critical condition due to compounded health issues exacerbated by captivity conditions.[34] Testimonies from freed hostages described limited food rations, isolation, and constant fear, underscoring the abusive environment in which they were held.[68] By the conclusion of the four-day truce on November 30, 2023, 105 hostages had been released in total, including 81 Israeli civilians such as elderly people and children, with the remainder being foreign nationals mainly from Thailand.[69] Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) personnel escorted the returnees via secure routes, including helicopter evacuations, to medical facilities for further evaluation and care.[70] These verifications not only confirmed the hostages' survival contrary to prior Hamas assertions of deaths from Israeli actions but also documented the physical and mental toll of their detention, highlighting discrepancies between captors' claims of adequate treatment and observable realities.[34][68]

Palestinian Prisoner Releases

Under the November 2023 ceasefire agreement between Israel and Hamas, Israel released a total of 240 Palestinian prisoners over the seven-day truce from November 24 to November 30. This included 150 individuals serving sentences for security-related offenses—primarily women and minors under 19 years old—and an additional 90 held under administrative detention without formal charges or trials.[71][72] The releases occurred in batches synchronized with hostage handovers by Hamas, with prisoners transferred from facilities including Ofer Prison near Ramallah, where crowds of Palestinian families awaited reunions amid celebrations. Among the convicted group, profiles revealed involvement in violent acts such as stabbings, shootings, and attempted murders, though the deal's criteria excluded senior militants convicted of multiple killings or leadership roles in groups like Hamas. High-security prisoners, including Yahya Sinwar, remained incarcerated, as the agreement focused on lower-tier categories to limit immediate operational threats.[72][71] Israeli intelligence assessments underscored the security costs, drawing on empirical data from prior swaps like the 2011 Gilad Shalit exchange, where roughly 30% of the 1,027 released Palestinians were rearrested for terrorism-related activities within a decade, including planning major attacks. Shin Bet reports indicated that at least 14 of the most dangerous from that deal resumed leadership positions, contributing to the intelligence failures preceding October 7, 2023; similar patterns were anticipated for the 2023 releases, with officials estimating a comparable recidivism risk for those with prior convictions.[73][59]

Aid Inflows and Ceasefire Monitoring

During the seven-day truce from November 24 to 30, 2023, Israel facilitated the entry of humanitarian aid convoys into Gaza as stipulated in the agreement, which called for up to 200 trucks per day carrying food, water, medicine, fuel, and other essentials. Reports confirmed at least 120 trucks entered on November 26, with similar daily volumes achieving several hundred trucks overall, marking a significant increase from pre-truce levels amid ongoing inspections at crossings like Kerem Shalom and Rafah to prevent dual-use materials from reaching militants.[74][75] Israeli authorities emphasized their role in enabling these inflows, including fuel for bakeries and hospitals, while IDF intelligence assessments indicated that Hamas systematically diverted 15-25% of aid supplies for its fighters or resale, based on intercepted documents and observations of confiscation policies. United Nations agencies acknowledged the entry of aid but highlighted distribution challenges within Gaza, including looting along routes, though U.S. government reviews later found no evidence of massive systematic theft by Hamas during the broader conflict, with misdirection affecting less than 1% of tracked USAID-funded items; IDF officials contested these findings, citing direct evidence from operations that UN monitors lacked access to verify.[76][77] Ceasefire compliance was monitored primarily through Israeli aerial surveillance, including drone overflights, to track militant movements and ensure no rearmament or attacks occurred, with no major incidents reported in the initial days. The truce permitted limited civilian evacuations southward for safety, though Israel restricted northward returns to prevent Hamas from exploiting population shifts for cover. Empirically, hostilities dropped to near zero, with no IDF airstrikes conducted and only sporadic, minimal rocket fire from Gaza until the truce's expiration on November 30.[78][79][80]

Violations and Breakdown

Hamas Rocket Fire and Failure to Release Additional Hostages

On November 30, 2023, negotiations to extend the truce beyond its initial seven days collapsed due to disagreements between Hamas and Israel over the terms for further hostage-prisoner exchanges, with Hamas refusing to proceed with the release of an additional group of hostages without concessions on prisoner releases and other conditions.[81][82] The following day, December 1, 2023, Hamas violated the ceasefire by launching a barrage of rockets toward southern Israel, including areas near Gaza such as Sderot and the surrounding envelope communities, shortly after the expiration of the truce extension.[83][84] These attacks numbered in the dozens and were intercepted primarily by Israel's Iron Dome defense system, resulting in no reported Israeli casualties or injuries from direct hits.[83][85] Concurrently, Hamas failed to fulfill its obligation under the truce framework to release the remaining women hostages, including an expected group of 10, and did not provide the required list of names or verification details for the next batch, effectively halting the exchange protocol.[84][83] This non-compliance prevented advancement to subsequent phases of releases, as the agreement stipulated sequential verifications and daily handovers of civilian hostages, primarily women and children, in return for Palestinian prisoners.[82] The rocket launches served as an explicit demonstration of Hamas's intent to resume hostilities, breaching the ceasefire's prohibition on offensive fire from Gaza, despite the group's claims that Israel had rejected extension proposals.[83][3] International observers, including mediators from Qatar and Egypt, noted the sequence of events placed responsibility on Hamas for initiating the breakdown through these actions, though United Nations statements focused more broadly on urging restraint without equivalent emphasis on the rocket violations compared to subsequent escalations.[85]

Israeli Military Resumption

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) resumed combat operations in Gaza on December 1, 2023, immediately following the expiration of the week-long truce at midnight, citing Hamas's violation through failure to release the remaining women and children hostages and subsequent rocket launches toward Israeli territory.[86] [87] This action was framed by Israeli officials as a legitimate exercise of self-defense against Hamas's aggression and non-compliance, restoring military pressure to neutralize threats after a pause that Hamas exploited to regroup.[83] Airstrikes commenced targeting Hamas command centers, weapon storage, and rocket launch sites across the Strip, with initial waves reported in northern and central areas shortly after the truce lapsed.[84] [88] Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu declared that "Hamas violated the terms of the truce" by not fulfilling its hostage release commitments and firing projectiles, necessitating the resumption to protect Israeli security and pursue remaining objectives against Hamas leadership.[84] During the truce, the IDF had halted major northern offensives, focusing instead on holding positions and enabling aid flows, but post-December 1 operations pivoted southward, with ground maneuvers intensifying around Khan Younis—identified as a Hamas operational hub housing senior figures like Yahya Sinwar.[89] [90] Evacuation warnings were issued to civilians in Khan Younis, designating it a combat zone to facilitate targeted engagements against militants embedded in urban areas.[89] The IDF maintained that strikes employed precision-guided munitions aimed at verified Hamas infrastructure to limit collateral damage, consistent with operational directives emphasizing intelligence-driven targeting over indiscriminate bombardment.[91] This approach was presented as proportionate to the threat posed by Hamas's truce breaches, enabling resumption without immediate re-engagement in previously cleared northern sectors.[83]

Causal Analysis of Collapse: Hamas' Strategic Calculations

Hamas leadership perceived the November 2023 truce as a tactical pause to reconstitute military capabilities rather than a step toward demilitarization, consistent with patterns observed in prior conflicts such as the 2014 Gaza War and 2021 escalation, where ceasefires enabled smuggling of weapons and reconstruction of infrastructure.[92] Captured Hamas documents seized by Israeli forces in subsequent operations revealed strategic planning for post-truce rearmament, including directives to exploit lulls for embedding forces deeper into civilian areas and preparing for renewed attrition warfare, underscoring a doctrinal commitment to perpetual confrontation over negotiated disarmament.[93] The collapse on December 1, 2023, stemmed directly from Hamas' insistence on advancing to phase two—demanding complete Israeli withdrawal from Gaza and an end to offensive operations—without reciprocal releases of the remaining hostages or commitments to neutralize its arsenal, effectively leveraging the pause to retain bargaining power. Hamas retained approximately 130 hostages post-truce, including military personnel and adult males, using them as human shields and negotiation assets to extract further Palestinian prisoner releases without yielding operational control.[58] During the seven-day halt in major combat, Hamas exploited the cessation to conduct repairs on its extensive tunnel network, which Israeli assessments later indicated remained largely operational (with over 60% intact in key areas like Rafah), allowing fighters to reposition munitions and evade detection while aid inflows provided logistical cover.[94] This approach mirrored historical cycles, where truces facilitated Iranian-supplied rocket rearming and tunnel fortification, prioritizing survival and future attacks over truce extensions that risked exposing vulnerabilities.[95] Such calculations reflected a core refusal to demilitarize, viewing concessions as existential threats amid ideological imperatives for Israel's elimination, as evidenced in pre-war planning documents.[96]

Aftermath and Strategic Consequences

Resumed Israeli Operations in Gaza

Following the ceasefire breakdown on December 1, 2023, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) resumed large-scale ground and aerial operations across Gaza, focusing on neutralizing Hamas command structures and infrastructure in northern and central areas. These actions included targeted raids in Jabalia, where the IDF reported fully dismantling three Hamas battalions by December 19, 2023, through combined arms maneuvers that destroyed tunnels, weapon caches, and fighting positions. Operations extended to Khan Yunis and Deir al-Balah, emphasizing precision strikes on high-value targets to degrade Hamas's operational capacity without broad territorial occupation at the outset.[97] By March 2024, the IDF stated it had dismantled 20 of Hamas's original 24 battalions, rendering them combat-ineffective through the elimination of leadership and destruction of organizational frameworks, though remnants retained limited guerrilla capabilities. This progress involved the confirmed killing of dozens of battalion commanders and senior operatives in the initial months post-resumption, contributing to a broader attrition of Hamas's military hierarchy. The IDF reported over 10,000 Hamas fighters killed overall by late February 2024, based on intelligence assessments of battlefield casualties, interrogated detainees, and forensic evidence from strikes.[98][99] Further advancements included the elimination of key figures such as Mohammed Deif, Hamas's military chief, confirmed killed in a July 2024 airstrike on a Khan Yunis compound, as verified by IDF intelligence and subsequent admissions from Hamas affiliates. By mid-2024, these efforts had secured IDF operational control over approximately 60% of Gaza's territory, including buffer zones and key urban corridors, facilitating sustained pressure on remaining Hamas holdouts in southern enclaves.[100]

Impact on Remaining Hostages

Following the November 2023 ceasefire, which resulted in the release of 105 living hostages in exchange for Palestinian prisoners, approximately 129 individuals remained in Hamas captivity, including soldiers and civilians abducted during the October 7 attacks.[101] Of these, at least 35 were confirmed dead by Israeli authorities as of early 2025, with forensic evidence from recovered bodies indicating execution-style killings, such as bullets to the head, and signs of prolonged starvation or neglect rather than combat-related deaths.[102] Hamas' deliberate withholding of these remains and refusal to provide information on their locations exacerbated families' anguish, as the group used hostages as bargaining chips to demand Israeli military withdrawals and prisoner exchanges beyond the agreed terms. The unreleased hostages endured severe conditions, corroborated by accounts from those freed in 2023 and later rescues, including systematic physical and psychological abuse such as beatings, sexual violence, food deprivation, and confinement in underground tunnels with minimal sanitation.[103] Hamas-released videos, such as one in October 2024 showing captive Evyatar David in a weakened state, depicted emaciated individuals pleading for rescue, highlighting starvation and isolation tactics employed to pressure Israel.[104] Autopsies on bodies returned sporadically, including those of the Bibas family children in February 2025, confirmed captors' direct responsibility for deaths through suffocation or execution, contradicting Hamas claims of natural causes or Israeli strikes.[105] Israeli military operations yielded limited rescues amid the stalemate, including four hostages—Almog Meir Jan, Andrey Kozlov, Shlomi Ziv, and Noa Argamani—freed in a June 8, 2024, raid in Nuseirat, and Qaid Farhan Alkadi extracted from a tunnel on August 27, 2024. These successes underscored Hamas' culpability in endangering lives by embedding captives in densely populated civilian areas, increasing risks during extractions. Post-ceasefire negotiations repeatedly stalled due to Hamas' maximalist demands for a permanent end to hostilities and full Gaza withdrawal, prioritizing organizational survival over humanitarian release, which prolonged captivity and contributed to additional deaths in tunnels.[106] This leverage strategy by Hamas, rather than any Israeli intransigence, directly sustained the hostages' suffering, as evidenced by the group's history of executing captives to manipulate ceasefires.[5]

Long-Term Effects on Hamas' Military Capacity

The seven-day ceasefire in November 2023 offered Hamas a brief operational respite, enabling minor resupply through humanitarian corridors and limited regrouping, but it failed to reverse the broader trajectory of military degradation inflicted by Israeli operations before and after the pause.[107] Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) estimates indicate that Hamas' pre-war rocket arsenal of approximately 15,000 projectiles had been reduced to a few hundred by January 2024, with production facilities largely dismantled and only dozens of long-range rockets remaining capable of threatening central Israel.[108] [109] [110] This depletion exceeded 90% in key categories, as subsequent IDF strikes targeted underground manufacturing sites and stockpiles, preventing meaningful rearmament during or after the truce.[111] Hamas' organized fighting structure suffered irreversible losses, with IDF assessments reporting the elimination of over 17,000 fighters out of an initial force of 25,000–30,000, including the dismantling of 22 battalions that no longer operate as coherent military units.[112] [113] High-level leadership was decimated, with key figures such as Yahya Sinwar and Mohammed Sinwar among those killed in targeted operations extending into 2025, disrupting command chains and operational planning.[114] [115] While the ceasefire pause allowed some tactical repositioning, empirical data from IDF intelligence shows no restoration of pre-war battalion-level capabilities, forcing a shift to decentralized guerrilla tactics amid exhausted and untrained replacements.[116] [117] Economically, the war's continuation post-ceasefire exacerbated Hamas' resource constraints, with Gaza's GDP contracting by 86% in the first half of 2024 alone, rendering the enclave's economy less than one-sixth of its pre-war size and unemployment surging to 80%.[118] [119] [120] Hamas' funding networks faced severe strain, leading to unpaid fighters and operational shortfalls despite ongoing Qatari transfers estimated in the hundreds of millions, as Israeli interdictions limited diversion to military rebuilding.[121] [122] [123] These factors collectively undermined Hamas' sustainment of conventional forces, confirming the ceasefire's net negative impact on its long-term military viability despite narratives of resurgence.[124]

Controversies and Debates

Criticisms from Israeli Security Perspective

Israeli security officials and analysts contended that the November 2023 ceasefire interrupted the Israel Defense Forces' (IDF) operational momentum against Hamas at a pivotal moment, when the group was sustaining heavy losses and facing potential collapse in northern Gaza. Critics, including hawkish figures within the security establishment, argued that the pause enabled Hamas to reorganize forces, restore command structures, and exploit the influx of humanitarian aid for rearmament, thereby prolonging the threat rather than achieving decisive elimination of capabilities. This view was echoed in assessments that the truce effectively granted Hamas a strategic respite, as evidenced by the group's resumption of rocket fire toward Israel on December 1, 2023, immediately following the deal's expiration, which prompted the IDF to restart ground operations.[87] A core concern centered on the release of approximately 240 Palestinian prisoners, many convicted of involvement in prior attacks, which was seen as heightening future risks based on historical patterns of recidivism. In the 2011 Gilad Shalit exchange, where Israel freed 1,027 prisoners for one captured soldier, Shin Bet chief Ronen Bar reported that most of those released subsequently returned to terrorist activities, including planning attacks that endangered Israeli civilians and soldiers. Security experts warned that similar outcomes from the 2023 deal could bolster Hamas' operational ranks, with data indicating that released militants often reintegrate into militant networks, contributing to renewed violence such as the October 7, 2023, assault's enablers.[125] Hawkish perspectives, including from IDF brass like Defense Minister Yoav Gallant, emphasized that sustaining pressure without interruption was essential to prevent Hamas from transitioning to guerrilla tactics in cleared areas, a prediction borne out by later reports of the group's regrouping in vacated zones. Proponents of this critique maintained that prioritizing hostage releases over total threat neutralization delayed Israel's path to victory, allowing Hamas to preserve enough infrastructure—such as tunnels and leadership—to sustain irregular warfare, rather than facing eradication under continued assault.[126]

Allegations of Unequal Burden and Hamas Empowerment

Critics from Israeli security establishments and aligned analysts contended that the November 2023 truce placed an asymmetric burden on Israel, requiring the IDF to suspend offensive operations and permit substantial humanitarian aid inflows without reciprocal full compliance from Hamas on hostage releases or cessation of military preparations.[40] [127] The agreement, mediated by Qatar and effective from November 24 to December 1, 2023, mandated a pause in Israeli airstrikes and ground advances in specified corridors, alongside daily aid convoys totaling over 200 trucks, but imposed no obligations on Hamas to dismantle military assets or allow unrestricted inspections, nor did it compel Israeli territorial withdrawals beyond temporary tactical halts.[64] [128] Hamas was accused of exploiting the respite to divert portions of the aid—IDF intelligence later documented systematic confiscation of supplies for fighters, with estimates indicating up to 25% redirection to military uses such as tunnel reinforcement and weaponry stockpiling—while Israel maintained verified restraint, refraining from unprovoked strikes and enabling Red Cross verification of releases.[76] [129] This divergence highlighted causal disparities: Israel's compliance stemmed from operational pauses tied to verifiable hostage handovers, whereas Hamas's partial adherence—releasing 105 captives short of full demands—facilitated selective empowerment without equivalent disarmament.[77] The truce afforded Hamas a propaganda advantage, with its leadership portraying the pause as a strategic thwarting of Israel's campaign to eradicate its governance in Gaza, thereby enhancing recruitment and sustaining financial inflows via Qatari transfers exceeding $30 million during the period, unencumbered by immediate Israeli interdiction.[130] Such gains were critiqued as illusory long-term, given the IDF's subsequent degradation of over 17,000 Hamas fighters by mid-2024, yet the initial asymmetry underscored how truces, absent enforced demilitarization, risk bolstering the initiator of aggression—Hamas's October 7 assault—over the defender's restraint.[131] Observers attuned to source biases in academia and media noted tendencies to equate violations despite empirical imbalances, with coverage often amplifying Gaza's aid dependencies while marginalizing Hamas's documented aid manipulations and the truce's origins in Israel's hostage-recovery imperatives, rather than mutual de-escalation.[129] This framing, prevalent in outlets influenced by institutional left-leaning priors, obscured the non-equivalent intents: Israel's tactical pause versus Hamas's opportunistic consolidation.[130]

Debunking Equivalence Narratives in Media Coverage

Media coverage of the 2023 Gaza war ceasefire collapse frequently employed a "cycle of violence" narrative, portraying Hamas' violations—such as rocket barrages and refusal to release additional hostages—as symmetrical responses to Israeli actions, rather than escalations by the initiator of the broader conflict.[132] This framing obscures the causal asymmetry: Hamas' October 7, 2023, attack, which killed over 1,200 civilians and took 250 hostages, stemmed from the group's foundational ideology of eradicating Israel, as articulated in its 1988 charter calling for jihad against Jews and reaffirmed in leaders' post-attack statements, not merely as retaliation to the Gaza blockade.[14] Empirical analysis rejects the cycle model, as data from prior conflicts show Palestinian terror decreases under firm Israeli deterrence, not concessions that embolden aggressors.[133] Hamas' tactics inherently targeted Israeli civilians, with thousands of unguided rockets fired indiscriminately at population centers since the ceasefire's end, lacking any precision or warnings, in violation of international humanitarian law.[134] In contrast, Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) operations post-ceasefire focused on dismantling Hamas infrastructure, employing measures like roof-knocking and evacuation warnings to minimize non-combatant harm, yielding combatant-to-civilian death ratios in Gaza estimated at around 1:1 to 1:2—exceptionally low for urban counterinsurgency against an embedded foe, per modeling of verified data.[135] Forensic reviews have found no credible evidence of systematic IDF civilian massacres, unlike Hamas' documented October 7 atrocities involving rape, mutilation, and execution of non-combatants.[136] Such disparities in intent and methods undermine equivalence claims, yet patterns in coverage—prevalent in institutions with documented left-leaning biases—often minimize Hamas' deliberate civilian endangerment through human shielding and propaganda, while scrutinizing Israeli self-defense as disproportionate.[132] This selective emphasis aligns with broader media tendencies to attribute Palestinian actions to reactive grievances, ignoring Hamas' strategic use of civilian casualties to sway global opinion, as evidenced by coordinated misinformation tactics.[134] Distinguishing aggressor from defender requires prioritizing verifiable intent and outcomes over balanced-but-misleading portrayals that erode accountability for the party that shattered the ceasefire through renewed aggression.[137]

Reactions

Israeli Government and Leadership

The Israeli security cabinet approved the hostage exchange agreement on November 22, 2023, enabling the release of 81 Israeli hostages—primarily women and children—over the course of the seven-day ceasefire from November 24 to December 1, 2023, in exchange for 240 Palestinian prisoners. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's government hailed the releases as a critical humanitarian achievement, with the Prime Minister's Office issuing statements embracing the returning captives and crediting the deal with saving lives amid ongoing military pressures.[138][139][140] Netanyahu's administration defended the truce as a tactical necessity to secure immediate hostage returns without compromising broader war objectives, repeatedly stressing its temporary status and reserving the right to resume full-scale operations upon any breakdown. When Hamas declined to extend the ceasefire by releasing additional hostages on December 1, the Israel Defense Forces promptly intensified ground operations in northern Gaza, with Netanyahu affirming in subsequent addresses that Israel would press forward until achieving "total victory" over Hamas, including the elimination of its leadership and military infrastructure. This stance aligned with pre-ceasefire cabinet resolutions prioritizing hostage recovery alongside the dismantlement of Hamas's capabilities.[141][142] Within the coalition, far-right partners such as National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir and Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich voiced sharp criticisms of the prisoner releases, labeling them as excessive concessions that empowered terrorists and inflicted "great harm" on Israeli security. Despite threats of resignation, these dissenters ultimately refrained from dissolving the government, fostering unity on the post-ceasefire resumption of hostilities to advance the campaign against Hamas. The administration underscored progress metrics, including the confirmed neutralization of thousands of militants and destruction of extensive tunnel networks in the initial resumption phase, as evidence of advancing toward Hamas's defeat.[143][144]

Hostage Families' Divisions

The families of Israeli hostages abducted by Hamas on October 7, 2023, exhibited deep divisions over the November 2023 ceasefire agreement, which facilitated the release of 105 captives—primarily women and children—in exchange for 240 Palestinian prisoners. Proponents of the deal, such as Noam Peri, whose father Chaim Peri remained in captivity after the truce (and was later confirmed dead), emphasized urgency in negotiations to prevent further loss of life, with Peri publicly urging compromises even amid stalled talks post-ceasefire.[145] In contrast, a minority of families opposed partial exchanges, arguing they risked abandoning remaining hostages—estimated at around 130 after the deal—and emboldened Hamas by freeing security prisoners without addressing the group's underlying military infrastructure or ideological commitment to further attacks.[146] These splits intensified within advocacy groups like the Hostages and Missing Families Forum, where debates centered on whether truces incentivized Hamas violations, as evidenced by the group's failure to release all eligible women and children under the agreement's terms. Families of unreleased hostages, including representatives for the Bibas family—whose wife Shiri and sons Ariel (4) and Kfir (9 months) were excluded from the deal and later confirmed killed by captors—voiced concerns that incomplete deals eroded leverage and prolonged suffering, with the family citing Hamas' breach of exchange protocols as justification for prioritizing sustained pressure over renewed concessions.[147] Post-release accounts from freed hostages underscored these tensions, detailing Hamas' psychological tactics on children, including isolation, limited nutrition, and exposure to captor narratives framing Israel as aggressor, which some families interpreted as indoctrination efforts aimed at long-term radicalization. Such testimonies, shared in debriefings, bolstered arguments against further deals without Hamas' total capitulation, as they highlighted the risk of returning captives psychologically altered and vulnerable to ongoing threats from a non-eradicated foe.[148][149]

Hamas and Palestinian Militant Views

Hamas publicly framed the November 2023 ceasefire as evidence of the efficacy of its armed strategy, highlighting the exchange of 105 Israeli hostages for 240 Palestinian prisoners and the delivery of increased humanitarian aid to Gaza as concessions extracted from Israel without requiring disarmament or surrender.[40] Senior Hamas figures, such as political leader Ismail Haniyeh, portrayed the truce as a step affirming the "resistance's" ability to withstand Israeli pressure, rejecting narratives of military defeat and insisting that the October 7, 2023, assault represented legitimate defense against occupation rather than aggression. Captured internal communications from Hamas operatives in Gaza, obtained by Israeli forces during operations, contrasted sharply with this portrayal, revealing admissions of heavy casualties—estimated at around 12,000 fighters killed—and extensive infrastructure damage that necessitated a tactical halt to regroup and mitigate further erosion of capabilities.[150] These documents depicted the ceasefire not as a strategic triumph but as a pragmatic pause urged by field commanders on exiled leaders, underscoring vulnerabilities masked by propaganda to sustain internal cohesion and external support. The Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), Hamas's primary militant ally in Gaza, aligned closely with this public stance, issuing statements that lauded the truce as validation of unified "resistance" efforts initiated on October 7 and dismissed Israeli claims of dominance as illusory.[151] PIJ leadership emphasized continuity in framing the conflict as existential jihad, with the prisoner swap symbolizing resilience amid destruction, though shared operational losses similarly prompted participation in the temporary de-escalation.

International Responses with Focus on Security Realities

The United States affirmed Israel's right to resume military operations following the collapse of the November 2023 ceasefire on December 1, when Hamas violated terms by withholding hostages and launching rockets, emphasizing the necessity of degrading Hamas's terrorist capabilities to prevent future attacks.[152] U.S. officials, including the Biden administration, provided ongoing military support and vetoed UN resolutions perceived as undermining Israel's self-defense against Hamas, whose 1988 charter explicitly calls for the destruction of Israel and endorses jihadist violence.[153] This stance prioritized security realities over premature halts, recognizing that incomplete hostage releases and Hamas rearmament during truces historically enabled renewed terrorism, as seen in the October 7, 2023, assault that killed 1,200 Israelis.[154] In the European Union, responses revealed divisions, with countries like Hungary vetoing efforts to impose sanctions on Israel and consistently backing its security requirements against Hamas, including the need for operational freedom to neutralize tunnels and rocket infrastructure used for cross-border attacks.[155] Hungary's government, under Viktor Orbán, condemned Hamas terrorism outright and opposed EU statements equating Israeli defensive actions with Hamas aggression, arguing that such false equivalences ignored the group's use of civilian areas for military purposes, which complicates precision targeting and inflates casualty narratives.[156] Other Eastern European states, including Czechia and Austria, aligned similarly, refusing UN votes that failed to address Hamas's charter-mandated eliminationism, thereby highlighting how some Western European critiques overlooked the causal role of Hamas's initiatory violence in necessitating Israel's response.[157] Qatar and Egypt, as mediators, touted the November 24, 2023, truce—yielding partial hostage releases for Palestinian prisoners—as a success, yet the deal's rapid failure due to Hamas non-compliance underscored their limited leverage over the group's intransigence, with Qatar hosting Hamas leaders who prioritized political survival over sustained peace.[158] Egyptian statements emphasized border security concerns but downplayed Hamas's diversion of aid for military rebuilding, a pattern that security experts link to the group's charter-driven rejection of coexistence.[159] United Nations bodies issued criticisms of Israeli operations post-ceasefire, focusing on civilian impacts while often omitting Hamas's deliberate embedding of command centers in populated zones and its charter's genocidal rhetoric against Jews, which analysts argue distorts assessments of proportionality by ignoring the terrorist group's strategy of maximizing its own casualties for propaganda.[160] This selective framing, evident in Security Council debates, was countered by pro-Israel voices noting the UN's historical underemphasis on Hamas's October 7 atrocities, including rape and mutilation documented in forensic reports.[161] Right-leaning governments, such as Argentina under President Javier Milei—who assumed office on December 10, 2023—reaffirmed support for Israel's campaign by designating Hamas a terrorist organization in 2024 and rejecting UN narratives that equate defensive warfare with aggression, explicitly debunking proportionality myths by highlighting Hamas's initiation of the conflict and use of human shields as the root causes of Gaza's hardships.[162] Milei's administration criticized international bodies for hypocrisy in ignoring Hamas's ideological commitment to Israel's annihilation, aligning with security-first analyses that view incomplete ceasefires as enabling cycles of terrorism rather than resolution.[163]
