Haitham al-Ghais


Haitham Al Ghais is a Kuwaiti oil executive and diplomat serving as Secretary General of the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) since 1 August 2022.[1] With over 30 years of experience in global oil and gas markets, he advanced through senior roles at the Kuwait Petroleum Corporation (KPC), including as Deputy Managing Director for International Marketing in 2021, Head of the Regional Office in Beijing, and Manager of the European Regional Office in London.[1] Earlier in his career, Al Ghais served in the Kuwaiti military during Operation Desert Storm and as a diplomat at the Ministry of Foreign Affairs from 1991 to 1993.[1] Al Ghais represented Kuwait on the OPEC Board of Governors from 2017 to 2021, chaired the Joint Ministerial Monitoring Committee in 2017 to support the Declaration of Cooperation with non-OPEC producers, and led Kuwait's delegation in negotiating the OPEC-non-OPEC Charter of Cooperation between 2018 and 2019.[1] Appointed by acclamation for an initial three-year term in January 2022, he was reappointed on 10 December 2024 for a second term effective from 1 August 2025.[2][3] In his leadership role, Al Ghais has overseen OPEC's market monitoring and production adjustment strategies amid fluctuating global demand, while advocating for energy policies grounded in empirical market realities over ideological prescriptions for rapid fossil fuel phase-outs.[1][4] He has critiqued forecasts like those from the International Energy Agency for underestimating oil's enduring necessity, emphasizing technological advancements and sustained demand growth in developing economies.[5]

Early life and education

Birth and family background

Haitham al-Ghais was born in October 1969 in Kuwait City, Kuwait.[6] Al-Ghais originates from a family with diplomatic connections, which influenced his early career path in Kuwait's foreign affairs.[7]

Academic training

Haitham al-Ghais received a bachelor's degree in political science from the University of San Francisco in 1990.[6][8] No further academic qualifications or advanced degrees are documented in available records.[1]

Career in the oil sector

Early positions at Kuwait Petroleum Corporation

Al-Ghais joined Kuwait Petroleum Corporation (KPC) in 1993, beginning his career there in the International Marketing Sector, where he focused on crude oil marketing and petroleum products sales.[1] These initial roles involved direct engagement with global sales operations, building on his prior diplomatic experience in Kuwait's Ministry of Foreign Affairs.[1] In subsequent early positions within KPC's global marketing framework, he served as Market Research Manager in Kuwait, analyzing market trends and supporting strategic planning for hydrocarbon exports.[1] He also headed the KPC regional office in Beijing, overseeing East Asian market development and sales coordination, and managed the European regional office in London, handling downstream marketing and partnerships in key consuming markets.[1][9] These assignments in various sales departments provided foundational expertise in international oil trade dynamics, emphasizing empirical market data over speculative forecasts.[9] By the early 2000s, his progression reflected KPC's emphasis on experienced personnel for expanding global footprints amid fluctuating oil prices.[2]

Advisory roles to Kuwaiti oil ministers

Haitham al-Ghais provided advisory counsel to six successive Kuwaiti oil ministers over the course of his more than 30-year career in the oil sector.[10][11][12] This role emerged from his progressive ascent within the Kuwait Petroleum Corporation (KPC), where he joined in 1993 and advanced through positions involving international marketing of crude oil and petroleum products, market research, and management of KPC's regional offices in Beijing and London.[13] As a senior executive, al-Ghais offered expertise on global oil market outlooks, strategic planning, and long-term hydrocarbon strategies, informing ministerial decisions on production, exports, and OPEC-related policies.[13] By 2021, he had risen to Deputy Managing Director for International Marketing at KPC, a position that encompassed oversight of worldwide market analysis and policy recommendations directly relevant to Kuwait's energy diplomacy.[13] These advisory contributions underscored his influence on Kuwait's positioning within international oil governance prior to his OPEC roles.[11]

Involvement in OPEC governance prior to leadership

Prior to his appointment as OPEC Secretary General on August 1, 2022, Haitham al-Ghais served as Kuwait's Governor for OPEC from 2017 to 2021, representing the country in key decision-making processes.[2][9] In this capacity, he participated in meetings of the OPEC Board of Governors, contributing to policy deliberations on production quotas and market stability.[1] He also acted as a leading member of Kuwait's delegation to OPEC conferences and those related to the Declaration of Cooperation (DoC) with non-OPEC producers, facilitating coordination on global supply adjustments.[2] Al-Ghais chaired the inaugural Joint Technical Committee (JTC) of OPEC and non-OPEC countries in 2017, a body established to provide technical analysis supporting ministerial decisions under the DoC.[14] He continued to engage with the JTC and the Joint Ministerial Monitoring Committee (JMMC), which oversaw compliance with production cuts agreed in 2016 and subsequent extensions, helping to navigate compliance challenges amid volatile oil prices.[1] Additionally, as a member of OPEC's Board of Governors from 2017 to 2021, he joined the Internal Audit Committee in 2019, serving as its chairman in 2020 to enhance organizational oversight and financial transparency.[1] Between 2018 and 2019, al-Ghais led Kuwait's team in negotiating and finalizing the Charter of Cooperation, a formal agreement solidifying the OPEC-non-OPEC alliance aimed at balancing oil markets through voluntary adjustments.[13] This role underscored his diplomatic efforts in bridging differences among producers, particularly during periods of U.S. shale output surges and demand fluctuations.[2] His tenure as governor ended in June 2021, after which he transitioned to a senior position at Kuwait Petroleum Corporation while maintaining influence in OPEC+ forums.[15]

Tenure as OPEC Secretary General

Appointment in 2022 and reappointment in 2024

Haitham al-Ghais was selected as OPEC Secretary General by acclamation during a special meeting of the OPEC Conference on 3 January 2022, succeeding Nigeria's Mohammed Barkindo for a three-year term beginning 1 August 2022.[16][1] This marked the first time a Kuwaiti national held the position, reflecting OPEC's rotational selection process among member states based on seniority and consensus among oil ministers.[17] Al-Ghais assumed office on 1 August 2022, following Barkindo's sudden death on 5 July 2022, with an interim arrangement in place until the scheduled transition.[1][18] On 10 December 2024, the 189th OPEC Conference renewed al-Ghais's term for an additional three years, effective from 1 August 2025 through 31 July 2028, again by consensus without opposition from the 12 member countries.[19][20] The reappointment occurred during a virtual ministerial meeting focused on administrative matters, underscoring continuity in leadership amid ongoing global oil market volatility.[21][3] This extension aligns with OPEC's practice of appointing secretaries general from different founding members periodically, prioritizing experienced oil sector executives capable of navigating producer-consumer dialogues.[19]

Key production and market stabilization decisions

Upon assuming the role of OPEC Secretary General on August 3, 2022, Haitham al-Ghais oversaw OPEC+'s initial major production adjustment amid market volatility following Russia's invasion of Ukraine and recession fears. On October 5, 2022, OPEC+ agreed to reduce output targets by 2 million barrels per day (bpd) effective November 2022, a decision al-Ghais described as pre-emptive to avert a supply surplus projected for the fourth quarter and to preserve spare capacity for potential crises.[22][23] This cut, the largest since the COVID-19 pandemic, aimed to stabilize prices hovering around $90 per barrel for Brent crude, countering downward pressure from anticipated economic slowdowns. In response to persistent weak demand signals and non-OPEC supply growth, al-Ghais supported further voluntary reductions in 2023. On April 3, 2023, Saudi Arabia announced a unilateral cut of 500,000 bpd from May, joined by others, while in November 30, 2023, eight OPEC+ members (Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, United Arab Emirates, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman) committed to additional voluntary cuts totaling 2.2 million bpd starting January 1, 2024.[24] These measures, extended repeatedly under al-Ghais' tenureâ€”to March 2025, then deferred to April 2025 with cuts prolonged through December 2026â€”built on core groupwide reductions of 3.66 million bpd established in 2022-2023, prioritizing market balance over short-term volume gains.[25][26] By mid-2025, as global demand recovery strengthened and inventories eased, al-Ghais endorsed a gradual unwinding of voluntary cuts to reclaim market share while maintaining stability. OPEC+ accelerated hikes, adding 138,000 bpd in March 2025â€”the first increase since 2022â€”followed by 548,000 bpd in August and 547,000 bpd in September, with further adjustments planned for October amid Brent prices stabilizing near $70 per barrel.[27][28][29] Al-Ghais framed these as data-driven responses to evolving conditions, emphasizing OPEC+'s proactive role in preventing volatility without undermining long-term investments.[30]

Diplomatic engagements with global stakeholders

Haitham al-Ghais has prioritized bilateral diplomacy with key oil producers and consumers to coordinate production policies and address market volatility. In non-OPEC nations, he has emphasized OPEC+'s role in stabilizing global supply, often highlighting empirical demand trends over accelerated transitions to alternatives.[32] On June 20, 2025, al-Ghais met Russian President Vladimir Putin on the sidelines of the St. Petersburg International Economic Forum, discussing enhanced cooperation within OPEC+ amid geopolitical tensions affecting energy flows.[33] The encounter underscored Russia's description as a "solid, stable partner" in OPEC initiatives, with al-Ghais noting the strength of their joint efforts in output adjustments.[34] In Europe, al-Ghais engaged Austrian officials, including a visit to the Federal Ministry for Economy, Energy and Tourism on June 30, 2025, to explore energy dialogue and market predictability.[35] Such meetings reflect efforts to bridge producer-consumer divides, particularly with EU stakeholders concerned about supply security. With Central Asian producers, al-Ghais held talks with Kazakh President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev on October 3, 2025, in Astana, where Tokayev reaffirmed support for OPEC+'s gradual output increases and praised Kazakhstan's integration as a key partner.[36] The discussions covered global oil outlooks and compliance with quotas, reinforcing non-OPEC adherence.[37] Al-Ghais also met Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian on February 5, 2025, in Tehran, urging unified OPEC policies to counter potential U.S. sanctions on Iranian exports that could destabilize markets.[38] Pezeshkian stressed political neutrality and cohesion among members, while al-Ghais affirmed OPEC's commitment to collective interests despite external pressures.[39] These engagements illustrate al-Ghais' focus on solidarity among producers facing sanctions or demand fluctuations.

Positions on energy policy

Emphasis on empirical demand forecasts

Haitham al-Ghais has repeatedly stressed that oil demand forecasts must prioritize empirical evidence from current consumption patterns, sectoral trends, and socioeconomic drivers over speculative assumptions about rapid decarbonization. Under his leadership, OPEC's World Oil Outlook 2025 projects global oil demand reaching 123 million barrels per day by 2050, an increase of approximately 25 million barrels per day from 2024 levels, attributed to verifiable factors such as world population growth to 9.7 billion, expanding urbanization rates, and per capita income rises in non-OECD countries like India and China, which are expected to account for over 60% of incremental demand.[40] [41] This approach contrasts with projections from agencies like the International Energy Agency (IEA), which al-Ghais argues embed policy prescriptionsâ€”such as accelerated electric vehicle adoption under net-zero scenariosâ€”without sufficient grounding in observed market behaviors, leading to repeated revisions downward in short-term demand estimates.[42] [43] In public statements, al-Ghais has highlighted real-time data as validation for sustained growth, noting that global oil demand hit record highs in 2024 at over 103 million barrels per day, defying earlier peak predictions and underscoring the limitations of models reliant on unproven technological substitutions.[44] [45] He advocates a "data-driven" methodology that incorporates bottom-up sectoral analysesâ€”covering transportation, petrochemicals, and aviationâ€”where hydrocarbons demonstrate resilience due to their energy density and infrastructure lock-in, rather than top-down ideological targets.[46] For instance, OPEC's forecasts anticipate petrochemical demand alone adding 7 million barrels per day by 2050, based on historical correlations between GDP growth and plastics consumption, not anticipated breakthroughs in bio-alternatives.[40] Al-Ghais has critiqued the IEA's forecasting inconsistencies, such as multiple upward adjustments to near-term demand since 2020 totaling over 5 million barrels per day cumulatively, as evidence that empirical realitiesâ€” including slower-than-expected EV market penetration and persistent aviation fuel needsâ€”outpace scenario-based modeling.[47] [48] This emphasis informs OPEC's policy recommendations, urging investments of $18.2 trillion in upstream oil projects through 2050 to match projected supply gaps, grounded in econometric models tracking historical supply-demand elasticities rather than voluntary emission pledges.[41] While OPEC's optimistic long-term outlook reflects its member interests, al-Ghais positions it as a counter to what he terms "flip-flopping" in Western agency forecasts, which he claims understate demand risks from underinvestment, as evidenced by tightening markets in 2025.[45] [49]

Critiques of ideological energy transitions

Haitham al-Ghais has consistently critiqued energy transition strategies that prioritize ideological commitments, such as rigid net-zero deadlines, over empirical assessments of global demand and supply dynamics. In a February 2025 article titled "Net zero: hype versus reality," he argued that such targets often impose "unrealistic timelines" with scant regard for energy security, affordability, or technological feasibility, potentially costing trillions annually while risking chronic underinvestment in hydrocarbons.[50] He highlighted how these policies foster oversimplified narrativesâ€”casting renewables as inherently virtuous and fossil fuels as villainousâ€”urging instead a data-driven approach that recognizes hydrocarbons' enduring role, supplying over 80% of global energy since the 1980s.[50] Al-Ghais emphasized persistent fossil fuel demand as evidence against hasty phase-outs, noting oil consumption at 104 million barrels per day in early 2025, natural gas at record highs in 2024, and coal production reaching 8.7 billion tonnes that year.[50] He warned of severe consequences from curtailed upstream investments, projecting a potential 23 million barrels per day oil supply deficit by 2030 if current trends persist, which could disrupt essential sectors like transportation, agriculture, and healthcare.[50] OPEC's forecasts underpin this view, anticipating a 24% increase in primary energy demand by 2050 amid a global population nearing 9.7 billion and an economy roughly doubling in size.[50] During his June 10, 2025, keynote at the Global Energy Show in Calgary, Al-Ghais described net-zero ambitions as "unrealistic," "fixated on deadlines," and "detached from reality," asserting there is "no peak in oil demand on the horizon" and projecting oil needs exceeding 120 million barrels per day by mid-century from current levels around 103 million.[51] He stressed the necessity of $17.4 trillion in oil sector investments over the next 25 years to avert shortages, advocating for policies that harness all energy sourcesâ€”including hydrocarbonsâ€”to meet rising needs, particularly in developing regions, while advancing emissions reductions through innovation rather than source-specific mandates.[51] Al-Ghais maintained that OPEC member states take climate change "very, very seriously," as evidenced by their Paris Agreement adherence, but reject transitions that undermine reliable supply in favor of unproven scalability in alternatives.[51]

Advocacy for sustained hydrocarbon investments

Haitham al-Ghais has consistently argued that sustained investments in hydrocarbon exploration, development, and production are essential to avert future energy supply shortfalls and maintain global economic stability. In the foreword to OPEC's World Oil Outlook 2025, published in October 2025, he stated that the global economy requires $18.2 trillion in upstream oil and gas investments through 2050 to meet projected demand, warning that underinvestment could exacerbate volatility and insecurity.[52] He projected oil comprising approximately 30% of the global energy mix by 2050, based on OPEC's empirical demand forecasts driven by population growth, urbanization, and economic expansion in developing regions.[52] Al-Ghais has highlighted chronic underinvestment as a primary risk, attributing recent market tightness partly to years of insufficient capital inflows into the sector, which totaled around $500 billion annually but fell short of needs during periods of low prices and policy uncertainty. In November 2022, he described this investment lag as "sowing the seeds for future energy crises," emphasizing that delayed spending on new capacities could lead to supply crunches amid rising consumption.[53] By June 2025, he reiterated OPEC's longstanding cautions against inadequate funding, linking it to potential price spikes and disruptions, while critiquing narratives that downplay hydrocarbon roles in favor of accelerated transitions without corresponding supply assurances.[44] His advocacy extends to upstream projects in non-OECD countries, where he sees untapped potential requiring capital to balance reserves depletion rates, estimated at 4-5% annually without replenishment. In a May 2023 interview, al-Ghais stressed that such investments underpin energy security, countering what he called "misguided narratives" from bodies like the International Energy Agency that might deter financing, potentially causing refinery closures and fuel squeezes in importing nations.[54] He maintains that hydrocarbons remain indispensable for petrochemicals, aviation, and heavy industry, where alternatives lack scalability, and that investment levels should align with realistic demand trajectories rather than ideological targets.[55]

Controversies and criticisms

Accusations of market manipulation from Western governments

In October 2022, shortly after Haitham al-Ghais assumed the role of OPEC Secretary General, the OPEC+ alliance announced production cuts totaling 2 million barrels per day, prompting sharp rebukes from the United States government under President Joe Biden, who described the decision as "shortsighted" and warned of reevaluating the U.S.-Saudi relationship due to its perceived alignment with Russia amid the Ukraine conflict.[56][57] U.S. officials, including White House spokespeople, accused the cuts of artificially propping up global oil prices, thereby aiding Russia's war funding and exacerbating inflation in Western economies, with Energy Secretary Jennifer Granholm labeling the move as contrary to efforts to stabilize markets.[58] Al-Ghais defended the action, stating that OPEC+ aimed to ensure "security and stability" in energy markets rather than political alignment, emphasizing that voluntary adjustments addressed existing underproduction by some members and responded to demand forecasts.[57][59] Similar criticisms recurred in 2023 amid discussions of further OPEC+ cuts, with Western governments, including the U.S. and EU members, accusing the group of manipulating prices to undermine economic recovery and heighten energy costs globally.[60] Al-Ghais countered that such blame overlooked underinvestment in supply and erroneous attributions of inflation solely to oil prices, arguing that OPEC's role was to balance markets based on empirical data rather than external pressures.[61] These exchanges highlighted tensions between OPEC's production coordinationâ€”intended to prevent oversupplyâ€”and Western demands for increased output to lower consumer prices, though no formal legal actions for manipulation were pursued against al-Ghais or OPEC.[62] Critics from U.S. Democratic lawmakers, such as Senator Sheldon Whitehouse, later probed potential indirect influences on domestic producers but focused accusations on OPEC's broader strategy rather than al-Ghais personally.[63]

Environmentalist opposition to OPEC strategies

Environmental organizations and climate advocacy groups have accused OPEC, under Haitham al-Ghais' leadership since August 2022, of obstructing international climate agreements by prioritizing fossil fuel production over emission reductions. In December 2023, al-Ghais circulated a letter to OPEC member states ahead of the COP28 summit in Dubai, urging them to "proactively reject any text that targets energy, i.e., fossil fuels, rather than emissions," which was interpreted by critics as an effort to block language on phasing out oil use.[64] [65] This stance drew sharp rebukes from groups like the High Ambition Coalition, a network of climate-vulnerable nations and NGOs, who viewed it as evidence of OPEC's resistance to transitioning away from hydrocarbons despite empirical data on rising global temperatures linked to CO2 emissions from oil combustion.[66] Critics, including Greenpeace and other NGOs, have further opposed OPEC's production cut decisionsâ€”such as the voluntary reductions extended through 2024 and into 2025â€”as mechanisms that sustain high oil prices and incentivize further extraction, thereby delaying the shift to renewables.[67] Al-Ghais defended these strategies by citing OPEC's World Oil Outlook, which forecasts oil demand reaching 116 million barrels per day by 2045 based on economic growth projections in developing regions, arguing that underinvestment risks energy shortages.[68] Environmentalists counter that such forecasts ignore policy-driven demand suppression through carbon pricing and electrification, accusing OPEC of conflating energy security with indefinite hydrocarbon reliance, even as renewable capacity grew by 510 gigawatts globally in 2023.[69] The opposition intensified in 2024-2025 amid OPEC's advocacy for increased oil investments, with al-Ghais stating in October 2025 that the sector requires sustained funding to meet demand amid geopolitical volatility, a position environmental groups like Oil Change International labeled as "fossil fuel denialism" that exacerbates climate risks.[70] These critiques often highlight OPEC's influence in forums like the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change, where its members' resistance to fossil fuel phase-out pledgesâ€”evident in the diluted COP28 language on "transitioning away" rather than phasing outâ€”has been attributed to coordinated lobbying under al-Ghais.[71] However, OPEC maintains that its strategies align with realistic supply-demand dynamics, as evidenced by oil's 30% share of primary energy in 2024 despite trillions invested in alternatives since 2000.[72]

Rebuttals to claims of fossil fuel denialism

Al-Ghais has faced accusations from environmental advocates and outlets like The Guardian of promoting a form of "fossil fuel denialism" by opposing explicit phase-out language at climate summits such as COP28, where he urged OPEC members to reject texts targeting hydrocarbons, arguing they impose "undue and disproportionate pressure" that risks prosperity.[64] [73] In rebuttal, he contends that such positions reflect empirical realities rather than denial, as evidenced by OPEC's World Oil Outlook 2025, which forecasts global oil demand rising to 123 million barrels per day by 2050â€”an 18.6% increase from 103.7 million barrels per day in 2024â€”driven primarily by growth in developing economies.[40] [74] These projections contrast with more aggressive phase-out scenarios from the International Energy Agency (IEA), which al-Ghais has criticized for "vilifying" the oil industry and underestimating sustained demand, noting that global primary energy demand could expand 23% by 2050 amid insufficient alternatives.[75] [76] He highlights that over $9.5 trillion has been invested in energy transitions since 2004, yet oil and gas remain over 50% of the global mix, with intermittent renewables like wind and solar failing to displace reliable hydrocarbons at scale.[68] Al-Ghais acknowledges climate imperatives, advocating emissions reductions via industry-led initiatives such as methane capture and carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS), rather than source-specific mandates that overlook energy access for 2.6 billion people lacking modern fuels.[77] At COP29 in November 2024, he affirmed oil and gas as essential while emphasizing that policy should prioritize lowering emissions over prescribing energy mixes, aligning with OPEC's support for the Paris Agreement's equitable framework.[78] This data-centric rebuttal underscores that abrupt phase-outs, absent viable substitutes, would trigger supply disruptions and economic harm without commensurate environmental gains, as recent demand recordsâ€”exceeding 100 million barrels per day in 2023â€”demonstrate hydrocarbons' enduring role.[79]
