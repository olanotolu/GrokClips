Kill City is a studio album by the American musicians Iggy Pop and James Williamson, both formerly of the rock band the Stooges. It was recorded as a demo in 1975 but released in November 1977 by record label Bomp! Records. It serves as a transitional work between the Stooges era and Pop's Berlin Trilogy with David Bowie.[1]

Background

After the Stooges disbanded in early 1974 following the release of their album Raw Power (1973), Iggy Pop struggled with severe heroin addiction and mental health issues, leading to a stay in a psychiatric hospital.[3] In the summer of 1975, guitarist James Williamson, Pop's collaborator from the Stooges, reunited with him to write and record demo sessions at King Buzzo's Garage in Los Angeles, with the goal of securing a new record deal.[1] Several tracks on Kill City, including "Johanna" and "I Got Nothin'", originated during the Williamson-era Stooges and were performed live by the band during 1973â€“74.[1]

Recording

Kill City was originally recorded in 1975 after the disintegration of the Stooges. It was to be used as a demo to give to record labels in hopes of getting Pop a new contract. His vocals were recorded on weekends when he received permission to leave a mental hospital he was staying in at the time for treatment of his long-standing heroin addiction. The sessions took place at Jimmy Webb's demo studio in Los Angeles, with James Williamson on guitar, and additional contributions from Scott Richardson (guitar), Ron Asheton (bass on select tracks), and other session musicians.[4] The original 1975 "demo" mix of the album remains unheard, with the exception of three tracks which have been released on the compilation Original Punks (2006): "Johanna", "Consolation Prizes" and "Kill City". These tracks sound markedly different from those on the final version of the album, with different guitar parts and, in the case of "Johanna", no saxophone.[5] Pop biographer Paul Trynka said that rock writer Ben Edmonds "played the first mixes to Seymour Stein in late January 1975".[3]

Release

There would be no takers for the album until 1977 when, following the success of Pop's solo albums The Idiot and Lust for Life, Williamson got an advance from Bomp! to release the album, some of which was used to fund studio time to finish off the original recordings by adding overdubs and remixing. The master tapes were lost shortly after the release of the original album and, until 2010, all CD releases were mastered from the original poor quality green vinyl pressing. This partly accounts for the somewhat muddy sound of the album. Pop appeared as himself, performing the album's title track, on the "For Cryin' Out Loud" episode of the Tales from the Crypt TV series, aired on May 22, 1990.[6] In 2010, the original multitracks, which had been lost, were recovered, and Williamson and engineer Ed Cherney remixed the album from them. The resulting mixes formed a new version of the album, released on Bomp!/Alive Records on October 19, 2010.[7] Subsequent reissues, including a 2024 vinyl edition, have used these 2010 remixes.[8]

Kill City has been generally well received by critics. Nick Kent of New Musical Express called it "a great album".[17] Mark Deming of AllMusic called the album "a minor triumph", writing: "The music is more open and bluesy than on Raw Power, and while Williamson's guitar remains thick and powerful, here he's willing to make room for pianos, acoustic guitars and saxophones, and the dynamics of the arrangements suggest a more mature approach after the claustrophobia of Raw Power".[1] Martin Aston of BBC Music praised the album, calling it "Iggy's most underrated album" and one that "helped him get back to real life".[18]

Legacy

The Wire placed Kill City in their list of "100 Records That Set the World on Fire (While No One Was Listening)".[19] In 2010, the album was remixed and remastered by James Williamson and reissued by Bomp! Records, receiving widespread critical acclaim. Mojo praised it as a "lost classic",[12] while Uncut called it "essential listening for fans of proto-punk".[16] The reissue helped cement its status as an influential work in Iggy Pop's post-Stooges career.[18][11]
