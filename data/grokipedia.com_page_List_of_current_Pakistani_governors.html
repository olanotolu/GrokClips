Governor (Pakistan)

The Governor of a province in Pakistan serves as the constitutional head of the provincial executive, appointed by the President on the advice of the Prime Minister to represent federal authority at the provincial level.[1] This office, established under Articles 101 to 105 of the 1973 Constitution of Pakistan, is held by individuals who must be Pakistani citizens over 35 years of age and eligible for election to the National Assembly, with a standard term of five years subject to earlier removal at the President's discretion.[2] Pakistan's four provincesâ€”Punjab, Sindh, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, and Balochistanâ€”each maintain a Governor, who typically exercises ceremonial duties while acting on the advice of the elected Chief Minister in routine governance.[1] In executive functions, the Governor summons, prorogues, and addresses the provincial assembly, grants assent to bills (or reserves them for the President's consideration), and appoints the Chief Minister and provincial ministers on the Chief Minister's recommendation.[1][2] Discretionary powers include appointing a Chief Minister during assembly deadlocks or assuming direct control in cases of constitutional crises, though such interventions are constrained by the Constitution's emphasis on parliamentary supremacy.[3] Historically, Governorsâ€”often aligned with the federal ruling partyâ€”have faced criticism for overreach in politically contentious situations, such as influencing assembly dissolutions or governor's rule impositions, exacerbating federal-provincial frictions when ruling coalitions differ.[4] These dynamics underscore the office's role as a federal check on provincial autonomy, though empirical instances reveal variable adherence to ceremonial limits amid Pakistan's volatile political landscape.[5]

Appointment and Tenure

Eligibility and Selection

Governors of Pakistan's provinces are appointed by the President under Article 101(1) of the Constitution of 1973, acting on the advice of the Prime Minister, a mechanism designed to align provincial leadership with federal priorities and mitigate risks of provincial autonomy challenging national unity.[1] [6] Eligibility criteria, as stipulated in Article 101(2), require that a appointee be qualified to stand for election to the National Assemblyâ€”entailing Pakistani citizenship, enrollment on an electoral roll, absence of disqualifications such as criminal convictions or insolvencyâ€”and be at least 35 years of age, ensuring a baseline of maturity and civic standing without mandating provincial residency or partisan affiliation.[1] [7] In practice, selections prioritize experienced figures such as politicians from federally aligned parties, retired bureaucrats, or occasionally military personnel who lack strong ties to the province's dominant ethnic or political factions, thereby fostering impartial oversight rather than local entrenchment.[8] Prior to the 1973 Constitution, gubernatorial appointments under the 1956 and 1962 Constitutions were more discretionary, vested directly in the President or central executive without explicit reference to National Assembly qualifications or a fixed age threshold, enabling selections based on administrative expertise or loyalty to the federal center amid frequent political instability and one-unit schemes that centralized power. This pre-1973 flexibility, while formalized in constitutional texts, often resulted in partisan choices to counterbalance provincial power bases, a pattern persisting post-1973 despite codified procedures, as evidenced by appointments favoring non-ruling provincial elements to enforce federal directives.[9]

Term and Removal

The Governor of a province in Pakistan does not hold a fixed term of office. Article 101(3) of the Constitution of 1973 stipulates that the Governor shall hold office during the pleasure of the President, permitting removal at any time without the need for specified cause or due process beyond presidential discretion.[10][11] This provision, rooted in the framers' intent to maintain federal oversight, contrasts with the five-year terms of elected provincial assemblies and chief ministers, positioning the Governor as a revocable appointee aligned with central priorities rather than provincial longevity.[10] In practice, gubernatorial tenures have been markedly short and unstable, averaging 1â€“3 years amid Pakistan's frequent federal government transitions and political upheavals. Since the adoption of the 1973 Constitution, provinces have seen over 100 gubernatorial appointments and removals collectively, with Punjab alone recording more than 25 changes, many lasting months or under two years due to shifts in prime ministerial advice to the President.[12][11] Such brevityâ€”exemplified by rapid successions during the 1977 martial law imposition and post-1988 democratic restorationsâ€”facilitates federal recalibration of provincial leadership without provincial consent, often coinciding with national elections or coalition realignments. This removability mechanism causally bolsters the Governor's function as a federal conduit, enabling swift dismissals to resolve center-province dissonances, such as during periods of assembly dissolutions or policy enforcement needs, without entrenching provincial autonomy. Empirical patterns link removals directly to federal-provincial tensions, as seen in the 1990s when multiple governors in Sindh and Punjab were replaced following assembly suspensions, prioritizing national stability over extended provincial incumbency.[11] The absence of tenure protections thus embeds accountability to Islamabad, mitigating risks of gubernatorial drift toward local interests.

Powers and Functions

Ceremonial and Representative Duties

The Governor functions as the nominal head of the provincial executive, representing the President and the federal government within the province to ensure alignment with national constitutional principles. This role emphasizes symbolic oversight, distinguishing it from the executive authority wielded by the Chief Minister, with the Governor primarily acting on ministerial advice under Article 105 while retaining discretion in legislative processes to safeguard federal sovereignty.[1] Key ceremonial duties include the power to summon the Provincial Assembly to meet at specified times and places, prorogue its sessions, and, upon advice from the Chief Minister, dissolve it, thereby facilitating orderly legislative proceedings.[13] The Governor may also address the assembly directly or send messages, often outlining policy directions or federal priorities at the start of sessions.[13] These functions underscore the Governor's position in bridging provincial autonomy with national unity, particularly in ethnically diverse regions where local interests might diverge from broader state objectives. In legislative matters, the Governor provides assent to bills passed by the Provincial Assembly under Article 116, which requires presentation for approval; the Governor may withhold assent and return the bill for reconsideration, or reserve it for the President's consideration if repassed, thereby exercising a check against provincial measures inconsistent with the Constitution.[13] If no action is taken within ten days of resubmission, assent is deemed given. Additionally, when the assembly is prorogued or not in session and circumstances demand immediate action, the Governor may promulgate ordinances under Article 115, which carry the force of law but must be laid before the assembly and expire after 120 days unless approved.[14] These mechanisms have been invoked historically to maintain constitutional fidelity, as seen in instances where governors withheld assent to bills perceived as exceeding provincial competence.[15] Through these duties, the Governor hosts federal dignitaries and participates in state events, reinforcing federal presence and countering parochial tendencies that could undermine national cohesion in provinces like Balochistan or Khyber Pakhtunkhwa.[16]

Executive and Legislative Powers

The Governor of a Pakistani province exercises significant executive authority by appointing the Chief Minister following provincial elections, as stipulated in Article 130(2) of the 1973 Constitution, which requires the Governor to select a member of the Provincial Assembly who, in the Governor's assessment, is most likely to secure the confidence of the majority.[3] This discretion proves crucial in scenarios of hung assemblies, where no single party holds a clear majority, enabling the Governorâ€”appointed by the federal Presidentâ€”to facilitate coalitions that align provincial governance with broader national objectives, thereby mitigating risks of fragmented or regionally parochial administrations that could undermine federal cohesion.[17] In practice, this power has historically allowed federal influence to stabilize provincial executives prone to deadlock or populist overreach, ensuring continuity in resource management and security matters intersecting with national priorities. Legislatively, the Governor serves as a gatekeeper under Article 116, receiving bills passed by the Provincial Assembly and empowered to grant assent, withhold it, return for reconsideration, or reserve them for the President's review if they appear repugnant to federal laws or the Constitution.[13] This reservation mechanism enforces uniformity on concurrent subjects such as taxation, natural resources, and inter-provincial trade, preventing provincial enactments that might erode federal revenue streams or exacerbate ethnic divisions through uneven policies.[14] By subjecting such bills to central scrutiny, the Governor counters potential excesses in elected assemblies, where majority sentiments might favor short-term local gains over sustainable national frameworks, as evidenced in instances where resource-sharing disputes, like those over water or minerals, have prompted federal intervention to maintain equilibrium.[2] Additionally, Article 105 grants the Governor authority to summon and prorogue sessions of the Provincial Assembly, while Article 112 outlines conditions for dissolution, including upon the Chief Minister's advice or in cases of failed no-confidence motions where no viable alternative emerges, with the Governor required to seek presidential approval for caretaker setups.[13] These powers enable the Governor to avert prolonged legislative paralysis, as seen in the 1980s and 1990s when federal-aligned governors prorogued assemblies amid coalition breakdowns in Punjab and Sindh, recommending dissolutions to forestall governance vacuums that could foster separatist narratives or administrative collapse.[1] Such interventions underscore the Governor's role in upholding constitutional order against elected majorities susceptible to factionalism, ensuring provincial functions remain operative under federal oversight until fresh mandates resolve impasses.[17]

Emergency Powers Including Governor's Rule

Article 234 of the Constitution of Pakistan empowers the President to assume control of a province's administration upon a report from the Governor indicating the failure of constitutional machinery, enabling the imposition of Governor's Rule.[18][19] Under this provision, if the President is satisfied that governance cannot proceed in accordance with the Constitution, a proclamation dissolves the provincial assembly, suspends its powers (transferring legislative authority to Parliament), and vests executive functions in the Governor, who administers through the provincial bureaucracy or advisors.[20] The proclamation must be laid before a joint session of Parliament within two months and lapses after two months unless approved by resolutions of both houses, with a maximum extension of four additional months, totaling six months.[18] Governor's Rule has been invoked over 20 times since Pakistan's independence in 1947, primarily in response to political instability, ethnic violence, fiscal breakdowns, or administrative paralysis in provinces.[21] Notable instances include Punjab from January 25, 1949, to April 5, 1951, following the dismissal of Chief Minister Iftikhar Mamdot amid corruption allegations and assembly deadlocks; Sindh in 1953 during communal riots and governance collapse; Balochistan multiple times in the 1970s and 1980s, such as February 1973 to April 1973 and periods amid insurgency and tribal unrest; and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa in the 1990s, including February 1994 to April 1994, preceding escalations in militancy.[21] These impositions often involved direct federal oversight to quell disorder, with the Governor relying on civil servants or military support for stabilization. In practice, Governor's Rule has facilitated short-term restoration of order in crises where elected governments proved ineffective, such as containing unrest in Balochistan's ethnic conflicts or addressing fiscal insolvency in smaller provinces, though extensions were rare and typically led to fresh elections or caretaker setups.[22] Historical records indicate quicker administrative interventions compared to ongoing provincial misgovernance, but outcomes varied, with some periods transitioning to military rule rather than democratic resumption.[21] The mechanism underscores federal authority to intervene in provincial failures, justified by the need for constitutional continuity amid incompetence or violence.[19]

Historical Development

From Independence to the 1956 Constitution

Upon Pakistan's attainment of independence on August 14, 1947, the governance of its provinces initially operated under the adapted framework of the Government of India Act 1935, which functioned as the interim constitution.[23] Provincial governors, appointed directly by the Governor-General, served as the executive heads of regions including Punjab, Sindh, the North-West Frontier Province, and East Bengal, inheriting colonial-era structures to maintain administrative continuity amid partition's disruptions.[24] Muhammad Ali Jinnah, sworn in as the first Governor-General on August 15, 1947, exercised authority to select these officials, prioritizing stability in a nascent state lacking unified institutions.[9] These early governors confronted acute state-building exigencies, such as resettling approximately 7.5 million Muslim refugees who fled to West Pakistan's provinces, organizing relief operations, and quelling communal riots that resulted in up to 1 million deaths across the subcontinent.[25] In Punjab, for instance, Governor Sir Francis Mudie, appointed on August 15, 1947, managed the province's bifurcation, coordinated security forces against cross-border incursions, and facilitated the absorption of princely states like Bahawalpur, which acceded to Pakistan in October 1947 before its 1955 merger into Punjab.[24] Similarly, Sindh's Governor Sir Ghulam Hussain Hidayatullah, serving from August 15, 1947, to October 4, 1948, addressed resource shortages and ethnic tensions exacerbated by the influx of muhajirs from India.[26] Such interventions underscored the governors' role in asserting central directives over fragmented provincial administrations to forge national cohesion.[27] Central authority's precedence over provincial autonomy manifested prominently in East Bengal, where the United Front coalition secured a landslide victory in the legislative assembly elections held between March 8 and 12, 1954, capturing 223 of 309 seats.[28] The ensuing provincial ministry, led by A.K. Fazlul Huq, pursued policies perceived as regionally assertive, prompting Governor-General Malik Ghulam Mohammed to dismiss the cabinet and impose Governor's Rule on May 30, 1954, invoking Section 92A of the 1935 Act to cite an "emergency" threatening federal integrity.[29] This measure, which dissolved the assembly and centralized control until June 1955, exemplified the use of gubernatorial mechanisms to suppress linguistic and economic grievances, thereby preempting fissiparous tendencies and reinforcing dominion-level overrides as a tool for unity.[30] The Constitution of 1956, promulgated on March 23, 1956, provided the first codified delineation of the governor's office, establishing Pakistan as an Islamic Republic and replacing the Governor-General with a President who appointed governors to represent federal interests in provinces.[9] Under its provisions, governors held office at the President's pleasure, exercised executive functions in consultation with provincial chief ministers, and retained latitude in crises to recommend assembly dissolutions or assume direct rule, thereby institutionalizing the interim era's emphasis on centralized checks against provincial drift.[1] This framework abolished the Governor-General's viceregal dominance while embedding governors as conduits for presidential oversight, aligning provincial governance with the republic's parliamentary ethos.[31]

Under the 1962 and 1973 Constitutions

Under the 1962 Constitution, promulgated on June 8, 1962, following President Ayub Khan's 1958 martial law declaration, the Governor's office was restructured within a presidential system that centralized authority at the federal level. Governors were appointed directly by the President without provincial input, functioning as de facto federal agents to administer provinces and ensure alignment with national policy.[9] This arrangement was particularly evident in West Pakistan under the One Unit scheme, enacted in 1955 and sustained until 1970, which merged Punjab, Sindh, North-West Frontier Province, and Balochistan into a single administrative unit governed by one Governorâ€”initially Mushtaq Ahmed Gurmaniâ€”empowered to issue ordinances and exercise executive oversight amid efforts to counter East Pakistan's demographic dominance.[32] The Constitution's Article 79, for instance, granted Governors ordinance-making powers subject to presidential approval, reflecting a viceregal model designed to suppress provincial autonomy and provincialize centrifugal tendencies. The 1973 Constitution, adopted on April 12, 1973, and remaining in effect as Pakistan's foundational document, shifted toward a bicameral parliamentary federalism, delineating the Governor's role in Articles 101â€“109 of Part IV. Article 101 stipulates a Governor for each province, appointed by the President (initially in discretion, later on Prime Ministerial advice post-amendments), serving as the ceremonial head while bound to act on the Chief Minister's advice per Article 105, thereby subordinating executive functions to elected provincial governments.[33] Governors retained legislative summoning and prorogation powers (Article 107), assent to bills (Article 106 with reconsideration option), and the ability to address assemblies (Article 108), alongside emergency provisions under Article 234 allowing assumption of provincial functions with presidential assent in cases of constitutional breakdownâ€” a mechanism invoked sparingly post-1973 to preserve federal unity without routine overreach.[1] Amendments refined this balance, with General Zia-ul-Haq's 1985 revival and modifications temporarily enhancing discretionary latitude during martial rule, but the 18th Amendment of April 19, 2010, curtailed federal interventions by requiring Governors to dissolve assemblies only on Chief Ministerial advice (Article 105 variant) and eliminating presidential discretion in appointments (Article 101), devolving more authority to provinces while preserving gubernatorial checks against instability.[34] Empirical data on Governor's Rule impositions post-1973 indicate reduced frequency compared to earlier erasâ€”e.g., Balochistan saw three instances (Februaryâ€“April 1973, December 1975â€“February 1977, and later under Zia)â€”but spikes in the 1990s amid political engineering, such as in Sindh and NWFP during coalition breakdowns, underscoring retained utility for federal stabilization amid ethnic and partisan fractures.[21] This evolution reflects causal tensions between federal cohesion and provincial self-rule, with gubernatorial powers calibrated to mitigate risks of disintegration without reverting to overt centralization.

Controversies and Criticisms

Allegations of Federal Overreach

Provincial political parties, such as the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) in Sindh, have long alleged that the governor's office serves as a mechanism for federal overreach, portraying it as an undemocratic extension of central authority that erodes provincial self-governance. These critiques frame governorsâ€”federal appointees under Article 48 of the Constitutionâ€”as viceroys who enable the suspension of elected assemblies and imposition of direct rule, often without sufficient evidence of constitutional breakdown, thereby subverting voter mandates in favor of Islamabad's preferences. Such views, prevalent among regionally dominant groups wary of ethnic federalism dilution, point to numerous historical impositions of governor's rule before the 1990s, including at least nine controversial exercises under prior constitutional provisions that facilitated provincial government dismissals amid political rivalries.[35][36] Counterarguments, grounded in assessments of governance failures, maintain that federal interventions via governors respond to tangible provincial breakdowns, such as acute law-and-order collapses, fiscal insolvency, and institutional paralysis that imperil national unity rather than mere partisan maneuvering. Constitutional safeguards under Article 232 require a failure of constitutional machineryâ€”evidenced by ungovernable conditions like widespread violence or administrative deadlockâ€”before governor's rule can be proclaimed for up to two years, with parliamentary approval needed within specified timelines, positioning the office as a pragmatic stabilizer against secessionist risks and corruption-fueled disintegration in ethnically fractious provinces. Defenses from perspectives emphasizing centralized resilience highlight how non-intervention in such crises has historically amplified divisionism, justifying the governor's supervisory role as essential for causal continuity in Pakistan's fragile federation despite biases in provincial narratives that downplay local accountability deficits.[37][38] The 18th Amendment, enacted on April 19, 2010, mitigated some overreach concerns by enhancing provincial legislative and fiscal autonomy, curtailing the governor's ability to unilaterally dismiss chief ministers, and devolving concurrent list subjects to provinces while capping emergency rule durations. Yet, invocations persist in contexts like assembly deadlocks or security vacuums, as seen in post-amendment cases requiring federal arbitration to restore functionality, reflecting an enduring tension where stability imperativesâ€”verified through breakdown metricsâ€”outweigh absolute devolution amid evidence of recurrent provincial mismanagement.[34][39]

Notable Historical Instances

In Punjab, the inaugural imposition of Governor's Rule took place on January 25, 1949, after the dismissal of Chief Minister Iftikhar Hussain Mamdot over documented corruption scandals and governance breakdowns that undermined provincial administration, with the direct federal oversight enduring until April 5, 1951, to stabilize institutions amid post-partition turmoil.[35][21] Governor's Rule in East Pakistan followed electoral upheavals, notably after the 1954 provincial elections where the United Front ousted the ruling Muslim League, prompting central dismissal of the assembly and imposition from May 30, 1954, to August 1955, to counter perceived instability and communist influences reported by provincial authorities.[40] This pattern recurred, with extended direct rule from 1958 onward amid escalating political paralysis and administrative lapses, culminating in governors' assessments that informed President Iskander Mirza's October 7, 1958, declaration of national martial law to avert total collapse.[41][42] In Balochistan, Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto invoked Article 234 of the interim constitution on February 15, 1973, to dismiss the National Awami Party government on treason charges tied to arms caches and insurgency coordination with Afghan elements, establishing Governor's Rule that enabled a four-year military campaign under Governor Rahimuddin Khan, which suppressed the uprising involving over 50,000 fighters through decisive operations, resulting in thousands of insurgent casualties and temporary restoration of order despite heavy-handed tactics.[43][44][45] Sindh experienced Governor's Rule from October 1988 to December 1989 under Lieutenant General Rahimuddin Khan, amid rampant ethnic violence between Muttahida Qaumi Movement (MQM) militants and Sindhi groups, including targeted killings and urban warfare that claimed hundreds of lives annually in Karachi and Hyderabad, with the intervention aimed at dismantling armed factions after provincial authorities failed to curb the anarchy fueled by MQM's territorial control tactics.[46][47] In the 1990s, recurrent MQM-instigated riots and retaliatory clashes, peaking in 1995 with over 1,000 deaths from gang warfare and bombings, prompted repeated federal advisories from governors for assembly dissolutions and security crackdowns, though full direct rule was avoided in favor of Rangers deployments that temporarily reduced homicide rates from ethnic strife.[48][49] Khyber Pakhtunkhwa saw a brief Governor's Rule from February 25 to April 24, 1994, during Prime Minister Benazir Bhutto's tenure, following the collapse of a fragile PPP-led coalition amid governance paralysis, fiscal mismanagement, and rising sectarian tensions that provincial executives could not resolve, allowing federal resumption of control to enforce fiscal reforms and security measures before fresh polls.[50] Post-2000 impositions have been infrequent, with Balochistan's 2013 episode under Article 232 lasting months to address targeted killings and administrative voids after a coalition government's dissolution, yielding short-term pacification via enhanced federal policing but exacerbating alienation.[51] In October 2025, speculation of Governor's Rule in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa surfaced amid federal-provincial disputes over counter-terrorism funding and PTI administration critiques, but was dismissed by Interior Minister Talal Chaudhry and Governor Faisal Karim Kundi, averting intervention as the provincial setup persisted without breakdown.[52][53] Across these cases, gubernatorial direct rule has empirically restored immediate security in 80-90% of instances per historical reviews, often via military augmentation that provincial forces lacked capacity for, though it consistently bred enduring distrust without precipitating viable separatist outcomes.[35]

Current Governors

Governors of Provinces

The governors of Pakistan's four provincesâ€”Punjab, Sindh, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, and Balochistanâ€”serve as federal representatives appointed by the President under Article 101 of the 1973 Constitution, facilitating coordination between the central coalition government (PML-N and PPP) and provincial administrations amid post-2024 election tensions, including PTI-led opposition challenges in Punjab and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa.[54] These appointments, often reflecting power-sharing formulas, have emphasized dialogue on security, economic development, and legislative assent in a politically fragmented landscape as of October 2025.[55] In Punjab, Sardar Saleem Haider Khan, a PPP stalwart and former federal minister, was appointed on May 4, 2024, and sworn in on May 11, 2024, to bridge federal interests with the PML-N-dominated provincial government, focusing on stability during economic reforms and urban governance transitions.[56] Khan's role has involved mediating federal-provincial fiscal disputes and overseeing ceremonial functions amid ongoing coalition adjustments.[57] Sindh's Governor Kamran Khan Tessori, a businessman appointed in October 2022, continues to navigate PPP's provincial stronghold against urban-rural divides, particularly in Karachi, by promoting investment initiatives and security coordination with federal agencies.[58] His tenure has emphasized public-private partnerships for infrastructure, aligning with central directives on counter-terrorism operations.[59] Faisal Karim Kundi, appointed Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Governor on May 4, 2024, represents PPP in a PTI-governed province, prioritizing federal-provincial collaboration on militancy threats and development projects like CPEC extensions, despite occasional assembly dissolutions.[60] Kundi has advocated for inclusive governance, including minority rights and investment facilitation, to counter security rumors and provincial autonomy claims.[61] Balochistan's Sheikh Jaffar Khan Mandokhail, a tribal leader sworn in on May 6, 2024, addresses federal overreach concerns in a coalition setup by focusing on security lapses and resource allocation, including anti-insurgency measures and Gwadar port coordination.[62] His background aids in tribal reconciliation efforts, supporting central policies on stability amid 2024-2025 insurgent activities.[63]

Governors of Administered Territories

Gilgit-Baltistan, administered by Pakistan as a non-provincial territory since its formal integration under the Gilgit-Baltistan Order of 2018, features a Governor appointed by the President of Pakistan on the advice of the Prime Minister to represent federal authority.[64] The Governor acts as head of state, with executive powers exercised in their name by the territorial government, including summoning and proroguing the Gilgit-Baltistan Legislative Assembly and assenting to legislation.[64] This structure ensures direct federal oversight in a strategically vital region bordering China and Afghanistan, facilitating integration into national infrastructure projects like the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor, while limiting local autonomy compared to provinces by excluding it from Article 1 of the Constitution of Pakistan.[65] Syed Mehdi Shah has served as Governor of Gilgit-Baltistan since his appointment on 15 August 2022, a role he continues to hold as of September 2025, during which he has engaged with federal leaders on regional development and assembly sessions.[66] [65] The position's enhanced federal sway, derived from the 2018 Order, contrasts with provincial governors by enabling greater intervention in local governance to counter external geopolitical pressures, such as those from India over the disputed territory, without granting full provincial status or fiscal independence.[64] Azad Jammu and Kashmir (AJK), another administered territory under Pakistani control pursuant to the 1949 Karachi Agreement and UNCIP resolutions, lacks a Governor equivalent, instead maintaining an elected President as ceremonial head of state and a Prime Minister as executive head under its 1974 Interim Constitution Act.[67] Federal influence operates through the AJK Council, chaired by Pakistan's Prime Minister with the AJK President as vice-chair, overseeing defense, foreign affairs, and currency to preserve strategic alignment amid the India-Pakistan dispute over Jammu and Kashmir.[68] This setup provides AJK nominal self-governance in internal matters but subordinates it to Islamabad's directives, differing from Gilgit-Baltistan's appointed governorship by emphasizing elected local leadership while ensuring federal veto on core security issues.[67]
