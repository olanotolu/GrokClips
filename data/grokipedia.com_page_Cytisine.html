Cytisine

Cytisine , also known as baptitoxine , cytisinicline , or sophorine , is an alkaloid that occurs naturally in several plant genera, such as Laburnum and Cytisus of the family Fabaceae. It has been used medically to help with smoking cessation.[1] It has been found effective in several randomized clinical trials, including in New Zealand[1] and the United States.[2] A 2021 non-inferiority trial in Australia found cytisine was not non-inferior to the smoking cessation aid varenicline (sold in the United States as Chantix).[3] Phase 3 trials in the US confirmed efficacy in 2023 and 2025, and a New Drug Application was submitted to the FDA in September 2025.[4][5] It has also been used entheogenically via mescalbeans by some Native American groups, historically in the Rio Grande Valley predating even peyote.[6] Cytisine is extracted from the seeds of Cytisus laburnum L. (Golden Rain acacia), and is found in several genera of the subfamily Faboideae of the family Fabaceae, including Laburnum, Anagyris, Thermopsis, Cytisus, Genista, Retama and Sophora.

Uses

Smoking cessation

Cytisine has been available in post-Soviet states for more than 40 years as an aid to smoking cessation under the brand name Tabex from the Bulgarian pharmaceutical company Sopharma AD.[7] In 1961, Bulgarian pharmacist Strashimir Ingilizov synthesized Tabex using the alkaloid Cytisine which was derived from the seeds of the golden rain tree (Laburnum anagyroides), formerly classified as Cytisus laburnum, a European decorative shrub prevalent in Bulgaria and commonly referred to as "golden rain". It was first marketed in Bulgaria in 1964 and then became widely available in the Soviet Union.[9] In Poland, it is sold under the brand name Desmoxan,[10] and it is also available in Canada under the brand name Cravv.[11] Its molecular structure has some similarity to that of nicotine, and it has similar pharmacological effects. Like the smoking cessation aid varenicline, cytisine is a partial agonist of nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs).[12] Cytisine has a short half-life of 4.8 hours.[13] As a result, the extract provides smokers with satisfaction similar to smoking a cigarette, alleviating the urge to smoke and reducing the severity of nicotine withdrawal symptoms, while also reducing the reward experience of any cigarettes smoked.[14] In 2011, a randomized controlled trial with 740 patients found cytisine improved 12-month abstinence from nicotine from 2.4% with placebo to 8.4% with cytisine.[15] A 2013 meta-analysis of eight studies demonstrated that cytisine has similar effectiveness to varenicline but with substantially lower side effects.[16] A 2014 systematic review and economic evaluation concluded that cytisine was more likely to be cost-effective for smoking cessation than varenicline.[17] As of 2025, cytisine was added to the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines and recommended by the UK's National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) for smoking cessation.[18][19] A synthetic analog, cytisinicline, has an FDA New Drug Application under review as of September 2025, with a decision expected in June 2026.[20]

Toxicity

Cytisine has been found to interfere with breathing and cause death in test mice; LD50 i.v. in mice is 1.73 mg/kg.[22] MÄmane (Sophora chrysophylla) can contain amounts of cytisine that are lethal to most animals. The palila (Loxioides bailleui, a bird), Uresiphita polygonalis virescens and Cydia species (moths) are not affected by the toxin for various reasons, and consume mÄmane, or parts of it, as food. U. p. virescens caterpillars are possibly able to sequester the cytisine to give themselves protection from predation; they have aposematic coloration which would warn off potential predators.[23] In humans, the lethal dose of cytisine is unknown. However, cases of overdose, such as ingestion of 40â€“50 tablets (approximately 60â€“75 mg), have been reported without severe side effects or long-term consequences.[24]
