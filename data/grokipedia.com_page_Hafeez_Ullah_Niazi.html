Hafeez Ullah Niazi

Hafeez Ullah Niazi is a Pakistani political analyst, columnist, and television commentator specializing in national politics and affiliated with the Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf party as a senior figure.[1] A cousin of former Prime Minister Imran Khan, Niazi contributes regular columns to the Daily Jang newspaper, where he critiques governance, military influence, and electoral dynamics from a perspective supportive of PTI's reformist agenda.[2][1] Niazi's prominence stems from his frequent appearances on programs like Geo News' Report Card, where he offers unfiltered analysis often praising Khan's resilience amid legal and political challenges while decrying institutional overreach.[3] His career has included electoral involvement with PTI, though without securing office, and notable friction with regulators, exemplified by a 30-day broadcast ban imposed by PEMRA in October 2019 for remarks deemed defamatory against Senator Azam Swatiâ€”a decision he contested in the Lahore High Court as an infringement on free speech.[3][4] This episode highlighted tensions between media commentators and state bodies, with Niazi resuming appearances after the ban's suspension, underscoring his role as a vocal, PTI-aligned voice in Pakistan's polarized discourse.[4][5]

Personal Background

Early Life and Education

Hafeez Ullah Niazi trained as a civil engineer at the University of Engineering and Technology, Taxila, where he is listed as an alumnus.[6] His professional background in engineering underpins his early career before transitioning to journalism and analysis.[7]

Family Connections

Hafeez Ullah Niazi is the brother-in-law of Imran Khan, former Prime Minister of Pakistan, as the father of Khan's nephew Hassan Niazi.[8][9] His son, Hassan Khan Niazi, is a lawyer who has represented Imran Khan and PTI in legal proceedings, and was sentenced to 10 years in prison by a military court on December 26, 2024, for alleged involvement in the May 9, 2023, riots following Khan's arrest.[8][10] Niazi's siblings include Inamullah Niazi, a politician and former Member of the National Assembly affiliated with PML-N, and the late Saeed Ullah Niazi, who died in early April 2025.[11][12] Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif and Information Minister Attaullah Tarar expressed condolences to Hafeez and Inamullah following Saeed's death, highlighting the family's prominence in politics and media.[13][12]

Professional Career

Engineering Profession

Hafeez Ullah Niazi is a professional civil engineer by training. He graduated with a bachelor's degree in civil engineering from the University of Engineering and Technology (UET), Taxila, a prominent institution in Pakistan specializing in engineering disciplines.[6][7] Niazi advanced his education with a master's degree from Texas Tech University in the United States, enhancing his technical expertise in civil engineering principles and practices.[14] While specific details on his engineering projects or employment history remain limited in public records, his professional background as an engineer preceded his later prominence in journalism and political analysis, reflecting a foundational career in technical infrastructure and construction-related fields typical for civil engineering graduates from UET Taxila.[7]

Journalism and Column Writing

Hafeez Ullah Niazi serves as a regular columnist for Daily Jang, one of Pakistan's leading Urdu-language newspapers, where he contributes pieces focused on political analysis and current affairs.[2] His columns typically examine power dynamics, institutional roles in governance, and key political figures, often drawing on historical context to critique systemic issues such as alleged manipulations within the military and civilian spheres.[15] Niazi's writing career spans over 20 years, during which he has developed a reputation for probing examinations of events like the interplay between former Army Chief General Qamar Javed Bajwa and PTI leader Imran Khan, positing that Bajwa instrumentalized Khan to fulfill specific objectives before shifting alignments.[16] In another piece, he forecasted challenges to the government's constitutional package, linking it to broader political negotiations and potential instability by the time of his subsequent column.[17] His columns frequently address themes of deception (dhokha) in Pakistan's political framework, including critiques of opposition strategies, establishment interventions, and electoral processes, as evidenced in discussions of U.S. resolutions on Pakistani elections and their implications for domestic actors.[18] Adaptations of his work, such as audio readings shared by Daily Jang on YouTube, have reached wide audiences, with playlists compiling hundreds of entries underscoring their sustained relevance in public discourse.[15] Niazi's approach emphasizes causal linkages between events, avoiding unsubstantiated speculation while attributing outcomes to verifiable actors and decisions.

Television Appearances

Hafeez Ullah Niazi serves as a regular political analyst on Pakistani television, frequently contributing to discussions on national politics, PTI strategies, and establishment influences. His appearances span multiple networks, where he offers candid commentary often drawing from personal connections to PTI leadership.[19] Niazi is a consistent panelist on Report Card aired on Geo News, addressing issues such as PTI's governance critiques and family-related legal matters. In a July 2023 episode, he accused PTI Chairman Imran Khan of destroying the country through mismanagement.[20] He became emotional discussing the March 2023 arrest of his nephew Hassan Niazi on the program, highlighting perceived political targeting.[21] Other segments included analyses of parliamentary fund withholdings in April 2023 and PTI's post-arrest dynamics in May 2023.[22][23] On On the Front, hosted by Kamran Shahid on Dunya News, Niazi participates in panel debates covering electoral rigging allegations, military trials, and PTI's alliances. Political Involvement

Electoral Attempts

Hafeez Ullah Niazi, a cousin of PTI founder Imran Khan and an early senior leader in the party, has maintained involvement in Pakistani politics primarily through advisory roles and public commentary rather than direct candidacy.[31] No official records from the Election Commission of Pakistan document Niazi contesting national or provincial assembly elections, including claims of participation in the 2002 general election for Punjab Provincial Assembly constituency PP-44 (Mianwali-II).[32] In that election, held on October 10, 2002, independent candidate Gul Hameed Khan of PML-Q secured victory with 32,213 votes, defeating PPP's Syed G.M. Shah who received 27,761 votes, while other candidates including family member Inam Ullah Niazi (PML-N) polled minimally at 152 votes.[32] Niazi's political efforts have instead centered on bolstering PTI's organizational and campaign strategies, drawing on family ties in Mianwali district to support the party's growth during its formative years.[31] As a vocal proponent, he has critiqued electoral processes and advocated for PTI candidates in subsequent polls, such as the 2018 and 2024 general elections, without mounting personal bids.[1] This approach aligns with his background in engineering and journalism, where he has influenced public discourse on PTI's platform rather than seeking mandates through the ballot.

Affiliation with PTI and Imran Khan Support

Hafeez Ullah Niazi is related to Imran Khan as his brother-in-law through marriage.[33] This familial connection has underpinned his longstanding alignment with Khan's political endeavors within the Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI).[4] Niazi served as a founding and senior member of PTI, contributing to the party's ideological foundation and messaging from the late 1990s until around 2011.[31] In this capacity, he advised Khan directly on political strategy, with PTI dissident Akbar S. Legal Matters

Personal Legal Challenges

Hafeezullah Niazi faced regulatory sanctions from the Pakistan Electronic Media Regulatory Authority (Pemra) in October 2019, when the body imposed a 30-day ban on his appearances on electronic media channels due to allegations of making defamatory remarks against Senator Azam Swati of the Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI).[3] Niazi contested the ban as unlawful and politically motivated, filing a petition in the Lahore High Court (LHC) on October 2, 2019, arguing it violated his freedom of expression and was imposed without due process.[4] Concurrently, Niazi was embroiled in a Supreme Court case stemming from accusations that he had influenced the transfer of former Islamabad Inspector General of Police Jan Mohammad to the Sindh police chief position in 2010 through then-interior minister Rehman Malik, a claim tied to broader allegations of undue interference in law enforcement appointments.[3] The case highlighted tensions between media figures and political actors, with Niazi maintaining the charges were fabricated to silence his commentary. No conviction resulted from these proceedings, and Niazi continued his professional activities post-resolution.

Writings and Public Commentary

Key Publications

Hafeez Ullah Niazi's published output centers on opinion columns in Daily Jang, Pakistan's largest Urdu-language newspaper, rather than authored books or monographs.[2] These columns, appearing regularly since at least the early 2010s, analyze domestic politics, military-civil relations, and foreign policy, often advocating for reforms aligned with PTI's agenda.[7] Recurring themes include critiques of establishment interference in elections and predictions on PTI's endurance amid legal and institutional pressures, as seen in discussions of Imran Khan's leadership strategies post-2018.[17] No comprehensive compilations of his writings into book form have been documented in available sources.[16]

Political Analyses and Opinions

Hafeez Ullah Niazi has frequently analyzed the interplay between Pakistan's civilian leadership and military establishment, critiquing narratives used to undermine politicians as tools for institutional unity prior to political interventions. In his commentary titled "The Bajwa Story," Niazi argued that such narratives are constructed to consolidate organizational cohesion before targeting civilian figures, reflecting a pattern in Pakistan's power dynamics.[42] Niazi has expressed skepticism toward proposals shifting Pakistan toward a presidential system, questioning in a January 2022 Geo News analysis whether the campaign serves genuine reform or conceals partisan maneuvers amid ongoing power struggles.[43] He has warned of deepening political polarization, stating in August 2024 that it risks unintentional damage to the nation and its populace, potentially irreversible without de-escalation.[44] Extending this, Niazi forecasted in mid-2024 a violent trajectory for Pakistan's political future within one to two months, likening risks to Bangladesh's unrest and attributing escalation to unresolved elite conflicts.[45] On leadership accountability, Niazi has defended major figures against treason charges, asserting in 2024 that no prominent Pakistani leaderâ€”Imran Khan, Nawaz Sharif, or othersâ€”qualifies as a traitor, emphasizing contextual harms over absolute betrayal.[44] His views on Imran Khan blend support and critique; he has praised Khan's bravery and anticipated political surprises from his resolve, while in October 2025 expressing frustration over perceived weaknesses in decision-making compared to other politicians.[30][46] Niazi has also delved into PTI internals, revealing in October 2025 potential deals threatening figures like Ali Amin Gandapur and insider dynamics around negotiation bans.[47][48] These opinions, aired on channels like ARY News and SAMAA TV, underscore Niazi's emphasis on causal links between institutional overreach and democratic erosion, though Pakistani media outlets vary in alignment with opposition narratives.[49][50]
