New Mexico's 1st congressional district

New Mexico's 1st congressional district is a United States congressional district serving central New Mexico, including the state's largest city of Albuquerque and most of Bernalillo County, as well as all of Torrance County and portions of Sandoval, Santa Fe, and Valencia counties.[1][2] The district covers an urban and suburban area with significant federal government presence, including military installations and national laboratories.[3] As of the 2024 American Community Survey, the district has a population of 711,081, with a median age of approximately 41 years and a median household income around $68,000.[4][5] It is represented by Democrat Melanie Stansbury, who won a special election in June 2021 to succeed Deb Haaland and was reelected in 2022 and 2024.[6][7][8] The district has shifted from Republican control in its early yearsâ€”held by figures such as Steven Schiff and Heather Wilsonâ€”to a consistent Democratic stronghold since Martin Heinrich's victory in 2008, reflecting Albuquerque's growing urban Democratic electorate amid demographic changes including increased Hispanic population shares.[9] Recent elections demonstrate strong partisan alignment, with Stansbury securing victories by wide margins in a district that supported Democratic presidential candidates in recent cycles.[9] Boundary adjustments following the 2020 census minimally altered its composition compared to other New Mexico districts, preserving its status as a safely Democratic seat.[1]

Geography and Demographics

Current Boundaries and Composition

New Mexico's 1st congressional district, as defined following the 2021 redistricting based on the 2020 census, covers most of Bernalillo County, including the entirety of Albuquerque, the state's largest city and economic hub. It extends to include portions of Sandoval County to the north, Santa Fe County to the northeast, and Torrance County to the east, forming a compact central region that prioritizes urban and suburban connectivity over expansive rural inclusion.[3][10] This configuration excludes more rural southern areas of the state, which were reassigned to the 2nd district to balance population loads across New Mexico's three congressional districts, each targeting roughly equal representation of approximately 727,000 residents statewide. The district's land area spans varied terrain, incorporating the Albuquerque Basin and extensions into the eastern plains of Torrance County.[11] The population of the district is estimated at 709,000 as of 2023, with a density of approximately 40 persons per square mile, underscoring the contrast between high-density urban cores in Albuquerqueâ€”where much of the populace concentratesâ€”and lower-density rural and semi-rural outskirts. Key geographic features include federally recognized tribal lands such as Sandia Pueblo, straddling Bernalillo and Sandoval counties, alongside managed public lands under the Bureau of Land Management, which influence local resource-based activities without dominating the district's primarily metropolitan character.[5][2][3]

Population Characteristics and Socioeconomic Data

The population of New Mexico's 1st congressional district was estimated at 711,081 in 2024, predominantly concentrated in the urban Albuquerque metropolitan area, which drives higher density and socioeconomic variation compared to rural districts.[4] The median age stands at 41.2 years, reflecting a relatively mature demographic influenced by the presence of federal facilities, universities, and retirees in Bernalillo County.[5] Racial and ethnic composition includes non-Hispanic Whites at approximately 46% (323,000 individuals), Hispanics or Latinos of any race at about 41%, Native Americans at 4%, Blacks or African Americans at 3%, Asians at 3%, and smaller shares for other groups or multiracial identifications, with Native American influences stemming from proximity to Pueblo communities and Isleta Pueblo within the district.[5] [12] This diversity arises from historical Spanish colonial settlement, indigenous populations, and modern migration to urban centers like Albuquerque.

Political Landscape

Partisan Voting Index and Lean

The Cook Partisan Voting Index for New Mexico's 1st congressional district stands at D+7 in the 2025 assessment, signifying that the district supported the Democratic presidential candidate by 7 percentage points more than the national two-party average across the 2020 and 2024 elections.[9][14] This metric underscores a structural Democratic advantage engineered through the 2021 redistricting process, which packed urban areas like Albuquerqueâ€”characterized by dense populations of younger, educated, and minority votersâ€”into the district, amplifying its partisan skew relative to rural or exurban counterparts elsewhere in the state.[9] Contributing to this lean, registered Democrats outnumber Republicans by a significant margin, holding about 41% of voters compared to 32% as of September 2025, with independents and others filling the gap.[15] The district's boundaries prioritize Bernalillo County's Democratic strongholds, fostering reliability for the party beyond statewide trends, where Democratic registration edges have narrowed amid gains for Republicans and independents since 2020.[16] This configuration renders NM-01 more solidly Democratic than New Mexico's overall political composition, which exhibits a milder leftward deviation in presidential voting patterns.[17]

Voting Patterns in Statewide Races

In presidential elections, New Mexico's 1st congressional district has demonstrated strong Democratic performance. In 2020, Joe Biden secured a margin of approximately 25-30 percentage points over Donald Trump in the district, outpacing the statewide Democratic advantage of 10.8 points.[18] In 2024, Kamala Harris maintained a comparable Democratic edge in the district despite national Republican gains, reflecting persistent partisan alignment amid shifts elsewhere.[19] Gubernatorial races further illustrate the district's Democratic tilt, with robust support for incumbents and candidates like Michelle Lujan Grisham. In 2018, Lujan Grisham won the governorship statewide by 14.3 points, drawing even stronger backing from the urban core encompassing much of the 1st district. Her 2022 reelection yielded an 8.2-point statewide victory over Mark Ronchetti, bolstered by solid margins in the district where Democratic incumbency reinforced voter preferences prior to boundary adjustments.[20] Earlier contests showed occasional Republican viability, but post-2010 trends solidified Democratic reliability in this area. U.S. Senate elections mirror this pattern, with consistent Democratic victories exceeding statewide results. Martin Heinrich's 2018 reelection produced a 25.1-point margin statewide, amplified in the 1st district's Democratic-leaning precincts. Historical Development

Formation and Early Boundaries (1963â€“2000)

New Mexico's 1st congressional district was established effective for the 88th Congress (1963â€“1965) following apportionment after the 1960 census, which recorded a state population of 951,023 and entitled the state to a second House seat beyond its prior at-large representation.[21] The district was configured to center on Bernalillo County, including Albuquerque as its urban core, while incorporating adjacent areas such as all of Santa Fe County, most of Sandoval County, and portions of Valencia County to approximate equal population shares with the newly formed 2nd district, which covered the state's rural and southern regions. This delineation reflected the concentration of population in the central urban corridor amid the state's sparse overall density. The 1970 census, reporting 1,016,000 residents and preserving two seats statewide, prompted minor boundary refinements enacted by the state legislature to equalize district populations amid Albuquerque's growth, with adjustments primarily involving small exchanges in Sandoval and Valencia counties to maintain contiguity and balance without altering the district's Bernalillo-focused character.[21] These changes addressed urban expansion driven by economic development, including federal research and military installations like Sandia National Laboratories and Kirtland Air Force Base, which spurred suburban population increases in eastern Bernalillo County and nearby areas. Following the 1980 census (1,299,968 residents), which added a third statewide seat effective for the 98th Congress, redistricting in 1982 reconfigured the 1st district by trimming some northern extensionsâ€”ceding parts of Santa Fe and Taos counties to the new 3rd districtâ€”while reinforcing its central boundaries around Albuquerque and incorporating growing suburbs in southern Sandoval County to comply with equal population mandates under the Voting Rights Act.[21] The 1990 census (1,515,069 residents, retaining three seats) led to further incremental tweaks in 1991, such as fine-tuning edges in Valencia and Torrance counties for demographic equity, but preserved the district's essential stability as an urban-suburban enclave through 2000, accommodating steady population gains from federal employment and regional migration without substantive territorial overhauls.[21]

Boundary Changes and Redistricting (2000â€“2020)

Following the 2000 United States Census, which recorded New Mexico's population at 1,819,046, the state legislature passed Senate Bill 12 during a special session in October 2001 to redraw congressional district boundaries. This legislation aimed to equalize district populations to approximately 606,351 residents each, addressing shifts from urban concentration in the Albuquerque area.[22] For the 1st district, adjustments incorporated portions of Torrance County and refined boundaries in adjacent areas to maintain population balance without evidence of partisan manipulation.[23] The redrawn 1st district continued to center on Bernalillo County while extending into parts of Sandoval, Valencia, and Torrance counties, reflecting the need to capture growth in the Albuquerque metropolitan statistical area, which increased from about 711,000 residents in 2000. These changes ensured compliance with federal equal protection standards under Article I, Section 2 of the U.S. Constitution, prioritizing numerical equality over political considerations. After the 2010 Census reported a state population of 2,059,179, requiring districts of roughly 686,393 each, legislative deadlock over partisan priorities stalled agreement during 2011 special sessions.[23] On December 29, 2011, the Second Judicial District Court issued a remedial plan in Maestas v. State of New Mexico, drawing compact, contiguous districts based on census blocks to achieve population parity and preserve communities of interest.[23] The court's map for the 1st district largely preserved the prior configuration but adjusted to include expanding suburban zones in Sandoval County, such as Rio Ranchoâ€”whose population grew from 72,895 in 2000 to 87,521 in 2010â€”to offset slower growth elsewhere and balance the district's total. This judicial intervention emphasized data-driven criteria like compactness scores and minimal splits of counties or precincts, avoiding the political wrangling evident in failed legislative proposals.[23] The resulting boundaries sustained the district's Democratic-leaning character rooted in urban demographics while introducing marginal competitiveness from suburban inclusions, and faced no successful legal challenges on fairness grounds through 2020.[23]

2021 Redistricting Process and Controversies

The Democratic-controlled New Mexico Legislature passed Senate Bill 1 (SB 1) on December 11, 2021, during its second special session, enacting new congressional boundaries to reflect 2020 census data showing population shifts toward urban areas like Albuquerque.[24] Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham signed the bill into law on December 17, 2021.[25] Democrats justified the map by emphasizing compliance with the Voting Rights Act, preservation of communities of interest, and relative compactness, though independent metrics later indicated low compactness with a Polsby-Popper score of 0.22 for congressional districts.[26] For the 1st district, the new boundaries retained the core Bernalillo County area while making adjustments to incorporate growing suburban populations in Sandoval County and shed some rural precincts to the 2nd district, resulting in a district lean of D+7 per post-redistricting estimates.[27] These changes contributed to an efficiency gap favoring Democrats by concentrating opposition votes elsewhere, enhancing the district's Democratic performance metrics beyond prior cycles.[28] Republicans challenged the map in a January 2022 lawsuit filed in Lea County District Court, alleging it constituted a partisan gerrymander by cracking rural Republican strength in the 2nd districtâ€”adding urban Democratic voters from areas like the 1stâ€”to create three safe Democratic seats and dilute statewide GOP voting power.[29] The Republican Party of New Mexico argued the process ignored traditional criteria for neutrality, evidenced by internal Democratic communications prioritizing electoral advantage over compactness.[30] A district court upheld the map in October 2023, and the New Mexico Supreme Court affirmed in November 2023, acknowledging partisan intent but ruling that the state constitution lacks a justiciable standard against such gerrymandering absent traditional districting violations.[31] [32]

Elections

Historical Trends and Competitiveness

From its establishment following the 1962 redistricting, New Mexico's 1st congressional district experienced periods of competitive elections with both parties securing victories, particularly through the 1960s and 1970s, before Republican dominance from 1969 to 2008 under representatives like Manuel Lujan and Steve Schiff, often with margins in the 5-15% range in contested races. This variability reflected the district's mix of urban Albuquerque voters and surrounding suburban and rural areas, where turnout and candidate quality influenced outcomes more than overwhelming partisan leans. Republican incumbents typically prevailed, but Democratic challengers occasionally narrowed gaps to under 10% in cycles like the late 1980s. Post-2000, the district trended toward Democratic solidification, with Republicans winning narrowly until 2006 (e.g., Heather Wilson's 0.4% victory that year) before Democrats captured the seat in 2008 and held it thereafter.[9] GOP vote shares, which exceeded 50% in 2000-2004 general elections, averaged around 44% from 2008-2024, declining amid demographic shifts such as Albuquerque's urban expansion, rising education levels, and stronger Democratic performance among Hispanic voters comprising over 40% of the district's population.[9] These factors, driven by in-migration and generational turnover rather than redistricting alone, contributed to the erosion, as evidenced by the district outperforming statewide Republican presidential shares by 5-10 points in early 2000s cycles but underperforming them post-2012.[5]

Recent General Elections (2018â€“2024)

In the 2018 general election for New Mexico's 1st congressional district, Democrat Deb Haaland secured victory with 58.7% of the vote (169,427 votes) against Republican Janice Arnold-Jones's 41.3% (119,139 votes), marking a continuation of Democratic control in an open seat following Michelle Lujan Grisham's run for governor.[49] Campaign issues centered on economic development, renewable energy transitions amid the state's oil dependency, and immigration enforcement, with Haaland emphasizing climate action and Native American representation as a member of the Laguna Pueblo.[50] Voter turnout reached approximately 289,000 ballots, reflecting midterm engagement in urban Bernalillo County.[51] Haaland won re-election in 2020 with 61.1% (212,853 votes) to Republican Michelle Garcia Holmes's 38.9% (135,855 votes), expanding her margin amid national Democratic gains and heightened focus on pandemic response, healthcare access, and energy policy debates over fossil fuels versus green jobs.[52] The race saw over 347,000 votes cast, bolstered by mail-in voting expansions.[18]

Special Elections

Following the death of incumbent Republican Representative Steven Schiff on March 25, 1998, New Mexico Governor Gary Johnson called a special election on June 23, 1998, to fill the vacancy for the remainder of the 105th Congress.[58] Republican Heather Wilson, an Air Force veteran and former state cabinet official, defeated Democrat Phil Maloofâ€”a former Republican Albuquerque city councilor who switched partiesâ€”and independents, including Green Party candidate Robert Anderson, whose presence split anti-Republican votes.[59][60] The Republican Party invested heavily, spending over $1 million to secure the win amid a competitive district where Democrats fielded divided candidates.[60] Voter turnout was low, consistent with special elections lacking broader ballot measures or high-profile national attention. In the 117th Congress, Democratic Representative Deb Haaland resigned on February 16, 2021, after her confirmation as U.S. Secretary of the Interior in President Joe Biden's cabinet.[61] Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham scheduled a special election for June 1, 2021, following party committee nominations rather than primaries.[62] Democratic state Representative Melanie Stansbury secured 60% of the vote against Republican state Senator Mark Moores (36%), Independent Aubrey Dunn (3%), and Libertarian Christopher Manning (1%), with write-ins negligible.[63] Early reporting indicated turnout around 27%, though final figures reflected typical off-year suppression due to the single-race focus and lack of coattails from other contests.[64] Both special elections underscored causal factors tied to vacanciesâ€”incumbent death in 1998 and executive appointment in 2021â€”while reinforcing the district's underlying partisan balance at the time: competitive with a Republican edge in the late 1990s, shifting Democratic by 2021 amid Albuquerque's urban growth and demographic changes. Outcomes did not disrupt broader representational continuity, as winners aligned with prevailing local strengths and advanced to full terms.[65]
