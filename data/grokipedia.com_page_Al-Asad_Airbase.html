Al-Asad Airbase

Al-Asad Airbase is a large military airfield in Al Anbar Province, Iraq, situated approximately 180 kilometers west of Baghdad near the town of Al-Asad.[1][2] Originally built in 1985 as Qadisiyah Airbase to support the Iraqi Air Force and expanded by 1995 to cover about 42 square kilometers, it transitioned into a primary hub for coalition forces after the 2003 U.S.-led invasion. During Operation Iraqi Freedom, it functioned as the second-largest U.S. airbase in Iraq, accommodating thousands of troops and facilitating air operations, logistics, and combat support.[1][3] In the post-2011 period, Al-Asad has primarily served U.S. and coalition personnel under Operation Inherent Resolve, focusing on advising and training Iraqi security forces against Islamic State militants.[4][5] The base supports combat search and rescue, medical evacuation, intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance missions, while enduring repeated rocket and drone attacks from Iranian-backed militias in western Iraq.[6][7] A defining event occurred on January 8, 2020, when Iran launched 11 ballistic missiles at the base in retaliation for the U.S. drone strike killing Qasem Soleimani, destroying aircraft and hangars while exposing over 100 U.S. troops to traumatic brain injuries from blast effects, though no fatalities resulted due to prior dispersal warnings.[8][9] This strike highlighted the base's strategic vulnerability amid escalating U.S.-Iran tensions and ongoing militia threats.[10]

Location and Infrastructure

Geographical Setting

Al-Asad Airbase is situated in Al Anbar Governorate in western Iraq, approximately 180 kilometers west of Baghdad and 12 kilometers southwest of Haditha, adjacent to the town of Al Baghdadi near the Euphrates River.[2][11] The facility lies within the expansive Syrian Desert plateau, which dominates the province's geography as Iraq's largest governorate by area, encompassing vast arid expanses with low population density.[12] The airbase's precise coordinates are 33Â°47.14â€²N 42Â°26.47â€²E, at an elevation of 618 feet (188 meters) above mean sea level.[13] Its terrain consists of flat to gently rolling desert landscape interspersed with rocky outcrops and scrublands, providing natural seclusion that enhances operational security.[1] A prominent wadi bisects the base, influencing its infrastructure division and contributing to a perimeter exceeding 15 miles, while the surrounding hot desert climate features extreme heat, minimal rainfall, and recurrent sandstorms typical of Al Anbar's western desert environment.[14][15]

Facilities and Capabilities

Al-Asad Airbase features two principal paved runways, measuring approximately 14,000 feet (4,267 meters) and 13,000 feet (3,962 meters) in length, designed to accommodate heavy fixed-wing aircraft such as fighters and transport planes, along with multiple parallel taxiways for efficient ground operations.[1][2] A tertiary dirt runway extends about 3,090 meters (10,100 feet), providing auxiliary landing options during maintenance or conflict scenarios.[16] The airfield includes extensive tarmac areas and 23 hardened aircraft shelters, enabling protected storage and rapid deployment of aviation assets amid threats like missile strikes.[16] Support infrastructure encompasses multiple hardened hangars, including large variants capable of housing heavy aircraft, positioned primarily south of the runways alongside revetments for blast protection.[11][17] The base spans roughly 42 square kilometers (16 square miles), incorporating logistics hubs, fuel storage, and maintenance facilities upgraded post-2003 to sustain prolonged coalition operations. A 35-megawatt power plant and distribution system ensure energy reliability for critical systems, minimizing disruptions during high-tempo activities.[18] Operationally, the airbase supports a wide array of missions, including close air support, reconnaissance, and troop transport, with ramp expansions enabling heavy cargo handling for combat sustainment. Its remote desert location in Al Anbar Governorate, at an elevation of 188 meters (617 feet), facilitates low-observable training and minimizes civilian interference, though sandstorms periodically challenge visibility and equipment.[19] Capabilities extend to accommodating up to several thousand personnel with basic amenities like barracks and medical facilities, though these have varied by occupying force. Modern enhancements, such as radar and drone integration, bolster surveillance and defensive postures against regional threats.[20]

Pre-2003 History

Construction as Qadisiyah Airbase

Qadisiyah Airbase was constructed in 1985 by the Iraqi government under Saddam Hussein as a state-of-the-art facility for the Iraqi Air Force, designed to enhance aerial capabilities amid regional threats. The base formed part of Iraq's "Project Super-Base," a 1975 initiative to develop five hardened super-bases incorporating hardened aircraft shelters and dispersed operations tactics derived from lessons of the 1973 Yom Kippur War.[21] Named after the 636 Battle of Qadisiyah, where Arab forces defeated the Persian Sassanid Empire, the site's selection evoked symbolic historical victory over Persian (Iranian) adversaries during the ongoing Iran-Iraq War.[2] Yugoslav contractors, funded by the then-Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, handled the engineering and construction, completing the initial phase at a reported cost of $280 million.[22] The base featured extensive runways, hardened shelters, and support infrastructure capable of sustaining large-scale fighter and bomber operations, reflecting Iraq's emphasis on fortified airfields to withstand potential strikes.[11] By the mid-1990s, amid post-Gulf War sanctions and military constraints, the facility was expanded to approximately 42 square kilometers but saw reduced use before abandonment.

Use in Iraqi Conflicts

The Qadisiyah Airbase, constructed in 1985 amid the ongoing Iran-Iraq War, served as a key facility for the Iraqi Air Force (IQAF), housing three fighter squadrons equipped with MiG-25, MiG-29, MiG-21, MiG-19, F-6, and F-7 aircraft to support air operations against Iranian forces.[11] [1] These squadrons conducted intercept and ground attack missions from the base's western location, leveraging its hardened aircraft shelters designed based on lessons from early war damage to Iraqi airfields.[11] The facility also stored chemical munitions, with reports indicating approximately 700 bombs held there for potential deployment in the conflict's later stages.[11] During the 1991 Gulf War, Qadisiyahâ€”by then commonly referred to as Al Asad Airbaseâ€”functioned as a primary IQAF hub, hosting squadrons including the 39th Squadron with MiG-29s and elements of MiG-25 units, from which Iraqi aircraft attempted defensive sorties against Coalition air superiority efforts.[11] [23] The base became a high-priority target in the Coalition's air campaign, sustaining multiple strikes beginning January 17, 1991, by RAF Tornado GR-1s, followed by U.S. F-111Fs on January 23-25 and F-117As through February 19, resulting in severe damage to runways, hangars, and approximately 50 Iraqi fighters destroyed or disabled.[11] Many surviving aircraft were evacuated to Iran or dispersed to evade further attacks, contributing to the rapid collapse of organized Iraqi air resistance.[11] In the war's aftermath, the base remained partially operational into the mid-1990s despite sanctions and infrastructure degradation, with residual MiG-21 and MiG-25 squadrons conducting limited patrols under no-fly zone constraints, though its role diminished as resources shifted to central and southern facilities.[11] [2] By the late 1990s, abandonment accelerated due to cumulative damage and economic pressures, leaving the site largely unused until the 2003 invasion.[2]

Coalition Era Operations (2003-2011)

Establishment and Expansion

Following the 2003 invasion of Iraq, Al-Asad Airbase was seized by elements of the Australian Special Air Service Regiment in April 2003, who captured the facility intact along with approximately 50 Iraqi MiG aircraft and millions of pounds of explosives.[1][24] The base, previously known as Qadisiyah Airbase and largely abandoned by Iraqi forces, was handed over to United States Air Force control in May 2003, marking the start of Coalition occupation.[1] U.S. military forces formally occupied the site from May 2003, utilizing its existing infrastructure including two parallel runways and hardened aircraft shelters to support initial air operations and logistics in western Iraq.[25] By early 2004, Al-Asad had evolved into one of the primary airbases for Coalition Forces during Operation Iraqi Freedom, serving as a hub for aviation support, troop rotations, and convoy operations amid escalating insurgency threats.[11] U.S. planners designated it as one of four "enduring" bases for long-term presence, prompting significant infrastructure investments; for instance, over $46 million was allocated by 2006 for expansions such as additional barracks, maintenance facilities, and perimeter defenses spanning a 21-kilometer security zone.[26][27] These developments transformed the base into the largest Coalition installation in western Iraq and the second-largest U.S. air facility in the country, capable of accommodating tens of thousands of personnel, fixed-wing aircraft, helicopters, and armored vehicles by the mid-2000s.[1] The expansion continued through 2011, peaking as the headquarters for major U.S. Marine Corps units like the II Marine Expeditionary Force until early 2010, with added amenities including internal transport systems to manage the site's vast scaleâ€”comparable to a small cityâ€”and to sustain prolonged deployments against insurgent activities in Al Anbar Province.[25][11] This growth reflected broader U.S. strategy to consolidate forward operating bases for counterinsurgency, though it drew scrutiny over perceived permanence despite official denials of indefinite occupation.[26]

Deployed Units and Missions

Al-Asad Airbase served as the headquarters for Multi-National Force-West (MNF-W) from 2004 onward, under the command of the II Marine Expeditionary Force (II MEF), which oversaw coalition operations across Al Anbar Province until January 2010.[1][3] The base hosted thousands of U.S. and coalition personnel at the height of Operation Iraqi Freedom, functioning as the second-largest U.S. airbase in Iraq and a key logistics and aviation hub for western operations.[3] Primary U.S. Marine Corps units included the II MEF (Forward), elements of the 2nd Marine Aircraft Wing (Forward), and Marine Wing Headquarters Squadron 2, supporting fixed-wing and rotary-wing aviation missions.[1][11] Ground forces encompassed rotating Marine infantry battalions and support squadrons, such as Marine Wing Support Squadron-472, alongside U.S. Navy Seabees from Naval Mobile Construction Battalion 23 for base infrastructure.[11] U.S. Army units, including the 3rd Armored Cavalry Regiment for initial securing operations in 2003 and the 2nd Battalion, 153rd Infantry for convoy security to the Syrian border, also deployed there.[2][28] Coalition partners contributed forces from nations including Australia, whose Special Air Service Regiment assisted in early base seizure.[2] MNF-W missions focused on counterinsurgency operations to stabilize Al Anbar, a hotspot for Sunni insurgent activity, including patrols, raids, and major offensives like Operation Phantom Fury in Fallujah.[2] Units conducted joint security operations with Iraqi forces, border interdiction to curb foreign fighter inflows, and training programs for Iraqi Army and police units to enable transition of authority.[29][28] The base facilitated aviation support for close air support, medical evacuations, and logistics convoys, while providing force protection against frequent mortar and rocket attacks. By 2010, as U.S. drawdown progressed, emphasis shifted toward advising Iraqi security forces ahead of full coalition withdrawal in 2011.

Counter-ISIS Operations (2014-2019)

Revival and Key Deployments

In response to the Islamic State of Iraq and Syria (ISIS) offensive that captured Mosul in June 2014 and threatened further advances in Anbar Province, the United States reestablished a military presence at Al-Asad Airbase under Operation Inherent Resolve, formally launched on October 15, 2014. By November 2014, approximately 50 U.S. troops deployed to the baseâ€”the first American personnel there since the 2011 withdrawalâ€”to assess conditions, secure the site, and prepare for advisory operations supporting Iraqi Security Forces (ISF) against ISIS.[30] This revival transformed the dormant facility, originally built as Qadisiyah Airbase, into a forward operating base for coalition logistics, training, and air support amid ISIS attacks on nearby areas.[31] Initial deployments focused on advise-and-assist missions, with U.S. forces embedding with ISF units to rebuild capabilities eroded by ISIS gains. In December 2014, U.S. troops at Al-Asad engaged in the first direct ground clash with ISIS militants attempting to overrun the base, repelling the assault with small arms and mortar fire.[32] The base hosted training for Iraqi personnel in infantry tactics, checkpoint operations, and counter-improvised explosive device measures, with U.S. Marine Corps elements from Task Force Al-Asad, including the 1st Battalion, 7th Marines, providing specialized ground task force support.[33] By early 2015, Al-Asad served as a hub for helicopter and fixed-wing operations enabling coalition airstrikes, contributing to the degradation of ISIS supply lines in western Iraq.[34] From 2015 to 2019, deployments expanded to include rotational Army and Marine units, such as Task Force Dragon, which trained thousands of ISF soldiers in basic combat skills and sustained operations.[35] Task Force Lion, centered at Al-Asad, advised Anbar-based ISF brigades on offensive maneuvers, facilitating the recapture of Ramadi in 2015 and Fallujah in 2016.[36] By 2017, the base supported large-scale training cycles, graduating over 300 Iraqi soldiers in patrol and clearing techniques in a single program, alongside logistics for artillery and special operations forces conducting raids.[37] U.S. troop levels at Al-Asad fluctuated between several hundred and over 2,000 at peak advisory phases, emphasizing capacity-building rather than direct combat, though special operations units occasionally accompanied ISF on missions.[38] This sustained presence enabled the base to function as a linchpin for coalition efforts in Anbar until ISIS territorial caliphate's collapse in 2019.[39]

Contributions to Defeating ISIL

During Operation Inherent Resolve, Al-Asad Airbase served as a primary hub for U.S. and coalition forces advising and assisting Iraqi Security Forces (ISF) in Anbar Province, a critical ISIS stronghold encompassing roughly one-third of Iraq's territory. Task Force Al Asad, established under U.S. Marine Corps command, embedded advisors with Iraqi units to enhance operational planning, intelligence sharing, and fire support coordination, enabling the liberation of key cities. This mission focused on building ISF capacity without direct U.S. combat involvement on the ground, aligning with the coalition's strategy of partnering with local forces to degrade ISIS territorially.[40] In support of the December 2015â€“February 2016 Battle of Ramadi, coalition personnel at Al-Asad provided real-time intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) assets, facilitating over 1,000 airstrikes that targeted ISIS command nodes, vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices, and fighting positions, contributing to ISF recapture of the provincial capital. Similarly, during the Mayâ€“July 2016 Fallujah offensive, advisors from the base coordinated close air support and artillery fires, helping Iraqi forces clear entrenched ISIS defenses in the city, which had served as a major insurgent hub since 2004. These efforts disrupted ISIS supply lines across Anbar, forcing the group to divert resources and personnel.[41][42] Al-Asad also hosted rotary- and fixed-wing aviation units, including AH-64 Apache helicopters and A-10 Thunderbolt II aircraft, which conducted precision strikes against ISIS targets in western Iraq from 2015 to 2017. These operations, integrated with joint terminal attack controllers embedded with ISF, accounted for significant attrition of ISIS fighters and equipment in Anbar, supporting the province's near-complete liberation by late 2017, including the October 2017 clearance of Al-Qa'im near the Syrian border. Overall, the base's logistics and sustainment roles sustained coalition enablers, enabling ISF advances that reduced ISIS-held territory in Iraq from 40% in 2014 to under 3% by 2017, marking a pivotal step in the group's territorial defeat.[40][41]

Security Incidents and Attacks (2020-Present)

2020 Iranian Ballistic Missile Strike

On January 8, 2020, at approximately 1:30 a.m. local time, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) fired 11 short-range ballistic missiles, primarily Qiam-1 variants, targeting Al-Asad Airbase in Iraq's Anbar Province as retaliation for the U.S. drone strike that killed IRGC Quds Force commander Qasem Soleimani five days earlier.[8][43] The attack, dubbed Operation Martyr Soleimani by Iran, also included strikes on a base near Erbil, marking the largest direct ballistic missile assault on U.S. forces abroad up to that point.[44] U.S. and coalition personnel at Al-Asad, numbering around 2,500 troops, had been warned of impending retaliation and took shelter in reinforced bunkers, mitigating potential fatalities from the incoming warheads.[8] The missiles impacted across the base, creating multiple craters and causing structural damage to aircraft hangars, runways, and equipment; assessments later confirmed up to 30 U.S. and coalition aircraft damaged or destroyed, alongside obliteration of living quarters.[8] Iranian state media claimed the strikes precisely hit U.S. command centers and air assets, portraying the operation as a successful demonstration of Tehran's missile capabilities without broader escalation.[45] U.S. officials, including the Department of Defense, reported no immediate deaths or severe injuries, emphasizing the sheltering protocol's effectiveness, though initial damage evaluations downplayed visible destruction to avoid signaling weakness amid tensions. Subsequent medical evaluations revealed the blast overpressure waves from the detonations caused concussive effects, leading to diagnoses of traumatic brain injuries (TBIs) in U.S. service members; the Pentagon progressively updated figures from 34 in late January to 50 by early February, and ultimately 109 by mid-February 2020, with symptoms including headaches, dizziness, and memory issues treated at U.S. facilities in Germany and stateside.[46][47] A 2021 Pentagon review highlighted near-misses that could have killed up to 150 troops had sheltering been delayed, underscoring the strike's intensity despite no fatalities.[8] Iranian assessments, reliant on state-controlled reporting, maintained claims of minimal U.S. evasion through prior evacuation, though independent analyses noted accuracy limitations in the barrage.[44]

Iran-Backed Militia Attacks

Following the 2020 Iranian ballistic missile strike, Al-Asad Airbase has been subjected to repeated assaults by Iran-backed Shia militias, including Kata'ib Hezbollah and other factions under the "Islamic Resistance in Iraq" banner. These groups, integrated into Iraq's Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF) but operating with Tehran's direction and materiel support, have employed improvised rockets, mortars, and loitering munitions in over a dozen documented strikes on the base since 2021. The attacks reflect a strategy of persistent low-level harassment to erode U.S. resolve, inflict psychological and material costs, and signal alignment with Iran's regional proxies amid broader tensions, such as U.S. support for Israel. Defensive systems like C-RAM and electronic warfare have intercepted many projectiles, limiting fatalities, though concussive effects from near-impacts have caused traumatic brain injuries among U.S. personnel.[48][49] Sporadic incidents marked the 2021â€“2023 period, often tied to militia grievances over U.S. presence or retaliatory cycles. On July 7, 2021, approximately 14 rockets struck the base, wounding two individuals but causing no U.S. casualties per official reports. In early January 2022, Al-Asad endured dual hits within days: two intercepted drones on January 4 followed by up to 12 rockets on January 5, with no reported injuries. These preceded a lull, as militias shifted focus amid internal Iraqi politics and U.S. deterrence strikes.[50][51] A sharp escalation followed the October 7, 2023, Hamas assault on Israel, with militias explicitly linking attacks to "solidarity" against perceived U.S. complicity in Gaza operations. Iranian proxies conducted at least 78 assaults on U.S. positions in Iraq alone by mid-2025, using over 145 projectiles, many aimed at Al-Asad as a high-value symbolic target hosting U.S. advisors and aviation assets. US Responses and Force Adjustments

In response to repeated rocket and drone attacks by Iran-backed militias on Al-Asad Airbase, the United States conducted targeted airstrikes against militia facilities in Iraq and Syria. For instance, on March 12, 2020, following a rocket attack on March 11 that killed two American service members and a British soldier at Camp Taji (linked to broader militia threats including Al-Asad), U.S. forces struck five Kata'ib Hezbollah weapons storage and command facilities in Iraq, destroying the sites but leaving more intact depots as acknowledged by U.S. Central Command.[57] Similar retaliatory actions occurred in November 2023, when U.S. airstrikes south of Baghdad killed eight Kata'ib Hezbollah members after drone attacks on U.S. positions, including Al-Asad.[58] In December 2023, further strikes targeted Kata'ib Hezbollah fighters in Iraq in direct response to attacks on Al-Asad earlier that week.[59] These airstrikes aimed to degrade militia capabilities without provoking full-scale escalation with Iran, focusing on Iran-aligned groups responsible for over 150 attacks on U.S. facilities in Iraq and Syria since October 2023. U.S. officials attributed many Al-Asad incidents to Kata'ib Hezbollah's tactics, prompting strikes on border facilities used for cross-border operations.[60] By 2024, the U.S. suspended some operations temporarily amid heightened threats but resumed defensive postures, including intercepts of incoming projectiles.[61] Force adjustments at Al-Asad reflected a broader U.S. drawdown in Iraq amid persistent risks and shifting priorities toward counter-ISIS missions over indefinite presence. Following the January 2020 Iranian missile barrage, which caused traumatic brain injuries to over 100 U.S. personnel but no fatalities, the U.S. enhanced base defenses with additional Patriot and counter-rocket systems, though specific deployments remained classified. By October 2025, U.S. troop levels in Iraq dropped from approximately 2,500 to under 2,000, yet a contingent of at least 250 troops remained at Al-Asad to support anti-ISIS operations and training.[62] Iraqi Prime Minister Mohammed Shia al-Sudani confirmed on October 20, 2025, that 250 to 350 U.S. military advisers and support personnel would stay, including at Al-Asad, reversing earlier partial withdrawal plans from the base.[63] This posture prioritized deterrence against militia resurgence while reducing vulnerability to attacks, as evidenced by sustained U.S. responses to incidents like the August 2024 rocket and drone assaults injuring servicemembers.[64]

Strategic Role and Debates

Geopolitical Significance

Al-Asad Airbase, located in Al Anbar Governorate in western Iraq near the Syrian and Jordanian borders, provides the United States with a critical forward position for projecting power across the Levant and monitoring cross-border threats from Syria.[65] This strategic placement supports intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance operations, enabling rapid responses to insurgent activities and facilitating logistics in the expansive desert region.[6] The base's proximity to key smuggling routes and unstable frontiers enhances its utility in containing spillover from the Syrian civil war and jihadist groups.[66] In the U.S.-Iran rivalry, Al-Asad symbolizes American resolve against Tehran's regional ambitions, serving as a deterrent to Iranian expansion through proxy militias in Iraq.[67] The base endured over a dozen Iranian ballistic missiles on January 8, 2020, in retaliation for the U.S. strike killing Quds Force commander Qasem Soleimani, an attack that inflicted structural damage but no fatalities, highlighting Iran's calibrated escalation tactics to test U.S. commitment without provoking full war.[68] Subsequent drone and rocket assaults by Iran-backed groups, numbering over 100 since 2020, underscore the base's role as a flashpoint, where persistent U.S. presence has empirically constrained militia dominance and prevented ISIS resurgence by maintaining operational tempo.[54][65] The U.S. footprint at Al-Asad bolsters Iraq's federal government against Iranian influence, as evidenced by Baghdad's decision in October 2025 to retain 250-350 American advisers there despite parliamentary calls for expulsion, prioritizing counter-ISIS capabilities and neutrality amid regional tensions.[69][70] This arrangement counters the systemic sway of Iran-aligned factions within Iraq's security apparatus, fostering a balance that empirical data links to reduced militia impunity and stabilized energy corridors.[71] Iraqi airspace, including over Al-Asad, has also aided allied defenses, such as intercepting Iranian drones targeting Israel in 2025, reinforcing the base's indirect contribution to broader Middle Eastern deterrence architectures.[72]

Controversies Over US Presence

The US military presence at Al-Asad Airbase has faced significant controversy since the January 3, 2020, US drone strike that killed Iranian Quds Force commander Qasem Soleimani near Baghdad International Airport, prompting Iran's ballistic missile retaliation against the base on January 8, 2020, which injured over 100 US troops with traumatic brain injuries but caused no deaths.[73] In response, Iraq's parliament, dominated by Shia factions aligned with Iran, passed a non-binding resolution on January 5, 2020, urging the government to expel all foreign troops, including US forces, citing violations of Iraqi sovereignty; however, the resolution lacked quorum due to boycotts by Sunni and Kurdish lawmakers, and the caretaker government under Prime Minister Adel Abdul-Mahdi declined to implement it, as it required formal treaty abrogation under the 2008 US-Iraq Status of Forces Agreement framework.[74][75] US officials maintained that the presence, initially invited by Iraq in 2014 to combat ISIS, remained legally grounded in bilateral security cooperation, with troop levels stabilizing around 2,500 nationwide despite the political pressure.[74] Subsequent controversies intensified with over 170 attacks on US positions in Iraq, including Al-Asad, by Iran-backed militias since October 2023, often using drones and rockets amid the Israel-Hamas conflict, resulting in US troop injuries and prompting retaliatory US strikes that killed militia members but drew accusations of infringing Iraqi sovereignty from pro-Iran politicians.[76][77] Critics in Iraq, including factions within the Coordination Framework alliance, argued that the US presence provokes these Iran-linked groupsâ€”such as Kata'ib Hezbollahâ€”and serves as a liability, exacerbating sectarian tensions and undermining national unity, while US defenders, including Iraqi Prime Minister Mohammed Shia al-Sudani's administration, contended that the bases enable intelligence sharing and training to prevent ISIS resurgence, as evidenced by coalition operations disrupting over 100 ISIS cells in 2024.[78] In the US, policy debates post-ISIS territorial defeat in 2019 questioned the necessity of maintaining fixed bases like Al-Asad, with analysts from intervention-skeptical organizations asserting that the risks from militia attacks outweigh diminishing returns against a degraded ISIS threat now reduced to insurgent tactics, though empirical data on foiled plots underscores a persistent need for partnered advising.[79][80] By 2024, US-Iraq talks culminated in a September agreement to transition from the US-led Global Coalition against ISIS to a bilateral security partnership, initiating a drawdown with US forces vacating Al-Asad by September 2025 alongside Baghdad headquarters, aiming to end the combat mission while preserving advisory roles against ISIS.[81][66] However, on October 20, 2025, Sudani announced a reversal, retaining a "small force" of at least 250 US advisors at Al-Asad specifically to support anti-ISIS operations in Iraq and Syria, citing ongoing threats and Iraqi military capacity gaps; this decision reignited debates, with some Iraqi officials warning of vulnerability to ISIS revival without US enablers, while militia-aligned voices decried it as capitulation to foreign occupation, highlighting the tension between empirical security needs and domestic political pressures from Iran-influenced groups.[82][83] The arrangement underscores causal factors in the controversy: Iraq's fragmented governance, where official bilateral ties clash with extralegal militia actions, perpetuates calls for full withdrawal despite data showing US presence correlating with suppressed ISIS activity.[78]
