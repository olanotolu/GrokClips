Italexit (political party)


Italexit for Italy (Italian: Italexit per l'Italia), commonly referred to as Italexit, is a sovereignist political party in Italy dedicated to achieving the country's withdrawal from the European Union and the eurozone.[1][2]
Founded on 21 July 2020 by Gianluigi Paragone, a former television journalist and independent senator previously affiliated with the Five Star Movement, the party emerged amid growing dissatisfaction with EU policies and Italy's economic constraints within the bloc.[2][1]
Italexit promotes referendums on EU membership and constitutional amendments to restore full national sovereignty, including revisions to articles addressing international constraints and military neutrality, positioning Italy outside alliances like NATO.[3][4]
In the 2022 general election, the party garnered 534,579 votes, equivalent to 1.9% of the national total, but did not obtain any parliamentary seats due to Italy's electoral threshold.[5]
As of 2025, Italexit continues to advance popular initiatives, including a published call in the Gazzetta Ufficiale for referendums on EU exit and neutrality reforms, reflecting persistent grassroots efforts to challenge supranational integration.[4][6]

Origins and Historical Development

Founding and Initial Launch (2020)

Gianluigi Paragone, a former journalist and senator elected with the Five Star Movement (M5S), founded Italexit in July 2020 following his expulsion from the M5S parliamentary group in November 2019 for opposing the coalition government's budget aligned with pro-EU policies.[2] The party emerged from Paragone's disillusionment with the M5S's shift toward European integration after forming a government with the Democratic Party (PD) in 2019, which he criticized as a betrayal of the movement's original anti-establishment and sovereignty-focused principles.[1] On July 23, 2020, Paragone formally presented Italexit as a vehicle to replicate the United Kingdom's Brexit by advocating Italy's withdrawal from the European Union and the Eurozone, describing the EU as a "Germanic union" that imposed austerity and eroded national sovereignty.[7] Initially named "Italexit con Paragone," the movement positioned itself as the sole Italian party explicitly committed to an EU exit agenda, drawing on populist rhetoric against supranational constraints on Italian fiscal and monetary policy.[2] Paragone, who had gained visibility through his Senate interrogations of EU officials and opposition to fiscal rules, leveraged his media background to promote the launch via public announcements and alignment with figures like Brexit Party leader Nigel Farage, whom he met shortly before the formal unveiling.[1]

Evolution from M5S Disillusionment

Gianluigi Paragone, a senator elected in 2018 under the Five Star Movement (M5S) banner, became a prominent critic of the party's ideological shift following its August 2019 coalition breakdown with the Eurosceptic League and subsequent alliance with the pro-European Democratic Party (PD). This realignment, formalized in September 2019, prioritized stability and EU-compliant policies over the original anti-establishment and sovereignist rhetoric that had propelled M5S to power, alienating figures like Paragone who advocated for greater national control over monetary and fiscal decisions.[2] Paragone's opposition culminated in his expulsion from M5S on January 2, 2020, after he voted against the government's 2020 budget bill, which he viewed as capitulating to EU fiscal constraints and betraying M5S's populist roots. The budget vote represented a confidence measure for the Conte II cabinet, and Paragone's dissent highlighted fractures within M5S between its pragmatic leadership, led by Giuseppe Conte, and a dissenting Eurosceptic faction resistant to deeper European integration. His removal reduced M5S's Senate seats and underscored the party's internal purge of voices challenging its pivot toward centrist governance.[8][7] This disillusionment extended beyond Paragone, as M5S's February 2021 endorsement of Mario Draghi's national unity governmentâ€”characterized by strong pro-EU technocratic policiesâ€”further eroded support among former adherents prioritizing economic sovereignty and anti-austerity stances. Paragone channeled this sentiment into Italexit's July 2020 launch, positioning the party as a vehicle for the unfulfilled Eurosceptic promises of early M5S, drawing initial backing from ex-M5S sympathizers who saw the parent party's evolution as a dilution of its founding principles against supranational overreach.[1]

Key Milestones Post-2020

In June 2022, Italexit convened its inaugural national congress in Rome, where on 26 June delegates formalized the party's organizational structure and approved its political platform, emphasizing Euroscepticism, national sovereignty, and opposition to supranational mandates.[9] [10] Gianluigi Paragone was unanimously elected as national secretary during the event, solidifying his leadership amid the party's expansion from its initial base of M5S defectors.[11] On 29 December 2023, Paragone tendered his irrevocable resignation as secretary, announcing in a public letter that the party's evolution had deviated from its founding principles as a platform of dissent, particularly in aligning with external influences that compromised its independence.[12] [13] He cited a duty of transparency toward supporters and his media roles, framing the departure as a principled exit from active politics rather than endorsement of the party's trajectory under new management.[14] This leadership vacuum prompted internal fragmentation, though no unified successor structure emerged immediately from the resignation.[15]

Leadership and Internal Organization

Gianluigi Paragone's Role and Background

Gianluigi Paragone, born on August 7, 1971, in Varese, Italy, began his professional career as a journalist and television presenter.[16] He gained prominence hosting the program La gabbia on the La7 network, where he frequently critiqued political and economic establishments, establishing a reputation for populist and anti-establishment commentary.[17] Paragone entered politics by joining the Five Star Movement (M5S) in 2017 and was elected to the Senate of the Republic in the March 4, 2018, general election. His tenure involved vocal opposition to the M5S's shift toward pro-European Union policies following the formation of the Conte II Cabinet in September 2019, which allied M5S with the Democratic Party. On December 30, 2019, he voted against the government's 2020 budget, prompting the M5S to expel him from the party on January 2, 2020, with the expulsion confirmed by the party's arbitration panel on January 17, 2020.[17][18] Following his expulsion, Paragone founded Italexit on July 23, 2020, positioning it as a vehicle to advocate for Italy's withdrawal from the European Union and the eurozone, drawing inspiration from the Brexit model after consulting with Nigel Farage.[1][2] As the party's founder and national secretary, he led Italexit through its initial electoral campaigns, emphasizing economic sovereignty and criticism of supranational institutions. Paragone resigned irrevocably from the secretary position on December 29, 2023, citing the need for fresh leadership within the party.[19][12]

Party Structure and Membership Dynamics

Italexit per l'Italia maintains a hierarchical structure centered on a national secretariat, supported by a Direzione Nazionale responsible for strategic decisions and appointing interim leadership bodies. Following Gianluigi Paragone's resignation as national secretary on February 14, 2024, the Direzione established a Consiglio di Reggenza to oversee party operations, comprising Andrea Perillo, Antonio IracÃ , and Roberto Robilotta.[20][21] This collective body handles day-to-day governance, emphasizing territorial reorganization and legal defenses of the party symbol, as demonstrated in April 2024 recourse filings against electoral exclusions.[21] Membership dynamics exhibit volatility, with internal disputes prompting key departures that highlight tensions in a nascent organization. In September 2024, Liguria regional coordinator Ilario Chiera withdrew his candidacy for regional elections and resigned, citing irreconcilable disagreements with two Consiglio di Reggenza members over organizational and political management.[22] Such incidents underscore challenges in coordinating regional efforts, particularly as the party prioritizes grassroots reactivation post-leadership transition without publicly disclosed membership figures.[23] The structure supports flexible territorial coordinators, but limited scale constrains broader enrollment, focusing recruitment on Eurosceptic activists amid electoral preparations.

Core Ideology and Policy Platform

Euroscepticism and the Italexit Agenda

Italexit's Euroscepticism stems from the conviction that the European Union's structure erodes Italy's sovereignty by subordinating national decision-making to unelected supranational bodies, particularly in monetary policy and fiscal rules. The party contends that the euro currency prevents Italy from implementing devaluations or tailored interest rates needed to address structural economic imbalances, such as high public debt exceeding 140% of GDP as of 2020, which EU treaties constrain through stability pacts.[7][24] The core of the Italexit agenda is an orderly withdrawal from both the EU and the eurozone, inspired by Brexit, to restore full control over borders, budget, and legislation. Proponents argue this would enable Italy to negotiate trade deals bilaterally, avoiding what they describe as the EU's bureaucratic inefficiencies and unequal burden-sharing, evidenced by Italy's net contributions to the EU budget totaling â‚¬3.7 billion annually pre-2020.[2][1] To achieve this, Italexit pursues a referendum on EU exit, formalized through a 2025 constitutional initiative amending Articles 11, 75, 117, and 138 of the Italian Constitution to prioritize national law over EU directives and facilitate binding referenda on international commitments. This effort, published in the Gazzetta Ufficiale on April 18, 2025, seeks to override the current legal precedence of EU law, which the party views as an undemocratic transfer of authority ratified without sufficient public consent.[6][3] Criticisms extend to the EU's migration policies, which Italexit claims facilitate uncontrolled inflows by limiting Italy's border enforcement, and recovery mechanisms like the â‚¬750 billion NextGenerationEU fund, portrayed as loans disguised as grants that increase dependency without addressing root causes of stagnation. The agenda emphasizes empirical precedents, such as post-Brexit UK adjustments, to counter narratives of inevitable economic collapse, prioritizing causal links between sovereignty loss and Italy's 0.3% average GDP growth from 2010-2019 under euro constraints.[25][26]

Economic Sovereignty and Anti-Euro Stance

Italexit positions economic sovereignty as central to its platform, advocating Italy's withdrawal from the eurozone to regain control over monetary policy and fiscal decisions constrained by EU rules. The party contends that the euro's fixed exchange rate regime has eroded Italy's competitiveness by preventing currency devaluation, a tool historically used to address trade imbalances and stimulate exports, leading to persistent low growth and high unemployment since its adoption in 1999.[27][28] Leader Gianluigi Paragone has criticized the euro as a mechanism that favors northern European economies, particularly Germany, at the expense of southern members like Italy, imposing austerity measures that stifle demand and public investment. In a 2020 interview, Paragone argued that EU membership facilitates resource extraction from Italy while suppressing wages, and endorsed modern monetary theory principles to enable full employment through sovereign currency issuance without inflationary risks post-exit.[27][7] The party's 2022 electoral program outlines a managed euro exit, overseen by technical experts, to restore national currency control and dismantle EU fiscal pacts like the Stability and Growth Pact, which cap deficits at 3% of GDP and debt at 60%. This would allow expanded public spending on infrastructure, healthcare, and small businesses, alongside tax reductions such as abolishing the cuneo fiscale (labor tax wedge) and introducing family deductions, aiming to boost domestic demand and protect "Made in Italy" sectors via tariffs and state aid.[28][29] Italexit frames the euro as a "destructive" force, supported by internal analyses showing Italy's industrial decline and public debt exceeding 150% of GDP under euro constraints, contrasting it with pre-euro flexibility that permitted tailored responses to recessions. Post-sovereignty measures include debt restructuring for small taxpayers, pension hikes to a minimum of â‚¬850 monthly, and opposition to digital currencies to preserve cash-based transactions up to â‚¬5,000, prioritizing economic autonomy over supranational integration.[28][27]

Positions on NATO, Immigration, and National Security

Italexit advocates Italy's immediate withdrawal from NATO, criticizing the alliance as a U.S.-dominated structure that subordinates national interests to foreign agendas. The party proposes repositioning Italy as a neutral power, akin to the "Switzerland of the Mediterranean," to prioritize independent foreign policy and avoid entanglement in extraterritorial conflicts.[30][31] This stance extends to opposition against arms shipments to Ukraine, favoring diplomatic negotiations and cessation of sanctions on Russia to foster peace without Italian military involvement.[30] Regarding immigration, Italexit rejects policies of unrestricted entry, linking uncontrolled inflowsâ€”particularly illegal migrationâ€”to elevated crime rates and social strain. The party's platform demands halting migrant departures from North African shores via a 50,000-strong international peacekeeping force coordinated under United Nations oversight and the Libyan government, emphasizing border sovereignty over EU-driven relocation quotas.[30][31] It criticizes supranational frameworks for exacerbating exploitation and security risks, advocating repatriation mechanisms and rejection of "welcome at all costs" approaches that undermine domestic welfare.[31] On national security, Italexit prioritizes internal reinforcement through urgent recruitment of law enforcement personnel and investments in surveillance technologies to combat foreign-influenced crime, including stricter regulations on immigrant-operated businesses and monitoring of high-risk zones.[30][31] The platform underscores reclaiming full control over defense and intelligence from international bodies, aligning security measures with restored monetary and territorial sovereignty to address threats like organized crime tied to migration routes.[31] This reflects a broader commitment to non-interventionist diplomacy, exiting supranational pacts that dilute Italian autonomy in threat response.[30]

Electoral History and Performance

2022 General Election Results

In the Italian general election on 25 September 2022, Italexit contested both the Chamber of Deputies and Senate as an independent list, without entering coalitions. The party failed to meet the 3% national threshold for proportional representation seats, securing no parliamentary representation.[32][33]

Italexit's performance reflected limited national appeal amid a fragmented electorate, where centre-right coalitions dominated with over 40% of votes, but the party's Eurosceptic platform drew insufficient support to breach entry barriers.[32][33] Pre-election polls had projected under 3%, aligning with the outcome, though party leader Gianluigi Paragone had targeted erosion of votes from larger right-wing lists like Fratelli d'Italia.[34]

European Parliament Elections (2024)

Italexit contested the 2024 European Parliament elections on June 8 and 9, aligning with the LibertÃ  electoral list, a coalition comprising multiple minor sovereignist and populist formations. This list, spearheaded by Cateno De Luca, incorporated Italexit's platform emphasizing Italy's withdrawal from the European Union, alongside demands for national sovereignty and opposition to supranational fiscal policies.[35][36] The LibertÃ  list garnered 1.22% of the valid votes nationwide, totaling approximately 284,000 ballots out of over 23 million cast, failing to surpass the 4% electoral threshold for proportional seat allocation in Italy's 76-delegate delegation.[35][37] Consequently, no seats were secured, reflecting Italexit's marginal electoral footprint amid a broader rightward shift where established Eurosceptic parties like Lega and Fratelli d'Italia captured significant representation. Voter turnout stood at 49.69%, with LibertÃ 's support concentrated in southern regions but insufficient to translate into parliamentary presence.[35]

Local and Regional Engagements

Italexit has maintained a limited presence in local and regional elections, prioritizing national and European campaigns amid its resource constraints as a smaller party. In municipal elections, the party has fielded candidates in select urban centers, often achieving vote shares below thresholds for representation. For instance, during the 2021 Milan municipal elections, Italexit garnered 2.99% of the valid votes, narrowly missing the 3% threshold required for seats in the city council.[38] Party leader Gianluigi Paragone, who received 14,366 personal votes as a mayoral candidate, publicly alleged potential irregularities, claiming the tally had briefly exceeded 3% during scrutiny before finalizing lower, though no formal challenges succeeded.[38] This performance exceeded that of the Five Star Movement's 2.7% in the same contest.[38] Further local engagements included participation in the 2021 Bologna administrative elections under a civic list alliance, Lista Civica per Bologna-Italexit, where preference votes were recorded but did not yield council seats.[39] In Asti during the 2022 administrative elections, Italexit nominated Chiara Chirio as its mayoral candidate, emphasizing grassroots issues like amplifying marginalized voices, though specific vote outcomes remained marginal and insufficient for victory.[40] By 2023, amid administrative contests in various municipalities, Italexit pursued tactical alliances, endorsing Fratelli d'Italia candidates in several cities while securing placements on their lists in return, reflecting pragmatic efforts to expand local influence without independent dominance.[41] At the regional level, Italexit's activities have been even more nascent, with announcements of candidacies often hampered by internal discord. In August 2024, the party declared intentions to contest upcoming regional elections in Emilia-Romagna, Liguria, and Umbria, aiming to promote its Eurosceptic platform territorially.[42] However, challenges emerged, including the September 2024 withdrawal of Liguria's regional coordinator due to disagreements with party leadership, which derailed planned candidacies there.[22] No regional council seats have been secured to date, underscoring the party's struggles to translate national messaging into subnational electoral success amid competition from established right-wing coalitions.[22]

Reception, Impact, and Controversies

Achievements and Supporter Base

Italexit's primary achievements include advancing legislative efforts to facilitate a potential referendum on Italy's exit from the European Union. On April 18, 2025, two Constitutional Popular Initiative Laws promoted by the party were published in the Gazzetta Ufficiale (issues 91), one seeking a referendum on Italexit and the other proposing amendments to Articles 11, 75, 117, and 138 of the Italian Constitution to enable withdrawal from international constraints like EU treaties.[43][44] These initiatives represent the party's most notable push toward institutional mechanisms for sovereignty restoration, though their success depends on collecting required signatures and parliamentary approval.[3] The party has also gained visibility through its founder's prior parliamentary experience and media outreach, including Gianluigi Paragone's 2020 meeting with Brexit advocate Nigel Farage ahead of the launch, which amplified Italexit's profile among international Eurosceptics.[45] However, internal challenges, such as Paragone's departure in December 2023 citing limited space within the center-right spectrum, have tested organizational stability without derailing core advocacy.[46] Italexit's supporter base comprises a niche of committed sovereignists, predominantly drawn from disaffected voters of former populist parties like the Five Star Movement and the League, who view EU membership as a barrier to fiscal autonomy and national decision-making.[2] This group emphasizes opposition to the eurozone's constraints and NATO commitments, reflecting broader sentiments among those prioritizing empirical critiques of supranational governance over integrated European structures. Specific demographic data remains sparse, but support aligns with regions and cohorts expressing economic grievances tied to EU policies, as seen in rising polls for exit options post-Brexit.[47] The base's dedication is evident in sustained advocacy efforts despite electoral hurdles, fostering a dedicated cadre focused on causal links between EU integration and Italy's stagnating growth.

Debates on Feasibility of Italexit and Empirical Counterarguments

Critics of Italexit argue that Italy's departure from the European Union and eurozone would entail severe economic disruptions, far exceeding those of Brexit due to Italy's monetary union membership and fiscal vulnerabilities. Unlike the United Kingdom, which retained its currency, Italy's exit would require abandoning the euro, potentially triggering a sovereign debt crisis amid its public debt ratio of 135.3% of GDP in 2024.[56] [57] Economists Lorenzo Codogno and Giampaolo Galli have described such a scenario as catastrophic, projecting incommensurable long-term economic, social, and political costs from capital flight, banking instability, and loss of ECB backstopping.[58] A core empirical counterargument centers on Italy's deep trade integration with the EU, where European countries absorb over half of its exportsâ€”Germany alone at 11.5%, France at 10.3%, and Spain at 5.3% of total exports in 2023, totaling $651 billion in goods shipped abroad.[59] Reimposed tariffs and non-tariff barriers post-Italexit would erode competitiveness in key sectors like machinery and vehicles, mirroring Brexit's effects where UK goods exports remain 30% below counterfactual levels without single market access.[60] Spatial econometric models simulating Italexit predict bilateral job shortfalls, with Italian regions facing heightened unemployment from reduced EU demand, compounded by supply chain disruptions.[61] Monetary sovereignty advocates claim Italexit could enable devaluation to boost exports and inflate away debt, but causal analysis reveals risks of hyperinflation and creditor lawsuits, as Italy holds much domestic-held sovereign bonds vulnerable to lira depreciation.[48] As a net EU budget contributorâ€”remitting approximately â‚¬6 billion more annually than receivedâ€”Italy might recoup direct payments, yet broader single market gains outweigh this, with post-Brexit UK evidence showing net GDP reductions from trade frictions and investment deterrence.[62] [63] No treaty mechanism exists for orderly euro exit, amplifying default risks akin to Greece's 2010s crisis but scaled to Italy's â‚¬2.8 trillion debt stock.[53] Public opinion and political feasibility further undermine Italexit, with surveys indicating low support for exit amid awareness of Brexit's underperformanceâ€”only one in five Leave-equivalent voters viewing it positively.[63] While Eurosceptics prioritize sovereignty over quantified losses, data-driven assessments from institutions like Brookings emphasize that sustained uncertainty alone hampers growth, as seen in Italy's pre-crisis bond spreads exceeding 300 basis points.[48] Mainstream economic consensus, despite potential institutional biases toward integration, aligns with observable post-Brexit metrics: diminished FDI, higher import costs, and forgone productivity from regulatory divergence.[64]
