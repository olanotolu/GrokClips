New Paltz, New York

New Paltz (locally /ˈnuː pɔːlz/) is an incorporated U.S. town in Ulster County, New York. The population was 14,407 at the 2020 census. The town is located in the southeastern part of the county and is south of Kingston. New Paltz contains a village, also with the name New Paltz. The town is named for Palz ([ˈpalts]), the dialect name of the Palatinate, called Pfalz (IPA: [ˈpfalts]) in standard German. Due to the presence of what is now the State University of New York at New Paltz, it has been a college town for over 150 years.

New Paltz

History

The town of New Paltz was founded in 1678 by French Huguenots by both patent from the governor and purchase from the local Esopus tribe of the Lenape people. Prior to the purchase of New Paltz during the 17th century, the Esopus tribe had been pressured off much of their land which is now present day Ulster and Sullivan counties, because of conflicts known as the Esopus Wars. As a result, the Esopus were willing to exchange the land for a hefty sum of goods. [1] The Huguenots were religious refugees from France who had immigrated via Mannheim in the German Palatinate, where they had settled after fleeing France during religious persecution. They settled in the area of the present-day village of New Paltz (on what is now known as Huguenot Street Historic District) and established their own local government. [2] The size of the town increased with annexation from surrounding regions in 1775 and 1809. In 1842, part of New Paltz was annexed to the Town of Esopus. [3] More of New Paltz was removed in order to form the towns of Rosendale (1844), Lloyd (1845), and Gardiner (1853).

Geography

The Wallkill River flows northward through New Paltz on its way to join the Rondout Creek, which in turn feeds into the Hudson River. A portion of the Shawangunk Ridge is in the town. According to the United States Census Bureau, the town has a total area of 34.31 square miles (88.9 km2), of which 33.88 square miles (87.7 km2) is land and 0.43 square miles (1.1 km2) (1.25%) is water.

Transportation

New Paltz is accessible by a number of different roads. Interstate 87 (the New York State Thruway) is the main thoroughfare and passes through in the eastern part and houses Exit 18 for Route 299. State routes that traverse through are Route 32, Route 208, and Route 299. The nearest train station is about 11 miles (18 km) away, in Poughkeepsie. Both Amtrak and Metro-North trains serve Poughkeepsie. Adirondack Trailways also provides bus transportation from New Paltz into various locations across multiple states.[[4]] Local bus service is available via Ulster County Area Transit (UCAT), including the UPL route connecting to the Poughkeepsie train station.[5] The nearest airport is Stewart International Airport (SWF) in Newburgh, approximately 23 miles (37 km) south.[6] The western terminus of the Hudson Valley Rail Trail is located here. The hiking/biking path eventually crosses the Walkway over the Hudson and becomes the Dutchess Rail Trail that ends in Hopewell Junction.

Education

New Paltz has four public schools (K-12) and is home to a college in the SUNY system:
  • Duzine Elementary School (K-2)
  • Lenape Elementary School (3-5)
  • New Paltz Middle School (6-8)
  • New Paltz High School (9-12)
  • State University of New York at New Paltz
The town is also home to several private schools, including Mountain Laurel Waldorf School (pre-K through grade 8), Montessori of New Paltz (prekindergarten through 6th grade), and Huguenot Street Cooperative Nursery School (pre-K).

Government

The town is governed by a town board consisting of a supervisor and four council members elected at-large. As of October 2025, the supervisor is Amanda Gotto (term ending December 2025), and the council members are Kitty Brown (deputy supervisor), Esi Lewis, Edgar Rodriguez, and Randall Leverette.[7] There is also a village of New Paltz within the town, governed by a mayor and a board of trustees. As of October 2025, the mayor is Tim Rogers (term ending 2027), deputy mayor is Alexandria Wojcik, and the trustees are William Wheeler-Murray, Stana Tomaszewska-Kozub, and McKenna Kelly.[8] In early 2025, the village board initiated a study on the potential dissolution of the village government and its merger with the town to streamline administration and services. Discussions and public hearings on this proposal are ongoing as of October 2025.[9]

2010 census

As of the 2010 census, the population was 14,003. The racial makeup of the town was 84.72% White, 5.35% Black or African American, 0.31% Native American, 4.36% Asian, 0.04% Pacific Islander, 2.46% from other races, and 2.76% from two or more races. Hispanic or Latino of any race were 8.81% of the population.

2020 census

As of the 2020 census, the population was 14,407. The racial makeup of the town was 74.01% White, 5.72% Black or African American, 0.26% Native American, 5.46% Asian, 0.06% Pacific Islander, 5.11% from other races, and 8.67% from two or more races. Hispanic or Latino of any race were 12.74% of the population.

Historic places

  • Huguenot Street Historic District, a National Historic Landmark District featuring stone houses built by early Huguenot settlers in the late 17th and early 18th centuries.
  • Elting Memorial Library, listed on the National Register of Historic Places since 2004, originally built in 1920 as a memorial library.
  • Mohonk Mountain House, a National Historic Landmark resort opened in 1869, located at the junction of New Paltz, Marbletown, and Rochester towns.
