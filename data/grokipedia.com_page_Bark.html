Bark

Bark is the outermost layer of stems and roots in woody plants, encompassing all tissues exterior to the vascular cambium and distinguishing it from the inner wood.[1] It comprises the inner bark, primarily phloem that conducts sugars and nutrients from leaves to other plant parts, and the outer bark, including protective periderm layers such as cork.[2][3] The outer bark renews continuously to shield against environmental stressors like moisture ingress, mechanical injury, insects, pathogens, and fire.[4] Bark morphologyâ€”ranging from thin and smooth in young trees to thick, fissured, or scaly in mature onesâ€”varies by species and environmental adaptation, often serving as a key trait for botanical identification.[5] While primarily protective, certain barks support secondary functions like water storage, limited photosynthesis in young tissues, and microhabitats for epiphytic organisms.[6] Economically, bark has been harvested for materials such as cork from Quercus suber and tannins for leather processing, though sustainable practices are essential to avoid damaging vascular tissues.[7]

Biological meanings

Tree bark

Tree bark constitutes all tissues external to the vascular cambium in stems and roots of woody plants, serving primarily as a protective barrier while facilitating secondary functions such as nutrient transport.[8] It develops through secondary growth, where the cork cambium (phellogen) produces the periderm, replacing the epidermis as the tree matures.[9] Bark thickness varies widely, from less than 1 mm in young saplings to over 30 cm in mature giant sequoias, influenced by species, age, and environmental pressures.[10] Structurally, bark divides into inner (living) and outer (non-living) components. The inner bark, or secondary phloem, consists of sieve tubes, companion cells, fibers, and parenchyma, enabling the downward transport of photosynthates from leaves to roots and storage tissues.[11] As phloem ages and lignifies, it contributes to the outer bark, which comprises dead cells, including the rhytidomeâ€”a layered accumulation of sloughed periderm and crushed phloem.[9] The periderm features phellem (cork cells impregnated with suberin, a hydrophobic polymer that impedes water loss and gas exchange) outward and phelloderm inward, forming a barrier against mechanical injury and invasion.[9] Bark also includes dilating tissues like sclereids and stone cells that accommodate stem expansion without cracking.[8] Bark's primary function is protection, shielding the cambium and vascular tissues from herbivores, pathogens, desiccation, and fire; for instance, thick, fibrous bark in fire-adapted species like pines insulates against lethal heat, with studies showing bark up to 10 cm thick preventing cambial temperatures exceeding 60Â°C during surface fires.[12] [13] It deters fungal and insect attacks via chemical defenses, such as tannins and resins, and regulates gas exchange through lenticelsâ€”porous regions in the periderm.[14] Ecologically, bark traits exhibit tradeoffs: thinner bark in shaded, moist environments prioritizes flexibility and photosynthate conduction, while thicker, rougher bark in exposed or arid habitats enhances durability but increases self-shading and hydraulic resistance.[8] In evolutionary terms, bark thickness correlates with habitat flammability, with thicker bark evolving independently in lineages facing frequent fires, as evidenced by comparative analyses across tropical clades.[15] Bark morphology diversifies across species for adaptive reasons; smooth bark, as in beeches, sheds parasites via exfoliation, whereas furrowed or plated bark in oaks provides mechanical strength and fire resistance.[10] Damage to bark, such as from girdling, disrupts phloem flow and can kill the tree by starving roots, underscoring its causal role in vascular integrity.[16] While bark is mostly non-photosynthetic, some species like eucalypts feature chlorophyllous inner bark contributing minimally to carbon fixation under stress.[8]

Animal vocalizations

A bark is defined as a brief, explosive vocalization produced by canines, typically lasting less than one second, with a frequency range spanning 200 to 6000 Hz and an average fundamental frequency around 650 Hz, often featuring broadband noise and segmented structure.[17] This sound is most prominently associated with domestic dogs (Canis familiaris), where it serves multiple communicative functions, including alerting to potential threats, territorial defense, seeking attention, expressing excitement or frustration, and facilitating social interaction with humans or conspecifics.[18] [19] In dogs, bark acousticsâ€”such as pitch, tonality, duration, and inter-bark intervalsâ€”encode emotional states; for instance, higher-pitched, repetitive barks often signal playfulness or urgency, while lower-pitched, harsher ones indicate aggression or fear, allowing both dogs and humans to infer context with reasonable accuracy.[20] [21] Among wild canines, barking occurs in species like wolves (Canis lupus), coyotes (Canis latrans), foxes, and jackals, though less frequently than in domesticated dogs, primarily for alarm signaling or intra-species coordination rather than human-directed communication.[18] Domestication has amplified barking in dogs, with selective breeding favoring vocal individuals for roles like guarding or herding, resulting in breeds varying in bark rate and intensity; for example, smaller breeds tend to produce higher-frequency barks correlated with perceived body size cues.[22] Barking frequency can exceed 200 instances per hour in some contexts, such as isolation or resource guarding, and serves to relieve tension or deter intruders by amplifying perceived threat.[23] [24] Beyond canines, bark-like vocalizationsâ€”defined acoustically as short, sharp burstsâ€”appear in diverse taxa for anti-predator alarm or territorial purposes, including muntjac deer (Muntiacus spp., known as "barking deer"), baboons, certain monkeys, rodents, and even some birds like macaws, though these differ in spectral qualities from canine barks and lack the domesticated dog's contextual versatility.[25] In seals and some primates, analogous sharp calls function similarly for group alerting, underscoring barking's evolutionary role in rapid threat dissemination across species.[25] Human interpretation of these sounds is influenced by familiarity, with dog barks rated more emotionally informative due to co-evolutionary pressures, whereas wild animal barks are often perceived generically as distress signals.[20]

Nautical and technical uses

Sailing vessel

A bark is a type of sailing vessel featuring three or more masts, with the foremast and mainmast rigged square and the mizzenmast (and any additional after masts) rigged fore-and-aft.[26][27] The term derives from the Latin barca, meaning barge, and the spelling "bark" predominates in American English, while "barque" is used in British and other variants.[26] The square rigging on the forward masts consists of sails set perpendicular to the mast, enabling efficient downwind and beam-reach sailing, whereas the fore-and-aft sails on the mizzenâ€”typically gaff or lug typesâ€”improve maneuverability, particularly when tacking or sailing close to the wind.[26][27] This hybrid configuration distinguishes the bark from a full-rigged ship, which has square sails on all masts, and from a barquentine, which squares only the foremast.[27] Barks often carried multiple tiers of square sails on the fore and main masts, including courses, topsails, topgallants, and royals, allowing for substantial sail area optimized for long ocean passages.[27] Historically, barks emerged in the Mediterranean as small, oar-assisted vessels with square sails before evolving into larger ocean-going cargo carriers by the 15th century, particularly in Portugal for transatlantic trade.[26] The rig gained prominence in the 19th century, becoming common after 1860 for merchant voyages, with some designs featuring up to five masts for increased capacity; wooden three-masted barks were prevalent in ports like San Francisco during the mid-1800s, while steel four-masted versions served as bulk cargo haulers into the early 20th century.[27] Their design offered advantages over full-rigged ships by requiring fewer crew members to handle the mizzenmast sails and providing better upwind performance due to the fore-and-aft rigging, which reduced the labor-intensive bracing of square yards on all masts.[28][26] Notable examples include HMS Endeavour, a coal bark refitted for Captain James Cook's 1768â€“1771 circumnavigation, valued for its shallow draft enabling landings in remote areas for repairs.[28] The Star of India, launched in 1863 as a three-masted iron-hulled bark, remains the oldest active sailing vessel globally and exemplifies the type's durability in emigrant and cargo trades.[29] Barks like the 19th-century Dunlap of Bangor, Maine, highlight their role in North American timber and general commerce.[26]

Other technical applications

In psychoacoustics and audio engineering, "bark" refers to a unit of frequency interval on the bark scale, which models the critical bands of human auditory perception where sounds of similar frequencies interfere most strongly. The scale divides the audible frequency rangeâ€”roughly 20 Hz to 16 kHzâ€”into 24 to 25 critical bands, each one bark wide, based on empirical data from psychoacoustic experiments measuring masking thresholds and frequency selectivity. This nonlinear scale compresses higher frequencies logarithmically to reflect the basilar membrane's uneven resolution in the cochlea, with band widths increasing from about 100 Hz at low frequencies to over 3 kHz at high ones. The bark scale was developed by Eberhard Zwicker in 1961, drawing on earlier work including subjective pitch scaling.[30][31] The bark scale finds applications in perceptual audio coding, where it informs bit allocation in codecs like MP3 and AAC by prioritizing quantization in perceptually salient bands, reducing data rates while minimizing audible distortionâ€” for example, by exploiting that imperfections below masking thresholds go unnoticed. Linguistic and behavioral senses

Verb forms and expressions

The verb bark refers to the emission of a sharp, explosive sound, originally imitating the cry of dogs or similar animals, with roots in Old English beorcan, from Proto-Germanic *berkanan denoting bleating or braying.[web:39][35] In human usage, it describes speaking or shouting in a curt, harsh, or abrupt manner, a sense attested from the mid-17th century.[web:39][35] Conjugations follow standard English patterns: present barks, past barked, present participle barking.[web:40][36] Idiomatic expressions often draw from canine behavior to convey human actions or traits. To bark up the wrong tree means to pursue a misguided course or direct efforts at an incorrect target, originating in 19th-century American hunting contexts where dogs would mistakenly bay at a tree without prey, first recorded around 1832.[web:26][37] His (or her) bark is worse than his (or her) bite describes a person who appears more threatening through verbal aggression than they prove in action, with roots in early 19th-century British usage comparing loud warnings to actual harm.[web:38][38] A variant, all bark and no bite, similarly emphasizes bluster without substance.[web:34][39] Other phrases include to bark orders, denoting the issuance of commands in a loud, peremptory tone, evoking a dog's assertive vocalizations.[web:59][40] To bark at the moon signifies futile or ineffective protest, recorded from the 17th century as vain outcry akin to a dog's howling.[web:5][41] British slang barking mad (or simply barking) denotes extreme irrationality or insanity, emerging in the early 20th century, possibly alluding to frenzied dog sounds rather than any specific locale like the London suburb of Barking, despite folk etymologies linking it to asylums there.[web:50][42] These idioms leverage the verb's auditory connotation to illustrate misguided effort, empty threats, or disordered behavior.

Human communication contexts

In English, the verb "to bark" describes speaking or shouting in a loud, harsh, or abrupt manner, often conveying irritation, authority, or urgency. This usage extends the onomatopoeic term for a dog's sharp vocalization to human utterance, implying a tone that is peremptory and lacking nuance. For instance, military commanders or supervisors may "bark orders" to subordinates, prioritizing rapid compliance over deliberation.[36][43] The term originates from Old English beorcan, meaning to emit a dog's cry, with the human sense emerging by the Middle English period as a metaphorical application to vocal harshness. By the 19th century, dictionaries like the American Heritage Dictionary formalized it as "to utter in a loud, harsh voice," as in a quarterback "barking out signals" during a game. This linguistic evolution reflects causal links between animal sounds and human mimicry in expressive speech, where phonetic similarity evokes immediacy or threat without requiring empirical validation beyond historical attestation.[35][44] In interpersonal and professional contexts, barking correlates with dominance signaling, potentially escalating conflicts due to its perceived aggressiveness, though direct psychological studies on its efficacy remain sparse compared to tonal analyses of animal barks. Anecdotal evidence from communication guides suggests it undermines rapport in civilian settings, such as parenting or sales, by triggering defensiveness rather than cooperation, but lacks controlled trials quantifying outcomes like compliance rates. Reputable style manuals advise against it for clarity, favoring measured articulation to align with evidence-based principles of persuasive discourse.[45][46] Idiomatically, phrases like "barking up the wrong tree" illustrate misdirected communication, originating from hunting dogs' erroneous alerts and applied to human error in addressing issues, as documented in 19th-century American English. This usage underscores bark's role in framing futile or misguided verbal efforts, with no verifiable link to improved resolution but utility in self-corrective rhetoric.[44]

Cultural and commercial applications

Food and cuisine

Cinnamon, obtained from the dried inner bark of Cinnamomum verum (Ceylon cinnamon) or Cinnamomum cassia trees, ranks among the oldest documented spices, with archaeological evidence of its use in ancient Egyptian culinary and embalming practices dating to approximately 2000 BCE.[47] In modern cuisine, it serves as a versatile flavoring in both sweet applications, such as baked goods, desserts, and chocolate preparations, and savory dishes like curries and stews, prized for its aromatic compounds including cinnamaldehyde.[48] Cassia bark, which constitutes the majority of commercial "cinnamon" sold globally due to its lower cost and higher yield, offers a bolder, more pungent profile and is integral to spice blends in Asian and Middle Eastern cooking.[49] Beyond commercial spices, the inner (cambium) layer of certain tree barks has featured in traditional and subsistence cuisines, particularly during famines or in indigenous practices. In Scandinavia, pine (Pinus sylvestris) inner bark has been harvested, dried, and ground into flour for leavened breads and porridges, providing a starchy, nutrient-dense supplement high in carbohydrates, fiber, and vitamin C.[50] [51] Similarly, birch (Betula spp.) inner bark yields a mildly sweet, sawdust-like edible layer that can be processed into flour for baked goods like shortbread cookies or boiled into strips for soups and stews, as utilized by North American indigenous groups and early settlers.[52] [53] Sassafras (Sassafras albidum) root bark, historically employed in early American recipes for its root beer-like flavor derived from safrole, flavored beverages and teas until regulatory restrictions in the 1960s due to safrole's carcinogenic potential in high doses; dried leaves rather than bark now produce filÃ© powder for thickening gumbos in Louisiana Creole cuisine.[54] These non-commercial uses underscore bark's role as a caloric emergency foodâ€”such as poplar or willow cambium chewed raw or added to brothsâ€”rather than everyday staples, with edibility varying by species and preparation to avoid tannins or toxins.[55]

Medicinal and industrial uses

Tree bark has served as a source of medicinal compounds for centuries, with notable examples including cinchona bark (Cinchona spp.), from which quinine was derived as an antimalarial agent. Jesuit missionaries in Peru documented its use among indigenous Andean peoples in the 17th century for treating fevers associated with malaria, with the alkaloid quinine first isolated in 1820 by French chemists Pierre Pelletier and Joseph Caventou, enabling more consistent dosing and widespread application until synthetic alternatives emerged in the 20th century.[56][57] Willow bark (Salix spp.) contains salicin, a phenolic glycoside hydrolyzed in the body to salicylic acid, providing analgesic, antipyretic, and anti-inflammatory effects akin to aspirin; historical records trace its use back over 3,500 years in ancient Egypt and Greece for pain and fever relief, with modern extracts standardized to 240 mg salicin showing efficacy in conditions like osteoarthritis, though contraindicated in aspirin-allergic individuals due to cross-reactivity.[58][59] Extracts from other barks, such as Juglans regia (walnut), exhibit antimicrobial and anticariogenic properties suitable for dental applications, including plaque reduction and tooth whitening.[60] In industrial contexts, cork harvested from the outer bark of Quercus suber (cork oak) provides a renewable material due to the tree's ability to regenerate bark every 9-12 years without harm, yielding products like wine stoppers, flooring, and insulation; a single mature tree can produce enough cork for approximately 4,000 bottles per harvest, supporting a global industry valued at billions while maintaining forest ecosystems in regions like Portugal and Spain.[61][62] Bark serves as a primary source of tannins for vegetable tanning in leather production, where polyphenolic compounds from species like oak (Quercus spp.) and hemlock (Tsuga spp.) bind to collagen proteins in hides, creating durable, water-resistant leather without synthetic chemicals; this method, dating to antiquity, relies on bark's high tannin contentâ€”often exceeding 10-20% in select speciesâ€”for extraction via hot water processes, though it requires longer durations (weeks to months) compared to chrome tanning.[63][64] Additional industrial applications include bark-derived extracts for adhesives, antioxidants, and wood preservatives, with over 23 million tons of bark residue annually available in the EU from forestry operations serving as feedstock for particleboard, fiberboard, and biofuels; research has also demonstrated bark liquefaction into oils for isocyanate-free polyurethanes, enhancing sustainability in composites and coatings.[65][66]

Named entities and references

People

Pyotr Lvovich Bark (April 6, 1869 â€“ January 17, 1937), also known as Peter Bark or Sir Peter Bark, was a Russian banker and statesman who served as the last Minister of Finance of the Russian Empire from July 1914 to February 1917 under Tsar Nicholas II.[67][68] Born in Yekaterinoslav (present-day Dnipro, Ukraine), Bark graduated from Saint Petersburg State University with a law degree and joined the Ministry of Finance in 1891, advancing to roles such as manager of the State Bank's foreign department and deputy governor.[69] During World War I, he managed imperial war financing, including issuing loans and stabilizing the ruble amid economic strains.[70] Following the February Revolution, Bark fled Russia, settled in England, acquired British citizenship, and was knighted by King George V in 1915 for financial services; he later directed the Anglo-International Bank until his death in Paris at age 68.[67][71] No other individuals bearing the surname Bark have achieved comparable historical prominence in verifiable records.[72]
