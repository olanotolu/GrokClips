Elizabeth Hamilton, Countess of Orkney

Elizabeth Hamilton, Countess of Orkney (c. 1657 – 19 April 1733), née Elizabeth Villiers, was an English courtier whose primary historical significance derives from her position as lady of the bedchamber to Queen Mary II and her reputed role as the principal and long-term mistress of Mary's husband, King William III, from approximately 1680 until his death in 1702.[1][2] Born into minor nobility as the daughter of Colonel Sir Edward Villiers, an army officer and politician, and Frances Howard, whose sister was governess to the Princess Royal (future Queen Mary), Elizabeth entered royal service through familial connections, initially attending the young princess in the Netherlands during William's tenure as stadtholder.[1][2] Contemporary accounts, including diplomatic correspondence from Paris by 1680, indicate the onset of an intimate relationship with William, substantiated by grants of land and money to her and her relatives, though direct empirical evidence such as correspondence or offspring remains absent, with claims resting on persistent court gossip and political exploitation by James II in 1685 to strain William and Mary's marriage.[1][2][3] Despite descriptions of her as plain or even unattractive—"squinting Betty" in some reports—Elizabeth exercised considerable influence at court, facilitating access to the king for petitioners and accumulating wealth through royal favors, including Irish estates valued at tens of thousands of pounds.[1][4] In 1695, she married Brigadier General George Hamilton, who was promptly elevated to Earl of Orkney, Viscount Kirkwall, and Baron Dechmont, securing her aristocratic status and producing several children, including Anne, who inherited the Orkney titles.[3][2] Widowed in 1737, she died childless in the direct line of her titles' succession but left a substantial fortune reflective of her earlier gains.[1]

Early Life

Birth and Parentage

Elizabeth Villiers, later Countess of Orkney, was born around 1657 in Richmond, Surrey, England.[1][5] She was the daughter of Colonel Sir Edward Villiers, an English politician and military officer associated with the court, and Lady Frances Howard, the youngest daughter of Theophilus Howard, 2nd Earl of Suffolk.[1][2][6] Sir Edward Villiers served in military capacities and held positions that connected the family to royal circles, while Lady Frances Howard's aristocratic lineage through the Howard family provided additional ties to nobility.[1] Elizabeth was one of eight children born to the couple, positioning her within a prominent family network that facilitated her eventual entry into court service.[2]

Entry into Court Service

Elizabeth Villiers, born around 1657 as the daughter of Colonel Sir Edward Villiers and Frances Howard, entered court service through familial connections tied to the Stuart royal household. Her mother served as governess to Princesses Mary and Anne, daughters of James, Duke of York, which facilitated Elizabeth's early association with Princess Mary. In November 1677, Elizabeth was appointed as a maid of honour in Mary's suite, accompanying her to The Hague for the princess's marriage to William, Prince of Orange (later William III of England). She traveled alongside her sister Anne Villiers and brother Edward Villiers, marking her introduction to the Dutch court environment where William held influence. This position placed her in close proximity to the future monarchs, though her service remained formally under Mary. Elizabeth continued as maid of honour through Mary's tenure in the Netherlands, returning to England with the princess in February 1689 following William's invasion and accession during the Glorious Revolution. Her role thus transitioned from the Orange court to the new joint monarchy in England, leveraging the Villiers family's established courtly ties.

Relationship with William III

Origins of the Affair

Elizabeth Villiers entered the household of Mary, Princess of Orange (later Queen Mary II), as a lady-in-waiting following Mary's marriage to William, Prince of Orange, on November 4, 1677.[7] In this capacity, she served in the Dutch court at The Hague, where proximity to the royal couple facilitated personal interactions.[6] William first made advances toward Elizabeth in 1679, shortly after her integration into the court. Initially, she discouraged his overtures, reportedly attempting to deflect them by encouraging the attentions of a military officer.[2] Despite this resistance, the relationship evolved into an affair by 1680, marking the acknowledged beginning of her role as William's mistress.[6] [2] The liaison remained relatively discreet in its early years, with rumors eventually circulating to Paris, suggesting Mary's likely awareness though no public confrontation ensued at the time.[2] This period coincided with William's political maneuvers against Louis XIV, during which Elizabeth's influence was not yet prominent but laid the foundation for later favor.[8]

Political Influence and Royal Favors

Elizabeth Villiers exerted influence over King William III primarily through securing personal and familial favors rather than overt political maneuvering, though she engaged in discreet interventions in court affairs. Following William's accession to the English throne in 1689, he granted her extensive Irish estates confiscated from Jacobite supporters, totaling over 90,000 acres initially valued at approximately £26,000 annually, though subsequent deductions reduced the effective yield to around £5,000 per year; these lands were ultimately revoked by Parliament in 1699 amid broader scrutiny of royal grants. In specific instances, Villiers acted as an intermediary in political communications, such as relaying messages between William and Charles Talbot, 1st Duke of Shrewsbury, during November and December 1693. She also disseminated gossip detrimental to John Churchill, 1st Duke of Marlborough, reflecting her opposition to the Churchill faction at court. Additionally, she advocated for the advancement of Arnold Joost van Keppel to offset the rising influence of her sister Anne's husband, William Bentinck, 1st Earl of Portland. Her sway extended to arrangements benefiting her future husband; after the cessation of her liaison with William following Queen Mary's death on 28 December 1694, Villiers married George Hamilton on 25 November 1695, who was promptly elevated to Earl of Orkney, Viscount Kirkwall, and Baron Dechmont on 6 January 1696, a promotion widely attributed to her prior relationship with the king. This elevation included grants such as the estate at Cliveden, underscoring the tangible royal patronage she secured.[9]

Controversies Surrounding the Relationship

The relationship between Elizabeth Villiers and William III, commencing around 1680, provoked significant scandal due to its occurrence during William's marriage to Mary II, whom Villiers had known since childhood as a lady-in-waiting.[2] Mary became aware of the affair, reportedly experiencing heartbreak, and on one occasion observed William entering Villiers' apartments, leading to confrontation where William denied adultery and Mary forgave him, possibly viewing their meetings as diplomatic rather than romantic.[10] [11] James II exploited rumors of the infidelity in 1685 to alienate Mary from William, instructing her staff to inform her of the liaison in hopes of fracturing their alliance.[12] This political maneuver failed, as Mary remained loyal, but it highlighted the affair's potential as a tool for dynastic intrigue amid tensions preceding the Glorious Revolution.[13] Debates persist regarding the affair's sexual nature, as Villiers bore no children to William and was described as lacking beauty, with a squint, awkward gait, and poor complexion, prompting speculation that the relationship served political ends or masked William's rumored homosexual inclinations toward figures like Hans Willem Bentinck.[2] [14] [15] Historians note Villiers initially resisted advances by promoting a rival suitor, yet yielded, raising questions about coercion or ambition.[6] Villiers' influence drew criticism for securing extensive royal favors, including Irish land grants in 1696 that founded Midleton Endowed School but infuriated both English and Irish parliaments, sparking public invective and legislative efforts to revoke such awards to William's favorites.[16] [17] These bestowals, totaling vast forfeited estates, fueled perceptions of corruption, exacerbating parliamentary discontent with William's administration.[18]

Marriage and Family

Union with George Hamilton


Elizabeth Villiers married her second cousin, George Hamilton, on 25 November 1695 at St Martin-in-the-Fields, Ludgate, London.[19][2] George, born around 1666, was the fifth son of William Douglas-Hamilton, 3rd Duke of Hamilton, and served as a brigadier general in the Royal Scots regiment, reflecting his established military career prior to the union.[2] The marriage occurred amid the decline of Elizabeth's longstanding relationship with King William III, with whom she had ceased public appearances by this time, though private influences persisted.[6] Shortly following the wedding, on 3 January 1696, William III elevated George Hamilton to the peerage as Earl of Orkney, Viscount Kirkwall, and Baron Dechmont in Scotland, a grant widely attributed to Elizabeth's favor at court rather than George's independent merits alone.[20] This ennoblement secured Elizabeth's status as Countess of Orkney, providing formal legitimacy and estates, including those in Orkney, amid parliamentary scrutiny over prior royal grants to her.[3] The union, arranged through familial and royal connections, yielded no immediate public discord, though contemporary observers noted its convenience for consolidating Hamilton's prospects under William's patronage.[2] George continued his military service, participating in campaigns such as the Siege of Namur in 1695 prior to the marriage, while the couple resided primarily in England, leveraging the new titles for social and political elevation.)

Children and Succession

Elizabeth and George Hamilton had three daughters, born after their marriage on 25 November 1695.[6][2] Their eldest daughter, Anne Hamilton (c. 1696–1756), inherited her father's titles as 2nd Countess of Orkney suo jure following his death on 29 January 1737, as the earldom lacked male heirs and the patent allowed succession through female lines.[2] She married her cousin William O'Brien, 4th Earl of Inchiquin, on 22 March 1717, producing several children who continued the Orkney line, including Mary O'Brien, who became 3rd Countess of Orkney.[2] The younger daughters, Frances Hamilton (d. c. 1730s) and Henrietta Hamilton (dates uncertain), did not inherit peerages but married into nobility; Frances wed John Campbell, while Henrietta's alliances remain less documented in primary records.[6] The absence of sons ensured the Orkney titles passed solely to Anne, reflecting the gendered limitations of 18th-century British peerage succession despite the family's royal connections.[2]

Later Life

Post-William Social Standing

Following the death of William III on March 8, 1702, Elizabeth Hamilton, Countess of Orkney, preserved her elevated social position through accumulated wealth from royal grants, strategic properties, and sustained court connections. Her marriage to George Hamilton, created 1st Earl of Orkney in 1696, had already secured extensive estates, including the purchase of Cliveden in Buckinghamshire in 1696, which bolstered their financial independence and aristocratic status independent of William's favor.[2][20] The couple's prosperity was further enhanced by George's appointment as deputy governor of Virginia from 1710 to 1711, yielding substantial income that Elizabeth helped manage.[2] During Queen Anne's reign (1702–1714), Elizabeth acted as a counselor to her husband, leveraging her political friendships to support his military campaigns, including his role in the War of the Spanish Succession, and his appointments such as Groom of the Bedchamber.[2] This advisory influence ensured the Orkneys' prominence amid the transition to Hanoverian rule, with George later serving as Lord of the Bedchamber to George I and Governor of Edinburgh Castle.[2] Under the early Hanoverians, Elizabeth maintained central involvement in royal circles, hosting King George I and King George II at Cliveden, which underscored her enduring social relevance despite the end of her liaison with William.[2][20][3] She resided primarily between London and her Buckinghamshire estates, including Taplow Court, reflecting a stable, affluent lifestyle until her death on April 19, 1733, at age 76, in her Albemarle Street home in London, where she was buried at Taplow Court.[2][20]

Death and Burial

Elizabeth Hamilton, Countess of Orkney, died on 19 April 1733 at her residence in Albemarle Street, London, at the age of 76.[2][20] No specific cause of death is recorded in contemporary accounts, consistent with her advanced age.[19] She was buried on 25 April 1733 in St. Nicolas' Churchyard at Taplow, Buckinghamshire, near her family estates.[20][19] Taplow had been granted to her by King William III, underscoring her enduring ties to royal favor even after his death in 1702.[2]

Legacy

Familial Descendants

Elizabeth Villiers and George Hamilton, 1st Earl of Orkney, married on 25 November 1695 and had three daughters but no surviving sons.[21][22] The eldest, Anne Hamilton (c. 1696 – 6 December 1756), succeeded her father as 2nd Countess of Orkney, Viscountess Kirkwall, and Baroness Dechmont in her own right upon his death in 1737.[23][24] Anne married her cousin William O'Brien, 4th Earl of Inchiquin (c. 1697 – 1777), on 26 February 1717, and their union produced several children, including Mary O'Brien (c. 1720 – 10 May 1767), who inherited the Orkney titles as 3rd Countess upon Anne's death.[2][25] Mary's marriage to Thomas Fitzmaurice, 1st Earl of Kerry (1722 – 1770), on 1 March 1740 continued the line, though the earldom eventually passed through further female succession and became extinct in 1790 with the death of the 5th holder without male heirs.[26] The second daughter, Frances Hamilton (d. 27 December 1772), married Richard Lumley, 1st Earl of Scarbrough (1650 – 1721), integrating into the Lumley peerage; their descendants included later Earls of Scarbrough.[22] The youngest, Henrietta Hamilton (d. 22 August 1732), wed Henry Boyle, Viscount Dungarvan (d. 1764), eldest son of the 3rd Earl of Cork, linking the family to the Boyle earldom; this line produced figures such as subsequent Viscounts Dungarvan but no direct continuation of the Orkney titles.[22] These matrimonial alliances preserved noble status across multiple peerages, though the Hamilton-Villiers direct patriline ended with the daughters.[24]

Historical Assessments and Debates

Historians regard Elizabeth Villiers as William III's principal and acknowledged mistress from approximately 1680 until the mid-1690s, a relationship that afforded her significant court access despite her lack of conventional beauty and awkward demeanor. Her role is characterized as that of a discreet intriguer who leveraged proximity to the king for personal and familial gain, including securing positions and influence for her children within the household of Queen Mary II. Contemporary accounts, such as those preserved in the Dictionary of National Biography, emphasize her quick wit and loyalty, though her family's favoritism provoked resentment from Princess Mary and contributed to court tensions. Debates persist over the precise nature of her liaison with William, with some assessments questioning whether it was consummated, given the absence of acknowledged children and persistent rumors of the king's homosexual inclinations toward favorites like Arnold Keppel.[27] While primary sources affirm her status as mistress, the lack of issue and William's reputed emotional reserve have led later interpreters to speculate on a primarily platonic or advisory bond, though direct evidence for sexual exclusivity or denial remains elusive. Her political influence draws scrutiny for instances of favoritism, notably the post-1689 coronation grant of over 90,000 acres of Irish forfeited estates yielding £26,000 annually, which Parliament curtailed to about £5,000 via rent-charges before revoking it entirely in 1699 amid complaints of undue royal largesse. Assessments portray her as meddling in factional rivalries—relaying gossip against the Marlboroughs, preferring Keppel to Bentinck, 1st Earl of Portland, and mediating between William and Charles Talbot, 1st Duke of Shrewsbury in 1693—yet her sway appears confined to personal patronage rather than broader policy direction, reflecting William's aversion to overt court cabals. Jonathan Swift later commended her wisdom, underscoring a posthumous view of her as astute if opportunistic.
