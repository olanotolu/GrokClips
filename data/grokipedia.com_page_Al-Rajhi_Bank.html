Alrajhi Bank


Alrajhi Bank is a Saudi joint stock company engaged exclusively in Sharia-compliant Islamic banking, established in 1957 by brothers Sulaiman, Saleh, Abdullah, and Mohamed Al Rajhi as a money exchange house and restructured as a full-fledged bank in 1988.[1][2] It has grown to become the world's largest Islamic bank by total assets, reported at SR 1,023 billion (approximately USD 273 billion) in the first quarter of 2025, and the leading retail bank in Saudi Arabia with a vast network exceeding 600 branches domestically and presence in Kuwait, Jordan, and Malaysia.[3][4] The bank's defining characteristics include its unwavering commitment to Islamic finance principles, prohibiting interest (riba) and emphasizing profit-sharing models, which have driven its dominance in Saudi Arabia's retail sector where it serves millions of customers through diverse products like financing, investment funds, and digital banking services.[4] Its rapid expansion reflects Saudi Arabia's economic diversification and rising demand for ethical banking, bolstered by the Al Rajhi family's enduring influence despite shifts toward public listing and professional management.[5] Notable achievements encompass consistent profitability, with assets multiplying over decades through organic growth and strategic innovations in fintech, positioning it as a benchmark for Islamic financial institutions globally.[6] While early post-9/11 allegations of indirect ties to terrorism financing were leveled in U.S. lawsuits and firmly denied by the bankâ€”leading to dismissals for lack of evidenceâ€”these claims, rooted in broader geopolitical scrutiny rather than substantiated operations, have not materially impeded its trajectory.[7][8]

Overview

Founding and Ownership

Al Rajhi Bank traces its origins to 1957, when brothers Saleh Abdul Aziz Al Rajhi, Sulaiman Al Rajhi, Mohammed Al Rajhi, and Abdullah Al Rajhi established the institution's foundational operations in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, initially focused on currency exchange and basic banking services amid the Kingdom's economic expansion following oil discoveries.[4][9] Saleh Al Rajhi, the eldest and primary initiator, had begun informal currency trading as early as 1947, leveraging family resources from prior scrap metal dealings to formalize the venture into a structured financial entity adhering to Islamic principles.[9] This establishment predated Saudi Arabia's formal banking regulations, positioning the Al Rajhi operation as a pioneer in Shariah-compliant finance before the sector's widespread institutionalization.[10] The bank's evolution into a full-fledged Islamic institution occurred in stages, with the formation of Al Rajhi Banking and Investment Corporation as a joint-stock company in 1987, enabling expanded deposit-taking, financing, and investment activities under royal decree.[10] By 1988, it had transitioned from exchange services to comprehensive banking, reflecting the brothers' vision of scaling operations while maintaining riba-free (interest-free) models rooted in profit-sharing and asset-backed transactions.[11] The founding brothers' emphasis on ethical, faith-based practices distinguished it from conventional Western banking models entering the region. Ownership resides with public shareholders following its listing on the Saudi Tadawul exchange, where approximately 75% of shares are held by institutional and retail investors, including major funds like Vanguard and BlackRock.[12] The Al Rajhi family retains significant influence as the largest shareholder group, with family members such as Abdullah bin Sulaiman Al Rajhi serving as board chairman and holding individual stakes around 2.2% as of recent filings.[13] This structure balances family oversightâ€”ensuring continuity of founding principlesâ€”with broad market participation, aligning with Saudi regulatory requirements for joint-stock entities since the 1980s.[6] No single entity dominates control, fostering stability through diversified ownership amid the bank's growth to over $200 billion in assets.[14]

Scale and Market Position

Al Rajhi Bank holds the position of the world's largest Islamic bank by total assets, which reached SAR 1,039 billion (approximately USD 277 billion) as of June 30, 2025, reflecting a 19.8% year-over-year increase driven by expansion in financing and investment portfolios.[15] In Saudi Arabia, it ranks as the largest retail bank, maintaining a 41.2% market share in retail lending as of December 31, 2024, supported by its extensive customer base and Sharia-compliant product offerings.[16] The bank's market capitalization stood at SAR 396 billion (USD 105.6 billion) in March 2025, securing its status as the most valuable bank in the Middle East and North Africa region and placing it among the top 30 banks globally by this metric.[17] With approximately 24,000 employees and the Kingdom's largest distribution networkâ€”including over 510 branches, more than 4,500 ATMs, and extensive POS terminals as of early 2024â€”the bank dominates retail accessibility in Saudi Arabia's banking sector.[4] This scale enables Al Rajhi to serve a significant portion of the population through physical and digital channels, contributing to its leading role in deposits and customer deposits, which underpin its asset growth amid Saudi Arabia's economic diversification under Vision 2030. Its position reflects sustained profitability, with net income rising 18.7% to USD 5.3 billion in 2024, outpacing many conventional peers despite operating exclusively under Islamic finance principles.[18]

Historical Development

Origins as a Money Exchange

The Al Rajhi family's involvement in financial services began with Saleh Abdul Aziz Al Rajhi, who, originating from a modest background in the Qassim region, initially engaged in small-scale trading of scrap items in the late 1930s, laying the groundwork for subsequent ventures.[19] By 1947, Saleh established a formal currency exchange operation in Riyadh, partnering with his younger brothers, including Sulaiman and Mohammed, to capitalize on the demand for foreign currency exchange amid growing pilgrimage traffic to Mecca and Medina, as well as the nascent oil-driven economic expansion in Saudi Arabia.[20][9] This shop provided essential services such as converting currencies for international visitors and expatriate workers, filling a critical gap in the absence of formal banking infrastructure.[10] The operation quickly gained prominence due to its reliability and the brothers' reputation for integrity, evolving into one of Saudi Arabia's leading money-changing businesses. The Saudi government relied on Al Rajhi for currency exchange needs prior to the establishment of the kingdom's first national bank, underscoring the enterprise's role in facilitating early economic transactions.[10][20] Over the ensuing decades, the family expanded the exchange activities, incorporating deposit-taking and basic trading functions while adhering to Islamic principles that prohibited interest-based lending, which positioned it as a precursor to Sharia-compliant finance.[21] By 1978, the brothers formalized their collective efforts under Al Rajhi Trading and Exchange Corporation, consolidating currency exchange, remittances, and commercial operations into a structured entity with a focus on ethical, riba-free practices.[6] This phase marked the transition from informal hawala-like exchanges to a more institutionalized model, building a customer base through trust and efficiency in handling high-volume transactions for pilgrims and traders, though it remained outside the regulated banking sector until later developments. The money exchange origins thus provided the operational expertise and capital accumulation that propelled the Al Rajhi enterprise toward full banking status.[9]

Establishment as a Full Bank

In 1988, the Al Rajhi family's exchange and trading operations, which had originated in 1957, were restructured and converted into a full-fledged Islamic bank named Al Rajhi Banking and Investment Corporation.[2] This transition formalized the institution as a Saudi joint stock company, enabling it to offer comprehensive Sharia-compliant banking services such as deposit mobilization, financing, and investment activities previously limited under exchange house regulations.[2][22] The conversion received regulatory approval through Royal Decree No. M/59 and Council of Ministersâ€™ Resolution No. 245, both issued in June 1987, which granted the necessary banking license from the Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority (SAMA) to operate as an Islamic commercial bank.[2][23] Following over six years of consultations with authorities, the bank was established to align with Saudi Arabia's growing emphasis on riba-free finance, positioning it as the third licensed Islamic bank in the kingdom after earlier pioneers.[21][22] At inception, Al Rajhi Banking and Investment Corporation focused on expanding its branch network and product offerings while adhering strictly to Sharia principles, leveraging the founders' established reputation in currency exchange and trade finance.[2] This establishment laid the groundwork for its rapid growth, with initial operations centered in Riyadh and supported by the Al Rajhi brothers' capital and expertise in compliant financial intermediation.[24]

In February 2010, Al Rajhi announced plans to open 90 additional branches over the next three years, targeting underserved regions to further boost its retail footprint and compete with state-backed institutions. These initiatives reflected sustained asset and deposit growth, positioning the bank as Saudi Arabia's preeminent Islamic financial entity by the decade's end.[2][26][27]

Core Operations

Retail and Consumer Banking

Alrajhi Bank's retail and consumer banking segment, known internally as the Retail Banking Group (RBG), delivers Sharia-compliant products addressing individual financial needs, including deposit accounts, retail financing, debit cards, insurance, and digital services. This division maintains a broad portfolio to serve personal banking requirements, supported by an extensive branch network across Saudi Arabia and integrated online platforms.[16][43] Deposit products feature current accounts enabling money transfers, cheque issuance, and free Mada ATM/POS card provision, alongside specialized savings options such as the Smart Saving Account and Awaeed Account, which offer profit-sharing mechanisms aligned with Islamic principles to incentivize deposits. Account-related services, including statements and transfers, incur minimal or no fees for core operations like new account openings and initial card issuance.[44][45][46] Retail financing encompasses personal loans up to SAR 5 million with repayment periods extending to 60 months, early repayment flexibility without penalties, and top-up options starting at an annual profit rate of 3.32%, alongside eligibility for end-of-service benefits. Home and real estate financing provides up to SAR 5 million over 30 years, covering up to 70% of property value with fixed profit margins and Sharia-compliant structures, including bundled personal and home packages for durations up to 25 years. Auto financing complements these offerings for vehicle purchases.[47][48][49] Payment solutions include free issuance of Mada debit cards for account access, while ancillary services integrate insurance coverage and the mokafaa loyalty program for rewards on transactions. In 2023, the bank launched innovative retail products, such as advanced real estate financing concepts, enhancing consumer accessibility. Digital channels, including a mobile app for 24/7 banking, facilitate account management, transfers, and product applications, contributing to awards for customer experience in financial services.[46][50][16]

Corporate and Wholesale Banking

Al Rajhi Bank's Corporate Banking Group (CBG) provides Sharia-compliant financing, trade, and treasury solutions to large corporations, institutions, and government entities in Saudi Arabia, positioning it as a key pillar of the bank's wholesale operations. The group focuses on supporting Vision 2030 initiatives through partnerships, such as memoranda of understanding with the Ministry of Investment, and emphasizes digital integration for efficiency.[51] In 2024, CBG achieved 31% year-over-year growth in total gross financing, reaching SAR 179 billion, with a market share of 13.6% and ranking among the top three corporate banks in the Kingdom.[51] Core offerings include trade finance, such as letters of guarantee and export financing to facilitate deferred payments without impacting client financial ratios; treasury products for foreign exchange and risk management; and cash management services via business solutions offices.[52] [53] The group also delivers corporate investment banking, with 427% year-over-year revenue growth in 2024, alongside wealth management and private banking tailored for high-value clients.[51] Embedded financing solutions target aggregators, while innovations like the first-in-Kingdom Travel Accountâ€”a virtual card for travel agenciesâ€”support sectoral expansion.[51] A flagship program is the Supply Chain Finance (SCF) initiative, a Sharia-compliant platform digitized in partnership with QUALCO's ProximaPlus in 2025, enabling automated, buyer-led invoice financing with features like receivables verification, concentration management, and ledger reconciliation.[51] [54] This facilitated the bank's first end-to-end digital SCF transaction in August 2025 with Masheed Trading and a SAR 1.625 billion supplier financing deal with Zain Saudi Arabia in May 2024.[55] [56] Additional products like the Shlha Program offer profit-free working capital financing sponsored by merchant partners, enhancing accessibility for corporate liquidity needs.[57] CBG's digital advancements include 14% year-over-year growth in e-Business accounts and a Net Promoter Score of 78, reflecting strong client adoption.[51] The group reported 23.7% growth in trade fee income and 1.47% in foreign exchange business in 2024, underscoring its role in wholesale trade support.[51] Awards such as Best Private Banking and Best Project Finance Bank (Middle East) from MEED affirm its competitive edge in structured financing for infrastructure projects.[51] All services adhere to Islamic principles, avoiding interest-based structures in favor of profit-sharing and asset-backed models.[52]

Investment and Treasury Services

Al Rajhi Bank's Treasury Group manages liquidity, funding, and investment portfolios through a diversified range of Sharia-compliant instruments, including sukuk, syndicated financing, certificates of deposit, trade loans, and term repurchase agreements. The portfolio is structured for balance across duration, credit ratings, and geographical exposure to mitigate risks while supporting the bank's overall funding needs. In 2024, the group expanded long-term funding sources, such as syndication and term repos, to capitalize on evolving market opportunities in Saudi Arabia's financial landscape.[39][58] Treasury operations also encompass foreign exchange, money market activities, and global markets trading, all adhering to Islamic finance principles approved by the bank's Sharia Board. The group employs advanced platforms like Murex MX.3 to handle Islamic finance transactions, ensuring compliance with local and international regulatory demands for risk management and reporting. Led by General Manager Abdulrahman Al-Ajaji, the treasury function integrates sales, trading, and structuring to optimize returns on surplus funds and hedge against interest rate and currency fluctuations via permissible derivatives equivalents, such as profit rate swaps.[59][60] Investment services are primarily delivered through subsidiary Al Rajhi Capital, a closed joint-stock company offering brokerage, asset management, and investment banking tailored to Sharia standards. Products include equity trading on the Saudi Tadawul exchange, mutual funds focused on compliant equities and sukuk, and structured advisory for debt and equity issuances. Al Rajhi Capital's asset management arm manages over SAR 100 billion in assets under management as of 2024, emphasizing diversified, low-risk Sharia-compliant portfolios for retail and institutional clients.[61][62] The bank also provides direct investment options like Sharia-compliant saving and protection programs, which allocate funds to long-term goals via profit-sharing mechanisms rather than interest, combining investment growth with takaful (Islamic insurance) elements for risk mitigation. These services support the bank's broader strategy of integrating treasury liquidity management with client-facing investment solutions, contributing to total assets exceeding SAR 800 billion by end-2024.[63][3]

Islamic Finance Framework

Sharia Governance and Compliance

Al Rajhi Bank's Sharia governance is anchored in its Council of Shariah Scholars, which provides independent oversight to ensure all banking activities conform to Islamic jurisprudence. The framework was formalized through the General Assembly's adoption of Sharia Board Regulations in 1999, establishing the bank as one of the pioneers in structured Sharia supervision among Islamic financial institutions.[64] The Council issues binding fatwas on products, contracts, and operations, prohibiting riba (usury), gharar (excessive uncertainty), and maysir (gambling), while promoting profit-and-loss sharing and asset-backed financing.[65] The Shariah Group, operating directly under the Council, executes compliance monitoring across the bank's operations, including retail, corporate, and investment services. Key functions encompass reviewing and approving Sharia-compliant contractsâ€”over 1,232 forms and agreements since the group's inceptionâ€”conducting internal Sharia audits via automated systems and field inspections, and training staff on Islamic finance principles to embed compliance in daily processes.[65][64] Audit findings and recommendations are reported to the Board's Audit and Compliance Committee, enhancing the group's operational independence from executive management.[66] As of 2024, the Council is chaired by Sheikh Abdulaziz Bin Hameen Al Hamain, with Sheikh Dr. Saleh bin Abdullah Al Lohaidan as vice chairman, and members including Sheikh Sulaiman bin Abdullah Al-Majed, Sheikh Dr. Saad bin Turki Al-Khathlan, and Sheikh Dr. Abdullah bin Nasser Al Salmi.[65] These scholars, selected for their expertise in fiqh al-muamalat (Islamic transactional law), collectively review proposed innovations and verify adherence to standards set by the Saudi Central Bank (SAMA) and the Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions (AAOIFI).[67] This multi-layered approach integrates Sharia oversight with the bank's overall corporate governance structure, which includes the General Assembly, Board of Directors, and specialized committees.[68]

Key Sharia-Compliant Products and Innovations

Alrajhi Bank provides a suite of financing products structured under Sharia principles, prominently featuring Murabaha contracts for cost-plus sales. The Flexible Finance Product exemplifies this, enabling real estate purchases through deferred payment Murabaha arrangements that disclose the full cost and profit margin upfront, compliant with Sharia prohibitions on riba (interest).[69] Similarly, Goods Murabaha underpins trade finance solutions, including letters of guarantee and working capital facilities for businesses.[70] Investment offerings include Mudarabah-based funds, such as the Al-Rajhi Commodities Mudaraba Fund, which pools investor capital (rabb al-mal) with the bank's management (mudarib) for profit-sharing in commodity trades, adhering to risk-sharing tenets without guaranteed returns.[71] The Al Rajhi Sukuk Fund targets medium- to long-term income and capital growth via Sharia-compliant debt instruments like sukuk certificates, avoiding speculative elements.[72] Capital market activities feature sukuk issuances, including a US$1 billion 5-year Sustainable Sukuk Wakala/Murabaha in March 2024 at a 5.047% annual profit rate, and a USD1.5 billion Additional Tier 1 Perpetual Sustainable Sukuk Wakala/Mudaraba in February 2025, bolstering Tier 1 capital while funding sustainable projects.[73][74] Innovations emphasize digital integration and supply chain efficiency, notably the May 2025 launch of a Sharia-compliant Supply Chain Finance (SCF) platform powered by QUALCO's ProximaPlus. This buyer-led, automated solution operates under Goods Murabaha, enhancing invoice visibility, reducing financing costs for suppliers, and streamlining processes without interest-based mechanisms.[54] The Shariah Group drives such advancements by reviewing and approving products, issuing over 1,232 Sharia contracts since inception, and proposing tech-enabled solutions like AI-assisted compliance monitoring to evolve Islamic finance dynamically.[65][67] Complementary services include Sharia-compliant takaful (cooperative insurance) through Al Rajhi Takaful, covering risks via mutual contribution models.[75]

Sustainability and Corporate Responsibility

ESG Integration with Sharia Principles

Al Rajhi Bank integrates environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors into its operations through a Shariah-compliant framework that aligns sustainable finance with Islamic principles, emphasizing ethical screening to exclude prohibited activities such as alcohol, gambling, and tobacco production. This approach is rooted in the bank's core identity as a provider of Shariah-led Islamic finance, where social responsibilityâ€”embodied in concepts like Zakat (obligatory almsgiving) and Maqasid al-Shariah (objectives of Islamic law, including preservation of life, wealth, and environment)â€”naturally overlaps with ESG goals, such as promoting long-term societal welfare and avoiding harm.[97] The framework adopts a sustainable value creation model focused on robust governance, risk management, and ethical practices, ensuring all initiatives comply with oversight from an independent Sharia Board.[98] The bank's Sustainable Finance Framework explicitly incorporates ESG by defining eligible projects that meet both international standards, like the International Capital Market Association's (ICMA) Green Bond Principles (2021) and Sustainability Bond Guidelines (2021), and Sharia requirements. Green-eligible categories include renewable energy projects (e.g., solar and wind installations), energy efficiency improvements, sustainable water and waste management, pollution prevention, clean transportation, and green building construction, all vetted for Sharia permissibility and positive environmental impact. Social-eligible projects encompass access to essential services like healthcare and education, affordable housing, and support for micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) to generate employment, with exclusions for sectors involving fossil fuel expansion or nuclear energy to mitigate climate risks.[97] This dual alignment enables instruments like Sharia-compliant sukuk and murabaha facilities to fund such initiatives without speculative elements prohibited under Islamic law. Governance mechanisms reinforce this integration via the ESG Committee, formally the Sustainable Finance Working Group, chaired by the Chief Financial Officer and including the Chief Risk Officer, Group Treasurer, and business unit heads, which reports biannually to the Board Risk Management Committee. The committee mainstreams ESG into decision-making, product development, and risk assessment while ensuring Sharia compliance across operations, supporting Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 and net-zero ambitions by 2060.[99] Practical implementations include the issuance of USD 3 billion (SAR 11.25 billion) in sustainable sukuk by September 2025 to finance environmental and social projects, alongside a landmark US$1.921 billion dual-tranche sustainability commodity murabaha facility in 2025, recognized as the world's best Islamic ESG deal that year.[100][101] Earlier, in September 2022, the bank concluded a three-year, over $1 billion dual-tranche sustainability commodity murabaha facility, demonstrating scalable Sharia-ESG financing.[102] These efforts position the bank as a leader in aligning Islamic prohibitions on exploitation with ESG's focus on sustainability, though external verification of impact metrics remains tied to self-reported allocation reports.[31]

Key Projects and Achievements

Al Rajhi Bank has financed renewable energy projects generating over 2,200 megawatts of capacity, contributing to Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 goals for clean energy transition while adhering to Sharia-compliant financing structures such as murabaha.[103] In 2025, the bank secured recognition for the world's best Islamic ESG deal through a US$1.921 billion dual-tranche sustainability commodity murabaha facility, which funded green and social projects including renewable energy and affordable housing initiatives aligned with Sharia principles.[101] The bank's Sustainable Finance Framework, introduced in 2022, has enabled the issuance of sustainable sukuks and syndicated loans, including the world's first investment-grade dollar AT1 sustainable sukuk in 2025, channeling funds toward Sharia-approved environmental and social outcomes like water conservation and sustainable urban development.[104][105] This framework integrates ESG criteria with Sharia governance, ensuring proceeds support eligible projects vetted by the bank's Sharia committee.[98] In social responsibility, Al Rajhi Bank has partnered with NGOs to provide educational scholarships for underprivileged students and launched the 2025 "Our Numbers Have a Footprint in Charity" campaign, extending Ramadan giving to amplify charitable impacts through Zakat and Sadaqah mechanisms.[106][107] Achievements include an MSCI ESG rating upgrade from "A" to "AA" in 2025, reflecting strengthened governance and reduced environmental risks, and a top-five global ranking in bank sustainability by September 2025, driven by increased female workforce participation and resource optimization efforts.[108][103] These initiatives align with the bank's 2024-reviewed sustainability strategy, which sets quantitative ESG targets like launching a sustainable fund while maintaining Sharia compliance across operations.[98]

Controversies and Criticisms

Terrorism Financing Allegations

In the early 2000s, U.S. intelligence assessments identified Al Rajhi Bank as a conduit exploited by Islamic extremists for terrorist financing, with a 2003 CIA report stating that such groups had utilized the institution since at least the mid-1990s to transfer funds supporting operations.[109] A 2004 U.S. diplomatic cable further detailed concerns over the bank's role in channeling donations to entities linked to al-Qaeda, including through its branches and affiliated charities.[110] These findings contributed to civil lawsuits by victims of the September 11, 2001, attacks, who alleged under the Antiterrorism Act that Al Rajhi Bank knowingly provided material support to terrorist organizations by facilitating wire transfers and maintaining accounts for suspected financiers.[111] A 2012 U.S. Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations report, examining HSBC's global operations, documented internal HSBC communications from 2000 to 2009 that flagged Al Rajhi Bank's disregard for evident terrorist financing risks, including accounts tied to extremists, yet HSBC continued correspondent banking relationships despite U.S. regulatory warnings.[112] The report cited specific instances, such as HSBC's awareness of Al Rajhi's exposure to Saudi-based terror networks post-9/11, but noted no formal sanctions were imposed on Al Rajhi itself by the U.S. Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC).[113] Al Rajhi Bank has consistently denied involvement in terrorist financing, asserting in 2013 that it condemns terrorism and has no role in supporting illegal organizations, while emphasizing enhanced internal controls.[114] In response to 2012 media reports on alleged links, the bank issued statements reassuring clients of its compliance with international anti-money laundering standards and conducted internal audits to sever ties with high-risk entities.[7] By 2025, the bank's public compliance framework includes dedicated monitoring for terrorist financing risks, aligned with Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority regulations, though no independent verification of resolution for prior allegations has been publicly confirmed.[115]

Other Legal and Operational Challenges

In 2010, Saudi Arabia's Capital Markets Authority (CMA) fined Al Rajhi Bank 1 million Saudi riyals for failing to notify the regulator of the resignations of two board members, an administrative lapse in governance disclosure requirements.[116] In 2014, the CMA imposed an additional penalty of 20,000 Saudi riyals on the bank for violating Listing Rules, specifically by submitting incorrect data on electronic disclosure form number 5 and failing to update it in accordance with a 2012 CMA resolution.[117] In 2020, the Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority (SAMA) levied penalties on Al Rajhi Bank, alongside 29 other financial institutions, for breaches of responsible finance principles, which encompass consumer protection standards such as fair treatment, transparency in fees, and grievance handling.[118] These regulatory actions highlight periodic compliance shortcomings in administrative and customer-facing operations, though the bank has maintained overall adherence to SAMA and CMA frameworks without escalating to license suspensions or major operational halts. Operationally, Al Rajhi Bank has navigated challenges in digital transformation and cybersecurity amid rapid fintech adoption, including regulatory hurdles in aligning Sharia-compliant innovations with SAMA's data protection and anti-fraud mandates under the Personal Data Protection Law.[119] Unverified claims of vulnerabilities, such as alleged IDOR flaws in APIs reported for sale in 2024 and potential credit card data leaks discussed on hacking forums, underscore persistent cyber risks, prompting enhanced internal controls like fraud detection systems and employee training, but no confirmed breaches have resulted in regulatory fines or customer data losses to date.[120]

Leadership and Organizational Structure

Key Executives and Family Involvement

Alrajhi Bank was founded in 1957 by brothers Sulaiman bin Abdulaziz Al Rajhi and Saleh Abdulaziz Al Rajhi as a currency exchange operation in Riyadh, evolving into a full-fledged Islamic bank by 1988 through the Al Rajhi family's entrepreneurial efforts in finance and trade across the Gulf region.[10][21] The Al Rajhi family, originating from modest beginnings, built the institution on principles of Sharia-compliant banking, with Sulaiman Al Rajhi recognized as a key architect who later became one of the world's wealthiest philanthropists, endowing billions to Islamic causes.[121] Although the bank transitioned to a public joint-stock company listed on the Tadawul exchange, the family retains substantial influence through board representation and ownership stakes, including Sulaiman Al Rajhi's position as the largest individual shareholder among family members as of 2025.[12] Current board leadership features prominent Al Rajhi family members, such as Abdullah bin Sulaiman bin Abdulaziz Al Rajhi serving as Chairman, providing strategic oversight rooted in the family's foundational legacy, alongside Badr bin Mohammed Al Rajhi as a director with expertise in industrial enterprises.[122][123] Other board directors include non-family executives like Ibrahim bin Mohammed Alromaih and Abdulaziz bin Khalid Alghufaily, ensuring a blend of familial continuity and external governance.[122] This structure reflects the family's ongoing commitment to the bank's direction while adhering to Saudi regulatory requirements for diversified leadership in publicly traded entities. In executive management, the bank is led by non-family professionals to operationalize daily functions, with Waleed bin Abdullah Ali Al-Mogbel as Managing Director and Chief Executive Officer since his appointment, overseeing a workforce of over 10,000 and driving digital and retail banking expansions.[124][125] Abdulrahman Abdullah Al-Fadda serves as Chief Financial Officer and Acting Chief Risk Officer, managing fiscal strategies amid the bank's assets exceeding SAR 700 billion as of mid-2025.[124][126] Additional key roles include Abdullah Saleh Al-Omari in risk and compliance, emphasizing the separation of family strategic input from professional execution to mitigate conflicts in a Sharia-compliant framework.[124] This delineation supports the bank's growth while preserving family stewardship without direct operational control by relatives in top executive posts.

Governance Mechanisms

Al Rajhi Bank's governance mechanisms form a hierarchical structure designed to align strategic decision-making with regulatory requirements, Sharia principles, and risk oversight. The framework centers on the General Assembly of shareholders, which elects the Board of Directors every three years and approves major policies, providing ultimate accountability. The Board of Directors, chaired by Abdullah bin Sulaiman Al Rajhi, comprises non-executive members including family representatives and independents, responsible for setting strategic objectives, approving budgets, and ensuring compliance with Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority (SAMA) and Capital Market Authority standards.[122][123] Integral to the structure is the Shariah Board, an independent body chaired by Sheikh Abdulaziz Bin Hameen Al Hamain, which supervises all products, transactions, and investments for Islamic compliance. Composed of senior Islamic scholars, it issues binding fatwas, reviews contracts, and conducted over 150 supervisory visits in a recent period while issuing 260 decisions to guide operations across the bank and subsidiaries. This dual oversightâ€”corporate via the Board and religious via the Shariah Boardâ€”distinguishes Islamic banking governance and mitigates risks of non-compliance with Sharia prohibitions on interest and speculation.[65][64][67] Five Board committees further operationalize governance: the Executive Committee handles interim approvals; the Nomination and Remuneration Committee oversees director selection and compensation; the Governance Committee enforces ethical policies and board evaluations; the Audit Committee reviews financial reporting and internal controls; and the Risk Management Committee (BRMC) monitors risk exposure, appetite, and mitigation in alignment with Basel III adaptations for Islamic finance. These committees, supported by executive-level groups, convene regularly to delegate Board duties while maintaining escalation protocols for material issues.[68][92] Complementing these are internal policies, including a Code of Conduct, Anti-Bribery and Corruption measures, and a Business Continuity Management System overseen by the Business Continuity Management Committee, which promote transparency and resilience. The framework emphasizes board independence, with non-executive directors comprising a majority, and annual evaluations to uphold SAMA-mandated practices amid the bank's growth as the world's largest Islamic bank by market value.[127][128]
