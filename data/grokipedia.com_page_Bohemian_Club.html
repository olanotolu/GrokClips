Bohemian Club

The Bohemian Club is a private, all-male social and artistic club founded in San Francisco in 1872 by a group of newspapermen, artists, actors, and musicians seeking respite from professional rigors through creative pursuits and camaraderie.[1][2] Incorporated on April 9 of that year, it originated as a gathering for bohemian types emphasizing theatricals, literature, and informal discourse, evolving to include affluent patrons to sustain its activities.[3][4] The club operates from a historic clubhouse in San Francisco's Nob Hill district and owns Bohemian Grove, a 2,700-acre redwood forest encampment near Monte Rio in Sonoma County, site of its annual two-week midsummer retreat since the late 1870s.[4][5] These encampments feature member-produced plays, concerts, and "Lakeside Talks" by invited speakers on topics ranging from science to policy, fostering bonds among approximately 2,500 members drawn from elite strata in business, government, and culture, though admission requires sponsorship and blackball-proofing.[4][6] Notable for its owl emblem symbolizing wisdom and its ritualistic opening ceremony, the "Cremation of Care," which dramatizes banishing worldly worries before a 40-foot owl statue, the club's opacity has fueled speculation of undue influence among power holders, yet documented evidence reveals primarily fraternal recreation and artistic endeavor rather than coordinated decision-making.[4] Its male-only policy and high barriers to entry underscore exclusivity, drawing criticism for elitism, while defenders highlight its role in preserving a space for unscripted intellectual exchange amid institutional pressures.[4][7]

History

Origins in Bohemianism and Founding (1872)

The Bohemian Club originated amid San Francisco's post-Gold Rush bohemian milieu, where journalists, writers, and performers cultivated informal networks to escape bourgeois constraints and pursue artistic expression. This local iteration of bohemianism echoed European precedents, emphasizing nonconformist lifestyles, literary salons, and mutual support among creative professionals in a rapidly commercializing city.[8][9] By the early 1870s, such gatherings had evolved from ad hoc tavern meetings into structured associations, reflecting a desire for dedicated spaces amid economic pressures on the press.[10] In late winter or early spring of 1872, approximately half a dozen San Francisco newspapermen, styling themselves as the city's artistic vanguard, formalized the Bohemian Club to promote fellowship, intellectual discourse, and cultural activities among like-minded men.[8][4] The founding cohort, including journalist Dan O'Connell, prioritized journalists initially but extended invitations to actors, lawyers, and musicians seeking respite from professional rigors.[10] Official incorporation occurred on April 22, 1872, with bylaws emphasizing conviviality over elitism, though membership remained male-only and invitation-based from inception.[11] Early activities centered on theatrical productions, readings, and debates at rented venues, embodying bohemian ideals of collaborative creativity without immediate commercial intent. This foundation distinguished the club from mere drinking societies, as participants viewed it as a bulwark against San Francisco's growing materialism.[8][12] Within months, the group adopted the owl as a nascent emblem of wisdom and vigilance, underscoring their self-perceived role as cultural custodians.[13]

Twentieth-Century Developments and Post-War Growth

In the early decades of the twentieth century, the Bohemian Club experienced significant membership expansion alongside infrastructural developments at Bohemian Grove. By 1914, total membership stood at 1,259, comprising 787 residents, 241 non-residents, and military affiliates including 19 Navy and 49 Army officers.[15] This growth reflected increasing appeal among San Francisco's elite, with one in four members of the exclusive Pacific Union Club also belonging to the Bohemian Club by 1906.[4] Concurrently, the Grove's acreage expanded through land acquisitions, reaching 2,700 acres by 1944 to safeguard against encroaching development, while internal features like a 100-by-400-foot lake were constructed in the mid-1920s and a concrete owl shrine completed in 1929; these supported 169 encampment camps by that year.[4][15] Membership composition shifted notably during this period, incorporating more business leaders and capitalists alongside its artistic origins, with 31% of local members listed in the Social Register by 1907.[4] By 1933, numbers approached 2,000, necessitating clubhouse renovations that temporarily relocated activities to the Sir Francis Drake Hotel.[15] This influx of industrialists, publishers, and professionals marked a transition toward a network of power elites, including presidents and corporate directors, while maintaining traditions like Grove Plays and Lakeside Talks.[8] World War II interrupted growth, with membership declining to 1,647 by 1941 and encampments suspended from 1943 to 1945.[15] Post-war recovery saw resumption of activities and steady rebound, culminating in thousands of membersâ€”predominantly wealthy conservativesâ€”by the club's 1972 centennial.[8] The Grove's camps later consolidated to 124 by 1994 amid ongoing maintenance, such as the 1981 lake relining with concrete and earth, underscoring sustained operational expansion.[15] This era solidified the club's role as a retreat for influential figures, including post-war political attendees like Ronald Reagan.[4]

Facilities and Properties

San Francisco Clubhouse

The San Francisco clubhouse of the Bohemian Club is located at 624 Taylor Street, at the corner of Post and Taylor Streets in the Lower Nob Hill neighborhood of San Francisco, two blocks west of Union Square.[16] This six-story masonry structure serves as the club's primary urban facility for year-round social gatherings, artistic performances, and business meetings among members, complementing the seasonal Bohemian Grove encampment.[17] The building includes dining rooms, a bar, library, art gallery, theater, meeting spaces, and guest accommodations on upper floors.[17][18] Following the 1906 San Francisco earthquake and fire, which destroyed early club properties, the Bohemian Club operated from temporary locations before establishing a permanent city headquarters.[19] An earlier clubhouse designed by Loring P. Rixford in 1909â€“1910 at the same site was a three-story brick building, but the current structure was constructed in 1934 under architect Lewis P. Hobart.[20][21] Hobart's design adopted a five-story brick-sheathed form with a Georgian Revival aesthetic, incorporating Mediterranean Revival and Art Deco elements prevalent in 1930s San Francisco architecture.[16][22] The building's facade features a three-part vertical composition: a high exposed base on Post Street tapering uphill to no base on Taylor Street, accented by decorative spandrel brickwork and Art Deco-style window bays.[16] Terra cotta bas-relief panels by sculptor Haig Patigian adorn the exterior between the second and third floors, depicting allegorical figures related to the club's bohemian ethos.[16][23] Plaques bearing the club's owl emblem and motto, "Weaving spiders come not here," are also prominent on the exterior, echoing traditions from the club's founding era.[13] Additional sculptures include a Jo Mora piece and a plaque illustrating characters from Bret Harte's works, honoring the club's literary roots.[24] Interior spaces support the club's focus on arts and camaraderie, with lounges for informal discussions and dedicated areas for theatrical "jinks" performances.[20] The clubhouse remains an active hub for elite networking, though access is restricted to members and invited guests, preserving its private character since inception.[18]

Bohemian Grove Encampment Grounds

The Bohemian Grove comprises a 2,700-acre private campground situated in Monte Rio, Sonoma County, California, approximately 75 miles north of San Francisco along the Russian River. Owned by the Bohemian Club, a San Francisco-based gentlemen's club established in 1872, the grounds serve as the primary site for the club's annual midsummer retreats. The property features a dense stand of ancient coastal redwoods, some exceeding 300 feet in height and over a thousand years old, preserved amid surrounding timberlands.[4][10] Initial encampments began in 1878 at rented sites nearby, transitioning to club-owned land with the purchase of 160 acres in 1899 for $27,000; subsequent acquisitions, including expansions in the 1910s to avert real estate development, enlarged the holdings to the current extent by 1944. Development emphasized rustic infrastructure integrated into the natural terrain, including selective clearing for campsites while maintaining the redwood canopy. An artificial lake was constructed as a central feature, alongside a mountain stream proximate to early camp areas, supporting both recreational and performative uses.[25][4][10] The grounds host over 100 individual campsâ€”clustered enclaves of cabins and leisure quartersâ€”such as Mandalay, Hill Billies, Owlâ€™s Nest, and Isles of Aves, designed for small groups of members and guests. Infrastructure includes tram systems for internal transport, a clubhouse architecturally styled by Bernard Maybeck, amphitheaters, and stages amid the forest, facilitating communal gatherings in a setting that blends seclusion with organized amenities. These elements underscore the site's evolution from informal artist retreats to a structured, elite preserve.[4][10]

Membership and Governance

Eligibility Criteria and Selection Process

Membership in the Bohemian Club is restricted to men, a policy the organization has maintained despite legal challenges under California's Unruh Civil Rights Act, successfully arguing its status as a private club with selective criteria focused on personal associations rather than public accommodation.[28] The club's bylaws require that at least 100 members be "professional" individualsâ€”such as artists, musicians, writers, actors, or other creative professionalsâ€”to preserve its founding emphasis on bohemian arts and intellectual pursuits, though the majority of members today consist of businessmen, politicians, and other elites deemed compatible with this ethos.[4] Prospective members must be nominated by at least two regular members in good standing, who provide endorsements attesting to the candidate's character, professional accomplishments, and qualities that would enhance the club's social and intellectual cohesion.[4] Nominations undergo review by a membership committee, which evaluates suitability based on alignment with the club's private, invitation-only nature; approval advances the candidate to a vote by the full membership, where consensus is required amid long waiting lists that can span years or decades due to the cap of approximately 2,500 members.[29] This process prioritizes personal networks and vetted compatibility over open applications, reflecting the club's origins in fostering exclusive bonds among those with influence in arts, business, and governance.[4] New members pay an initiation fee of about $25,000 upon acceptance, in addition to substantial annual dues that contribute to operational costs exceeding millions annually, as reported in the club's nonprofit filings.[29][30] These financial barriers, combined with the nomination and vetting requirements, ensure selectivity, with historical data indicating waitlists often extending 10 to 15 years for high-profile candidates.[29]

Composition and Notable Members

The Bohemian Club's membership comprises influential men from diverse professional backgrounds, including business executives, government officials, artists, musicians, and journalists, reflecting its origins in fostering camaraderie between creative "bohemians" and practical leaders.[31][8] The club enforces an all-male policy and limits total membership to around 2,500 individuals, prioritizing those who can contribute to its artistic and intellectual traditions while upholding exclusivity through sponsorship by existing members.[32] This composition has evolved from its 19th-century focus on San Francisco's literary and journalistic elite to a broader assembly of national and international figures post-World War II, with businessmen and policymakers increasingly predominant.[33] Notable political members have included U.S. Presidents Herbert Hoover, Richard Nixon, Ronald Reagan, and Dwight D. Eisenhower, alongside figures such as California Governor Edmund G. Buckley Jr., maintaining the club's foundational emphasis on cultural pursuits amid its elite networking.[34] Early 20th-century artist-members included painters like Maynard Dixon and Norton Bush, who helped establish the club's bohemian ethos through exhibitions and performances.[36]

Internal Structure and Leadership

The Bohemian Club operates under a governance structure centered on a board of directors comprising 15 members, elected exclusively from and by the club's regular members.[4] This board holds authority over major decisions, including financial oversight, facility management, and policy formulation for both the San Francisco clubhouse and Bohemian Grove encampment. Regular members, predominantly businessmen who pay full dues, constitute the voting electorate, ensuring that leadership reflects the interests of this core group, which has grown dominant since the club's early years when artistic founders sought financial backing from affluent patrons.[4] Executive leadership is provided by officers including a president, one or more vice presidents, a secretary, and a treasurer, positions typically drawn from the board or senior membership and serving terms aligned with annual or biennial cycles as reflected in tax filings.[30] The president, for instance, directs high-level operations and represents the club in external matters, such as approvals for property use.[4] These roles emphasize administrative efficiency and continuity, with the treasurer managing the club's substantial assets derived from dues and endowments. Membership structure supports this hierarchy through differentiated classes: regular members exercise full privileges including voting and encampment participation; performing members, often artists or musicians, contribute to entertainments like theatrical productions in exchange for reduced dues; and honorary members, elected for eminence in arts, business, or public life, incur no fees but lack voting rights.[4] This tiered system, evolved from the club's 1872 origins among journalists and bohemians, incentivizes artistic input while prioritizing fiscal stability from regular members, who now form the majority. Committees subordinate to the board handle specialized functions, such as encampment logistics and artistic programming, fostering operational specialization without diluting board authority.[4]

Activities and Traditions

Annual Midsummer Encampment

The Bohemian Club's Annual Midsummer Encampment originated in 1878 as a farewell gathering for a departing actor member, held initially near Lagunitas Creek in Marin County, California. Early iterations rotated among sites in Northern California, including Duncans Mills, Guerneville, and Camp Meeker, before settling in the Monte Rio area in 1893 on leased land from the Sonoma Lumber Company. To secure the redwood forest for ongoing use and prevent logging, the club purchased the Bohemian Grove propertyâ€”spanning approximately 2,700 acresâ€”in 1899 from Melvin Cyrus Meeker, who had operated a logging venture there; subsequent acquisitions expanded holdings through the mid-20th century.[37][38][4] Held annually in July at Bohemian Grove in Monte Rio, Sonoma County, the encampment typically lasts two weeks, encompassing three weekends and attracting peak attendance of around 2,000 male participantsâ€”primarily club members with a limited number of invited guests. The grounds feature over 115 private camps, communal enclaves equipped with cabins, tents, and amenities ranging from rustic to elaborate, complete with personal staff in some cases; notable examples include Mandalay, an upscale hilltop site, and Hill Billies, emphasizing intimate socializing within the redwood canopy.[37][4][39] The event operates as an all-male retreat, employing roughly 300 male staff to handle logistics, maintenance, and services, with no women permitted on the premises. It structures daily life around communal meals, outdoor recreation, and member-led entertainments designed to promote artistic expression, relaxation, and bonds among attendees, insulated from external professional demands. Midweek numbers are lower, allowing quieter interludes, while weekends intensify activity across the site's trails, lakeside areas, and performance venues.[40][37][4]

The Cremation of Care Ritual

The Cremation of Care is an elaborate theatrical ritual performed annually by Bohemian Club members to open their two-week midsummer encampment at Bohemian Grove, a 2,700-acre redwood forest retreat near Monte Rio, California.[41] First conducted in 1880 during an early club outing at Meeker's Grove in Sonoma County, it has since become a scripted tradition emphasizing escape from daily burdens.[4] The ceremony, lasting approximately 50 minutes, involves around 250 participants portraying roles such as hooded priests and torchbearers, and is attended by roughly 1,500 members and guests, including business leaders, politicians, and artists.[41] The ritual begins with a procession carrying a coffin containing an effigy representing "Dull Care"â€”symbolizing worldly worries and responsibilitiesâ€”to an artificial lake and a 30- to 40-foot concrete owl-shaped altar.[41] [40] Elements include chants such as "Begone, detested Care, begone!", performances by a hamadryad figure, music, and a climactic symbolic cremation of the effigy accompanied by fireworks.[41] This mock-Druidic production unfolds before the owl statue, a central Grove symbol, on the first Saturday night of the encampment, typically in July.[4] [42] Its purpose is to ritually expel participants' cares, promoting fellowship and relaxation while adhering to the club's motto, "Weaving spiders come not here," which prohibits business discussions during the retreat.[41] Members view it as a key event fostering group cohesion and tradition, with informants describing it as a "big deal" that reinforces shared identity among attendees.[4] Documented in club pamphlets and historical reports, the ceremony maintains a formal, symbolic structure without evidence of deviation from its established form across over a century of performances.[41]

Artistic Performances, Talks, and Social Customs

The Bohemian Club's artistic performances originated with the "High Jinks," first staged in 1872 shortly after the club's founding as an unpretentious, all-male entertainment featuring intellectual revels with moderate drinking, inspired by literary traditions.[8] These evolved by incorporating a "Low Jinks" counterpart of more vulgar elements, separated by drinks and dinner, and shifted outdoors in 1878 for midsummer gatherings in coastal forests.[8] By the late 1890s, performances had developed into elaborate Grove Plays, formalizing as musical pageants in 1902 within a natural redwood amphitheater during the annual encampment.[4][8] Grove Plays typically feature mythical or fantastical themes, with some historical settings, staged with grand sets, costumes, and orchestral accompaniment, often carrying moral undertones and achieving a quasi-religious tone through institutional tradition.[4][8] Examples include Truth, Life, Wings, and The Quest of the Gorgon, reflecting influences from theatrical producer David Belasco's majestic style.[8] A specific production, The Eldorado in 1977, dramatized greedy gold miners displacing Spaniards in 19th-century California, with club members like Casper Weinberger in minor roles.[34] Lakeside Talks, held daily at 12:30 p.m. beside an artificial lake, consist of informal addresses by prominent figures assuming the audience's capacity for influence on discussed issues.[4] Historical speakers have included Dwight D. Eisenhower prior to his presidency and Robert F. Kennedy during his senatorial tenure, alongside regular appearances by Herbert Hoover after his 1913 club membership.[34] In 1977, former California Governor Edmund G. "Pat" Brown spoke on state water issues and the Central Valley Project, while Gerald R. Ford outlined national recovery plans and John McCosker addressed sex-changing fish species near the Galapagos.[34] Social customs during the encampment emphasize relaxation and cohesion through camp-based activities, with over 100 themed camps like Mandalay and Cave Man hosting intimate gatherings, rivalries, and specialized events such as the Poison Oak camp's Bulls' Balls Lunch featuring roasted cattle testicles.[4][34] Traditions include all-day music, nighttime programs of comedy skits, singing, and acting open to all members, alongside toasts, lamb-chop feasts, mint juleps, and recreations like golf, skeet shooting, and canoeing, supported by substantial wine and liquor provisions but prohibiting radios, televisions, and most external media.[4][8][34] These practices foster a fraternity-like atmosphere of tribal camaraderie, detached from professional concerns.[8]

Cultural and Societal Impact

Fostering Creativity and Elite Networks

The Bohemian Club promotes creativity through its longstanding tradition of producing original Grove Plays, elaborate musical dramas crafted and staged by members at the annual Bohemian Grove encampment. These works, which trace their origins to the club's inaugural "High Jinks" theatrical gatherings in 1878, feature custom-composed music, scenery built on-site, and narratives drawing from mythology, history, and nature, involving collaboration among artists, composers, and performers within the membership.[43] By 1908, critic and member Porter Garnett defended the plays as a structured outlet for artistic expression, countering perceptions of amateurism with their scale and member-driven innovation.[9] This participatory format has sustained creative output for over a century, with annual productions requiring hundreds of hours from volunteers, fostering skills in writing, directing, and production among professionals who balance these pursuits with primary careers.[14] Complementing artistic endeavors, the club cultivates elite networks via the relaxed, sequestered environment of Bohemian Grove, where approximately 2,000 to 3,000 attendeesâ€”predominantly affluent men from business, politics, and cultureâ€”engage in unstructured socializing around campfires, concerts, and private tents.[4] The daily Lakeside Talks, held since at least the early 20th century, feature addresses by high-profile speakers on topics ranging from economic policy to scientific advancements, enabling direct access and rapport-building among influencers; for instance, talks have included Treasury officials elucidating fiscal intricacies to audiences of bankers and executives.[44] Empirical studies of membership rosters reveal a statistically significant overrepresentation of corporate leaders and policymakers, with network analyses indicating that Bohemian affiliations correlate positively with positions in the upper echelons of American power structures, facilitating informal alliances and resource exchanges beyond formal hierarchies.[45] Such dynamics underscore the club's role in bridging creative and establishment spheres, though academic examinations of these ties, often conducted by sociologists critiquing concentrated influence, highlight potential risks of insular decision-making detached from broader societal input.[4]

Contributions to American Arts and Leadership

The Bohemian Club, chartered in 1872 in San Francisco, emerged as a hub for artistic innovation during the post-Gold Rush era, drawing writers including Ambrose Bierce, Joaquin Miller, Henry George, and honorary members Mark Twain and Bret Harte, with subsequent affiliates such as Jack London and Frank Norris.[36][8] From the 1870s to about 1915, it nurtured a peak period for West Coast visual arts, particularly landscape painting, through members like William Keith, Thomas Hill, Maynard Dixon, and Jules Tavernier, whose 1878 work Dance in a Subterranean Roundhouse at Clear Lake, California exemplified the club's encouragement of regionally inspired creativity.[36][6] Artistic traditions solidified with the debut of "High Jinks" intellectual revels in 1872, followed by the first outdoor performance on June 29, 1878, in Marin County, and the inception of elaborate Grove Plays in 1902 at Bohemian Grove near Monte Rio.[8] These annual musical pageants, staged in a natural amphitheater with large-scale sets and orchestras, integrated theatrical elements inspired by figures like David Belasco, sustaining a bohemian ethos amid evolving membership.[8] Shifting toward leadership influence, the club integrated business and political elites, with members serving as CEOs of entities including Bank of America, General Motors, and Procter & Gamble, alongside government roles held by George H.W. Bush, George Shultz as Secretary of State, and Caspar Weinberger as Secretary of Defense.[4] Network analyses indicate its structural centrality (scoring 0.62 among elite organizations) fostered cohesion, evidenced by overlaps like 31% of local members in the 1907 San Francisco Social Register.[4] The annual Midsummer Encampment's Lakeside Talks exemplified this by hosting policy-oriented addresses; Richard Nixon's July 1967 speech urged a "long view" of global shifts, including Soviet-Chinese tensions and African instability, while calling for foreign aid modernization focused on agriculture and private enterprise, alongside sustained military edge over the Soviet Union.[46] Such gatherings reinforced informal bonds among influencers, contributing to elite coordination without formal decision-making structures.[4]

Role in Countercultural and Conservative Bonding

By the late 19th century, however, membership expanded to include wealthy businessmen and conservative elites, with 31% of local members listed in the San Francisco Social Register by 1907, shifting the club's composition toward affluent, predominantly Republican participants. Conspiracy Theories and Empirical Debunkings

The Bohemian Club's secrecy and elite membership have spawned numerous conspiracy theories, particularly centered on the annual midsummer encampment at Bohemian Grove, where proponents allege occult rituals, human sacrifices, and covert plotting by global power brokers to orchestrate events like wars or the New World Order.[32] These narratives often interpret the club's owl symbol as a pagan deity akin to Moloch and claim that decisions such as the development of the atomic bomb were hatched there, though declassified documents attribute the Manhattan Project's origins to Los Alamos and other sites in 1942.[32][4] A pivotal event fueling these claims was conspiracy theorist Alex Jones' unauthorized entry into Bohemian Grove on July 15, 2000, alongside journalist Jon Ronson, during which Jones secretly filmed portions of the Cremation of Care ceremony from a hillside vantage point and subsequently portrayed it in his documentary Dark Secrets: Inside Bohemian Grove as ritualistic mock human sacrifice to an ancient Canaanite god.[32] Jones' footage, capturing robed figures chanting before a large owl statue and the burning of an effigy, has been cited by theorists as proof of satanic worship and elite immorality, with extrapolations linking it to broader Illuminati-style cabals.[32][50] Empirical examinations reveal the Cremation of Care as a scripted theatrical pageant first performed in 1881, symbolizing the symbolic incineration of "Dull Care"â€”personified as an intrusive figureâ€”to permit participants a temporary escape from professional burdens, enacted annually on the encampment's opening night with pyrotechnics, music, and actors but involving no violence or supernatural intent.[4] Sociologist G. William Domhoff's analysis, drawn from participant-observation and archival review in his 1974 book The Bohemian Grove and Other Retreats, describes the ritual and encampment activities as mechanisms for fostering interpersonal bonds through art, lectures, and recreation among affluent men, rather than venues for policy dictation or esoteric rites, noting that "Lakeside Talks" feature prepared speeches on topics like science or economics but lack binding outcomes.[4][41] Skeptic Michael Shermer, evaluating attendee accounts and historical records, likens Bohemian Grove to a fusion of TED conferences and artistic festivalsâ€”exclusive and eccentric, with no women or recording devices to preserve an atmosphere of uninhibited discussionâ€”yet devoid of evidentiary support for conspiratorial malfeasance, attributing persistent myths to the human tendency to infer malice from opacity rather than mundane networking.[50] Despite over 140 years of operations involving thousands of participants, including U.S. presidents like Richard Nixon and Ronald Reagan, no verifiable leaks, legal convictions, or physical traces substantiate claims of criminality or occultism; instead, documented outcomes emphasize cultural productions and informal camaraderie, with secrecy rooted in private property norms rather than cover-ups.[4][32] The BBC has noted that while protests and infiltrations, such as those by activist groups in the 1980s, have publicized guest lists revealing high-profile attendees, these disclosures yielded no proof of illicit coordination, underscoring how confirmation bias in fringe interpretations amplifies theatrical elements into unfounded doomsday scenarios.[32][51]

Legal Challenges and Worker Treatment Allegations

The Bohemian Club has faced multiple lawsuits alleging violations of California labor laws, primarily concerning wage theft, overtime non-payment, and inadequate breaks for seasonal workers at its Bohemian Grove encampment. In June 2023, three former valets from the Monastery Camp filed a class-action complaint in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, claiming they endured 16-hour workdays without meal or rest breaks during the annual July encampment, with captains from individual camps directing off-the-books cash payments to evade overtime, minimum wage, and benefit requirements.[52][53] The suit named the Bohemian Club as a joint employer alongside camp operators and payroll firms, asserting the club's oversight of encampment operations contributed to systemic underpayment affecting hundreds of workers across approximately 100 camps.[54] In January 2024, the court dismissed the Bohemian Club from the case, ruling plaintiffs failed to sufficiently allege joint employer liability, though claims against other defendants proceeded.[55][56] A prior 2016 class-action suit raised identical wage and hour violation claims against the club for Grove workers, resulting in a $7 million settlement without admission of liability.[49] In May 2025, a former cook at the club's San Francisco headquarters initiated another class-action lawsuit alleging similar failures to provide breaks and pay owed wages, seeking certification for affected employees.[57] These actions highlight recurring patterns of labor practices at the Grove, where temporary staff handle serving, cleaning, and maintenance for elite members, often under captains who prioritize operational demands over compliance.[58] Allegations of worker mistreatment extend to interactions with club members, as detailed in amended 2023 filings from the valets' suit. Plaintiffs accused billionaire William Koch of demanding a valet hand-wash his underwear during the 2022 encampment, describing it as humiliating exploitation, while three then-members of Congressâ€”David Valadao, Tom McClintock, and Doug LaMalfaâ€”allegedly made disparaging remarks about Islam in the presence of staff.[59][60] Such claims portray a hierarchical environment where low-wage workers serve high-profile attendees, including politicians and executives, amid the club's emphasis on seclusion and informality.[61] On discrimination fronts, the club defended its male-only membership and hiring policies in a 1986 appellate case against the California Fair Employment and Housing Commission, arguing exemption as a private social club under the Fair Employment and Housing Act despite accusations of discriminatory employment practices.[62] A 2006 federal complaint by employee Miriam Greenwald alleged sex and disability harassment, sexual assault, retaliation, and denial of accommodations at the club's facilities, though the case's resolution details remain limited in public records.[63] These challenges underscore tensions between the club's exclusionary traditions and state anti-discrimination mandates, with courts often scrutinizing its nonprofit status and operational scope.[64]
