Anzac Day match

The Anzac Day match is an annual Australian rules football fixture in the Australian Football League (AFL) between the Collingwood Football Club and Essendon Football Club, played on 25 April at the Melbourne Cricket Ground as a key event commemorating the ANZAC tradition.[1][2] Inaugurated in 1995, it originated from a proposal by Essendon coach Kevin Sheedy in the mid-1990s, developed in partnership with the Returned and Services League of Australia to blend intense club rivalry with public remembrance of military service, beginning with a dramatic draw attended by over 87,000 spectators.[1][2] The event features pre-game ceremonies such as the Last Post, wreath-laying by club captains, and a march-past by current and former service personnel, emphasizing its dual role in sport and commemoration while generating significant funds for veteran support organizations.[3][4] It has evolved into one of the AFL's highest-profile games, routinely drawing crowds exceeding 90,000, with the 2023 match setting a post-1950s attendance record of 95,179 at the MCG.[5][6] Collingwood holds a historical edge with more victories, though outcomes like the inaugural draw and recent thrillers underscore its reputation for closely contested, high-stakes encounters.[1][3]

Historical Background

Early Football on Anzac Day

The observance of Anzac Day on April 25, commencing in 1916, initially precluded organized Australian rules football matches in Victoria due to government prohibitions on public sporting events and entertainment, intended to maintain the day's solemn commemorative focus amid ongoing World War I grief.[7] These restrictions, rooted in state legislation treating Anzac Day as a sacred half-holiday, persisted for over four decades, limiting football activities to informal or military contexts overseas, such as soldiers' games in Egypt that year, rather than domestic competitive fixtures.[4] An early domestic exception occurred in 1925, when the Melbourne Football Club staged an intraclub exhibition match at the Melbourne Cricket Ground, featuring players divided into red and blue teams in a non-competitive format described contemporaneously as a "delightful exhibition" that drew spectators without violating broader entertainment bans.[8] Such events remained rare, as Victorian authorities upheld prohibitions to prioritize dawn services, marches, and wreath-laying, reflecting a cultural emphasis on remembrance over recreation in the interwar period. The legislative landscape shifted in the late 1950s, with the ban on Anzac Day sports lifted conditionallyâ€”requiring half of gate receipts to benefit the Returned and Services League (RSL)â€”paving the way for official Victorian Football League (VFL) premiership matches in 1960.[7] On April 25 that year, two games marked the debut: Fitzroy defeated Carlton by 7 points (9.13.67 to 9.6.60) at Brunswick Street Oval, while Melbourne prevailed over St Kilda at Junction Oval, with the latter match drawing around 15,000 attendees despite its suburban venue.[9][10] These fixtures, scheduled as mid-week rounds, represented a tentative integration of football into Anzac traditions, though attendance and prominence remained modest compared to Saturday games, often confined to peripheral grounds until broader AFL expansion.[1]

Match Execution and Records

Format, Venue, and Rules

The Anzac Day match is an annual Australian Football League (AFL) fixture contested between Collingwood Football Club and Essendon Football Club on 25 April, forming part of the league's premiership season schedule, typically in Round 7.[29][1] The game adheres to standard AFL playing rules, including four quarters of 20 minutes each plus time-on for stoppages, with teams of 22 players on the field (18 on ground plus four interchange rotations, subject to substitution rules).[29] No unique rule variations apply to this match beyond the league's general regulations, such as those governing scoring (six points for a goal, one for a behind), marking, tackling, and umpire decisions.[29] The venue has been fixed at the Melbourne Cricket Ground (MCG) in Melbourne, Victoria, since the match's modern inception in 1995, accommodating capacities exceeding 100,000 spectators and hosting all editions without relocation.[1][5] This choice reflects the MCG's status as a traditional AFL heartland and its logistical suitability for large-scale commemorative events tied to Anzac Day observances.[1] Ground management protocols, including open-roof stipulations for enclosed stadiums, apply as per AFL standards but are not uniquely enforced here.[29]

Anzac Eve Matches

The Anzac Eve match is an annual Australian Football League (AFL) fixture played on 24 April between Melbourne and Richmond at the Melbourne Cricket Ground (MCG), established to extend commemorative events leading into Anzac Day.[41][42] The AFL announced the matchup on 30 October 2014, with the inaugural game occurring in 2015 as part of efforts to honor military service through sport, mirroring the Collingwood-Essendon Anzac Day tradition.[43] Crowds have consistently exceeded 50,000, peaking at over 71,000 in recent years, reflecting strong public engagement with the event's blend of competition and remembrance.[44] Pre-match ceremonies emphasize Anzac themes, featuring the lighting of a cauldron with a torch carried from the Shrine of Remembrance's Eternal Flame, performances by army bands, a motorcade of veterans, and the playing of The Last Post.[41][45][46] Players emerge through a shared banner after a minute's silence, fostering unity and tribute to Australian and New Zealand service personnel.[47] These rituals, introduced from the match's outset, aim to instill reflection amid the high-stakes contest, though attendance and execution vary with weather and team form.[48]

Interstate and International Clashes

The Australian Football League (AFL) schedules multiple matches during the Anzac Day round, extending the commemorative traditions beyond the flagship Collingwood-Essendon fixture at the Melbourne Cricket Ground. These additional games, often featuring interstate rivalries between Victorian-based clubs and teams from other states, incorporate pre-match ceremonies such as the Last Post, wreath-laying, and moments of silence to honor Australian and New Zealand military service members.[1] For instance, on April 25, 2025, Adelaide faced Fremantle at Optus Stadium in Perth, marking the Crows' fourth Anzac Day appearance and highlighting cross-state competition with tribute elements adapted to the host venue.[52] Similarly, historical matchups have included West Coast Eagles games, contributing to the broader round's attendance exceeding 100,000 across venues in some years.[53] Interstate clubs have advocated for hosting standalone Anzac Day blockbusters to decentralize the event from Melbourne, arguing it would amplify national engagement while preserving the day's solemnity. Proposals emphasize pairing traditional rivals or regional derbies, such as potential Adelaide-Port Adelaide or Sydney-GWS Giants clashes, to draw crowds comparable to the MCG event when Anzac Day aligns with public holidays.[54] However, logistical challenges, including fixture equity and television rights, have limited such shifts, with most interstate games remaining part of the standard round rather than elevated traditions.[55] Internationally, the ANZAC Cup represents an extension of the theme to Europe, organized annually by AFL Europe since 2009 in Villers-Bretonneux, France. This event pits Australian Spirit representative teams (selected from expatriate players based on merit) against French national sides in men's and women's matches, commemorating the 1918 Battle of Villers-Bretonneux where Australian forces reclaimed the town from German occupation on April 24-25, resulting in 2,473 Australian casualties.[56][57] Played on or near Anzac Day, the cup fosters Australia-France ties through footy, with ceremonies including torch-lighting and national anthems; the 2025 edition marked its 15th year, emphasizing the event's growth in promoting the ANZAC spirit abroad.[58]

Debates and Challenges

chair the four-person panel selecting the medal winner drew fan backlash, with accusations of lacking gravitas for such a poignant honor tied to Anzac remembrance. These incidents underscore recurring public frictions where partisan or procedural elements intersect with the match's ceremonial core, including player commitments to wear commemorative guernseys and participate in silences, amid calls to preserve the event's apolitical focus on sacrifice.[91]

On-Field and Logistical Disputes

In the 2019 Anzac Day match, several umpiring decisions in the final quarter sparked significant controversy, particularly among Essendon supporters who argued they cost the team a potential victory. Veteran umpire Shaun Ryan officiated the game and later defended five key calls, including interpretations of holding the ball and deliberate out of bounds, stating they were consistent with AFL rules at the time.[92] The AFL umpires' review subsequently cleared the decisions, affirming their correctness under the prevailing guidelines, though Essendon coach John Worsfold expressed frustration over perceived inconsistencies in application.[93] These incidents contributed to heightened tensions, culminating in Essendon fans booing Collingwood captain Scott Pendlebury post-match, an event linked by observers to the disputed officiating rather than unrelated player actions.[94] Logistical challenges have also arisen, notably in the 2025 Anzac Day clash at the Melbourne Cricket Ground, where heavy rain disrupted Essendon's interchange bench technology. Essendon coach Brad Scott reported a complete blackout of communications, video replay access, and player statistics for much of the game, leaving the coaching staff "as helpless as I've felt in a quarter" and unable to make informed substitutions.[95] In contrast, Collingwood's bench systems remained operational, prompting Scott to describe it as an "unfair advantage" exacerbated by the wet conditions.[95] The AFL acknowledged the issue stemmed from weather-related equipment failures but noted no formal protest was lodged by Essendon, highlighting vulnerabilities in match-day infrastructure under adverse conditions.[95] Earlier logistical disputes trace back to the match's origins, including a failed 1995 doubleheader experiment at a single venue that drew only 40,117 spectators due to poor scheduling and access issues, leading the AFL to abandon such formats.[96] While not recurring annually, these incidents underscore ongoing tensions between preserving the event's traditional fixture status and adapting to operational demands, such as equitable technology provision and rule clarity to minimize on-field inequities.[96]
