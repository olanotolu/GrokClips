Anwar Hossain Manju

Anwar Hossain Manju (born 1 January 1944) is a Bangladeshi politician, businessman, and chairman of the Jatiya Party (Manju), a splinter faction of the original Jatiya Party founded by Hossain Mohammad Ershad.[1][2] Elected seven times to the Jatiya Sangsad as the representative for Pirojpur-2 constituency—in 1986, 1988, 1991, 1996, 2001, 2014, and 2018—he served in multiple ministerial roles under Ershad's regime and subsequent Awami League-led governments, including as Minister of Water Resources, Communications, and Energy and Mineral Resources.[1][3] A graduate of the University of Dhaka with honors in geography, physics, and mathematics, and with further studies in international relations at Georgetown University, Manju has been recognized for contributions to infrastructure and energy development, such as expanding power generation capacity from 700 to 2,500 megawatts between 1985 and 1988, initiating the Jamuna Multipurpose Bridge project, and laying foundations for expanded road networks and the first Bangladesh-Kolkata railway link during 1996–2001.[1][4] His career has included vocal opposition to certain policies, such as Ershad's move to declare Islam the state religion, which he argued undermined Bangladesh's secular foundations, and more recently, criticisms of the interim government's handling of political figures following the 2024 political changes.[1][5] Manju faced legal challenges, including a 2024 arrest followed by bail on health grounds, amid Bangladesh's turbulent political landscape.[6]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Anwar Hossain Manju was born in 1944 in Pirojpur District, now in Bangladesh.[1][7] He is the son of Tofazzal Hossain Manik Mia (1911–1969), a leading journalist, political activist, and founder of the Dainik Ittefaq, established on 24 December 1953 as the first major Bengali-language daily in the region, which grew to become one of the country's most influential newspapers through its advocacy for democratic reforms and Bengali cultural identity.[6][8][9] Manju's family background placed him within Dhaka's journalistic and political circles, where his father faced repeated arrests by Pakistani authorities for editorials opposing military governance, fostering an environment of public engagement and media influence that extended to Manju's later roles at the newspaper, including as editor from 1972 to 1975.[9][3]

Academic background

Anwar Hossain Manju earned his honors degree in Geography, with supporting subjects in Physics and Mathematics, from the University of Dhaka.[1] [10] During his time at the university, he was active in student politics, serving as vice president of the Student League.[1] Manju pursued postgraduate studies in International Relations at Georgetown University in Washington, D.C., though specific degree details are not publicly documented.[1] [10] His academic training in geography and international relations has been noted in profiles as foundational to his later involvement in politics and media.[1] The University of Dhaka recognizes him as a notable alumnus.[4]

Pre-political endeavors

Business and entrepreneurial activities

Anwar Hossain Manju founded the Aegis Holdings Group, a conglomerate engaged in diversified manufacturing and service sectors in Bangladesh.[1] He holds the position of chairman of the board for MAST Packaging Ltd. and Zenith Packaging Ltd., firms recognized for their role in the packaging industry and clientele that includes international companies such as British-American Tobacco.[1] These enterprises represent his primary entrepreneurial pursuits outside of media and politics.

Involvement in journalism and media

Anwar Hossain Manju began his career in journalism at the Daily Ittefaq, a leading Bengali-language newspaper founded by his father, Tofazzal Hossain Manik Mia, in 1953. Following his father's assassination on June 1, 1969, Manju, along with his brother Mainul Hosein, assumed management responsibilities for the publication.[11][6] He served as editor and publisher of Daily Ittefaq from 1972 until 2007, during which the newspaper grew into one of Bangladesh's most influential vernacular dailies, known for its role in shaping public discourse. Manju's tenure spanned over three decades of direct involvement, contributing to its editorial direction and business operations amid Bangladesh's post-independence media landscape.[1][12][6] In addition to Ittefaq, Manju held the position of deputy chief news editor at Bangladesh Sangbad Sangstha (BSS), the country's national news agency, where he influenced wire service reporting on domestic and international affairs. Prior to his full entry into politics, these roles established him as a prominent figure in Bangladesh's media sector, recognized for building commercial success in print journalism.[13][1]

Political career

Initial entry and party affiliations

Anwar Hossain Manju entered national politics as a candidate of the Jatiya Party during the military regime of Hussain Muhammad Ershad, securing election to the Jatiya Sangsad for the first time in the May 1986 general election from the Pirojpur-4 constituency.[14] He retained the seat in the 1988 election and continued as a Jatiya Party lawmaker in subsequent parliaments, including victories in 1991 and 1996.[3] His early political involvement aligned with Ershad's ruling party, which was established in January 1986 to consolidate support for the regime's transition to civilian rule, and Manju rose to prominence within its ranks, serving in ministerial roles such as communications minister under Ershad's government in the late 1980s.[5] Manju's party affiliations remained tied to the Jatiya Party through the 1990s, where he held the position of secretary-general and advocated for alliances with the Awami League, opposing Ershad's 1994 decision to align with the Bangladesh Nationalist Party (BNP)-led opposition.[3] This pro-Awami League stance facilitated his appointment as communications minister in Sheikh Hasina's 1996-2001 coalition government, despite lacking a formal pre-election pact, as the Jatiya Party provided post-election support to form the administration. Tensions escalated in 2001 when Ershad opted to join a BNP-led coalition ahead of the October election, prompting Manju—having been involved with the party since the early 1980s—to break away and establish the Jatiya Party (Manju) faction, contesting independently under the bicycle symbol and securing his personal victory from Pirojpur-2.[3] The splinter group positioned itself as ideologically continuous with the original party but prioritized opposition to BNP alignments, later formalizing ties with the Awami League through the 14-party alliance for elections including 2014 and 2018. ![Anwar Hossain Manju at Rashtrapati Bhavan in New Delhi][float-right] Subsequent affiliations under the Jatiya Party (Manju) banner emphasized pragmatic coalitions for parliamentary seats, with Manju as chairman negotiating support extensions to Awami League governments in exchange for reserved berths, though the faction maintained autonomy from Ershad's mainline group.[1] This approach yielded wins in 2014 and 2018 from Pirojpur-2 but ended with defeat in the 2024 election, marking his first parliamentary loss after seven terms.[3]

Parliamentary elections and terms

Anwar Hossain Manju first entered the Jatiya Sangsad as a member from the Pirojpur-2 constituency in the February 1986 general election, representing the Jatiya Party under President Hussain Muhammad Ershad's regime.[3] He secured re-election from the same seat in the 1988 election, which was conducted on a non-party basis amid ongoing military rule.[3] Following the restoration of democracy, Manju won successive terms in the 1991, 1996, and 2001 general elections, aligning with the Jatiya Party faction amid shifting coalitions. These victories established him as a consistent representative of Pirojpur-2, drawing on familial political legacy and local influence. He did not contest the 2008 election, during which he faced legal proceedings related to corruption charges.[15] Manju returned to parliament in the 2014 general election as part of the 14-party alliance supporting the Awami League government, securing the Pirojpur-2 seat.[3] He repeated this success in the 2018 election, again under the alliance banner, marking his seventh term overall. During these terms, he contributed to parliamentary committees and held ministerial portfolios outside this section's scope. In the January 7, 2024, general election, Manju contested Pirojpur-2 under the Jatiya Party (Manju) but lost to independent candidate Mahiuddin Maharaj, marking his first electoral defeat after seven prior victories.[3] The upset occurred amid low voter turnout and widespread independent candidacies challenging alliance nominees.[16]

Alliances and opposition roles

Anwar Hossain Manju's Jatiya Party (Manju) has primarily aligned with the Awami League in electoral coalitions, reflecting a pragmatic approach to securing parliamentary representation amid Bangladesh's polarized politics. In July 2023, the party joined the Awami League-led 14-party alliance, agreeing to contest the January 2024 general elections under coordinated seat-sharing arrangements, with Manju nominated for Pirojpur-2.[17] [16] This alliance positioned his faction as a supporting partner to the ruling coalition rather than a confrontational opponent, consistent with prior collaborations such as the 2014 elections, where Jatiya Party (Manju) fielded 28 candidates backed by Awami League support during the Bangladesh Nationalist Party's boycott.[18] Despite these alliances, Manju's parliamentary tenure included periods on opposition benches during Bangladesh Nationalist Party-led governments. Following the 2001 elections, Jatiya Party (Manju) secured one seat, enabling it to function as part of the opposition to the BNP administration from 2001 to 2006.[19] In instances of intra-party tensions within broader Jatiya Party dynamics, Manju-led lawmakers occasionally defied official opposition boycotts, such as attending the president's address in parliament despite party directives, highlighting selective engagement over rigid adversarialism.[20] Manju's faction has rarely mounted sustained opposition campaigns against the Awami League, instead leveraging alliances for ministerial berths, including his own role as Environment and Forest Minister from 2014 onward under the Awami League government.[21] This pattern underscores a history of coalition-building over principled opposition, with the party criticizing ruling dispensations only sporadically, as seen in Manju's 2013 declaration against joining the then-proposed Awami League alliance before ultimately aligning.

Leadership of Jatiya Party (Manju)

Split from main Jatiya Party

In 1999, the Jatiya Party experienced a significant internal schism when secretary general Anwar Hossain Manju refused to endorse the party's alignment with the BNP-led four-party opposition alliance in preparation for the 2001 general elections.[22] Manju, who had previously supported the Awami League's 1996 electoral victory and served as a minister in its coalition government, advocated maintaining ties with the ruling Awami League rather than shifting to opposition politics under Hussain Muhammad Ershad's leadership.[23] This disagreement, rooted in strategic differences over coalition partnerships and loyalty to the incumbent administration, prompted Manju's expulsion from the main party and the formation of a dissident faction.[24] The resulting splinter group, known as Jatiya Party (Manju), was established with Anwar Hossain Manju as chairman and Sheikh Shahidul Islam as secretary-general, retaining core ideological elements of the original party such as support for military-era policies while emphasizing pragmatic alliances with progressive forces.[19] The split weakened the main Jatiya Party's cohesion ahead of the 2001 polls, where Ershad's faction secured seats through the BNP alliance, but Manju's group positioned itself as an independent voice favoring continuity with Awami League governance.[25] This division highlighted recurring factionalism within the Jatiya Party, driven by personal ambitions and electoral opportunism rather than substantive policy rifts.[26]

Organizational development and ideology

The Jatiya Party (Manju) emerged as a splinter faction from the original Jatiya Party in 1999, formed by dissidents led by Anwar Hossain Manju following internal disagreements over leadership and electoral alliances. The split was precipitated by Manju's opposition to the main party's alignment with the Bangladesh Nationalist Party (BNP) in the mid-1990s, prompting him to contest the 1996 elections independently with support from the Awami League, after which he was expelled from the party's presidium.[25] This factional break formalized Manju's leadership, positioning the group as a smaller, independent entity focused on parliamentary representation in Manju's home constituency of Pirojpur-2, where it has historically secured seats through targeted alliances rather than broad organizational expansion.[27] Organizationally, the party remains tightly centralized under Manju's chairmanship, with limited evidence of robust grassroots structures or nationwide branches comparable to larger parties like the Awami League or BNP. Its electoral success has depended heavily on Manju's personal candidacy and ad hoc coalitions, such as the 2008 grand alliance with the Awami League, which enabled Manju to secure a ministerial portfolio and retain parliamentary influence.[18] The faction has not demonstrated significant membership growth or institutional development, often operating as a vehicle for Manju's political maneuvering amid the original Jatiya Party's repeated schisms, which have produced at least six factions by 2023.[28] This leader-centric model has constrained its autonomy, rendering it vulnerable to alliance dynamics and electoral seat-sharing negotiations. Ideologically, the Jatiya Party (Manju) aligns with the centrist-conservative orientation of the parent party, emphasizing nationalism, democratic governance, and moderate Islamic principles alongside respect for religious freedoms.[29] It prioritizes Bangladesh's sovereignty and independence, reflecting the original Jatiya Party's foundational tenets established under Hussain Muhammad Ershad, without notable deviations into left-leaning or Islamist extremes.[27] Pragmatism in alliances, such as periodic support for Awami League governments, underscores a non-doctrinaire approach focused on policy influence over rigid ideological purity, though splits within the broader Jatiya Party ecosystem have been driven more by power struggles than substantive belief divergences.

Ministerial positions

Tenure as Environment and Forest Minister

![Anwar Hossain Manju with Bangladeshi officials at Rashtrapati Bhavan][float-right] Anwar Hossain Manju was appointed Minister of Environment and Forests on 12 January 2014 as part of the Awami League-led coalition cabinet sworn in after the January 2014 general elections.[30] Representing the Jatiya Party faction allied with the ruling coalition, his tenure lasted until early January 2018, when he was reassigned to the Ministry of Water Resources.[31] Early in his term, Manju prioritized climate change in departmental policies, stating in August 2014 that work plans should address the crisis on a priority basis to mitigate environmental risks.[32] He publicly lamented the ministry's insufficient project pipeline, noting in September 2014 that unutilized funds were being diverted to other sectors due to a lack of viable environmental initiatives.[33] Domestically, he advocated for grassroots pollution control, urging citizens in September 2014 to foster pollution-free environments beginning at the household level to curb national degradation.[34] Manju engaged actively in international climate diplomacy, participating in workshops on Bangladesh's Nationally Determined Contributions in July 2013—prior to formal appointment but aligned with his portfolio—and subsequent global forums.[21] At COP21 in December 2015, he endorsed initiatives to reduce short-lived climate pollutants, crediting three years of progress and integrating such reductions into Bangladesh's revised National Environment Policy.[35] He further pushed for maximizing gains from COP22 in 2016 and critiqued U.S. policy shifts under President Trump in November 2017, arguing they undermined developing nations' climate efforts.[36][37] In 2017, Manju delivered keynotes emphasizing sustainable forest management amid economic expansion, highlighting challenges in maintaining forest cover during rapid development.[38] He also promoted green growth strategies as vital for Bangladesh's transition to upper-middle-income status, aligning environmental policies with broader economic goals.[39] His tenure coincided with ongoing inter-ministerial efforts on deforestation drivers and forest investment planning.[40]

Policy implementations and outcomes

During his tenure as Minister of Environment and Forests from January 2014 to January 2019, Anwar Hossain Manju emphasized climate adaptation strategies over mitigation efforts in international negotiations, arguing that Bangladesh, as a low-emission nation, suffered disproportionate impacts from industrialized countries' pollution and required focus on resilience measures like infrastructure upgrades.[41] This stance aligned with proposals to repair and elevate 1960s-era embankments to counter sea level rise and coastal flooding, though empirical data on completion rates or effectiveness during his term remains limited, with ongoing vulnerabilities evident in subsequent events like Cyclone Amphan in 2020.[42] Manju's ministry supported diagnostic studies, including a 2017 trend analysis of sea level rise along Bangladesh's coast, which projected annual rises of 3.4–7.9 mm and informed adaptive planning but did not translate into widely documented large-scale implementations.[43] Internationally, Bangladesh under his leadership joined the Bonn Challenge in 2017, pledging restoration of degraded lands toward a global 350 million hectare target by 2030, contributing to the initiative's milestone crossings; however, domestic forest cover outcomes showed minimal net gains, with deforestation pressures persisting amid competing land uses.[44] Domestically, Manju highlighted institutional shortcomings, publicly criticizing his own ministry in September 2014 for lacking viable projects, which led to unutilized funds being reallocated elsewhere, underscoring implementation gaps and limited tangible environmental gains.[33] Efforts to integrate short-lived climate pollutant reductions into national planning were advanced, as noted in 2015 COP21 engagements, but measurable emission declines attributable to these policies were not quantified in available reports from the period.[35] Overall, while advocacy for funding and adaptation elevated Bangladesh's profile in global forums—such as calls for clearer climate finance roadmaps at 2016 UN talks—outcomes reflected constrained domestic execution amid budgetary and capacity constraints.[45]

Achievements versus criticisms

During his tenure as Minister of Environment and Forests from November 2014 to January 2019, Anwar Hossain Manju emphasized international climate diplomacy, including advocacy for reducing short-lived climate pollutants at COP21 in 2015, where Bangladesh integrated such measures into national policy planning.[35] He also urged stakeholders to maximize benefits from COP22 in 2016 and delivered remarks on forest sustainability at UN-FAO events and COP23 side events, highlighting challenges like rapid urbanization's impact on forest cover.[36][38][46] Domestically, Manju established an 18-member taskforce in May 2015 to safeguard forest resources against illegal logging and encroachment, and contributed to Bangladesh's Third National Communication to the UNFCCC in 2018, documenting advancements in adaptation and mitigation strategies.[47][48] Critics, however, argued that Manju's ministry failed to curb ongoing environmental degradation, particularly wetland encroachment, as he inherited and extended approvals for filling such areas despite legal protections under the Bangladesh Environment Conservation Act.[49] In response to the December 2014 oil spill in the Sundarbans mangrove forest—caused by a tanker collision releasing over 350,000 liters of fuel oil—Manju downplayed long-term ecological damage, stating impacts were "not as bad as perceived earlier" and less severe than initially feared, a position contradicted by joint government-UN assessments recommending sustained monitoring and cleanup.[50][51][52] This stance drew scrutiny amid reports of inadequate enforcement, with rivers reopening to shipping routes through the forest exacerbating risks to biodiversity.[53] Manju himself lamented the ministry's lack of robust projects, noting in 2014 that unspent funds were redirected elsewhere due to implementation shortfalls, reflecting broader institutional constraints in prioritizing conservation over development pressures.[33] These issues were attributed by observers to coalition dynamics, where Manju's Jatiya Party role in the Awami League-led government limited confrontations with pro-growth policies, resulting in persistent forest loss—estimated at over 2,500 hectares annually during the period—despite taskforce efforts.[54][55]

Legal challenges

Historical charges and sentences

In June 2007, a Dhaka court sentenced Anwar Hossain Manju to five years' imprisonment in absentia for illegally possessing 21 bottles of alcohol at his residence without a permit, in violation of Bangladesh's narcotics laws.[56][57] Manju, who was abroad at the time, later secured bail in this case but the conviction's final status remained unresolved in public records beyond initial enforcement.[58] On June 10, 2008, another Dhaka court convicted Manju in absentia of extortion, sentencing him to seven years' rigorous imprisonment for allegedly demanding Tk 50 lakh from a contractor during his tenure as communications minister.[15][59] This case stemmed from complaints filed under the caretaker government's anti-corruption campaign, with no documented appeal or acquittal in subsequent judicial proceedings.[60] In a related graft probe, on August 27, 2008, a special court sentenced Manju to 13 years' imprisonment and a Tk 10 lakh fine (with additional jail time in default) for amassing Tk 1.72 crore in illegal wealth disproportionate to his known income sources between 2001 and 2006.[57][61] The Anti-Corruption Commission had initiated the charge sheet in October 2007, accusing him of concealing assets acquired through abuse of power.[62] Manju appealed the verdict; the High Court acquitted him on August 10, 2010, citing insufficient evidence of corruption, a decision upheld by the Supreme Court on November 15, 2015.[63][64] These convictions occurred amid the 2007–2008 military-backed caretaker administration's widespread purge of politicians on graft and related charges, many of which were issued in absentia against fugitives like Manju, who had fled the country.[65] While the extortion and alcohol cases lacked recorded reversals, the wealth acquittal highlighted evidentiary challenges in retrospective asset probes, as noted in court rulings.[66]

2024 arrest and subsequent release

On September 2, 2024, Anwar Hossain Manju, chairman of Jatiya Party (Manju faction), was arrested by the Detective Branch (DB) of Dhaka Metropolitan Police from his residence in Dhaka's Dhanmondi area.[67][11] The arrest stemmed from his implication in five separate cases filed at various police stations, including charges related to murder.[11][68] Manju was granted bail and released from DB custody approximately four hours later, around 8:00pm, with custody transferred to Daily Ittefaq Executive Editor Md. Moniruzzaman pending further proceedings.[6][8] The release was approved on grounds of his advanced age and reported illness, as stated by police officials.[6][11] Party joint secretary Rubel Howlader confirmed the release to media outlets, noting Manju's brief detention.[69]

Debates on political motivations

Manju's supporters and some international observers have contended that several of his legal cases, particularly those arising during the 2007-2008 military-backed caretaker government, were driven by political motives to neutralize figures allied with the opposition Jatiya Party. For instance, charges of graft leading to a 13-year sentence in absentia on August 27, 2008, and a separate five-year term for possessing 21 bottles of alcohol at his residence, were criticized as selective prosecutions amid a broader crackdown on politicians.[57] The U.S. Department of State's 2008 Human Rights Report highlighted patterns of politically motivated detentions and trials during this period, including against Jatiya Party leaders like Manju, noting the caretaker regime's use of anti-corruption drives to target rivals.[65] Analysts have pointed to the timing—amid Jatiya Party's opposition role—as evidence of causal links to power consolidation efforts rather than purely evidentiary merit.[70] Higher court interventions have fueled these arguments, with the High Court overturning key convictions, such as a graft-related sentence on August 23, 2010, and the Supreme Court upholding Manju's acquittal in the primary graft case on November 15, 2015, citing insufficient evidence of illicit wealth accumulation beyond Tk 1.72 crore alleged on October 7, 2007.[71][72] Critics of the original trials, including party affiliates, maintain that the reversals validate claims of fabricated or exaggerated charges to discredit Manju's political influence, especially given his prior ministerial roles under Jatiya Party governance. Opponents, however, argue the initial findings reflected genuine accountability for corruption prevalent in Bangladesh's patronage-driven politics, though without overturning the evidentiary basis in appeals.[63] In the context of the 2024 arrest on September 2, tied to longstanding murder accusations across five police stations, debates have been muted due to the brief four-hour detention and swift bail on health grounds the same day.[6] Manju's subsequent public statements have broadly accused the interim government of systematic "insults" and targeting of veteran politicians, implying a pattern of harassment amid post-2024 political realignments, but without directly attributing vendetta to his specific case.[2] This aligns with wider concerns over selective enforcement against Awami League coalition remnants, though verifiable evidence of orchestration remains contested and unadjudicated as of October 2025.

Recent political activities

Criticisms of interim government

Anwar Hossain Manju, chairman of Jatiya Party (Manju), has accused the interim government, led by Chief Adviser Muhammad Yunus since August 2024, of inflicting unprecedented humiliation on political leaders. On August 9, 2025, he stated that the manner of these actions "will be remembered in history," emphasizing the severity of the perceived insults directed at politicians during the government's tenure.[5][73][2] Manju warned that such conduct risks eroding public confidence in the electoral process, potentially leading to doubts about the credibility of future elections under the interim administration. He argued that the government's approach to handling opposition figures undermines the stability needed for a fair transition to elected governance.[73] These criticisms reflect broader tensions between established political parties and the student-led movement that propelled the interim government into power, with Manju positioning his party as a defender of traditional political norms against what he views as overreach by unelected authorities.[5]

Stances on reforms and elections

Anwar Hossain Manju, as chairman of Jatiya Party (Manju), has consistently advocated for structural reforms to the electoral system to promote fairness and independence. In January 2022, his party proposed that the next Election Commission be established via a dedicated constitutional law, emphasizing the inclusion of respected, non-bureaucratic personalities in the selection process to enhance acceptability and impartiality.[74] This stance underscored the need for executive cooperation to enable the commission to conduct free and fair polls, reflecting concerns over past bureaucratic dominance in electoral bodies.[74] Following the July 2024 political upheaval and the formation of an interim government, Manju expressed deep skepticism regarding the pace and sincerity of ongoing reforms. In August 2025, he warned that the government's approach risked indefinite delays, questioning whether reforms would ever conclude and casting doubt on the holding of elections altogether.[73] He highlighted potential irregularities in any future polls, sarcastically suggesting that parties might as well preemptively divide seats and ministries rather than invest in a Tk 50 billion election process lacking credibility.[73] Manju has criticized the interim administration for sidelining key political actors, including those involved in Bangladesh's independence struggle, and for relying on foreign advisers to draft reforms without clear implementation strategies.[73] He argued that such "humiliation" of leaders would be historically recorded, potentially eroding public trust in the electoral process and prioritizing a "politics of grabbing" over substantive democratic renewal.[73] These positions align with broader calls from opposition factions for expedited, transparent reforms to restore electoral integrity ahead of anticipated 2026 polls.[5]

Personal life

Family and personal relationships

Anwar Hossain Manju is married to Tasmima Hossain, who serves as editor of The Daily Ittefaq, the Bengali newspaper of which Manju is publisher.[9] Their daughter Tareen Hossain acts as the newspaper's publisher, reflecting family involvement in the media enterprise.[9] Another daughter, Seema Hossain, has been referenced in connection with the family's business and legal matters during Manju's ministerial tenure.[15]

Health concerns and travels

Anwar Hossain Manju, born in 1944, has faced health challenges associated with advanced age, reaching 80 years in 2024.[11] In September 2024, following his arrest by the Detective Branch of Dhaka Metropolitan Police on charges including murder and corruption, he was granted bail within hours due to illness and poor health condition.[6] [11] Earlier, in June 2009, Manju received bail while receiving heart treatment at BIRDEM hospital in Dhaka, after convictions totaling 32 years in multiple cases.[75] Manju's notable travels include an official visit to India in April 2016 as Minister of Environment and Forests, during which he met President Pranab Mukherjee at Rashtrapati Bhavan in New Delhi to discuss bilateral matters.[76] In September 2024, a Dhaka court imposed an international travel ban on him, alongside other former ministers, in connection with corruption investigations filed by the Anti-Corruption Commission.[77] No records indicate foreign travels specifically for medical treatment.
