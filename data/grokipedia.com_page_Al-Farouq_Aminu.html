Al-Farouq Aminu

Al-Farouq Ajadi Aminu (born September 21, 1990) is a Nigerian-American professional basketball player who last played for the Chicago Bulls of the National Basketball Association (NBA). He also represented the Nigeria national basketball team internationally. Aminu was selected by the Los Angeles Clippers in the 2010 NBA draft with the eighth overall pick, and has also played for the New Orleans Pelicans, Dallas Mavericks, Portland Trail Blazers, and Orlando Magic. High school career

Aminu went to Norcross High School and was ranked as one of the top college recruits in the nation from the class of 2008. He was ranked #7 in the nation by Rivals.com and #13 by Scout.com.[1] Aminu transferred to Norcross between his freshman and sophomore years from Wesleyan School, but was ruled ineligible and had to play on Norcross' JV team his sophomore year.[2] In his junior year, Aminu and teammate Gani Lawal led Norcross to a 30â€“3 record and a #12 national seed. Aminu averaged 13.7 points and 9.5 rebounds per game in his junior year. He led Norcross to back to back Georgia 5A state titles in 2007 and 2008. He averaged 23.1 points and 11.2 rebounds a game as a senior. Norcross finished 29â€“2 and ranked #6 in 2008. Aminu was a member of the 2008 McDonald's All-American Team and played in the Jordan Brand Classic where he had 12 points and 13 rebounds.[3] In 2008, Aminu was named first-team Parade All-American and Mr. Georgia Basketball.

College career

Aminu committed to Wake Forest in July 2007 and in November 2007 Aminu signed a Letter of Intent to play basketball at Wake Forest. He chose Wake Forest over Georgia Tech. As a freshman during the 2008â€“09 season, he was a unanimous selection for the ACC All-Freshman Team. Aminu posted 10 double-doubles on the year, including five in conference action. He led all freshmen and ranked fifth in the ACC with 8.2 rebounds per game. Aminu was also second among all league rookies with 12.9 points per game. As a sophomore in the 2009â€“10 season, Aminu averaged 15.8 points and 10.7 rebounds per game, leading the ACC in rebounding. He recorded 19 double-doubles, the most in the conference, and earned second-team All-ACC honors. On April 1, 2010, Aminu declared for the 2010 NBA draft.

On April 1, 2010, Aminu hired an agent and declared for the 2010 NBA draft.[4] He was selected with the eighth overall pick by the Los Angeles Clippers. In just his eighth game for the Clippers on November 9, 2010, he had a season-best game with 20 points (a career-high until March 31, 2016) and 8 rebounds against the New Orleans Hornets.[5]

International career

Aminu represented the Nigerian national basketball team. He competed at the 2012 Summer Olympics, where Nigeria finished 12th. He also played in the 2013 FIBA Africa Championship, averaging 11.9 points and 5.7 rebounds per game as Nigeria placed fourth. On August 30, 2015, Aminu with the D'Tigers of Nigeria won the 2015 FIBA Africa Championship (AfroBasket) in Tunisia by defeating Angola 74â€“65.[33] He was also named in the All-Star Five of the 2015 Afrobasket.[34] Aminu further represented Nigeria at the 2019 FIBA Basketball World Cup, where he averaged 8.2 points over five games, helping the team secure qualification for the 2020 Summer Olympics.

Personal life

Al-Farouq Aminu is married to Helina Tekeste Aminu. The couple have a daughter, Emanah, together. He is the child of a Yoruba father from Nigeria and an African American mother from New York. Aminu is descended from a line of Nigerian kings.[14] He was raised Muslim and observes aspects of the faith, such as Ramadan.[14] His name translates to "the chief has arrived."[35] (This also helps explain his nickname, "The Chief.") His brothers, Alade Aminu and Al-Wajid Aminu, have also played basketball; Alade is a professional forward.[36] Aminu and his wife are avid collectors of African art and have promoted it through exhibitions and galleries in Portland.[37] Aminu and his wife's foundation, Aminu Good Works Foundation, organizes a yearly basketball camp in Nigeria since 2016. The camp takes place in Ibadan.[37]
