New Testament passages and accusations of antisemitism

Accusations of antisemitism in the New Testament focus on Gospel passages depicting Jewish religious authorities and crowds as complicit in Jesus' arrest, trial, and execution by Roman authorities, including Matthew 27:25 where a Jerusalem crowd declares, "His blood be on us and on our children," and the Gospel of John's repeated portrayal of "the Jews" as adversarial to Jesus, exemplified by John 8:44's claim that certain opponents are "of your father the devil."[1][2] These texts, written in the first century CE amid emerging separations between Jesus' Jewish followers and other Jewish groups, have been interpreted by critics as imputing collective culpability to Jews for deicideâ€”the killing of Godâ€”thereby laying a theological foundation for later hostilities, while proponents of contextual readings argue they constitute targeted critiques of specific leaders and reflect prophetic-style rebukes akin to those in the Hebrew Scriptures rather than blanket ethnic condemnation.[1][3] The controversy encompasses not only the Passion narratives but also broader New Testament elements, such as Matthew 23's denunciations of scribes and Pharisees as hypocrites and Paul's theological arguments in Romans 9â€“11 about Israel's partial hardening, which some view as supersessionistâ€”positing Christianity's replacement of Judaismâ€”potentially fueling displacement narratives.[1] Historically, these passages were selectively invoked in patristic writings and medieval sermons to justify expulsions, forced conversions, and violence against Jewish communities, transforming intra-religious disputes into enduring stereotypes of Jewish perfidy, though empirical analysis reveals the texts' original intent aligned with first-century Jewish sectarian rivalries rather than proto-racial animosity.[2][3] Post-Holocaust theological reassessments, including the Catholic Church's Nostra Aetate (1965) and subsequent documents, have emphasized the shared Jewish roots of Jesus and early Christianity, rejecting collective guilt interpretations and advocating readings that highlight ignorance or divine plan over malice in the crucifixion accounts.[1] Debates persist among scholars, with Jewish analysts often highlighting the evolution of blame narrativesâ€”from Roman execution in the earliest strata to amplified Jewish agency in later Gospelsâ€”as a catalyst for Christian self-justification against Judaism, while Christian exegetes stress the documents' emergence from a Jewish milieu using familiar midrashic techniques and prophetic invective, cautioning against anachronistic projections of modern racial antisemitism onto ancient religious polemics.[2][1] This tension underscores causal links between textual ambiguities and historical misuse, yet underscores that the New Testament's primary orientation remains affirmative toward Israel's election and the possibility of reconciliation, as in Acts 3:17's attribution of Jewish actions to ignorance amenable to repentance.[1]

Historical Context

First-Century Jewish-Christian Tensions

The early Christian movement emerged within first-century Judaism following Jesus' crucifixion circa 30 CE, initially comprising Jewish followers who viewed him as the Messiah while continuing synagogue attendance and Torah observance. Tensions arose primarily from disputes over Jesus' messianic claims, resurrection, and interpretations of Jewish scripture, leading to opposition from Pharisaic and Sadducean authorities in Jerusalem. The Sanhedrin's intervention is evidenced by the arrests of apostles Peter and John (Acts 4-5) and the stoning of Stephen around 35 CE, charged with blasphemy for equating Jesus with God, which triggered a scattering of believers beyond Judea (Acts 7-8).[4] Saul (later Paul) of Tarsus actively persecuted these groups pre-conversion circa 33-36 CE, ravaging assemblies in Jerusalem and Damascus, reflecting intra-Jewish enforcement against perceived heresy.[4] Paul's post-conversion mission to Gentiles, emphasizing faith over circumcision and full Mosaic Law compliance, exacerbated frictions with Jewish Christians insisting on Torah fidelity for all believers. This intra-community divide, documented in Paul's epistles (e.g., Galatians 2:11-14 on the Antioch incident circa 45-49 CE), pitted "Judaizers" against Pauline advocates of Gentile inclusion without proselytizing requirements. The Council of Jerusalem circa 49-50 CE, convened by apostles including Peter, Paul, and James, resolved that Gentile converts needed only to abstain from idol-sacrificed food, blood, strangled animals, and sexual immoralityâ€”accommodating Jewish sensitivities without imposing circumcision (Acts 15:19-29). This pragmatic decree prioritized unity but alienated stricter Jewish elements, foreshadowing Christianity's divergence from rabbinic Judaism.[5] The Roman destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE amid the First Jewish-Roman War intensified separation, as Judaism pivoted to synagogue-centered Pharisaic traditions while Christianity, already decentralized and less Temple-dependent, expanded among non-Jews. Local hostilities persisted, including social ostracism of Jewish Christians, but formal synagogue expulsions lack robust first-century attestation beyond Johannine allusions (John 9:22; 16:2), which scholars debate as reflecting later second-century experiences like the birkat ha-minim curse or informal exclusions rather than widespread policy.[6] By century's end, the Fiscus Judaicus tax imposed on Jews post-70 CE prompted some Christians to dissociate publicly to evade it, accelerating identity distinctions without eradicating hybrid "Jewish-Christian" groups. These tensions, rooted in theological disagreement rather than ethnic animus, mirrored ancient polemical norms where rival sects vied for legitimacy within Judaism.[7]

Rhetorical Norms in Ancient Jewish and Greco-Roman Polemics

In Second Temple Judaism, intra-Jewish polemics frequently employed hyperbolic and condemnatory language to denounce rival sects or interpretations as deviations from Torah observance, framing opponents as existential threats to the covenant community. Texts from the Qumran corpus, associated with the Essene-like group, exemplify this through pesharim (commentaries) that curse figures like the "Wicked Priest," interpreted as a Hasmonean leader, accusing him of ritual impurity, violence, and betrayal of divine law, such as in 1QpHab, where he is said to have perished "by the judgment of the house of Peleth." Similarly, sectarian hymns and curse texts like 4Q280 and 4Q286 invoke Belial's lot against "those who share the lot of Belial," targeting fellow Jews who rejected the community's dualistic worldview and purity rules, labeling them "seekers of smooth things" (a pejorative for Pharisees).[8] This rhetoric of exclusion and damnation mirrored prophetic traditions, where intra-Israelite critiques invoked divine wrath, as in Hosea 9:7-8 portraying prophets as fools amid a "perverse" people, adapting such motifs to sectarian disputes without implying ethnic hatred but rather theological betrayal.[9] Flavius Josephus, a first-century Jewish historian, further illustrates these norms in his polemics against revolutionary factions during the Jewish-Roman War (66-73 CE). In Jewish War, he vilifies the Zealots and Sicarii as "bandits" and "tyrants" who murdered fellow Jews, desecrated the Temple, and provoked Roman destruction, estimating their internal killings at thousands and portraying their leader John of Gischala as a deceitful demagogue driven by lust for power rather than piety.[10][11] Philo of Alexandria, in works like Every Good Man is Free, praises Essene asceticism while implicitly critiquing laxer Jewish groups in the Diaspora for compromising with Hellenistic customs, urging separation from "pleasure-loving" compatriots who neglected ancestral laws. Post-Temple rabbinic literature continued this pattern, as seen in the Birkat ha-Minim (ca. late first-early second century CE), a liturgical curse against minim (heretics or sectarians, possibly including Jewish Christians), invoking divine destruction on those who "err" in Torah matters, reflecting ongoing familial contestation over orthodoxy.[12][13] Greco-Roman rhetorical traditions paralleled these Jewish practices, normalizing invective (vituperatio) in deliberative and forensic oratory to discredit opponents through personal attacks on character, morality, and legitimacy. Aristotle's Rhetoric (ca. 350 BCE) outlines ethos attacks as standard, advising speakers to portray rivals as intemperate, unjust, or cowardly, as Demosthenes did against Aeschines in On the Crown (330 BCE), branding him a "hireling" actor and traitor whose "base" birth disqualified his counsel.[14] In Rome, Cicero's Philippics (43 BCE) against Mark Antony exemplify escalation, accusing him of drunkenness, incest, and aspiring tyranny, equating him with beasts and declaring "you will die most miserably," a trope echoing Catilinarian orations labeling conspirators as societal cancers.[15] Quintilian's Institutio Oratoria (ca. 95 CE) codifies such abuse as essential for persuasion, noting its prevalence in assemblies where orators invoked divine curses or familial dishonor, as in accusations of parricide against political foes, without these escalating to systematic ethnic persecution but serving to rally audiences in factional strife.[16] This cultural expectation of vituperative hyperbole influenced Judeo-Hellenistic discourse, contextualizing New Testament critiques within shared ancient norms of ideological combat rather than unprecedented malice.[17]

Key Passages in the Gospels

Synoptic Gospels: Mark, Matthew, and Luke

The Synoptic Gospelsâ€”Mark, Matthew, and Lukeâ€”present Jesus' ministry amid opposition from Jewish religious authorities, including scribes, Pharisees, and chief priests, who are depicted as rejecting his teachings and orchestrating his arrest and handover to Roman prefect Pontius Pilate for crucifixion. These portrayals have drawn accusations of antisemitism, particularly for emphasizing Jewish leaders' role in the events leading to Jesus' death, such as plotting against him (Mark 3:6; Matthew 12:14; Luke 6:11) and inciting crowds during the trial (Mark 15:11; Matthew 27:20; Luke 23:4-5).[18][19] However, the texts consistently attribute the actual execution to Roman authority, with Pilate ordering the crucifixion despite declaring Jesus innocent multiple times (Mark 15:1-15; Matthew 27:11-26; Luke 23:1-25), reflecting the historical reality that only Romans could impose capital punishment under occupation.[20] In Mark, the earliest Gospel dated to around 65-70 CE, critiques target specific Jerusalem elites rather than Judaism broadly, portraying the chief priests and scribes as fearful of Jesus' influence on the temple economy and crowd support (Mark 11:18). The passion narrative describes the crowd, stirred by leaders, choosing Barabbas over Jesus, but lacks generalized condemnation of "the Jews," focusing instead on elite manipulation and Roman complicity.[21] Scholars note Mark's relative restraint compared to later Gospels, viewing the rejection motif as echoing Hebrew prophetic traditions of Israel resisting God's messengers, not as ethnic animosity.[22] Accusations of inherent antisemitism overlook the Gospel's Jewish authorship and audience, where such polemics served to explain messianic rejection within familial Jewish debate.[23] Matthew, likely composed circa 80-90 CE for a Jewish-Christian community, intensifies rhetorical critique, as in the seven woes against scribes and Pharisees (Matthew 23:1-36), labeling them "hypocrites" and "brood of vipers" for prioritizing tradition over mercy and justice. The passion account uniquely includes the crowd's declaration, "His blood be on us and on our children!" (Matthew 27:25), interpreted by some as a collective curse fueling later deicide charges against Jews.[24] Yet, first-century context reveals this as narrative acceptance of prophetic responsibility for rejecting the Messiah, paralleling Old Testament imprecatory language (e.g., 2 Samuel 1:16), not a perpetual racial malediction; the verse appears only in Matthew's expansion of Mark, amid intra-sectarian tensions post-70 CE temple destruction.[25][26] Matthew elsewhere affirms Jewish priority in salvation (Matthew 15:24) and includes righteous Jews like Joseph of Arimathea, countering blanket vilification claims.[27] Luke, dated around 80-90 CE and possibly authored by a Gentile sympathizer, balances critique with affirmations of Jewish piety, portraying figures like Simeon and Anna as recognizing Jesus' significance (Luke 2:25-38). Anti-Judaism accusations focus on Jerusalem's leaders rejecting prophets (Luke 13:33-34) and the city's prophesied destruction (Luke 19:41-44; 23:27-31), linking it to crucifixion demands.[19] These elements reflect Luke's theme of universal salvation amid Jewish repudiation, but scholarly analysis attributes them to historical recollection of elite opposition, not ethnic hatred; positive depictions of Jews (e.g., Luke 7:1-10 centurion's faith via Jewish elders) and shared Synoptic material underscore targeted polemic against corrupt authorities, akin to Greco-Roman and Jewish rhetorical norms.[28][29] Later supersessionist readings detached these from their original disputational purpose, but the texts evince no call for violence or devaluation of Jewish covenant identity.[30]

Gospel of John

The Gospel of John, dated by scholars to approximately 90â€“110 CE, features the Greek phrase hoi Ioudaioi ("the Jews") over 70 times, more frequently than the other Gospels combined, often portraying this group as antagonists to Jesus and his followers.[31] This usage has drawn accusations of antisemitism, particularly for depicting hoi Ioudaioi collectively as responsible for persecuting Jesus, such as in John 5:16â€“18, where they seek to kill him for violating the Sabbath and claiming equality with God, and in the Passion narrative (John 18â€“19), where they demand his crucifixion from Pilate while disclaiming responsibility for Roman law.[32] A particularly contentious verse is John 8:44, where Jesus addresses a group of disputants as "of your father the devil," interpreted by critics as imputing inherent evil to Jews, though the context specifies opponents rejecting his messianic claims amid a heated debate.[33] These passages employ rhetorical invective common to first-century Jewish polemics, but their generalization of "the Jews" as adversaries has been cited as fostering later Christian hostility toward Jewish communities.[34] In John 9:22 and 16:2, the text references expulsion from synagogues (aposynagÅgos), reflecting real tensions between Jesus' followers and synagogue authorities, possibly linked to the post-70 CE Birkat ha-Minim prayer cursing sectarians, which pressured Jewish Christians to separate.[35] Here, hoi Ioudaioi likely denotes not ethnic Jews universally but Judean religious leaders or a specific faction enforcing communal boundaries, a nuance supported by geographical cues (e.g., John 11:54 referencing Judea) and the Gospel's Jewish authorship, as evidenced by its emphasis on Jesus fulfilling Torah and festivals.[36] Accusations of antisemitism often overlook this intra-Jewish dynamic, projecting modern racial categories onto ancient theological disputes; the Evangelist, writing from a Jewish-Christian perspective, critiques rejection of Jesus as messiah rather than advocating hatred of Jews as a people.[37] Scholarly defenses argue that labeling John antisemitic anachronistically conflates anti-Judaism (opposition to non-messianic Judaism) with antisemitism (racial or ethnic prejudice), noting the Gospel's positive portrayals of Jewish figures like Nicodemus and the healed blind man, and Jesus' self-identification as fulfilling Jewish prophecy.[32] Historical misreadings, such as medieval supersessionist interpretations, amplified these texts' potential for abuse, but the original intent aligns with familial critique within Judaism, akin to prophetic rebukes in the Hebrew Bible.[38] While some post-Holocaust analyses from academic circles emphasize harm from Johannine rhetoric, potentially influenced by institutional pressures to highlight Christian culpability, empirical examination of the text's first-century Sitz im Leben reveals a document rooted in Jewish self-understanding, not gentile xenophobia.[39]

Passages in Epistles and Other Writings

Pauline Corpus, Including 1 Thessalonians

The Pauline corpus, comprising seven undisputed epistles (Romans, 1â€“2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, and Philemon) and several disputed ones, contains rhetorical critiques of certain Jewish opponents to the emerging Christian movement, which some interpreters have labeled as antisemitic. These texts, written primarily between approximately 48â€“58 CE, reflect Paul's efforts to defend his gospel among Gentile converts amid persecution from synagogue authorities and other Jewish leaders resistant to messianic claims about Jesus. While Paul's Jewish heritage and affirmations of Israel's enduring role (e.g., Romans 11:1â€“2, 25â€“29) complicate blanket accusations, passages like 1 Thessalonians 2:14â€“16 have drawn particular scrutiny for attributing collective culpability to "the Jews" for historical killings and obstructing evangelism.[40][41] In 1 Thessalonians, dated to around 50â€“51 CE and addressed to a mixed Gentile-Jewish church in Thessalonica facing local opposition, Paul draws a parallel between the Thessalonian believers' suffering from their "own countrymen" (likely pagan Greeks) and the Judean churches' persecution by "the Jews" (Greek: hoi Ioudaioi). Critics argue the passage promotes a deicide chargeâ€”imputing divine murder to Jews collectivelyâ€”fueling later antisemitic tropes of eternal guilt, especially via the phrase "the Jews" without qualifiers, which could imply ethnic totality rather than specific factions.[42][43] However, the text's context mitigates universal condemnation: Paul, a Pharisee by training (Philippians 3:5), uses "the Jews" idiomatically for Judean persecutors analogous to Thessalonian pagans, not all ethnic Jews, as evidenced by his self-identification as Israelite (Romans 11:1) and mission to synagogues (Acts 17:1â€“4, corroborated by 1 Thessalonians 2:14's reference to Judean believers). Scholarly debate persists on authenticity, with a minority proposing interpolation due to stylistic tensions and perceived anachronism (e.g., seeming post-temple destruction tone), but textual evidence, including uniform manuscript inclusion and alignment with Pauline persecution themes (e.g., Galatians 1:13; 1 Corinthians 15:9), supports genuineness per mainstream consensus.[41][44][40] Beyond 1 Thessalonians, other Pauline texts accused of anti-Jewish bias include Galatians 3:10â€“13, where reliance on Torah is termed a "curse" redeemed by Christ's crucifixion, interpreted by some as devaluing Jewish covenantal law. In Philippians 3:2â€“9, Paul rejects "confidence in the flesh" (circumcision, heritage) for faith in Christ, renouncing his prior zeal as "rubbish," which detractors see as self-hating Judaism, though it mirrors prophetic critiques (e.g., Jeremiah 9:23â€“26) and affirms Israel's election elsewhere. Disputed epistles like Ephesians 2:11â€“15 describe Christ abolishing the "dividing wall of hostility" in the law, enabling Gentile inclusion, sometimes read as supersessionist erasure of Jewish distinctiveness, but the undisputed corpus emphasizes Israel's future restoration (Romans 11:26: "all Israel will be saved"). These passages, rooted in familial polemic amid synagogue expulsions (e.g., Paul's own in 2 Corinthians 11:24), prioritize theological disagreement over ethnic animus, with accusations often amplified by modern lenses projecting later Christian-Jewish fractures onto Paul's era.[45][46]

Epistle to the Hebrews

The Epistle to the Hebrews, an anonymous homiletic treatise dated to approximately 60-70 CE, targets an audience of Jewish Christians facing pressure to revert to synagogue practices and Mosaic observances amid emerging persecution.[47] Its author, likely a Hellenistic Jewish Christian familiar with Second Temple traditions, utilizes typological and midrashic exegesisâ€”methods prevalent in contemporary Jewish literatureâ€”to argue Christ's fulfillment and superiority over angelic mediators, Moses, Aaronic priesthood, and Levitical sacrifices.[48] This framework affirms the validity of prior Jewish revelation (Hebrews 1:1) while positing the new covenant as its eschatological consummation, drawing extensively from Jewish Scriptures with over 30 direct quotations.[49] Central to potential accusations of anti-Judaism are passages contrasting old and new covenants, such as Hebrews 7:11-19, which deems the Levitical priesthood insufficient for perfecting worshippers, and Hebrews 8:6-13, which declares the Mosaic covenant obsolete and aging in light of Jeremiah 31:31-34's prophecy.[50] Hebrews 10:1-9 further critiques the old system's repetitive sacrifices as shadowy and ineffective, establishing Christ's once-for-all offering as definitive. These elements have been charged with supersessionism, implying Judaism's obsolescence and contributing to later Christian triumphalism that marginalized Jews.[51] Scholarly analysis, however, distinguishes this as intra-communal polemic rather than ethnic vilification, akin to debates in Qumran texts or Philo's allegorical interpretations where shadows yield to realities without rejecting Jewish heritage.[49] The author's sustained synkrisis (comparative rhetoric) engages Judaism's institutions to exhort perseverance, not to deny Jewish theological legitimacy, as evidenced by the epistle's respect for patriarchal figures and prophetic witness.[49] McCullough emphasizes that labeling it anti-Jewish overlooks its dialogical nature within first-century Judaism, protecting converts from assimilation without hatred.[49] Barnard highlights neglected contextual factors, including Judaism's diversity and Hebrews' focus on the cultus amid post-Temple realities, rejecting blanket supersessionist readings that anachronistically impose later separations.[51] While post-Holocaust critiques acknowledge misuse of these texts to justify antisemitismâ€”such as in patristic supersessionismâ€”the epistle evinces no call for collective Jewish guilt or exclusion, aligning instead with familial critique to affirm Christianity as Judaism's telos.[50] Its eschatological tension, blending realized fulfillment with future hope, precludes total repudiation of Jewish covenantal identity, consistent with the author's Jewish provenance and audience.[51] Empirical review of early reception shows varied Jewish-Christian interactions, not inherent promotion of animosity.[49]

Book of Revelation

The Book of Revelation, composed circa 95 AD during the reign of Emperor Domitian, reflects a Jewish-Christian apocalyptic worldview steeped in Old Testament imagery, with its author, traditionally identified as the apostle John exiled on Patmos, addressing seven churches in Asia Minor amid Roman persecution and local opposition.[52] While the text employs symbolic language drawn from Jewish prophetic traditionsâ€”such as the sealed 144,000 from the tribes of Israel in Revelation 7 and 14â€”certain passages have drawn accusations of anti-Judaism due to their pointed rhetoric against groups claiming Jewish identity. These elements arise in the letters to the churches of Smyrna (Revelation 2:8-11) and Philadelphia (Revelation 3:7-13), where opposition from "those who say they are Jews and are not" is highlighted, framing the conflict as spiritual rather than ethnic in a blanket sense.[20] In Revelation 2:9, the Smyrna church is commended amid "tribulation and poverty" for enduring "the blasphemy of them which say they are Jews, and are not, but are the synagogue of Satan," with a parallel in Revelation 3:9 warning Philadelphia of similar adversaries who will be forced to acknowledge the Christians' God-given status.[53] Scholarly exegesis interprets "synagogue of Satan" not as a condemnation of Judaism writ large but as hyperbolic polemic against specific persecutorsâ€”likely local Jews or Judaizing Gentiles in Smyrna and Philadelphia who excluded messianic believers from synagogues and aligned with Roman authorities against the church, echoing intra-Jewish disputes over messianic claims seen elsewhere in first-century sources.[54] This rhetoric mirrors ancient Jewish sectarian critiques, such as those in the Dead Sea Scrolls labeling rival Jews as "sons of darkness," rather than inventing ethnic hatred; the author's own Jewish heritage is evident in Revelation's extensive use of Ezekiel, Daniel, and Zechariah motifs.[55] Accusations of antisemitism stem from decontextualized readings that project these verses onto all Jews, fueling later supersessionist theologies, yet such charges overlook the text's affirmation of ethnic Israel (e.g., Revelation 11:1-2's temple focus and the martyrs' cry in 6:9-11 rooted in Jewish covenantal language) and the absence of collective guilt attribution.[56] Defenses emphasize that the "false Jews" likely refer to insincere claimantsâ€”possibly Gentiles masquerading as Jews to gain synagogue privileges or apostate insidersâ€”persecuting faithful assemblies, a view supported by patristic interpreters like Irenaeus and modern analyses rejecting anachronistic racial framing.[53] Revelation's broader eschatology, portraying ultimate vindication for Israel against cosmic evil (Revelation 12's woman and dragon symbolizing Israel's trials), aligns with prophetic restoration themes, not eradication, underscoring the book's role in early Jewish-Christian resilience rather than prejudice.[20]

Distinctions Between Anti-Judaism and Antisemitism

Defining Terms and Anachronistic Applications

Antisemitism denotes a form of hostility toward Jews conceptualized primarily in racial or ethnic terms, a phenomenon that emerged in the nineteenth century amid pseudoscientific theories of race and culminated in events like the Holocaust.[57] The term was coined in 1879 by Wilhelm Marr, a German agitator who founded the Antisemiten-Liga to promote opposition to Jews as a supposed biological threat to Aryan society, shifting prejudice from religious grounds to purported innate differences.[58] In contrast, anti-Judaism refers to theological critiques or misrepresentations of Jewish religious doctrines, laws, and practices, often framed as superseding or rejecting Judaism's salvific efficacy without targeting Jews as an ethnic collective.[59] This distinction underscores that anti-Judaism operates on doctrinal grounds, evaluating Judaism's tenets against alternative beliefs, whereas antisemitism imputes inherent flaws to Jewish identity irrespective of faith or conversion.[59] Applying the label of antisemitism to New Testament passages constitutes an anachronism, as first-century authors lacked modern racial categories and viewed Jewish-Christian disputes as intra-communal polemics among religious kin rather than inter-ethnic warfare.[60] New Testament writers, predominantly Jewish, addressed audiences including Jews and Gentiles in a context where Christianity was not yet a separate religion but a messianic sect within Judaism, rendering charges of ethnic hatred incompatible with their self-identification and rhetorical aims.[60] Scholarly analyses emphasize that such texts reflect "sibling rivalry" over interpretation of Torah and prophecy, not the dehumanizing stereotypes of later antisemitism, which projected Jews as eternal outsiders based on fabricated biological determinism absent in ancient Mediterranean thought.[60] Retroactively equating theological anti-Judaism with antisemitism overlooks this historical discontinuity, potentially inflating the original critiques' intent while diminishing awareness of how patristic and medieval exegeses transformed them into vehicles for ethnic persecution.[59]

Intra-Jewish Polemic as Familial Critique

Scholars such as James D. G. Dunn have identified echoes of intra-Jewish polemical rhetoric in New Testament texts, particularly in Paul's Epistle to the Galatians, where arguments against Judaizing tendencies mirror debates within Second Temple Judaism over Torah observance and covenant fidelity.[61] This internal contestation parallels the sharp rebukes found in Hebrew prophetic literature, such as Jeremiah's denunciations of Israel's priests and prophets as deceitful shepherds (Jeremiah 23:1-4) or Amos's condemnation of Israel's elite for social injustice (Amos 5:10-12), which were familial corrections aimed at restoring covenantal loyalty rather than rejecting Jewish identity outright.[1] In the Gospels, Jesus' critiques of Pharisees and scribes, as in Matthew 23's series of woes accusing hypocrisy and legalism, exemplify this mode of discourse, akin to rabbinic disputations or Qumran sectarians' invectives against the Jerusalem establishment in the Dead Sea Scrolls, where intra-communal rivalry employed hyperbolic language to assert interpretive authority without implying ethnic exclusion.[62] Jewish New Testament scholar Amy-Jill Levine emphasizes that such passages preserve authentic first-century Jewish debates over halakhah and messianic expectations, warning that detaching them from their ethnic and cultural matrix distorts them into anachronistic antisemitism, as the authors and initial audiences shared a common Jewish framework of self-critique.[63] Paul's self-identification as an Israelite (Romans 11:1) and his olive tree metaphor affirming Israel's enduring role (Romans 11:17-24) underscore the familial nature of his polemics against certain Jewish practices, positioning his theology as a renovation within Judaism rather than a break, much like the Essenes' rejection of Temple purity while maintaining Jewish eschatological hopes.[64] The Gospel of John's references to "the Jews" (e.g., John 8:44) target specific synagogue authorities opposing the Johannine community, reflecting localized factional strife documented in Josephus's accounts of Pharisaic-Sadducean conflicts, not a blanket condemnation of Judaism.[64] This interpretive lens counters charges of inherent antisemitism by highlighting how later gentile Christian dominance transformed in-group rhetoric into out-group hostility, as Dunn notes in tracing the shift from Paul's intra-Jewish echoes to patristic supersessionism.[65] Empirical comparison with non-canonical Jewish texts, such as the sectarian polemics in 4QMMT from Qumran urging separation from impure practices, reveals comparable intensity, suggesting NT language aligns with normative Jewish rhetorical norms for reform rather than ethnic animus.[62] While some post-Holocaust scholarship amplifies anti-Judaism readings due to institutional sensitivities, primary textual evidence and archaeological correlates prioritize this contextual familial dynamic for accurate historical assessment.[54]

Accusations of Antisemitism

Patristic and Medieval Interpretations Leading to Supersessionism

Early Church Fathers interpreted New Testament passages, particularly in the Epistle to the Romans (9â€“11) and Hebrews, as evidencing the transfer of God's covenant from ethnic Israel to the Christian Church, establishing supersessionism as a foundational doctrine. Justin Martyr, in his Dialogue with Trypho (c. 155 AD), argued that the true spiritual Israel consists of Christians who adhere to Christ's teachings, asserting that Jewish rejection of Jesus nullified their prior covenantal status and transferred blessings to Gentile believers.[66] This view drew on interpretations of passages like Romans 11:17â€“24, where Gentile branches are grafted into the olive tree, portraying Jews as broken off due to unbelief.[67] Subsequent patristic writers reinforced this framework. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130â€“202 AD) and Tertullian (c. 155â€“220 AD) echoed supersessionist themes by emphasizing the obsolescence of Mosaic law under the new covenant described in Hebrews 8:13, viewing Judaism as preparatory but ultimately superseded by Christian fulfillment.[68] Origen of Alexandria (c. 185â€“253 AD) allegorized Old Testament promises as applying spiritually to the Church, diminishing literal Jewish restoration prospects.[69] While some, like Hilary of Poitiers (c. 310â€“367 AD), nuanced future hope for Israel based on Romans 11:26, the dominant patristic consensus framed Jewish unbelief as divine judgment, legitimizing the Church's exclusive claim to Abrahamic inheritance.[69][67] Augustine of Hippo (354â€“430 AD) systematized these interpretations into the "witness people" doctrine, positing that Jewish dispersionâ€”evident since the Roman destruction of Jerusalem in 70 ADâ€”served as perpetual testimony to Christianity's triumph, per Psalm 59:11 and interpretations of Deuteronomy 28's curses.[70] He advocated sparing Jews from eradication to fulfill this role, yet upheld supersessionism by deeming their rituals abrogated post-Christ, influencing ecclesiastical policies of toleration amid subordination.[71] In the medieval period, these patristic exegeses evolved into institutionalized supersessionism, underpinning canon law and theology that portrayed Jews as relics of a revoked dispensation. Thomas Aquinas (1225â€“1274 AD), building on Augustine, interpreted John 8:44 and Matthew 27:25 as indicting Jews collectively for deicide, justifying their marginalization while affirming the Church as the new Israel.[72] This framework contributed to measures like the Fourth Lateran Council's (1215 AD) mandates for Jewish badges and residential segregation, framed as preserving the witness function amid perceived threats to Christian society.[73] Scholastic emphasis on Hebrews' superior covenant reinforced economic and social restrictions, viewing Jewish persistence in unbelief as confirmation of prophetic fulfillment in the Church's ascendancy.[74] Such interpretations, while theological, causally linked to escalating hostilities, including expulsions from England (1290 AD) and France (1306 AD), rationalized as responses to an obsolete covenantal status.[73]

Post-Holocaust Charges and Selective Readings

In the aftermath of the Holocaust, which resulted in the systematic murder of approximately six million Jews between 1941 and 1945, scholars intensified scrutiny of New Testament texts, charging them with embedding anti-Judaic elements that allegedly fostered centuries of Christian hostility toward Jews, culminating in modern antisemitism. Rosemary Radford Ruether's 1974 book Faith and Fratricide: The Theological Roots of Anti-Semitism exemplifies this critique, positing that the New Testament's portrayal of Judaism as obsolete and Jews as collectively responsible for Jesus' rejection forms an irreducible "Adversus Judaeos" tradition inherent to Christianity, necessitating excision for reconciliation.[75] Ruether attributes to these texts a causal chain linking early Christian polemics to medieval pogroms and Nazi ideology, though her analysis has been faulted for conflating theological disagreement with racial hatred and overlooking the New Testament's affirmation of Jewish scriptures and covenantal continuity elsewhere.[76] [77] Specific passages, such as Matthew 27:25â€”"His blood be on us and on our children"â€”and John 8:44â€”"You are of your father the devil"â€”became focal points for accusations of promoting deicide (Jews as "Christ-killers") and demonic characterization, with critics like Ruether arguing these verses inculcated perpetual Jewish guilt in Christian consciousness.[78] [79] Post-1945 Jewish-Christian dialogues, influenced by figures such as Jules Isaac's 1948 Jesus and Israel, amplified claims that such texts provided scriptural warrant for supersessionismâ€”the replacement of Israel by the Churchâ€”fueling expulsions, inquisitions, and ultimately the Shoah's racial pseudoscience.[80] Yet these indictments often selectively amplify invective against Jewish leaders (e.g., Pharisees or "the Jews" as Judean authorities in John) while minimizing parallel intra-Jewish rhetorical harshness in Second Temple literature, such as the Dead Sea Scrolls' curses against Jewish sectarians or rabbinic polemics against minim (heretics).[37] This approach entails anachronistic projection of 19th-20th century ethnic antisemitism onto first-century familial disputes within Judaism, where New Testament authorsâ€”predominantly Jewishâ€”critiqued synagogue rejection of Jesus amid Roman occupation, not an abstract "Jewish people" as perpetrators of cosmic evil.[32] Selective omission of contextual qualifiers, such as John's Gospel addressing post-70 CE temple destruction tensions or Paul's Romans 9-11 lament over Israel's unbelief alongside promises of restoration, distorts the texts' intent as prophetic intra-communal rebuke rather than genocidal blueprint.[39] Critics of these charges, including John G. Gager, note that blaming the New Testament overlooks how pre-Christian Greco-Roman antisemitism and medieval economic resentments independently drove pogroms, with NT passages more often invoked post-facto to rationalize preexisting biases than as primary instigators.[80] Such readings, prevalent in 1960s-1980s theological circles amid Vatican II reforms, reflect a post-Holocaust moral imperative but risk ahistorical essentialism by privileging trauma-driven narratives over philological evidence of the texts' Jewish provenance and non-collective ascriptions of blame.[77]

Defenses Against Antisemitism Charges

Original Jewish Authorship and Audience

The New Testament was authored entirely by individuals of Jewish ethnicity or heritage, reflecting a first-century Jewish milieu amid intra-communal debates over messianic claims and Torah observance. The Apostle Paul, a former Pharisee from Tarsus (Acts 23:6; Philippians 3:5), composed the core of the Pauline corpus (Romans through Philemon, excluding disputed texts like the Pastorals), addressing primarily Jewish-Christian assemblies and synagogues where Gentiles were grafted into Jewish-rooted covenants (Romans 11:17-24).[81] The Gospel of Matthew, attributed to the apostle Levi (Matthew 9:9), structures Jesus' teachings around Jewish festivals and prophetic fulfillments (e.g., Matthew 1-2 linking to Micah 5:2 and Isaiah 7:14), targeting Greek-speaking Jewish Christians proximate to the homeland, as evidenced by its halakhic disputes mirroring post-70 CE synagogue tensions.[82] Similarly, the Gospel of Mark, linked to John Mark of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), presumes familiarity with Jewish customs without explanation (e.g., no Passover glosses), suggesting an audience of Jewish believers in Rome or Syria.[83] This Jewish authorship underscores passages critiqued for antisemitismâ€”such as John's "the Jews" (e.g., John 8:44)â€”as rhetorical devices in familial polemics, akin to prophetic rebukes in the Hebrew Bible where Israel is indicted for covenant infidelity (e.g., Hosea 1-3; Amos 3:2). Jewish scholar Amy-Jill Levine emphasizes that the New Testament emerges from Jewish interpretive traditions, with Johannine expulsion motifs (John 9:22; 16:2) reflecting real synagogue Birkat ha-Minim curses against minim (heretics), not ethnic demonization but boundary-drawing within Judaism.[84] The Epistle to the Hebrews, likely penned by a Hellenistic Jewish author versed in Septuagint typology, exhorts Jewish recipients to persevere amid priestly cult disruptions post-Temple destruction (Hebrews 8-10), paralleling Qumran sectarian critiques rather than gentile supersessionism.[85] Revelation's John, exiled on Patmos (Revelation 1:9), deploys Jewish apocalyptic symbolism (e.g., Danielic beasts, Levitical plagues) for persecuted Jewish-Christian communities, framing opposition as eschatological judgment on apostate leaders, not collective racial guilt. Such original contexts refute anachronistic antisemitism charges by highlighting causal continuity with Tanakh precedents: prophets like Jeremiah (Jeremiah 7:4-15) or Ezekiel (Ezekiel 16) issued stark warnings to "stiff-necked" Israel without abrogating election (Deuteronomy 7:6-8). Levine notes that detaching these texts from their Jewish matrixâ€”ignoring first-century pluralism among Pharisees, Sadducees, and Essenesâ€”distorts them into gentile-Christian animus, whereas empirical reconstruction via Dead Sea Scrolls and Josephus reveals shared rhetorical hyperbole in Jewish disputations.[86] Scholarly consensus, including from Jewish NT experts, affirms no evidence of racial hatred in the autographs; criticisms targeted specific Temple elites or synagogue authorities rejecting Jesus' messiahship, with universalistic extensions (e.g., Matthew 28:19) inviting Gentiles as proselytes, not replacements.[87] This intra-Jewish dynamic, verifiable through linguistic Hebraisms (e.g., Matthew's "kingdom of heaven" euphemism) and midrashic exegesis, positions the New Testament as extension of prophetic Judaism, not its antithesis.[88]

Contextual Exegesis Rejecting Collective Guilt

Scholars interpreting the New Testament passion narratives argue that passages attributing responsibility for Jesus' death to Jewish figures refer specifically to the Sanhedrin leaders, a Jerusalem crowd, and colluding Roman authorities at the time, rather than imposing perpetual guilt on all Jews across generations. In Matthew 27:25, the crowd's declarationâ€”"His blood be on us and on our children"â€”arises in the context of Pilate's hand-washing gesture during the Passover custom of releasing a prisoner, representing a localized acceptance of immediate consequences by participants, not a universal ethnic curse; this echoes Old Testament formulas of covenantal accountability limited to specific acts, as seen in Deuteronomy 27's curses on particular sins, without implying inherited damnation.[89][90] Amy-Jill Levine, a Jewish New Testament scholar, views such language as rhetorical intensification typical of intra-Jewish disputes, akin to prophetic rebukes in Hosea or Jeremiah that target covenant unfaithfulness without racial condemnation, and cautions against decontextualized readings that fuel later persecutions.[91] In Acts 4:27-28, Peter's prayer explicitly names "Herod and Pontius Pilate, along with the Gentiles and the peoples of Israel" as actors in Jesus' death, but frames their actions as fulfilling God's predetermined purpose, thereby distributing agency across Roman and Jewish elites while subordinating human culpability to divine sovereignty; this exegesis underscores that the text rejects any notion of autonomous Jewish deicide by integrating the event into God's plan, as paralleled in Acts 2:23 where foreknowledge precludes blame on any single group.[92][93] Similarly, John's Gospel passages like 8:44, where Jesus tells certain Pharisees "You are of your father the devil," constitute a moral-theological critique of specific opponents' rejection of messianic claims, drawing on Jewish scriptural motifs of diabolic influence in idolatry (e.g., Wisdom 2:24), not a biological or eternal indictment of Jewish descent; contextual analysis reveals this as familial polemic amid synagogue tensions post-70 CE, where "the Jews" denotes synagogue authorities excluding Jesus' followers, not the entire people.[32][94] Broader New Testament theology reinforces individual accountability over collective guilt, as Romans 3:23 universalizes sinâ€”"all have sinned"â€”and Romans 11:25-26 anticipates Israel's future restoration, incompatible with perpetual condemnation; exegeses thus maintain that any "guilt" language targets willful rejection by contemporaries, mitigated by Jesus' prayers for forgiveness (Luke 23:34) and the early church's Jewish composition, which would preclude self-condemnatory ethnic curses. This approach aligns with historical-critical methods emphasizing first-century Jewish-Roman power dynamics, where temple elites sought Roman execution to preserve status quo amid unrest, without implicating diaspora Jews or descendants.[89][95]

Scholarly Debates and Consensus

Pre-20th Century Views

Prior to the 20th century, Christian theological scholarship interpreted New Testament passages depicting Jewish involvement in Jesus' trial and execution, such as those in Matthew 27 and the Gospel of John, as accurate historical accounts underscoring Israel's covenantal rejection of the Messiah. This perspective framed the texts not as ethnic animosity but as prophetic fulfillment of Old Testament warnings against unbelief, with Jewish leaders bearing primary responsibility for handing Jesus over to Roman authorities. Early patristic writers, including Tatian in the 2nd century, emphasized direct Jewish culpability in the crucifixion, marking a shift from solely Roman execution to theological accountability for deicide.[2] Such interpretations supported supersessionism, viewing the Church as inheriting Israel's role, without the modern lens of racial antisemitism, which emerged in the late 19th century. Medieval and Reformation-era scholars continued this exegesis, treating passages like Matthew 27:25â€”"His blood be on us and on our children"â€”as a literal invocation of generational consequences for collective rejection, often linking it to ongoing Jewish dispersion as divine judgment. Thomas Aquinas, in his Summa Theologica (c. 1270), affirmed Jewish leaders' role in the Passion while distinguishing it from eternal damnation, allowing for individual conversion. Martin Luther, in early works like That Jesus Christ Was Born a Jew (1523), urged kindness toward Jews in hopes of their turning to Christ but later, in On the Jews and Their Lies (1543), invoked New Testament critiques to decry persistent unbelief and deicide as warranting social exclusion, reflecting a hardening yet biblically grounded polemic.[96] John Calvin, more moderately, echoed supersessionist readings in his commentaries, attributing crucifixion demands to corrupt Jewish authorities without extending to indiscriminate hatred.[97] In the 19th century, amid rising historical criticism, orthodox Protestant and Catholic scholars largely upheld the historicity of Gospel accounts, seeing Jewish responsibility as consistent with Roman legal records of crucifixion under Pilate. While Jewish reformers like Isaac Mayer Wise argued Gospel narratives exaggerated events to fuel prejudice, Christian academics such as those in confessional seminaries rejected such revisions, prioritizing textual and patristic tradition over emerging skepticism. This era's scholarship rarely framed the passages as inherently prejudicial, instead viewing them as causal explanations for Christianity's divergence from Judaism, with empirical support drawn from Josephus and Tacitus on Pilate's governance.[98] Debates focused on doctrinal implications rather than ethical condemnation of the texts themselves, predating post-Holocaust reevaluations.

Modern and Contemporary Scholarship

Modern scholarship on New Testament passages accused of antisemitism emphasizes historical and literary context to distinguish intra-Jewish polemics from later racial hatred, arguing that terms like "the Jews" in texts such as the Gospel of John often refer to specific opponents of Jesus rather than the entire Jewish people.[99] Post-Holocaust biblical studies, influenced by events like the Shoah in 1941â€“1945, have largely rejected anachronistic applications of modern antisemitism to first-century writings, viewing accusations of inherent NT antisemitism as overstated given the Jewish authorship and audience of the Gospels.[54] Scholars like Bart Ehrman contend that while the Gospels contain anti-Judaismâ€”rhetorical critiques of certain Jewish leaders akin to prophetic rebukes in the Hebrew Bibleâ€”the concept of antisemitism as ethnic or racial prejudice did not exist in the Roman era, rendering such labels inapplicable to the original texts.[99] Jewish New Testament expert Amy-Jill Levine, in works published since 2006, maintains that passages like John 8:44, where Jesus calls certain interlocutors "children of the devil," reflect familial disputes within Judaism rather than blanket condemnation of Jews, cautioning that decontextualized readings have fueled historical violence but insisting the NT itself is not antisemitic when exegeted properly.[54] Levine differentiates anti-Judaism, which critiques theological positions, from antisemitism, defined as hatred of Jews as an immutable ethnic group, noting that NT authors operated within a Jewish framework where such language was commonplace in sectarian debates.[100] Contemporary consensus, as articulated in peer-reviewed analyses, holds that the deicide chargeâ€”alleging collective Jewish responsibility for Jesus' death in Matthew 27:25â€”misrepresents Roman execution practices under Pontius Pilate in circa 30 CE, where crucifixion was a imperial punishment, not a Jewish one, and attributes eternal guilt to "the blood of all people" in a non-ethnic sense.[101] Evangelical and Protestant scholars in the late 20th and early 21st centuries, such as Greg Boyd in 2016, argue that Gospel narratives distribute culpability across Jewish leaders, the crowd, Pilate, and Roman soldiers, rejecting any promotion of antisemitism and highlighting how selective emphasis on Jewish involvement ignores the shared human sinfulness theme central to Christian theology.[18] Recent studies, including those post-2000, underscore that John's frequent use of "the Jews" (over 70 instances) targets Judean authorities opposing early Jesus followers, not diaspora Jews or Judaism writ large, with linguistic analysis showing it parallels Hebrew Bible idioms for adversaries.[102] Despite these views, some academics acknowledge lingering theological anti-Judaism in supersessionist interpretations persisting into the 20th century, though empirical textual criticism since the 1970s has shifted focus to socio-historical settings, reducing claims of systemic NT bias.[80] Debates continue on passages like 1 Thessalonians 2:14â€“16, where Paul accuses "the Jews" of killing Jesus and prophets, with scholars dating it to circa 50 CE and interpreting it as referring to specific persecutors in Judea amid Roman-Jewish tensions leading to the 70 CE Temple destruction, not a timeless curse.[99] Overall, modern exegesis prioritizes causal realismâ€”tracing misuses to patristic allegorizations rather than authorial intentâ€”and empirical data from Dead Sea Scrolls and Qumran texts, which reveal similar intra-Jewish invective, undermining charges of unique NT hostility.[54] This approach, evident in monographs from the 1990s onward, fosters consensus that while NT rhetoric contributed to later abuses, it does not endorse antisemitism when read through first-century lenses.[80]

Christian Responses and Reforms

Vatican II and Nostra Aetate

The Second Vatican Council, convened from October 11, 1962, to December 8, 1965, under Popes John XXIII and Paul VI, sought to renew the Catholic Church's engagement with the modern world, including reevaluating its relations with non-Christian religions amid post-World War II reflections on historical animosities.[103][104] This ecumenical council produced 16 documents, among them Nostra Aetate, which directly addressed longstanding theological tensions with Judaism, particularly accusations that New Testament passages had been misinterpreted to justify antisemitism.[105] Promulgated by Pope Paul VI on October 28, 1965, Nostra Aetate (Latin for "In Our Time") is a declaration emphasizing the Church's spiritual bond with Judaism as the root of Christianity, while explicitly repudiating charges of collective Jewish guilt for the death of Jesus.[105] In its fourth section, the document states: "What happened in His passion cannot be charged against all the Jews, without distinction, then alive, nor against the Jews of today," rejecting any notion derived from Scripture that Jews are "rejected or accursed by God."[105] It further condemns "hatred, persecutions, displays of anti-Semitism directed against Jews at any time and by anyone," framing such attitudes as incompatible with Christian doctrine.[105] This declaration marked a deliberate shift from prior Catholic teachings, such as those in medieval catechisms or papal bulls that had amplified deicide accusations based on selective New Testament readings (e.g., Matthew 27:25), by prioritizing contextual exegesis over supersessionist interpretations that portrayed Jews as eternally culpable.[105] Theologically, it affirmed the enduring covenant with Israel while urging Catholics to view Jews not as adversaries but as "elder brothers" in faith, influencing subsequent liturgical reforms like the 1970 Roman Missal's removal of references to Jewish perfidy in Good Friday prayers.[1] In response to post-Holocaust critiques of Christian complicity in antisemitism, Nostra Aetate facilitated institutional dialogues, such as the 1974 establishment of the Commission for Religious Relations with the Jews, fostering joint biblical studies that emphasize shared scriptural heritage over divisive passages.[106] Scholarly assessments credit it with reducing theological justifications for prejudice, though implementation varied, with some traditionalist Catholics viewing it as a rupture from patristic exegesis.[107] By 2015, on its 50th anniversary, Vatican statements highlighted its role in promoting "fraternity and collaboration" without altering core Christological claims.[108]

Evangelical and Protestant Reassessments

In response to post-Holocaust charges of antisemitism in the New Testament, many Evangelical scholars and organizations have emphasized the Jewish authorship and intra-Jewish polemical context of the texts, arguing that passages like Matthew 27:25 represent prophetic self-critique rather than ethnic condemnation.[109] For instance, New Testament scholar Darrell Bock, a professor at Dallas Theological Seminary, contends that the Gospel writers, being Jewish, directed critiques at specific religious leaders, not the Jewish people as a whole, and that references to "the Jews" in John target oppositional groups amid first-century synagogue tensions.[109] This approach rejects interpretations imposing collective guilt or deicide charges, viewing such readings as anachronistic distortions detached from the historical setting of Roman-occupied Judea.[78] Evangelicals have also reassessed supersessionismâ€” the notion that the Church fully replaces Israel in God's covenantsâ€”often rejecting it in favor of a future restoration for ethnic Israel based on Romans 11:25-29.[110] Organizations like Chosen People Ministries argue that supersessionism undermines evangelism to Jews and ignores unconditional Abrahamic promises, a view reinforced post-1948 with Israel's reestablishment as evidence of divine fidelity.[111] Similarly, the Friends of Israel Gospel Ministry labels any residual Evangelical antisemitism as sin requiring repentance, urging adherence to biblical mandates against hatred (e.g., Matthew 5:44).[112] These reassessments prioritize grammatical-historical exegesis, affirming the New Testament's continuity with Old Testament prophecies rather than portraying Judaism as obsolete.[113] Among broader Protestant traditions, figures like Karl Barth critiqued punitive supersessionism by the mid-20th century, positing that God's election of Israel persists alongside the Church's role. Post-1960s, Evangelical perspectives, as articulated in works like Francois du Toit's analysis, seek an "anti-Judaism-free" reading by highlighting the New Testament's endorsement of Torah observance (e.g., Matthew 5:17-18) and framing critiques as familial disputes within Judaism.[114] Such efforts, including Michael Brown's documentation of historical Christian failings, aim to purge misreadings that fueled pogroms while upholding the texts' soteriological claims.[109] This contrasts with some liberal Protestant strains that retain supersessionist undertones but has gained traction in confessional circles emphasizing Israel's enduring election.[115]
