New Right (Denmark)

Nye Borgerlige, commonly translated into English as the New Right or New Bourgeois, was a national-conservative and right-wing populist political party in Denmark active from 2015 to 2024.[1][2] Founded on 23 November 2015 by architect Pernille Vermund and lawyer Peter Seier Christensen, both former members of the Conservative People's Party, the party positioned itself as a defender of classical liberal economics, including lower taxes and reduced welfare state expansion, alongside stringent immigration policies such as zero asylum intake, mandatory repatriation of non-integrated migrants, and opposition to multiculturalism.[2][3][4] In its 2019 parliamentary debut, Nye Borgerlige received 2.6% of the national vote but failed to secure seats due to vote fragmentation in certain constituencies; however, it gained traction by the 2022 general election, capturing approximately 3.7% of the votes (129,524 ballots) and initially winning six seats in the Folketing, though one mandate was lost amid post-election defections and recounts.[5][6] The party's influence extended beyond electoral gains, as its hardline stances on migration contributed to a broader rightward shift in Danish policy discourse, pressuring established parties like the Danish People's Party to adopt tougher positions on asylum and integration.[3][7] Internal challenges, including a reported 64% membership decline since early 2023 and leadership tensions, culminated in Vermund's announcement of the party's dissolution on 10 January 2024, a move framed as strategic to streamline the fragmented right-wing opposition ahead of future elections.[8][9][10]

History

Founding and Early Development (2015–2018)

The New Right (Nye Borgerlige) was founded in October 2015 by Pernille Vermund, an architect and former member of the Conservative People's Party, and Peter Seier Christensen, a chemical engineer who also departed from the Conservatives.[2] The party's creation stemmed from dissatisfaction with established center-right parties' approaches to immigration, cultural integration, and economic policy, particularly amid the 2015 European migrant crisis that heightened public concerns over multiculturalism's compatibility with Denmark's welfare model and national identity.[2] Vermund cited her previous party's shift toward excessive liberalism as a key impetus, arguing it failed to robustly defend Danish values against perceived threats from mass immigration and Islamization.[2] From inception, the party articulated a platform blending national conservatism with libertarian economics, advocating stricter immigration controls beyond those of the Danish People's Party, which emphasized welfare chauvinism, while prioritizing tax reductions and market liberalization to sustain fiscal viability amid demographic pressures.[2] This positioning aimed to appeal to voters disillusioned with mainstream parties' compromises on cultural preservation and welfare sustainability, framing multiculturalism as unaffordable given empirical strains on public resources and social cohesion evidenced by integration challenges and rising welfare costs.[11] Early organizational efforts focused on grassroots mobilization and securing electoral viability outside the Folketing. By September 2016, the party collected sufficient supporter signatures to gain ballot access for national elections, demonstrating initial public resonance through media appearances and public debates highlighting immigration's causal links to security and economic issues.[12] Participation in the 2017 municipal elections, though yielding no seats, built visibility and organizational infrastructure via local activism, without achieving parliamentary entry during this period.[2]

Entry into Parliament (2019 Election)

In the snap general election to the Folketing on 5 June 2019, Nye Borgerlige secured 2.6 percent of the valid votes cast nationwide, amounting to 116,619 votes and earning the party four seats in the 179-member parliament for the first time.[13] This modest but threshold-surpassing result positioned the party as a minor player amid a fragmented right-wing spectrum, where the Danish People's Party saw its support halve and a new challenger, Stram Kurs, drew attention with more radical rhetoric but failed to enter parliament.[13] [3] The campaign, led by founder and chairperson Pernille Vermund, focused on stringent immigration controls, arguing from data on disproportionate welfare costs and integration failures associated with non-Western immigrants, which official statistics indicated net fiscal burdens exceeding contributions for many such groups.[14] Vermund's platform critiqued multiculturalism as incompatible with Danish values, appealing to voters disillusioned with the perceived moderation of parties like the Conservatives and Danish People's Party, while advocating tax cuts and deregulation to counter welfare state expansions.[2] The party's direct, data-driven messaging on immigration's empirical impacts resonated in debates heightened by ongoing asylum inflows and crime statistics, helping it capture support from younger demographics skeptical of establishment narratives.[3] Upon entering the Folketing, Nye Borgerlige's four MPs—including Vermund as immigration spokesperson—aligned with non-socialist opposition forces on key votes restricting family reunifications and asylum access, opposing left-leaning amendments that risked loosening controls despite the Social Democrats' own tightening under Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen.[14] The party initiated inquiries into immigration's long-term costs, citing government reports on higher incarceration rates and social service usage among non-Western populations, establishing an early role in pressuring for evidence-based policy over ideological concessions.[14] This parliamentary foothold marked the transition from fringe status to national influence, though limited seats constrained broader impact initially.[3]

Expansion and Peak Influence (2020–2022)

The Nye Borgerlige party gained heightened visibility in Danish politics from 2020 onward through its vocal opposition role in the Folketing, particularly in debates centering on cultural assimilation and immigration enforcement. Party leader Pernille Vermund positioned the group as a proponent of uncompromising rules requiring immigrants to fully embrace Danish norms, including language proficiency and secular values, differentiating it from perceived leniency in established parties like the Danish People's Party.[3] This advocacy aligned with persistent public concerns over integration failures, as evidenced by parliamentary discussions on restricting family reunifications and revoking residency for non-assimilating migrants, where Nye Borgerlige consistently voted against expansions of asylum rights.[14] The party's emphasis on these issues contributed to a rightward shift in mainstream discourse, pressuring the Social Democratic government to implement tighter controls, such as expanded deportation criteria and reduced welfare access for newcomers, measures initiated under right-wing influence since 2015 but reinforced amid 2020-2022 migration debates.[15] [16] Empirical data from integration metrics showed stagnant employment rates among non-Western immigrants at around 50% by 2021, bolstering Nye Borgerlige's arguments for causal links between lax policies and social cohesion breakdowns, though government sources attributed challenges more to individual factors than systemic policy failures.[17] Opinion polls reflected this momentum, with Nye Borgerlige support rising from a median of approximately 2% in early 2020 to 3-4% by late 2021, correlating with Denmark's post-COVID economic rebound—GDP growth exceeding 4% in 2021—and heightened scrutiny of welfare sustainability amid fiscal recovery.[18] [19] The party bolstered its platform with internal policy development on EU relations, advocating skepticism toward deeper integration to preserve national sovereignty in areas like border control, though specific legislative votes on EU matters during this period highlighted divisions, with Nye Borgerlige opposing expansions of supranational authority.[20] This pre-electoral positioning capitalized on voter disillusionment with centrist compromises, fostering alliances with other conservative factions on fiscal restraint and cultural preservation without formal coalition commitments.

Post-2022 Decline and Internal Crises (2022–2025)

Following the November 2022 general election, in which Nye Borgerlige secured six seats in the Folketing, the party experienced rapid internal destabilization.[21] On January 10, 2023, party founder and chairman Pernille Vermund announced her resignation from leadership, stating she would not seek reelection at the annual conference and intended to exit politics.[22] Lars Boje Mathiesen was elected as her successor on February 7, 2023, but was ousted by the national executive on March 9 after just 30 days, amid disputes over financial management and personal expenditures from party funds.[23] [24] Vermund temporarily resumed de facto leadership as the party deferred a permanent replacement until October 2023, but membership eroded sharply, dropping 64% by November 2023 due to ongoing factionalism and loss of key figures.[8] This infighting undermined organizational cohesion, preventing the party from consolidating its parliamentary presence despite external right-wing policy advances on immigration.[7] By January 2024, with the parliamentary group reduced to three MPs after prior departures, Vermund formally left the party on January 10, recommending its dissolution to the national executive to avert vote fragmentation among center-right forces ahead of future elections.[21] [25] She joined Liberal Alliance on January 17, citing the need to unify right-wing support.[26] The parliamentary group dissolved shortly thereafter, leaving Nye Borgerlige without Folketing representation.[25] Despite Vermund's call for dissolution, remnants of the party persisted, electing former Danish People's Party MP Martin Henriksen as chairman in April 2024 at an extraordinary meeting.[27] Henriksen's tenure lasted until April 2025, when he was replaced by Susanne Borggaard as political leader, marking the fourth leadership change in under two years and further evidencing chronic instability.[28] These recurrent crises, rooted in personal disputes and failure to retain talent, contributed to voter base attrition and positioned the party on the brink of irrelevance by mid-2025, as polling reflected splintered support amid broader far-right fragmentation.[7]

Ideology and Core Principles

National Conservatism and Populism

The New Right in Denmark embodies national conservatism by advocating for the primacy of Danish sovereignty and cultural continuity, viewing the nation-state as the fundamental unit for preserving social order and individual freedoms. This stance rests on the principle that enduring societies emerge from organic, shared values rather than imposed pluralism, with policies evaluated through observable consequences rather than abstract ideals of global harmony.[2] The party critiques supranational entities like the European Union for eroding national decision-making, favoring instead a realist assessment of how cultural dilution undermines trust and cohesion essential to Denmark's welfare model.[9] Right-wing populism forms a complementary element, framing political discourse as a contest between the authentic concerns of the Danish populace and an out-of-touch elite favoring progressive internationalism. This manifests in rhetoric decrying the disconnect between ordinary citizens' experiences of neighborhood changes and official narratives celebrating diversity, positioning the New Right as a defender of the "common people" against bureaucratic overreach.[2] Empirical data on persistent challenges, such as the overrepresentation of non-Western immigrants in crime statistics—where foreign-origin individuals exhibited conviction rates 1.86 times higher than native Danes in 2022—bolsters this populist critique, highlighting elite failures to address causal factors like selective migration sources.[29][30] Central to this ideology is a rejection of multiculturalism's premise that demographic diversity inherently strengthens societies, countered by evidence of integration shortfalls including high unemployment among non-Western groups (17 percentage points below Danish-origin persons in recent labor metrics) and the emergence of segregated "ghetto" areas with elevated criminality.[31][32] Danish authorities' own designations of 22 such parallel societies in 2018, characterized by non-Western majorities and social issues, exemplify these outcomes, prompting a causal focus on cultural incompatibility over assimilationist optimism.[32][33] By privileging such verifiable metrics—over academic or media assertions downplaying disparities amid institutional left-leaning biases—the New Right grounds its conservatism in outcomes that prioritize national resilience.[34]

Cultural Preservation and Identity

The New Right (Nye Borgerlige) prioritizes assimilation as the pathway to cultural cohesion, rejecting multiculturalism as incompatible with Danish societal norms. Party leaders argue that immigrants must fully adopt Danish values, including loyalty to the nation, to prevent the formation of parallel societies. This stance includes support for policies dismantling ethnic enclaves, such as Denmark's ghetto laws, which aim to disperse non-Western concentrations exceeding 30% of residents in certain areas by 2030 through mandatory relocations and heightened social requirements.[14][35] Nye Borgerlige advocates restrictions on visible religious symbols in public spaces to reinforce secular Danish identity, building on existing national bans like the 2018 prohibition on face coverings. Party co-founder Pernille Vermund has described Islam as a "culture of violence" prevalent across Islamic-majority regions, citing empirical patterns of unrest and honor-based conflicts observed in immigrant communities. This critique is underpinned by surveys revealing stark value divergences: a 2024 study found 20% of Danish Muslims favor implementing Sharia law locally, while 54% prioritize the Quran over conflicting Western democratic principles, contrasting with native Danes' overwhelming commitment to liberal equality and secular governance.[36][37][38] To bolster national identity, the party seeks reforms in education emphasizing Danish history, language, and Enlightenment heritage over imported ideologies. It opposes public funding for schools rooted in Islamic worldviews, proposing their defunding to ensure curricula align with core Danish norms like gender equality and free speech. In media, Nye Borgerlige critiques state broadcaster DR for promoting narratives eroding traditional values, advocating reduced subsidies to foster content celebrating Denmark's cultural legacy rather than accommodating divergent customs.[39][40]

First-Principles Approach to Governance

The New Right emphasizes policy formulation rooted in empirical evidence and experiential knowledge, prioritizing causal mechanisms over dogmatic adherence to prevailing norms. Party leader Pernille Vermund articulated this ethos in 2018, stating that legislation should be built "på viden og erfaring" (on knowledge and experience), advocating for straightforward acknowledgment of societal realities rather than evasion.[41] This approach manifests in the party's "faktabasen," a compendium of statistics highlighting disproportionate public assistance usage among non-Western immigrants, such as high shares of Afghans reliant on welfare, to argue that universal access without national prioritization incentivizes dependency and fiscal strain.[42] Such analysis counters assumptions of welfare universality by tracing causal links between lax eligibility and resource depletion, evidenced by estimates of 9 billion Danish kroner annually in integration costs for refugees and immigrants.[43] In governance, this methodological rigor extends to skepticism of supranational structures like the EU, where the party favors pragmatic nationalism over integrationist ideals, viewing centralized mandates as detached from verifiable national benefits. EU-skepticism aligns with critiques of overreach that undermine sovereignty, as seen in opposition to expansive federalism alongside parties like Dansk Folkeparti.[44] Similarly, on climate policy, the New Right rejects alarmist narratives in favor of cost-benefit evaluations, criticizing binding laws like Denmark's 2019 climate legislation for lacking empirical grounding in domestic priorities and potential economic harms.[45] This contrasts with broader political avoidance of data-driven scrutiny, such as understating immigration's demographic impacts on crime rates—where non-Western male descendants exhibit over double the criminality of Danish men—or cultural cohesion, privileging uncomfortable empirics over consensus-driven politeness.[46] Internally, the party's culture fosters truth-seeking by challenging institutional biases, including those in academia and media that inflate supranational virtues or downplay welfare misappropriation by select groups.[47] This undiluted reasoning underpins a rejection of ideological conformity, positioning governance as an experimental pursuit of causal realism—evident in the party's self-description as a failed "eksperiment" aimed at realigning policy with observable outcomes rather than performative equity.[48] By privileging verifiable metrics, such as integration expenditures and offender demographics, the New Right seeks to recalibrate state functions toward sustainability, eschewing narratives that obscure fiscal causality for multicultural presuppositions.

Policy Positions

Immigration and Asylum Policies

The New Right (Nye Borgerlige) promotes a zero-asylum policy within Denmark, limiting intake to strictly vetted cases under UN resettlement quotas for those arriving directly from camps and possessing verifiable job offers or skills aligned with Danish labor needs. This approach aims to eliminate spontaneous asylum applications by processing claims externally, thereby deterring irregular migration routes. The party explicitly advocates withdrawing from the 1951 UN Refugee Convention, viewing it as an obstacle to sovereign border control and repatriation efforts.[2][49] Central to their platform is mandatory repatriation for non-integrated migrants, including incentives such as financial bonuses for voluntary returns and enforced deportation for those convicted of crimes or reliant on public funds. Temporary protection status would replace permanent residency, with automatic reviews tied to origin-country conditions, echoing Denmark's existing but intensified self-sufficiency programs. Nye Borgerlige cites empirical data on fiscal impacts, including a 2021 Ministry of Immigration and Integration analysis estimating non-Western immigrants and descendants impose a net annual cost of 31 billion Danish kroner (approximately 4.2 billion euros), or 85,000 kroner per person, to justify halting benefits like welfare and housing subsidies for asylum seekers and recent arrivals. These measures, they argue, address Denmark's disproportionate per-capita immigration load compared to neighbors, where inflows have strained public resources despite recent policy tightenings reducing asylum grants by over 80% since 2015 peaks.[50][14] The party staunchly opposes family reunification for refugees, proposing indefinite bans or high-threshold requirements like multi-year self-sufficiency proofs to prevent chain migration. This differentiates Nye Borgerlige from the Danish People's Party, which they criticize as complacent; instead, they demand a full paradigm shift toward deterrence, including border fortifications and international agreements for rapid returns, positioning immigration control as a prerequisite for preserving fiscal sustainability and public order. Such policies, per party statements, have precedent in Denmark's post-2015 reforms but require escalation to achieve near-zero net migration from non-Western sources.[3][5]

Economic and Fiscal Stance

The New Right (Nye Borgerlige) advocates a libertarian-leaning economic framework emphasizing tax reductions, deregulation, and fiscal restraint to enhance individual incentives and economic growth. The party proposes comprehensive tax cuts totaling 100 billion Danish kroner (DKK) across labor income, corporate profits, capital gains, inheritance, and vehicle registrations, arguing that lower taxation stimulates productivity and investment without compromising core public services.[51] This stance critiques Denmark's high-tax model, which the party claims burdens competitiveness, as evidenced by public expenditures rising 136 billion DKK since 2000 amid stagnant per-capita growth relative to peers.[52] Fiscal policy prioritizes downsizing the public sector through eliminating up to 100,000 bureaucratic positions by 2035, redirecting savings toward efficiency gains rather than expansion. Nye Borgerlige contends that excessive regulation in the welfare system fosters dependency and inefficiency, advocating deregulation to restore personal responsibility while preserving universal access for Danish citizens.[53] The party supports lowering corporate and top marginal tax rates, alongside slashing stock taxation, to attract capital and align Denmark's model with lower-tax Nordic outliers like Sweden post-reforms, citing empirical evidence that such measures boost GDP without eroding fiscal stability.[54] Welfare reforms focus on sustainability amid demographic pressures, including an aging population projected to strain pension and healthcare systems, with the party favoring "welfare chauvinism" that reserves benefits for contributing nationals to prevent insolvency. This approach draws on data showing Denmark's welfare spending at 25-30% of GDP, higher than most OECD peers, and posits that targeted cuts in administrative overhead—rather than benefit levels—ensure long-term viability through first-principles efficiency.[3][55] Critics from left-leaning outlets label this neoliberal shift as undermining social equity, but Nye Borgerlige counters with evidence from tax-cut episodes, such as the 2000s reductions that correlated with employment rises, emphasizing causal links between fiscal discipline and prosperity over redistributive expansion.[56][2]

Social and Cultural Reforms

Nye Borgerlige promoted family policies emphasizing parental autonomy and financial relief to bolster the nuclear family structure, arguing that rigid state mandates undermine family decision-making. The party proposed flexible parental leave systems where families could allocate leave periods independently, rather than adhering to predefined quotas, to accommodate diverse family needs and encourage greater involvement from both parents.[57] They also advocated expanding home-based childcare options, including tax incentives or subsidies for parents opting out of institutional daycare, positing that such measures foster stronger family cohesion and child development over universal daycare mandates.[58] [59] Party leader Pernille Vermund critiqued egalitarian policies for eroding traditional family priorities, stating that Denmark had "moved away from the family in the pursuit of equality," and called for reforms restoring balance to support familial stability amid declining birth rates, which stood at 1.55 children per woman in 2022.[60] In education, Nye Borgerlige favored decentralizing control from politicians to educators and parents, advocating school choice mechanisms and reduced bureaucratic oversight to prioritize practical skills and merit-based advancement over ideological curricula. Undervisningsordfører Mette Thiesen emphasized that "the school is the people's—not the politicians'," pushing for greater institutional autonomy in pedagogy while supporting a national core curriculum to instill shared Danish values, including cultural heritage and critical thinking free from progressive indoctrination.[61] [62] The party opposed excessive focus on standardized testing and equity-driven interventions, instead promoting freedom for schools to tailor programs, with less emphasis on grades to encourage holistic development aligned with labor market demands.[63] On gender and identity, Nye Borgerlige rejected expansive interpretations of gender, affirming biological binaries and opposing state facilitation of transitions for minors. They sought to prohibit medical interventions for gender dysphoria in children under 18, highlighting inconsistencies such as allowing 15-year-olds legal gender changes while restricting alcohol purchases, which they deemed a "steep slope" prioritizing ideology over evidence of long-term harms.[64] Party figures, including youth representatives, denounced feminist ideology as a "cancerous growth" antithetical to merit and individual liberty, favoring policies that recognize innate sex differences without quotas or affirmative measures that distort equality of opportunity.[65] [66] Regarding law and order, the party supported harsher penalties for serious and organized crime, including expanded use of expulsion for foreign offenders and streamlined processes to deny residency to convicted non-citizens, aiming to deter recidivism through swift enforcement. Nye Borgerlige endorsed tougher sentencing frameworks, opposing plea bargains that dilute accountability, and aligned with broader conservative calls for zero-tolerance approaches to offenses undermining social cohesion, such as gang-related activities prevalent in immigrant-heavy areas.[67] [68] This stance reflected their cultural preservation ethos, where stricter enforcement preserved traditional Danish norms against imported criminal subcultures.[69]

Foreign Policy and EU Skepticism

The New Right (Nye Borgerlige) maintains a staunchly Eurosceptic stance, advocating for Denmark's full withdrawal from the European Union to restore national sovereignty and address perceived democratic deficits in Brussels' decision-making processes. The party argues that EU integration erodes member states' autonomy, particularly through supranational policies on trade, regulation, and migration that impose uniform rules ill-suited to Denmark's interests. Pernille Vermund, the party's founder, has described the EU's trajectory toward federalism as incompatible with Danish self-determination, emphasizing that ordinary citizens increasingly recognize the bloc's overreach. This position aligns with the party's broader critique of EU migration pacts, such as the New Pact on Migration and Asylum adopted in 2024, which they view as facilitating uncontrolled inflows and burden-sharing that undermine border security and fiscal independence.[70][71] In contrast to its EU opposition, the New Right strongly endorses Denmark's NATO membership as a cornerstone of national defense, prioritizing alliance commitments to counter threats like Russian aggression while insisting on decisions guided by Danish strategic interests rather than multilateral consensus. The party has repeatedly called for Denmark to achieve NATO's 2% of GDP defense spending guideline—reached by Denmark in 2024 but urged earlier by Nye Borgerlige since 2016—through targeted investments in military capabilities, including long-range weapons and personnel increases. This includes support for abolishing Denmark's EU defense opt-out in a June 2022 referendum, where 66.9% voted in favor, to enable flexible cooperation on security without full EU political union. Such measures, they contend, enhance deterrence without compromising sovereignty.[72][73][74] The party's foreign policy emphasizes pragmatic bilateral alliances with like-minded nations, particularly the United States, over entanglement in EU-led initiatives or supranational moral crusades. Nye Borgerlige proposes integrating Denmark's trade policy more closely with U.S. partnerships to offset EU dependencies, rejecting what it sees as the bloc's ideological foreign policy that prioritizes globalism over realism. This approach extends to skepticism of United Nations frameworks, including human rights conventions interpreted to constrain national migration controls, favoring instead aid to refugees in proximate regions to prevent mass movements into Europe. Overall, the New Right's outlook prioritizes causal national security gains—rooted in empirical defense needs and sovereignty preservation—over abstract internationalist ideals.[71][75]

Electoral Performance

National Elections (Folketing)

Nye Borgerlige contested its first Folketing election in 2019, achieving 2.6% of the national vote and securing 4 seats out of 179, surpassing the 2% threshold required for representation through a combination of constituency and leveling seats.[76] This breakthrough positioned the party as a minor but viable force on the right, drawing votes primarily from disillusioned conservatives prioritizing stringent immigration controls and fiscal restraint over the more established Danish People's Party (DF), which garnered only 8.7% and 16 seats in the same election amid its ongoing decline from prior peaks.[76] In the snap election of November 1, 2022, Nye Borgerlige expanded its foothold, attaining 3.7% of the vote and 6 seats, reflecting modest growth amid broader right-wing fragmentation that saw new entrants like the Denmark Democrats siphon votes from DF.[77] The increase correlated with intensified campaigning on national identity and opposition to EU overreach, appealing to voters in constituencies where mainstream center-right parties were perceived as insufficiently assertive on cultural issues.[78]

Subsequent polling through 2025 indicates a sharp downturn, with averages hovering at 1.6%—below the representational threshold—attributable to internal schisms, including the January 2024 departure of founder Pernille Vermund to Liberal Alliance, which eroded organizational cohesion and voter confidence.[1][27] Regional patterns reveal uneven support, with relatively higher shares in urban conservative enclaves like Greater Copenhagen suburbs compared to rural strongholds of agrarian parties, underscoring the party's niche draw among educated, middle-class skeptics of multiculturalism rather than broad populist bases.[77] This contrasts with DF's historical rural emphasis, highlighting Nye Borgerlige's targeted appeal in areas blending economic liberalism with identity concerns.[7]

Local and Regional Elections

In the 2017 local elections, Nye Borgerlige, as a newly formed party, fielded candidates in only a limited number of municipalities due to resource constraints, resulting in minimal representation and no significant national breakthrough, though it aligned with emerging right-wing voter shifts observed nationally.[79] By the 2021 local elections, the party expanded its outreach, achieving a national vote share of 3.57% and securing 65 seats across Danish municipalities, exceeding its internal target of at least 10 mandates and marking a record performance that reflected growing grassroots appeal amid low overall turnout of around 66%.[80][81] This progress correlated with the party's national polling gains but remained hampered by uneven candidate slates and competition from established conservative parties. Regional election outcomes in 2021 were more restrained, with Nye Borgerlige gaining isolated seats in select regions—such as one or two per council in areas like the Capital Region and Zealand—totaling fewer than 10 mandates overall, underscoring limited penetration into regional governance structures dominated by larger parties.[79] Factors including lower voter turnout in regional races (typically below municipal levels) and the party's focus on urban and suburban strongholds contributed to these modest results, without achieving mayoral influences or policy veto power. Following the 2022 national election, Nye Borgerlige experienced a sharp decline in local engagement, attributed to internal divisions and leadership instability, leading to a collapse in organizational capacity.[82] By preparations for the 2025 local elections, the party planned to contest only 17 municipalities—a fraction of its 2021 scope—signaling empirical seat losses and weakened grassroots infrastructure, with many local politicians defecting or retiring amid the party's broader fragmentation.[83] This downturn contrasted with its prior upward trajectory, highlighting vulnerabilities in sustaining subnational momentum without national parliamentary leverage.

Leadership and Internal Dynamics

Founders and Initial Leadership

Nye Borgerlige was co-founded in 2015 by Pernille Vermund and Peter Seier Christensen after the pair broke with the Conservative People's Party over disagreements regarding EU policy and immigration restrictions.[84] Vermund, a trained architect, assumed the position of party leader, drawing on her prior involvement in conservative politics to establish a platform emphasizing national sovereignty and cultural preservation.[2] Christensen, a chemical engineer by background, co-led the initiative, contributing to the party's early emphasis on libertarian-leaning economic reforms alongside strict migration controls.[2] The initial leadership structure centered on Vermund's chairmanship, with Christensen serving in a prominent co-founding capacity to shape policy foundations.[84] Early organizational efforts included the formation of a national committee to oversee operations and the prompt establishment of a youth wing, Nye Borgerliges Ungdom, to engage younger supporters and develop future cadres.[85] This setup prioritized grassroots mobilization and policy development committees focused on core issues like fiscal restraint and border security, reflecting the founders' vision for a disciplined, ideologically coherent alternative to established right-wing parties.[2]

Succession Crises and Key Figures

Pernille Vermund, the party's founder and long-serving chairman, announced her resignation on January 10, 2023, citing a desire to step away from politics after eight years of leadership.[22][86] Lars Boje Mathiesen was elected as her successor at an extraordinary party meeting on February 7, 2023, but his tenure lasted only 30 days. On March 9, 2023, the national executive unanimously expelled Mathiesen over a dispute involving party finances and unauthorized salary payments to himself and staff.[23][87][24] Following Mathiesen's expulsion, two Members of Parliament had already defected from the party, exacerbating internal disarray, and Vermund temporarily reclaimed the chairmanship to stabilize operations.[23] The party deferred electing a permanent chairman until its ordinary annual meeting in October 2023, but ongoing turmoil contributed to a sharp membership decline, with 64% of members lost by November 2023 compared to January.[8] This leadership vacuum and expulsions undermined party cohesion, as key figures' personal conflicts over resources highlighted deeper fractures in decision-making structures. In April 2024, amid continued instability, Martin Henriksen, a former Danish People's Party MP, was elected chairman, marking an attempt to inject experienced leadership and refocus on systemic critiques of Danish elites.[88] Henriksen's tenure faced challenges from internal dissent, including calls for his replacement by figures like Susanne Borggaard in December 2024. By April 2025, Henriksen announced his full withdrawal from politics, declining to seek re-election as chairman or run in municipal elections, further signaling leadership fatigue.[89][90][91] Susanne Borggaard succeeded Henriksen as chairman on April 27, 2025, in the party's fourth leadership change since the 2022 Folketing election, underscoring persistent succession instability. Concurrently, high-profile exits intensified, including Vermund's defection to the Liberal Alliance and MP Kim Edberg Andersen becoming an independent, reducing parliamentary representation and eroding the party's organizational unity. These repeated crises, driven by interpersonal disputes and strategic disagreements among key figures, have causally contributed to diminished internal cohesion and electoral viability.[92][89]

Controversies and Debates

Internal Party Conflicts

In early 2023, following the November 2022 parliamentary election, Nye Borgerlige experienced significant internal turmoil during a leadership transition. Pernille Vermund, the party's founder and initial leader, stepped down in February 2023, paving the way for Lars Boje Mathiesen to assume the chairmanship as the sole candidate.[93][23] This shift reignited longstanding factional tensions, culminating in Mathiesen's dismissal and expulsion from the party on March 10, 2023, after he demanded the transfer of approximately 350,000 Danish kroner from a party reserve fund to an external account under his control, which party members viewed as a breach of financial protocols.[87][24][23] The expulsion of Mathiesen, a prominent figure known for his fiscal conservatism and anti-establishment rhetoric, highlighted deeper divisions between purist elements advocating strict ideological adherence and those favoring pragmatic financial management to sustain party operations.[87] This event eroded trust within the parliamentary group, which had secured six seats in 2022, and prompted additional resignations, including that of vice president Henriette Ergemann in early 2023 over controversial social media posts opposing COVID-19 vaccinations, further exposing rifts over public messaging discipline.[94] By mid-2023, these conflicts had diminished the party's cohesion, with reports of ongoing disputes over resource allocation and strategic direction contributing to a loss of key activists and reduced internal morale.[93] Tensions persisted into 2024, manifesting in the departure of founder Pernille Vermund on January 10, 2024, who announced the dissolution of the party's parliamentary group and its overall disbandment, citing the proliferation of fragmented center-right parties as a barrier to effective opposition.[21][9] Vermund's exit, followed by other senior members joining entities like Liberal Alliance, reflected a strategic pivot toward consolidation but also underscored causal fractures between ideological hardliners resistant to merger and pragmatists seeking broader alliances to amplify influence.[27] Although remnants of the party elected a new chairman in April 2024 in an attempt to revive operations, the cumulative departures had already halved the active parliamentary representation and severely hampered organizational efficacy, as evidenced by the failure to maintain unified policy platforms.[27]

Policy Criticisms and External Accusations

Critics from left-leaning Danish media and political opponents, such as the Social Democrats and Socialist People's Party, have accused Nye Borgerlige of fostering Islamophobia through policies targeting Muslim immigration and practices, including proposals to suspend asylum from Muslim-majority countries and enforce stricter integration requirements.[95] These accusations portray the party's stance on halting non-Western immigration as rooted in cultural exclusion rather than pragmatic concerns, with outlets like Politiken framing such positions as contributing to a broader societal hostility toward Islam.[96] Party leaders, including Pernille Vermund, have rejected these labels, asserting that their policies address verifiable integration challenges, such as the overrepresentation of non-Western immigrants—predominantly from Muslim countries—in welfare dependency and criminal activity, where Statistics Denmark data shows employment rates for this group at around 50% compared to 75% for native Danes as of 2022.[97] External reports, including U.S. State Department assessments, have highlighted Nye Borgerlige's platform as explicitly critical of Islam, interpreting calls for bans on face coverings and parallel society dispersal as discriminatory against religious expression, though these align with Denmark's 2018 nationwide burqa prohibition enacted under a center-left government.[98][99] Accusations of extremism intensified following party proposals to classify Islam as incompatible with Danish values in certain contexts, drawing parallels to historical xenophobia in European Islamophobia indices produced by advocacy groups, which attribute rising anti-Muslim sentiment to parties like Nye Borgerlige despite lacking direct causal evidence beyond policy rhetoric.[100] Defenders, including conservative commentators, counter that such criticisms ignore empirical failures in multiculturalism, pointing to Denmark's "ghetto laws" targeting high-immigrant areas with elevated crime rates—up to five times the national average in some non-Western dominated neighborhoods—and sustained public backing for restrictive measures.[101] Public opinion data undermines claims of fringe extremism, with polls from 2017-2018 showing 62-83% Danish support for burqa and niqab bans, reflecting widespread concern over visible symbols of non-integration rather than blanket prejudice.[102][103] Nye Borgerlige has faced no successful legal challenges deeming its policies unconstitutional, with Danish courts upholding similar immigration curbs under the European Convention on Human Rights, as integration statistics reveal persistent disparities: non-Western immigrants and descendants comprise 14% of the population but account for disproportionate social costs, including 32% reliance on transfer income versus 27% for natives in recent analyses.[104] Left-wing sources often amplify accusations without engaging this data, a pattern attributable to institutional biases favoring expansive migration narratives over causal evidence of policy-induced welfare strain and cultural friction.[105]

Impact and Reception

Achievements and Policy Influences

The New Right (Nye Borgerlige) contributed to tightening Denmark's immigration framework by advocating policies exceeding those of larger right-wing parties, such as zero asylum grants and mandatory repatriation for non-integrated migrants, which amplified demands for border controls in parliamentary debates from 2019 onward.[3] This positioning pressured mainstream parties, including the Social Democrats under Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen, to sustain and expand restrictive measures post-2019, such as the 2021 external asylum processing law and tightened family reunification rules requiring 28 years of residency for sponsors.[14][106] By 2023, Denmark's asylum approval rate had fallen to 35% from over 50% pre-2015, reflecting a consensus on limiting inflows to safeguard welfare sustainability—a shift partly driven by competition among right-wing actors like Nye Borgerlige criticizing the Danish People's Party for insufficient rigor.[14][5] In welfare policy, Nye Borgerlige supported parliamentary initiatives for public sector efficiency, including votes favoring reduced administrative burdens and tax cuts to counter immigration-related fiscal strains, aligning with their neoliberal critique of expansive state spending.[2] Their six seats secured after the November 2022 election enabled case-by-case backing of government reforms, such as 2023 proposals to streamline integration requirements tying benefits to employment, which echoed the party's emphasis on self-reliance over universal entitlements.[23] These stances reinforced broader efforts to recalibrate welfare amid demographic pressures, with Denmark's 2024 budget incorporating efficiency mandates that reduced non-Western immigrant benefit dependency by 15% through stricter activation policies.[15] The party's focus on cultural preservation influenced public discourse, normalizing views on prioritizing Danish norms in integration, as evidenced by 2022 polls showing 65% support for mandatory value-based citizenship tests—up from 45% in 2015—and heightened acceptance of repatriation incentives once deemed marginal.[107] This agenda-setting role extended to critiques of multiculturalism, contributing to legislative wins like the 2021 burqa ban reinforcement and school curriculum mandates emphasizing national history, which gained cross-party traction by framing cultural assimilation as essential for social cohesion.[14]

Criticisms and Opposition Views

Critics from left-leaning organizations have accused Nye Borgerlige of fostering xenophobia, particularly through its emphasis on restricting Muslim immigration and cultural assimilation, labeling the party as part of a splintered far-right movement.[108] Such accusations often originate from advocacy groups with ideological opposition to nationalist policies, though Danish crime statistics reveal immigrants from non-Western countries commit offenses at rates 1.86 times higher than native Danes in 2022, with 36 independent studies confirming overrepresentation relative to population share.[29][30] This empirical disparity supports the party's focus on integration failures as a causal factor in social costs, rather than unfounded prejudice. Centrist and center-right commentators have criticized Nye Borgerlige for electoral ineffectiveness, attributing poor results—such as securing only 3.6% in recent votes despite polling at 8.5% earlier—to chronic internal divisions and vote-splitting on the right.[7] Multiple parliamentary exits, including three MPs departing post-2022 election and the party's temporary dissolution in January 2024, are cited as evidence of leadership instability undermining broader conservative coalitions.[9][10] The move to dissolve was interpreted by some as a tactical concession to consolidate right-wing support, highlighting perceptions of the party as a disruptive fringe rather than a viable partner.[10] Mainstream media outlets frequently frame Nye Borgerlige as "far-right" or extremist, amplifying left-wing narratives while downplaying policy overlaps with now-mainstream Danish restrictions on immigration and EU skepticism.[2][21] This portrayal aligns with observed institutional biases in European journalism toward pathologizing right-wing populism, even as the party's advocated measures—like stricter migrant controls—have influenced governing coalitions without endorsing the extremism label empirically.[7]

Broader Effects on Danish Politics

The New Right's advocacy for stringent immigration controls and cultural assimilation has accelerated a consensus among Danish mainstream parties toward restrictive policies, compelling even the Social Democrats to prioritize border security and repatriation to recapture voter support traditionally drawn to populist platforms.[109][7] This dynamic exemplifies causal pressures from radical right challengers, where electoral competition forces centrist adaptations rather than ideological convergence, resulting in Denmark's implementation of measures like mandatory integration contracts and reduced asylum approvals since the mid-2010s.[14] The party's influence extends beyond domestic borders, contributing to the "Denmarkization" of European migration debates, as evidenced by Denmark's 2025 EU presidency push for external processing and stricter returns, which has prompted discussions in countries like Germany and Sweden about emulating Danish-style limits to deter irregular entries.[110][111] In terms of populist sustainability, the New Right's trajectory underscores the fragility of right-wing movements reliant on charismatic leadership and ideological purity, where recurrent internal schisms—such as leadership transitions and factional exits—have fragmented voter loyalty and stalled organizational growth despite early policy breakthroughs.[7] Empirical patterns from Danish politics reveal that while such parties effectively widen policy windows through voter mobilization, their long-term viability hinges on mitigating elite infighting, as unchecked disputes dilute bargaining power and enable mainstream absorption of their agendas without reciprocal concessions.[5] This contrasts with more enduring populists elsewhere, highlighting Denmark-specific lessons on balancing radical rhetoric with pragmatic alliances to avoid self-inflicted electoral erosion. Amid its weakened position in 2025 opinion surveys, registering support below 2 percent in recent aggregates, the New Right's legacy endures through the entrenchment of its core demands in the national Overton window and the emergence of splinter entities drawing from its nationalist base, potentially seeding future right-wing realignments.[1][7] By normalizing zero-net migration stances once dismissed as fringe, the party has indirectly bolstered center-right coalitions' leverage in parliament, fostering a political environment where laxer alternatives face sustained scrutiny from an activated conservative electorate.[2] This residual impact suggests that, even in decline, such formations catalyze enduring shifts in causal equilibria, prioritizing empirical policy outcomes over partisan dominance.
