Barnes & Thornburg

Barnes & Thornburg LLP is a full-service national business law firm headquartered in Indianapolis, Indiana, formed in 1982 through the merger of two established firms based in Indianapolis and South Bend.[1][2] The firm has expanded significantly since its inception, opening offices across the United States to serve clients in diverse sectors, and now ranks among the 100 largest law firms in the country with over 1,000 employees and annual revenue exceeding $575 million as of recent reports.[3][4][5] Barnes & Thornburg provides counsel in more than 50 practice areas, including intellectual property, commercial litigation, corporate transactions, and labor and employment law, representing a client base that spans Fortune 500 companies to emerging businesses.[6][7][8] Notable achievements include consistent recognition in legal rankings for its IP and litigation capabilities, double-digit revenue growth in recent years, and commitments to professional development and community service initiatives.[7][5][9]

History

Founding and Early Years

Barnes & Thornburg was established in 1982 through the merger of two prominent Indiana law firms: the Indianapolis-based Barnes, Hickam, Pantzer & Boyd and the South Bend-based Thornburg, McGill, Deahl, Harman, Carey & Murray.[1] The Indianapolis firm had been founded on September 13, 1940, by Harvard Law School graduate Kurt Pantzer, who assembled a team of attorneys to build a practice focused on corporate and business law amid the economic challenges of the era.[10] The South Bend firm, tracing its roots to the early 20th century, had similarly developed a strong regional presence serving manufacturing and agricultural clients in northern Indiana.[1] The merger combined complementary strengths, creating one of the largest law firms in the Midwest at the time, with approximately 100 attorneys across its Indianapolis and South Bend offices.[1] This union enabled broader service capabilities, particularly in areas like corporate transactions, litigation, and real estate, while leveraging shared Indiana roots to attract clients from industry sectors dominant in the state, such as manufacturing and finance.[11] In its initial years post-merger, Barnes & Thornburg emphasized strategic diversification by absorbing boutique practices in labor and employment law as well as intellectual property, enhancing its competitive edge in specialized fields.[1] Geographic expansion followed client-driven needs, with the opening of offices in Washington, D.C., to handle federal regulatory matters and in Chicago to tap into Midwestern commercial hubs, marking the firm's transition from a regional to a national player by the late 1980s.[1]

Expansion and Strategic Growth

Barnes & Thornburg has pursued strategic growth primarily through organic expansion, lateral partner hires, and selective acquisitions of boutique practices rather than large-scale mergers with peer firms. This approach allowed the firm to target high-value markets and practice areas such as intellectual property, life sciences, and corporate services without diluting its Midwestern-rooted culture. Unlike some competitors that consolidated via mega-mergers, firm leadership emphasized sustainable scaling aligned with client demands in emerging sectors.[12][1] In the late 2000s and 2010s, the firm methodically broadened its geographic footprint beyond the Midwest. It opened a Columbus, Ohio office in 2009 to strengthen its presence in the Rust Belt manufacturing hub, which grew to contribute significantly to regional litigation and corporate work. Subsequent openings in Nashville, Tennessee, and Dallas, Texasâ€”celebrating 10-year anniversaries by 2025â€”focused on energy, healthcare, and finance sectors, enhancing the firm's Southern capabilities and client proximity in high-growth economies.[13][14] The firm accelerated expansion from 2019, launching eight new offices over the subsequent five years to capture demand in technology, biotech, and East Coast markets. In December 2019, it acquired the nine-partner Brinks Gilson & Lione life sciences patent boutique, enabling simultaneous openings in Raleigh, North Carolina; Ann Arbor, Michigan; and Salt Lake City, Utah, bolstering IP and innovation practices. The New York office followed in June 2020, staffed by 11 lawyers from elite Manhattan firms, targeting asset management and finance. Additional launches included Boston in 2021 and a Wilmington, Delaware presence via a seven-attorney team from Faegre Drinker, focusing on corporate and regulatory work.[5][15][16][17] Recent initiatives underscore ongoing momentum, with the firm renewing and expanding its Chicago lease by over 75,000 square feet in 2024â€”the largest such growth in downtown Chicago among major tenantsâ€”and welcoming nearly 100 new lawyers and professionals since April 2025, including its largest fall associate class. These efforts, coupled with partner promotions and targeted hires in corporate and litigation, drove double-digit revenue increases by positioning the firm in resilient, client-centric markets amid economic shifts.[18][19][5]

Recent Developments

In 2024, Barnes & Thornburg reported gross revenue of $747,226,000, securing the 73rd position on The American Lawyer's Am Law 200 ranking for 2025.[20] The firm elected a new Management Committee for the 2024-2025 term on January 11, 2024, with Patrick as a key appointee among the changes.[21] The firm expanded its partnership ranks by promoting 19 attorneys effective January 1, 2025.[22] In April 2025, it added five partners across corporate, litigation, and labor and employment practices in multiple offices, continuing a pattern of lateral hires that included three healthcare attorneys from Bass, Berry & Sims in Washington, DC.[23][24] Further hires in January 2025 brought in corporate, labor and employment, litigation, and real estate specialists to offices in Atlanta, Indianapolis, Los Angeles, and Philadelphia.[25] Barnes & Thornburg released its third annual Investment Funds Outlook Report on July 22, 2025, surveying over 70 U.S. investment professionals who expressed optimism for private credit (nearly 80% anticipating stronger markets) and cryptocurrency investments (84% positive).[26] In May 2025, the firm highlighted strategic growth driven by client investments and recruiting, yielding strong revenue gains.[27] It also adopted Everlaw as its eDiscovery technology partner in a multi-year agreement announced March 28, 2025.[28]

Organizational Structure

Leadership and Governance

Barnes & Thornburg LLP is governed through a partnership structure typical of large U.S. law firms, where equity partners hold ownership interests and collectively elect a Management Committee to direct firm strategy, operations, and policy. This committee, refreshed periodicallyâ€”most recently for the 2024-2025 termâ€”comprises representatives from multiple offices to reflect the firm's national footprint and ensure balanced decision-making.[21][29] The Management Committee is chaired by Andrew J. Detherage, who serves as Firm Managing Partner, overseeing executive functions including growth initiatives and client relations. Melissa A. Vallone acts as Vice Chair for Administration, focusing on operational efficiency and administrative oversight. Other key committee members for 2024-2025 include Ryan J. Barncastle (Los Angeles office), Joseph G. Eaton (Indianapolis), and Patrick D. This hybrid model supports decentralized execution under unified governance, with partners retaining voting rights on major decisions such as elections and compensation policies.[32][33][7]

Practice Areas

Core Business Services

Barnes & Thornburg offers core business services centered on corporate transactions and counseling, including mergers and acquisitions, joint ventures, and capital raising activities tailored to midmarket and upper-midmarket clients.[43][44] The firm advises business owners and managers on navigating legal compliance challenges, balancing operational risks, and structuring deals through negotiation, documentation, and closing.[44] In corporate governance, the practice emphasizes practical solutions for entity formation, ongoing compliance, and strategic decision-making to mitigate liabilities while aligning with business objectives.[44] Attorneys draw on decades of experience in complex transactions, providing pragmatic counsel that considers client budgets and specific needs rather than one-size-fits-all approaches.[44] Financial services within the core portfolio include structuring investments, debt financing, and regulatory compliance for domestic and international operations, often intersecting with government relations to facilitate business expansion.[45][46] This integrated approach supports clients in sectors requiring robust transactional support, such as manufacturing and technology, ensuring efficient handling of acquisitions, dispositions, and partnership formations.[43]

Specialized and Industry-Focused Practices

Barnes & Thornburg provides industry-focused legal services across sectors including agriculture and food, aviation, energy and utilities, healthcare, manufacturing, and entertainment, integrating specialized knowledge of regulatory, transactional, and litigation challenges unique to each.[47] These practices draw on interdisciplinary teams to address sector-specific issues, such as supply chain disruptions in manufacturing or intellectual property protection in entertainment.[48][49] In healthcare, the firm employs more than 100 dedicated attorneys who counsel hospitals, health systems, physician groups, pharmaceutical manufacturers, and medical device companies on compliance, transactions, and government investigations.[50] This practice emphasizes practical advice informed by in-house experience within healthcare organizations, covering areas like drug and device regulation, healthcare finance, and False Claims Act defense.[51] The agriculture and food practice serves producers, processors, manufacturers, distributors, cooperatives, retailers, and suppliers throughout the supply chain, handling matters from regulatory compliance to international trade disputes.[52] Similarly, the manufacturing group supports U.S. and global clients with legal strategies for logistics, transportation, supply chain optimization, and risk management amid evolving trade policies.[48] Specialized offerings extend to niche areas like motorsports, where attorneys assist teams, tracks, and sponsors with contracts, liability, and event-related litigation; Native American law and policy, focusing on tribal sovereignty, economic development, and federal relations; and aviation, addressing regulatory approvals, aircraft financing, and operational disputes.[47] In entertainment, particularly music, the firm provides creative solutions for licensing, artist agreements, and platform distribution across digital and traditional media.[49] These targeted practices enable the firm to deliver customized counsel that aligns with industry dynamics, often leveraging cross-practice expertise in intellectual property and government affairs.[53]

Notable Representations

High-Profile Litigation and Transactions

Barnes & Thornburg attorneys secured a complete dismissal of all criminal and civil charges against Maximiliano Pilipis, an early Bitcoin adopter and operator of the cryptocurrency exchange Bitcoin.com Exchange, in a U.S. Department of Justice case alleging money laundering and unlicensed money transmission activities dating back to 2014. The victory, announced on February 5, 2025, followed a multi-year defense highlighting the absence of prosecutable evidence and resolved potential penalties exceeding $100 million.[54] In commercial litigation, the firm has defended clients in complex antitrust and class action disputes, including resolutions for multinational corporations facing Sherman Act claims under Sections 1 and 2. Partners such as Kendall Millard have led high-stakes matters involving restraint of trade allegations, leveraging federal court experience to achieve favorable outcomes without detailed public settlement figures.[55] On the transactions side, Barnes & Thornburg advised BrightSpring Health Services, Inc., a leading provider of home- and community-based health services, in a $431.7 million secondary public offering completed on October 24, 2025, involving the sale of shares by selling stockholders and structured compliance with SEC regulations. Earlier in the year, the firm represented the same client in a $304.5 million secondary offering, facilitating capital raising amid healthcare sector growth.[56][57] The firm also counseled Sporos Capital Partners LP, a private investment firm targeting emerging technologies, in strategic investments into X-Energy, a developer of advanced nuclear reactors, and Quantum Space Netherlands B.V., a satellite technology company, announced March 21, 2025, to support innovation in energy and aerospace sectors. In real estate, Barnes & Thornburg represented Summit Equity Investments in the acquisition of Federal Hill Apartments, a mixed-use development in Noblesville, Indiana, emphasizing zoning and financing elements typical of urban redevelopment deals.[58][59] Additional M&A representations include advising Transom Capital Group portfolio company HALO Dream Inc. in its October 5, 2024, acquisition of BreathableBaby LLC, a producer of infant safety products, focusing on integration of consumer goods operations, and Secur-Servâ€”a Scantron Corporation divisionâ€”in the April 16, 2024, purchase of BTech Inc., enhancing security technology capabilities. These deals underscore the firm's role in mid-market transactions across healthcare, technology, and manufacturing, with annual handling of hundreds of such matters.[60][61][62]

2020 Election Challenges

Barnes & Thornburg partners were involved in post-2020 U.S. presidential election litigation efforts, primarily through individual roles rather than firm-sanctioned representations. Matthew Morgan, identified as a partner at the firm, served as general counsel for the Donald Trump presidential campaign, overseeing its nationwide strategy to contest election results in multiple states.[63][64] The campaign filed over 60 lawsuits alleging procedural irregularities, voter fraud, and violations of election laws in battleground states including Pennsylvania, Michigan, Georgia, Wisconsin, and Arizona, though the vast majority were dismissed by courts for lack of evidence, standing, or merit.[65] Morgan's prior experience included advising Vice President Mike Pence and lobbying for Indiana interests while at the firm.[66] In a related 2021 matter tied to 2020 election claims, Minneapolis partner Alec Beck filed a federal lawsuit on behalf of MyPillow CEO Mike Lindell against Dominion Voting Systems and other election technology firms, accusing them of conspiracy and rigging the vote in favor of Joe Biden.[67] The 82-page complaint sought damages exceeding $1.6 billion and was filed without the firm's prior approval or knowledge, prompting Barnes & Thornburg to state that Beck no longer represented the firm and that it did not endorse the action.[68] Beck resigned amid the dispute but continued as local counsel in the case, which was later dismissed with sanctions imposed on Lindell for failing to substantiate claims. These instances highlight individual attorneys' engagements in high-stakes, politically charged litigation, distinct from official firm representations.[69]
