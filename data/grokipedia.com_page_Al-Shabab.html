Al-Shabab

Harakat al-Shabaab al-Mujahideen, commonly known as al-Shabaab or "the Youth," is a Salafi-jihadist militant organization and formal affiliate of al-Qaeda that seeks to overthrow the Somali government and establish an Islamic emirate governed by a strict interpretation of sharia law across Somalia and potentially the broader Horn of Africa.[1] Emerging as the youth militia of the Islamic Courts Union in the mid-2000s, the group gained prominence following Ethiopia's 2006 invasion of Somalia, which radicalized its ranks and prompted a shift toward transnational jihadism.[1] Under the leadership of Ahmed Diriye (also known as Abu Ubaidah), who has headed the organization since the 2014 U.S. drone strike that killed his predecessor Ahmed Abdi Godane, al-Shabaab has demonstrated resilience against Somali federal forces, African Union troops, and international counterterrorism efforts, maintaining an estimated 7,000 to 12,000 fighters and generating approximately $100 million annually through extortion, taxation in controlled territories, and illicit trade.[1] The group formally pledged allegiance to al-Qaeda in 2012, solidifying ties that trace back to the mid-2000s via training and ideological exchange, though internal factions have occasionally debated broader global ambitions versus a Somalia-focused insurgency.[1] Al-Shabaab controls significant rural swaths of central and southern Somalia, including recent gains like the town of Mahaas in July 2025, despite territorial losses in regions such as Hirshabelle and Galmudug amid Somali government offensives launched since 2022.[2] It employs guerrilla tactics, suicide bombings, and assassinations, with major operations including the 2010 Kampala bombings that killed 74, the 2013 Westgate Mall siege in Nairobi claiming 67 lives, the 2015 Garissa University attack that resulted in 148 deaths, and the 2017 Mogadishu truck bombings exceeding 500 fatalities.[1] These actions, alongside routine attacks on civilians, peacekeepers, and infrastructure, have inflicted thousands of casualties and perpetuated Somalia's instability, even as U.S. airstrikesâ€”such as one on September 12, 2025â€”and the drawdown of the African Union Transition Mission in Somalia (ATMIS) test the group's adaptability.[2]

Harakat al-Shabaab al-Mujahideen (Somalia)

Overview and etymology

Harakat al-Shabaab al-Mujahideen, commonly known as al-Shabaab, is a Sunni Islamist militant group operating primarily in Somalia, designated as a foreign terrorist organization by the United States since 2008 and subject to United Nations sanctions.[3][1] The group emerged in 2006 as the radical youth faction of the Islamic Courts Union (ICU), a coalition of Sharia courts that briefly controlled Mogadishu amid Somalia's ongoing civil war and power vacuum following the 1991 collapse of the central government.[1][4] Al-Shabaab's insurgency intensified after the Ethiopian military intervention in December 2006, which targeted the ICU and prompted the group's formal split and reorganization as an independent jihadist entity committed to armed resistance against perceived foreign occupiers and the transitional Somali government.[1] The term "al-Shabaab," meaning "the Youth" in Arabic, derives from its initial role as the militant youth wing within the ICU, emphasizing recruitment of younger Somalis radicalized by clan rivalries, poverty, and opposition to foreign-backed governance structures.[1][4] This nomenclature underscores the group's strategy of mobilizing disaffected youth through ideological indoctrination and promises of empowerment via strict Salafi-jihadist interpretations of Islam, distinguishing it from more moderate Islamist factions in Somalia.[5] Over time, al-Shabaab has evolved into one of al-Qaeda's most resilient affiliates, publicly pledging allegiance in 2012 while maintaining operational autonomy to pursue local dominance.[1][6] As of 2025, al-Shabaab controls significant rural territories in southern and central Somalia, deriving revenue from extortion, taxation, and illicit trade while conducting asymmetric attacks, suicide bombings, and cross-border operations, including in Kenya, resulting in thousands of civilian and military casualties.[2][1] The group's persistence stems from exploiting Somalia's weak state institutions, clan divisions, and limited African Union and Somali National Army penetration into its strongholds, despite sustained counterterrorism efforts involving U.S. drone strikes and special operations.[2][6]

Ideology and objectives

Al-Shabaab adheres to a Salafi-jihadist ideology, drawing from radical Islamist interpretations that emphasize a puritanical form of Sunni Islam influenced by al-Qaeda's global framework.[1][6] This ideology combines defensive jihad against perceived foreign occupation with offensive global jihadist aspirations, framing Somalia as a battleground in a broader struggle against Western and secular influences.[6] The group employs takfirâ€”declaring fellow Muslims as apostatesâ€”to justify violence against the Somali government, rival clans, and civilians who oppose its strict religious edicts, while promoting sectarian attacks on those rejecting its extremist views.[6][7] Formally pledging allegiance to al-Qaeda in February 2012, Al-Shabaab positions itself as the organization's East African affiliate, aligning its propaganda and operations with transnational jihadist narratives.[1][6] The group's core objectives center on overthrowing Somalia's federal government, which it deems illegitimate and apostate, and expelling all foreign military forces, including African Union troops under AMISOM (now ATMIS) and Ethiopian contingents.[1][7] In territories under its control in central and southern Somalia, Al-Shabaab seeks to establish an Islamic emirate governed by its interpretation of Sharia law, enforcing hudud punishments such as stonings and amputations, and prohibiting activities like smoking, khat chewing, and Western-style entertainment.[1][8] This governance model, implemented through mobile courts and taxation systems since at least 2008, aims to legitimize its rule and sustain operations, though it prioritizes insurgency over sustained administration.[1] Beyond Somalia, Al-Shabaab's ambitions include unifying ethnic Somali regionsâ€”encompassing Somaliland, Puntland, northeastern Kenya, and Ethiopia's Ogadenâ€”under a single Islamist state, while supporting al-Qaeda's worldwide goals through attacks on regional targets.[6][8] Statements from group spokespersons, such as those following the July 2010 Kampala bombings, underscore threats to extend violence to nations contributing troops to Somalia, reflecting a strategy of deterrence and recruitment via anti-interventionist rhetoric.[1] Despite occasional internal debates over local versus global priorities, the leadership has maintained a commitment to both, rejecting purely nationalist framing in favor of ideological purity.[6][7]

Organizational structure and leadership

Al-Shabaab maintains a hierarchical structure centered on an emir who holds ultimate authority, supported by a shura majlis (consultative council) comprising 7 to 14 senior leaders appointed by the emir to oversee key functions including military operations, intelligence, finance, and propaganda.[6][9] The shura reflects Somalia's clan dynamics, particularly the 4.5 power-sharing formula with Hawiye clan dominance, facilitating internal cohesion amid clan-based recruitment and appointments, though the group publicly rejects clannism.[9] The current emir, Ahmed Diriye (also known as Ahmed Umar Abu Ubaidah), assumed leadership on 23 September 2014 after predecessor Ahmed Abdi Godane (alias Mukhtar Abu Zubayr) was killed in a U.S. airstrike near Barawe.[1][10] Diriye, from the Dir clan, has emphasized rebuilding the shura for consensus-based decision-making, contrasting Godane's more authoritarian style that often overrode council input, and has prioritized al-Qaeda alignment while suppressing internal dissent through the Amniyat security apparatus.[9][6] Specialized committees operate under the shura, including the military wing led by figures such as Abukar Ali Aden since mid-2013, responsible for guerrilla tactics and territorial control; the Amniyat al-Mujahideen, an intelligence and internal policing unit headed by Mahad Karate, notorious for rooting out spies and enforcing loyalty through assassinations and purges; and the Jaysh al-Hisbah, a religious police force maintaining order in controlled areas.[9][6] Regional governance occurs through wilayat (provinces) each led by a wali or emir, appointed based on clan ties to manage local commanders, taxation, and sharia courts, enabling the group to function as a shadow administration despite military pressures.[9] This decentralized yet emir-centric model, estimated to command 5,000â€“12,000 fighters including conscripts, has sustained resilience against Somali and African Union offensives.[1][6]

Historical development

Origins and formation (2006â€“2007)

Harakat al-Shabaab al-Mujahideen, commonly known as al-Shabaab, originated as the militant youth wing of the Islamic Courts Union (ICU), a coalition of Sharia-based courts that seized control of Mogadishu in June 2006 amid Somalia's ongoing civil conflict following the 1991 state collapse.[1][6] The group's roots trace to earlier Salafi networks, including al-Ittihad al-Islami (AIAI), a 1990s militant organization that promoted jihadist ideology in Somalia and had ties to al-Qaeda training camps in Afghanistan.[1] Al-Shabaab's early members, numbering around 400 fighters, operated as the ICU's radical enforcement arm, rejecting the broader ICU's pragmatic elements that included negotiations with secular warlords and the Transitional Federal Government (TFG).[1][9] The Ethiopian military intervention in December 2006, supported by the United States and aimed at bolstering the TFG, decisively defeated the ICU and scattered its leadership, serving as a pivotal catalyst for al-Shabaab's transformation into an independent insurgency.[1][6] This invasion, framed by al-Shabaab as a foreign "Christian" occupation, radicalized the group and fueled recruitment by exploiting Somali nationalist resentment, allowing it to retreat southward and reorganize for guerrilla warfare against Ethiopian and TFG forces.[1][9] By mid-2007, al-Shabaab had formally splintered from ICU remnants, prioritizing a strict Salafi-jihadist agenda over the ICU's clan-inclusive approach, with early operations focusing on hit-and-run attacks in southern Somalia.[6][9] In August 2007, al-Shabaab consolidated its structure when 18 leaders, including Hussein Ali Fiidow, selected Ahmed Abdi Godane (also known as Ahmed Diriye) as its first emir, marking a shift toward centralized jihadist leadership influenced by al-Qaeda networks.[9] Godane, who had prior AIAI connections and Afghan training, steered the group away from ICU-style negotiations, emphasizing the establishment of an Islamic state through unrelenting holy war.[9] This period saw initial cross-clan recruitment and territorial footholds in rural areas, setting the stage for expansion despite heavy losses from the 2006 battles.[1][9]

Insurgency and expansion (2008â€“2011)

Following the split from the Islamic Courts Union in 2007, Harakat al-Shabaab al-Mujahideen transitioned to a dedicated insurgency in 2008, targeting Ethiopian forces supporting the Transitional Federal Government (TFG) through ambushes, roadside bombings, and assassinations of officials.[1] The United States designated the group a foreign terrorist organization on February 26, 2008, citing its attacks on civilians and ties to al-Qaeda.[11] By mid-2008, al-Shabaab had besieged the strategic town of Baidoa, a TFG stronghold, pressuring government lines in central Somalia amid ongoing guerrilla operations.[12] The Ethiopian military withdrawal on January 13, 2009, created a power vacuum that al-Shabaab exploited, rapidly expanding territorial control in south-central Somalia through coordinated assaults on TFG positions and the African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM).[11] In 2009, the group made significant gains during the protracted Battle of Mogadishu, employing guerrilla tactics such as hit-and-run attacks and improvised explosive devices (IEDs) against AMISOM convoys and bases, while also clashing with rival Islamist faction Hizbul Islam before absorbing elements of it in December.[13] By early 2010, al-Shabaab held most territory in central and southern Somalia south of Puntland, including up to 60 percent of Mogadishu neighborhoods, enabling it to impose strict sharia governance, extort taxes, and recruit fighters estimated in the thousands.[12][14] Al-Shabaab's expansion extended beyond Somalia with its first external operation on July 11, 2010, when suicide bombers struck crowds in Kampala, Uganda, killing 74 in retaliation for Uganda's AMISOM contributions.[11] Domestically, the group launched a major "Ramadan Offensive" in August 2010, assaulting AMISOM and TFG targets in Mogadishu with waves of fighters, though it suffered heavy casualties without dislodging defenders.[12] A second such offensive in August 2011 similarly faltered amid intensified AMISOM-TFG counterattacks, prompting al-Shabaab's "strategic withdrawal" from Mogadishu by late August, after which it reverted to asymmetric warfare while retaining rural strongholds.[12] External interventions escalated with Kenya's Operation Linda Nchi invasion of southern Somalia on October 16, 2011, targeting al-Shabaab supply lines near the border.[11] Despite these setbacks, the group's control over key ports and agricultural areas sustained its operations through 2011.[1]

Alignment with al-Qaeda and internal shifts (2012â€“2014)

In February 2012, Al-Shabaab's leader Ahmed Abdi Godane (also known as Mukhtar Abu Zubayr) publicly announced the group's formal pledge of allegiance to al-Qaeda, framing it as a unification under the global jihadist banner led by Ayman al-Zawahiri.[15] This alignment, declared on February 9 via an audio message, sought to bolster Al-Shabaab's international profile amid territorial losses from African Union and Somali government offensives, while emphasizing shared Salafi-jihadist ideology over purely local Somali nationalism.[16] Al-Qaeda accepted the pledge, designating Al-Shabaab as its official affiliate in East Africa, which facilitated tactical exchanges, funding, and recruitment of foreign fighters, though operational autonomy persisted due to geographical separation.[1] The merger exacerbated internal divisions within Al-Shabaab, pitting Godane's globalist faction against nationalists and clan-based elements wary of alienating local support through rigid transnational jihadism.[6] Prominent dissent emerged in March 2012 when American-Somali commander Omar Hammami (Abu Mansur al-Amriki) accused Godane of authoritarianism, embezzlement, and marginalizing foreign fighters, prompting Hammami's flight from party-controlled areas and a public rift that highlighted tensions over resource allocation and decision-making. Figures like Mukhtar Robow (Abu Mansur) and Ibrahim al-Afghani voiced opposition to the al-Qaeda merger, arguing it prioritized external agendas over Somali reconciliation and risked clan fractures in a society defined by kinship loyalties.[1] Godane responded with systematic purges to consolidate control, leveraging his Amniyat (intelligence) unit to eliminate rivals between 2013 and mid-2014. In late June 2013, al-Afghaniâ€”a senior deputy and Afghan-trained ideologueâ€”and Mu`allim Burhan were arrested and executed on charges of conspiracy against Godane, underscoring clashes over ideological purity and foreign fighter integration.[16] By September 2013, Hammami was killed in a firefight near Mogadishu, followed by the deaths of several associates, which Godane attributed to intra-group skirmishes but analysts linked to deliberate targeting of globalist skeptics.[16] These actions, amid military setbacks like the loss of key ports, reduced factionalism but strained cohesion, as evidenced by defections and subdued propaganda from survivors, ultimately centralizing power under Godane's vision of al-Qaeda-aligned expansion despite localist pushback.[6]

Military pressures and tactical evolution (2015â€“2020)

From 2015 onward, Al-Shabaab encountered intensified military operations by the African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM) and Somali National Army (SNA), supported by U.S. intelligence and special operations. In July 2015, AMISOM initiated Operation Jubba Corridor, targeting Al-Shabaab strongholds in southern Somalia to disrupt supply lines and training camps, leading to the temporary recapture of key towns like Bariire and Marka districts.[17] These efforts built on prior territorial gains but faced challenges from Al-Shabaab's guerrilla ambushes and infiltration, limiting enduring control over rural hinterlands where the group maintained de facto authority through local alliances and extortion.[2] U.S. involvement escalated with expanded authorities for airstrikes granted in 2015, focusing on high-value targets and fighter concentrations, though initial operations remained limited compared to later years.[18] By 2017, under revised rules of engagement, strikes surged to target Al-Shabaab leadership and operational cells, contributing to the elimination of several commanders and inflicting hundreds of fighter casualties annually, as reported by U.S. Africa Command assessments.[18] However, Al-Shabaab's decentralized structure and mobility in remote areas mitigated decisive degradation, allowing recruitment and regrouping despite losses estimated in the thousands from combined coalition actions over the period.[2] In response to these pressures, Al-Shabaab accelerated its shift to asymmetric tactics, prioritizing hit-and-run raids, vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices (VBIEDs), and suicide operations over conventional battles to impose costs on overstretched adversaries.[12] Notable adaptations included sophisticated assassinations of Somali officials and AMISOM personnel, as well as mass-casualty attacks in urban centers like the October 2017 Mogadishu truck bombings that killed over 500 civilians and underscored the group's infiltration capabilities.[12][2] By emphasizing economic disruption through road ambushes and port extortion in contested zones, Al-Shabaab sustained funding and morale, compensating for territorial erosion while probing coalition vulnerabilities, such as the 2016 El Adde base assault that killed approximately 150 Kenyan troops.[12] This evolution preserved the insurgency's viability, enabling selective territorial reclamation and persistent threats against Mogadishu and border regions.

Resurgence and recent campaigns (2021â€“2025)

Following the tactical adaptations to drone strikes and ground offensives in prior years, al-Shabaab sustained its insurgency through improvised explosive devices, assassinations, and hit-and-run attacks, enabling a gradual resurgence amid Somali federal government infighting and the African Union Transition Mission in Somalia (ATMIS) drawdown. In 2021, the group executed a series of suicide bombings in Mogadishu, including assaults on military and civilian targets that killed dozens and prompted the U.S. to resume drone strikes after an August pause.[19] These operations highlighted al-Shabaab's continued access to urban networks despite territorial constraints. The 2022 election of President Hassan Sheikh Mohamud marked a shift, as his administration declared a "total war" offensive in August against al-Shabaab-held areas in central and southern Somalia, initially reclaiming pockets of territory with support from clan militias and U.S. airstrikes. However, al-Shabaab responded with escalated violence, including twin suicide bombings on March 24 that targeted electoral officials and security forces in Mogadishu, killing at least 20 and wounding over 30. Clashes with Somali forces rose 19% from 2021 levels, while civilian-targeted attacks surged 41%, exploiting clan divisions and governance vacuums to retain influence in rural strongholds and launch cross-border raids into Ethiopia's Somali region.[20][21] By 2023â€“2024, the government's offensive stalled due to logistical failures, inter-clan rivalries, and al-Shabaab's sabotage of supply lines via roadside bombs and ambushes, allowing the group to regroup and intensify assassinations of local administrators to erode state legitimacy. Al-Shabaab maintained pressure on Mogadishu with mortar strikes, such as the April 9 attack near Villa Somalia targeting a minister's residence, and rural operations that prevented consolidated federal control. U.S. forces conducted over 30 declared strikes in 2023 alone, killing key figures like Moalim Ayman, but these yielded limited strategic disruption amid al-Shabaab's decentralized command.[22][23][24] Al-Shabaab's resurgence peaked in 2025 with a coordinated offensive launched in late February, dubbed the Shabelle campaign, which reversed federal gains by overrunning positions in central Somalia's Middle Shabelle and Hiraan regions through combined infantry assaults and vehicle-borne improvised explosives. By July, the group recaptured the strategic town of Moqokori and secured a control triangle encompassing Tardo and Buq-Aqable, encircling government outposts and disrupting aid corridors; attacks across southern and central areas rose 50% year-on-year. Notable actions included a July 1 suicide car bombing on a Djiboutian-Somali base and an October 5 prison assault in Mogadishu using fighters and explosives to free detainees. Federal disunity, including stalled African Union Support and Stabilization Mission in Somalia (AUSSOM) deployments and clan-based defections, amplified these advances, with U.S. airstrikes on September 9 targeting militants but failing to halt momentum.[25][26][27]

Operations and activities

Guerrilla warfare and territorial control

Al-Shabab employs asymmetric guerrilla tactics, including ambushes on supply convoys, improvised explosive devices (IEDs), small-scale raids, and assassinations, to target Somali National Army (SNA) positions and African Union forces while minimizing exposure to superior firepower. IEDs constitute the primary cause of SNA casualties, supplemented by vehicle-borne IEDs (VBIEDs) and suicide bombings in urban strikes, such as the June 25, 2023, attack on Aden Adde International Airport in Mogadishu.[28] The group prioritizes hit-and-run operations along vulnerable routes like Mogadishu-Baidoa, avoiding direct engagements to erode enemy morale and logistics, a strategy evident in early gains from 2009 guerrilla assaults on Somali and AU troops.[28][13] In its February 2025 offensive, Al-Shabab executed coordinated multi-pronged attacks across Hiraan, Middle Shabelle, and Lower Shabelle regions, incorporating infiltration of SNA lines, clan alliances for local support, and high-impact operations like the March 2025 IED strike on President Hassan Sheikh Mohamud's convoy, which killed at least 10. These tactics reversed Somali government advances from prior years, reclaiming towns such as Adan Yabal and encircling key districts near Mogadishu through checkpoints on roads to Afgooye and Balcad.[25][29] Al-Shabab maintains territorial dominance in rural south-central Somalia, controlling swathes of Hiraan, Galgaduud, and southern strongholds like Jilib, Saakow, and Buale, alongside northern enclaves in the Al-Madow mountains. As of March 31, 2025, the group held towns including Jowhar, Balcad, and Awdheegle, leveraging an estimated 7,000â€“12,000 fighters for enforcement via coercion, annual extortion revenues exceeding $100 million, and captured equipment from SNA bases.[28][29] This control facilitates freedom of movement and recruitment, including over 900 child soldiers in 2022, though holdings remain fluid amid U.S. airstrikesâ€”killing more than 300 militants in 2022â€”and government pushes like the July 2025 recapture efforts, followed by Al-Shabab's seizure of Mahaas.[28][2]

Terrorist attacks and suicide bombings

Al-Shabaab routinely employs suicide bombings, often via vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices (VBIEDs), as well as coordinated gun attacks and sieges, to target Somali government forces, African Union troops, and civilians in urban centers, aiming to destabilize authorities and deter foreign intervention. These tactics have inflicted heavy casualties, with the group claiming responsibility for dozens of such operations since 2008, frequently in Mogadishu and cross-border in Kenya and Uganda.[3][11] The bombings exploit crowded areas for maximum lethality, while assaults on soft targets like malls and universities seek to amplify psychological impact and provoke overreactions.[30] One of the earliest high-profile suicide operations occurred on July 11, 2010, when Al-Shabaab detonated multiple bombs in Kampala, Uganda, during the World Cup final viewing, killing 74 people and injuring over 85 in retaliation for Uganda's role in the African Union mission.[11] In Somalia, the group escalated with truck bombings, such as the October 15, 2017, attack in Mogadishu that killed over 500 and wounded 300, marking the deadliest single incident in the country's history and attributed to Al-Shabaab despite initial uncertainty.[11] Cross-border raids have included the September 21-24, 2013, Westgate mall assault in Nairobi, Kenya, where gunmen held hostages and killed 67 civilians over four days before security forces neutralized the attackers.[11] Similarly, on April 2, 2015, Al-Shabaab gunmen attacked Garissa University College, separating non-Muslims and executing 148 people, mostly students, in a siege that ended with the deaths of four perpetrators.[11][31] Suicide VBIEDs persisted into later years, including the December 28, 2019, Mogadishu bombing that killed 82 and injured about 150 near a checkpoint.[11] A double suicide car bombing on October 29, 2022, outside a Mogadishu hotel targeted by Al-Shabaab killed at least 121 and wounded over 300, primarily civilians.[11] The group also struck African Union bases, such as a suicide car bomb attack on a Mogadishu facility that killed eleven Burundian soldiers.[13] These attacks demonstrate Al-Shabaab's tactical adaptation to maintain pressure despite territorial losses.[30]

Economic operations and extortion

Al-Shabaab sustains its operations primarily through a sophisticated extortion network that generates over $100 million annually by targeting businesses, trade, and civilians across Somalia.[32] This revenue, equivalent to a significant portion of the Somali federal government's annual budget of approximately $250 million, funds an estimated 5,000â€“10,000 fighters and operatives.[33] The group's economic model relies on enforced "zakat" taxesâ€”typically 2.5% of assets or transactionsâ€”collected via its Amniyat intelligence wing, alongside direct levies on ports, roadways, and urban commerce, even in areas outside its direct territorial control.[33] Checkpoints form a core component of Al-Shabaab's extortion apparatus, with the group operating 45 such sites in south-central Somalia as of early 2023, comprising 23% of the region's total 204 checkpoints and concentrated in Jubaland.[34] These impose transit fees (gadiid) on vehiclesâ€”ranging from $9 for small loads to $450 for trailersâ€”and cargo taxes (dalag or badeeco) based on volume and type, yielding an estimated $25 million yearly across major corridors like Kismayoâ€“Garissa and Mogadishuâ€“Baidoaâ€“Dollow.[34] Fees are often documented with receipts to enforce compliance, distorting trade by inflating transport costs and redirecting flows toward alternative routes, which burdens small-scale farmers and raises market prices.[34] In urban centers like Mogadishu, which accounts for about one-third of Al-Shabaab's extortion revenue, the group infiltrates economic sectors through intermediaries, notaries, and port officials to access transaction data and demand payments on imports, real estate, and trade.[35] Businesses face coerced investments or direct threats, enabling collection despite military offensives; for instance, post-2022 government crackdowns temporarily disrupted mobile money channels, but payments resumed amid violence following mandates like CCTV installations.[35] Funds are laundered via hawala systems and compromised financial networks extending to Kenya and the UAE, sustaining operations amid territorial setbacks.[32] Refusal to pay invites targeted attacks, reinforcing compliance without overt governance.[35]

Governance and sharia implementation in held areas

In areas under its control, primarily in southern and central Somalia including regions like Middle Juba, Lower Shabelle, and Galgaduud, al-Shabab establishes a hierarchical administrative structure led by regional governors (walis) and local emirs, enforcing governance through a strict interpretation of Hanbali Sharia law to maintain order and legitimacy.[36][37] This system, operational since at least 2008, prioritizes Sharia over customary clan law (xeer), with policies extending to security provision, dispute resolution, and resource management, often contrasting with the perceived corruption of the Somali federal government.[38][36] Al-Shabab controls or co-governs nearly 25% of district capitals as of 2025, using checkpoints, surveillance, and a monopoly on force to suppress inter-clan violence and crime.[36] Judicial authority resides in qadi courts staffed by appointed judges whose identities are often concealed, delivering swift rulings without classical evidentiary hurdles, which facilitates rapid enforcement but bypasses due process.[39] These courts handle criminal, civil, and moral cases, applying hudud (fixed Quranic punishments) for offenses like theft, adultery, and apostasy, alongside ta'zir (discretionary penalties).[37][39] The jaish al-hisba, an armed morality police, patrols to enforce compliance, punishing violations publicly to deter dissent.[37] Hudud punishments are prominently applied, including amputations for theftâ€”such as a hand and foot severance in Qunyo Barrow in January 2025â€”and stonings or executions for adultery among married individuals, as in Buqda Caqable in March 2025.[37] Floggings, often 100 lashes for unmarried adultery or alcohol consumption, occur frequently, exemplified by a public whipping in Ceel Buur in May 2023.[37] Apostasy draws execution, as with convert Hassan Tohow Fidow in El Buur in 2021, while earlier instances include stonings in Kismayo in November 2008 and amputations there in October 2009.[37][38] These are conducted publicly to instill fear and ideological conformity, rooted in jihadi-Salafi doctrine.[39] Social regulations mandate veiling for women, prohibit Western media, music, soccer, and qat chewing, with violations drawing floggings or detention, as in Kismayo in July 2009 for improper dress.[38] Economically, al-Shabab collects zakat taxes efficiently, issuing receipts at roadblocks to prevent overlaps and funding operations through fixed market levies in places like Mogadishu, while banning exports like charcoal to preserve resources.[36] Humanitarian efforts emphasize local zakat over Western aid, contributing to no reported starvation deaths during the 2017 drought in controlled zones.[36] This blend of coercion and service provision has fostered perceived legitimacy among some residents, despite the repressive framework.[36]

International dimensions

Ties to global jihadist networks

Al-Shabaab formally aligned with al-Qaeda in February 2012, when its leader Ahmed Abdi Godane (also known as Mukhtar Abu Zubayr) announced the group's merger with the global jihadist network in a joint video statement alongside Ayman al-Zawahiri, al-Qaeda's emir at the time.[40][41] This pledge reaffirmed al-Shabaab's commitment to al-Qaeda's transnational ideology of establishing a caliphate through violent jihad against perceived enemies of Islam, including Western powers and their allies.[1] The alliance provided al-Shabaab with ideological legitimacy, access to al-Qaeda's propaganda channels, and limited operational support, such as training and expertise in bomb-making, though the group has primarily operated independently in Somalia due to its focus on local territorial control.[2] In 2015, al-Shabaab's leadership rejected overtures from the Islamic State (ISIS), executing dissenting members who advocated defection and suppressing a small faction in Puntland that pledged allegiance to ISIS, thereby preserving unity under al-Qaeda's banner.[42][43] This decision reflected ideological fidelity to al-Qaeda's gradualist approach over ISIS's apocalyptic expansionism, with al-Shabaab viewing the latter as a rival that fragmented the global jihadist ummah.[44] Despite sporadic defectionsâ€”estimated at fewer than 100 fighters forming the nascent ISIS-Somalia provinceâ€”al-Shabaab has maintained its status as al-Qaeda's most capable African affiliate, conducting cross-border attacks in Kenya and Uganda as retaliation for international interventions, in line with al-Qaeda's doctrine of defensive jihad.[3] Al-Shabaab has cultivated ties with other al-Qaeda branches, including al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP), through shared smuggling routes across the Gulf of Aden and exchanges of foreign fighters, enabling tactical enhancements like drone usage and IED improvements.[45] Leaders have invoked global jihad rhetoric to justify operations beyond Somalia, such as the 2013 Westgate Mall attack in Nairobi, which echoed al-Qaeda's emphasis on striking "far enemies."[46] From 2020 to 2025, these connections persisted amid military pressures, with al-Shabaab's amir Ahmed Umar (successor to Godane after his 2014 death in a U.S. airstrike) reaffirming loyalty to al-Qaeda's central leadership, positioning the group as a resilient node in the network despite internal purges and competition from ISIS affiliates.[47][2] While rank-and-file fighters often prioritize Somali nationalism, senior cadres enforce globalist orientation to deter defections and sustain recruitment from diaspora networks in the U.S., U.K., and East Africa.[3]

Foreign interventions and counterterrorism efforts

The African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM), authorized by UN Security Council Resolution 1744 in February 2007, deployed multinational troops primarily from Uganda, Burundi, Kenya, and Ethiopia to counter Al-Shabaab's insurgency and protect the Transitional Federal Government. By 2011, AMISOM forces, numbering around 22,000 at peak, had recaptured Mogadishu and key southern cities like Baidoa and Kismayo in joint operations with Somali allies, significantly degrading Al-Shabaab's urban control.[48] However, the mission faced heavy casualties, including over 1,500 peacekeepers killed by 2021, often in ambushes exploiting troop convoy vulnerabilities, as seen in the January 2016 El Adde attack where Somali and AMISOM forces suffered hundreds of deaths.[49] AMISOM transitioned to the African Union Transition Mission in Somalia (ATMIS) in 2022 under UN Resolution 2611, with a mandate to support Somali security forces (SSF) in offensive operations while gradually handing over responsibilities amid phased troop reductions from 19,626 in 2023 to a target of zero by December 2024, though extensions were sought due to SSF capacity gaps.[50] ATMIS operations focused on joint clears in rural strongholds, but Al-Shabaab's asymmetric tactics, including improvised explosive devices and hit-and-run raids, limited territorial gains, leaving vast ungoverned areas under insurgent influence.[51] U.S. funding, channeled through the Department of State, supported AMISOM/ATMIS logistics and training, totaling hundreds of millions annually, yet critiques from Somali officials highlighted dependency on foreign forces without building sustainable local capabilities.[52] Ethiopia's military intervention began in December 2006 with an invasion to oust the Islamic Courts Union (ICU), Al-Shabaab's precursor, leading to the group's radicalization and guerrilla shift as a response to perceived occupation.[53] Ethiopian National Defense Forces (ENDF) contributed contingents to AMISOM from 2007, aiding offensives in southern Somalia, but withdrew in 2021 amid domestic Tigray conflicts, enabling Al-Shabaab territorial recoveries in regions like Galmudug.[54] Clashes persisted, including ENDF ambushes on Al-Shabaab convoys in 2023 near the border, though Ethiopia's operations strained relations with Somalia over unilateral actions and port access disputes, indirectly benefiting the insurgents.[55] Kenya launched Operation Linda Nchi on October 14, 2011, deploying Kenya Defence Forces (KDF) into southern Somalia to neutralize cross-border threats after Al-Shabaab kidnappings and attacks, capturing Kismayo port in October 2012 alongside AMISOM and Somali forces, disrupting the group's revenue from extortion.[56] KDF integrated into AMISOM/ATMIS, maintaining bases in Jubaland, but faced retaliatory strikes, such as the January 2020 Manda Bay raid killing three Americans and destroying aircraft.[57] Kenyan operations emphasized buffer zones, yet Al-Shabaab exploited ethnic Somali grievances in Kenya for recruitment and infiltration, sustaining domestic bombings despite joint patrols.[58] U.S. counterterrorism efforts, coordinated via Africa Command (AFRICOM) since 2007, emphasize airstrikes in collective self-defense of Somali and partner forces, with over 250 confirmed strikes by 2025 targeting Al-Shabaab leadership and infrastructure.[59] Operations escalated post-2017, including drone strikes like the March 15, 2025, action near Xudur killing fighters, and September 2025 strikes in Badhan, though some admitted civilian casualties prompted internal reviews without reparations.[60] Ground raids, such as the 2018 Barawe attempt on a senior operative, complemented special forces training for SSF Danab commandos, aiming to build indigenous capacity amid Al-Shabaab's adaptation to dispersed cells.[61] These efforts disrupted plots but have not eradicated the group, which retains operational resilience in Somalia's rural expanses.[62]

Designations as a terrorist organization

Al-Shabaab (Harakat al-Shabaab al-Mujahideen) has been formally designated as a terrorist organization by multiple national governments and international bodies, primarily due to its orchestration of suicide bombings, assassinations, and attacks on civilians, government targets, and international forces in Somalia and neighboring countries. These designations typically impose asset freezes, travel prohibitions, arms embargoes, and prohibitions on material support, aiming to disrupt financing, recruitment, and operations.[63][64] The United States designated al-Shabaab as a Foreign Terrorist Organization (FTO) in 2008 under Section 219 of the Immigration and Nationality Act, making it unlawful for U.S. persons to provide support and subjecting members to immigration restrictions.[61][63] The U.S. Treasury Department has also sanctioned numerous al-Shabaab leaders and facilitators under Executive Order 13224 since then, targeting financial networks.[65][66] The United Nations Security Council has imposed sanctions on al-Shabaab, initially integrating it into the Al-Qaida sanctions regime (UNSCR 1267/1989) around 2010 for its ties to al-Qaeda, with ongoing listings of leaders and entities for involvement in terrorist acts.[13] In December 2023, UNSCR 2713 established a dedicated Al-Shabaab sanctions committee, enforcing a complete arms embargo, asset freezes, and travel bans to counter its military capabilities and financing.[67][68]

Impact and consequences

Casualties and humanitarian effects in Somalia

Al-Shabaab's insurgency has inflicted heavy casualties on Somali civilians, security forces, and international personnel, with attacks often targeting urban centers, military bases, and public spaces. Since its emergence in the mid-2000s, the group has been responsible for thousands of fatalities, including over 500 deaths in the October 2017 twin truck bombings in Mogadishu, one of the deadliest incidents in the country's history.[2] In 2022, political violence linked to Al-Shabaab activity resulted in an estimated 2,000 fatalities in the first seven months alone, marking heightened lethality amid government offensives.[74] More recently, in August 2023, Al-Shabaab killed 178 Somali soldiers in an assault on a military base, while a June 2023 attack on an African Union base claimed 54 Ugandan peacekeepers.[2] Civilian-targeted violence persisted into 2024, with 127 such events recorded, causing 187 fatalities.[75] The group's tactics, including suicide bombings, improvised explosive devices, and guerrilla raids, have disproportionately affected non-combatants, exacerbating Somalia's fragile security. A 2022 Mogadishu bombing killed over 100 people, underscoring Al-Shabaab's capacity for mass-casualty operations despite counterterrorism pressures.[2] United Nations reports indicate 2022 as the deadliest year for civilians since 2017, with a significant uptick in fatalities from Al-Shabaab reprisals following Somali military advances.[76] These losses compound intergenerational trauma, as Al-Shabaab also recruits child soldiers and conducts executions in controlled areas, though precise breakdowns between civilian and combatant deaths remain challenging due to underreporting in remote regions.[77] Humanitarian consequences include widespread internal displacement and aggravated food insecurity, with conflict driving over 40% of new displacements in recent years. By late 2024, Somalia hosted more than 3.8 million people in registered IDP sites, many fleeing Al-Shabaab violence in central and southern regions.[78] An estimated 3.5 million remain displaced as of 2025, linked to ongoing clashes that disrupt livelihoods and access to services.[79] Al-Shabaab's territorial control and extortion tactics hinder aid delivery, as seen in historical bans on humanitarian operations during the 2011-2012 famine, which killed over 250,000 and was worsened by the group's restrictions on relief efforts.[80] In areas under its influence, Al-Shabaab's governance has occasionally mitigated starvation during droughtsâ€”such as in 2017, with no reported famine deathsâ€”but its insurgency broadly fuels cycles of displacement and vulnerability to climatic shocks.[36] Conflict-induced movements strain urban centers like Mogadishu and Baidoa, where IDPs face overcrowding, disease outbreaks, and exploitation, including recruitment by the group itself.[81] By 2024, 4.3 million Somalis faced crisis-level or worse food insecurity, with violence impeding agricultural recovery and market access.[2] These effects perpetuate poverty and undermine state resilience, as displaced populations contribute to informal economies vulnerable to further predation.

Regional and global security threats

Al-Shabaab conducts cross-border attacks and incursions that directly threaten neighboring states in the Horn of Africa, destabilizing regional security through targeted violence against military, civilian, and infrastructure targets. In Kenya, the group maintains a persistent presence along the Somali border, launching sporadic assaults that primarily affect security forces and local populations; for instance, on October 27, 2025, Kenyan elite counter-terrorism units intercepted eight militants attempting to deploy improvised explosive devices against road users in Garissa County, recovering assembled bombs and neutralizing the plot.[82] These operations, continuing from patterns observed in 2022, exploit porous frontiers to extend influence and retaliate against Kenyan military involvement in Somalia.[83] In Ethiopia, Al-Shabaab has executed deadly raids and territorial probes, particularly in 2022, clashing with regional security forces and underscoring vulnerabilities in border defenses amid Somalia's instability.[84] Ethiopian intelligence prioritizes the group as a core transnational threat originating from Somalia, with incursions risking escalation into broader ethnic and interstate conflicts in the region.[85] Such actions compound fragility across the Horn, impeding economic integration efforts like Somalia's entry into the East African Community and fostering cycles of retaliation that strain multilateral counterterrorism coordination.[86] On a global scale, Al-Shabaab's formal pledge of loyalty to al-Qaeda in 2012 embeds it within expansive jihadist networks, enabling recruitment of foreign fightersâ€”including Western nationalsâ€”and advocacy for attacks that transcend African borders.[64][7] The group's estimated 7,000â€“12,000 fighters sustain operational resilience, posing risks through ideological propagation, diaspora financing, and potential coordination with affiliates for plots against international aviation, embassies, or diasporic communities.[84][2] This transnational integration amplifies homeland security concerns in the West and allies, as Al-Shabaab exploits Somali ungoverned spaces to project power beyond the region.[87]

Socioeconomic disruptions and resilience factors

Al-Shabaab's extortion practices significantly hinder economic activity across Somalia, particularly in urban centers like Mogadishu, where the group operates a sophisticated network of road checkpoints and demands levies on imports and direct payments from businesses, generating an estimated $100 million annually in illicit revenue.[33][35] This system raises operational costs for traders and firms, discouraging investment and formal commerce while fostering a climate of fear that limits market expansion and supply chain reliability.[88] Terrorist attacks further exacerbate these disruptions, with empirical studies showing immediate declines in household consumption, increased poverty depth among affected populations, and reduced labor participation following bombings in civilian areas.[89] The group's control over rural territories disrupts agriculture and pastoralism, key sectors employing over 60% of Somalis, by imposing zakat taxes, banning certain crops or practices deemed un-Islamic, and attacking infrastructure like irrigation systems, contributing to food insecurity and famine risks amid recurrent droughts.[2] Population displacement from violence has created over 3.8 million internally displaced persons as of 2023, straining urban economies with informal settlements, heightened unemployment, and pressure on scarce resources like water and housing.[90] These factors compound Somalia's baseline challenges, including high poverty rates exceeding 70% and precarious employment conditions, amplifying vulnerability in Al-Shabaab-affected regions.[91] Resilience emerges from Somalia's decentralized, clan-based social structures and adaptive informal economies, which enable local trade networks to bypass Al-Shabaab checkpoints through smuggling routes and barter systems rooted in pastoral mobility.[92] Remittances from the Somali diaspora, accounting for approximately 25% of GDP, provide a critical buffer, funding household consumption and small-scale enterprises even in contested areas, sustaining livelihoods independent of formal banking disrupted by extortion.[90] In relatively stable regions like Puntland and Somaliland, autonomous governance and private security initiatives have fostered localized economic recovery, with livestock exports and fisheries generating revenue despite national instability.[2] Counter-financing measures by Somali authorities, supported internationally, bolster resilience by targeting Al-Shabaab's revenue streams; for instance, the Financial Reporting Center and Central Bank of Somalia's anti-money laundering efforts have disrupted hawala transfers and business compliance networks in Mogadishu since 2022.[88] Military operations reclaiming territory, such as those in central Somalia from 2023 onward, have enabled agricultural resumption and market reopening, reducing extortion exposure and demonstrating causal links between security gains and economic stabilization.[30] Community-level adaptations, including clan militias and diversified income from urban informal sectors, further mitigate impacts, though these remain fragile amid ongoing jihadist recruitment exploiting economic grievances.[93]

Controversies and critiques

Human rights violations and internal purges

Al-Shabaab enforces a strict interpretation of Sharia law in territories under its control, imposing corporal punishments such as amputations, floggings, and stonings for offenses including theft, adultery, and rape. In June 2009, a court operated by the group sentenced four men in Mogadishu to amputation of one hand and one leg each for robbery.[94] In October and November 2009, the group conducted multiple stonings in southern Somalia, including one man in Merka for rape and two others reported earlier that month.[95] By July 2009, al-Shabaab beheaded at least seven individuals accused of spying in the Bay and Bakool regions.[96] In November 2010, the group publicly executed two teenage girls in central Somalia on unsubstantiated charges of espionage.[97] These punishments, often carried out publicly to instill fear, violate international humanitarian law prohibitions on cruel treatment.[98] The group systematically recruits and uses child soldiers, targeting boys aged 12 to 24, who constitute the majority of new fighters. United Nations data from 2022 documented 232 verified cases of child recruitment by al-Shabaab, predominantly boys.[99] Coercive tactics include abduction from schools, forced conscription during checkpoints, and indoctrination in madrasas, with children deployed in combat, suicide bombings, and as human shields.[100] Al-Shabaab also perpetrates targeted killings of civilians, including aid workers and journalists, with the U.S. State Department reporting credible instances of extrajudicial executions in 2023.[101] Human Rights Watch documented attacks on civilians, sexual violence, and restrictions on humanitarian access in 2023-2024.[102] Internally, al-Shabaab maintains discipline through purges targeting suspected defectors, spies, and factional rivals to prevent fragmentation and enforce loyalty to its al-Qaeda-aligned leadership. Under emir Ahmed Abdi Godane (killed in a U.S. airstrike in September 2014), the group assassinated senior figures like deputy leader Ibrahim al-Afghani in 2013 amid disputes over strategy and allegiance.[16] Such internal executions, often via suicide bombings or summary killings, have eliminated dozens of mid-level commanders and foreign fighters suspected of ISIS sympathies since 2015.[9] Defectors face assassination even after fleeing, as seen in targeted killings of former members in Mogadishu and Kenyan border areas.[103] These purges, justified by the group as eliminating apostates, have consolidated centralized control but exacerbated operational losses.[3]

Debates on root causes versus jihadist motivations

Scholars and policymakers debate whether Al-Shabaab's persistence stems primarily from underlying socio-political and economic grievances in Somaliaâ€”such as state collapse following the 1991 ouster of Siad Barre, clan rivalries, poverty affecting over 70% of Somalis in 2023, and foreign military interventions like the Ethiopian invasion in 2006 and AMISOM's deploymentâ€”or from its explicit adoption of Salafi-jihadist ideology as a transnational revolutionary doctrine.[104][105] Proponents of the root causes perspective, often advanced in development-focused reports, argue that marginalization and lack of governance create fertile ground for recruitment, with Al-Shabaab exploiting these vacuums by providing parallel administration and economic incentives in controlled areas covering up to 40% of Somalia at its peak in 2011. However, empirical analyses find no strong causal link between poverty or inequality and jihadist violence, as terrorist incidents do not correlate robustly with such metrics across regions, suggesting these factors enable but do not motivate the group's ideological commitment.[106] In contrast, Al-Shabaab's foundational documents and actions underscore Salafi-jihadism as the core driver, with the group emerging in 2006-2007 from the youth wing of the Islamist Courts Union (ICU) to pursue a purist agenda of establishing an emirate under strict Sharia, rejecting Somalia's transitional federal government as apostate and framing conflicts as defensive jihad against "Christian" invaders.[107] Leaders like Ahmed Abdi Godane, who formalized ties to Al-Qaeda in 2012 via a public pledge, emphasized global ummah solidarity and takfir (declaring fellow Muslims infidels) over local grievances, as evidenced by fatwas and propaganda videos justifying attacks on civilians, Sufi shrines, and rival clans for deviating from Salafi norms.[105] Recruitment patterns support this: While economic inducements play a role, ideological indoctrination through madrasas and sermons dominates, drawing youth not merely from desperation but from narratives of transcendent purpose, with foreign fighters comprising up to 10-20% of forces by 2014 despite improved local opportunities.[104] Critics of overemphasizing root causes, including counterterrorism experts, contend that such viewsâ€”prevalent in some academic and multilateral analysesâ€”stem from reluctance to confront Islamist ideology's agency, prioritizing palatable policy solutions like aid over ideological countermeasures, yet Al-Shabaab's resilience amid territorial losses (e.g., losing Mogadishu in 2011 and Baidoa in 2019) and revenue from taxes exceeding $100 million annually demonstrates ideological cohesion trumping material incentives.[104][106] The group's evolution from ICU's localized control to Al-Qaeda affiliation, coupled with suicide bombings and beheadings atypical of pre-jihadist Somali insurgencies, indicates Salafi-jihadism as the causal engine, enabling expansion beyond Somalia into Kenya and Ethiopia while subordinating clan loyalties to religious absolutism.[105] This prioritization of ideology explains targeted purges of moderates and rejection of peace talks, as seen in the 2009 assassination of ICU moderates and ongoing excommunications of pragmatists.[107]

Effectiveness of international responses

International responses to Al-Shabaab have primarily involved African Union-led peacekeeping missions, U.S. counterterrorism operations, and multilateral sanctions, yet these efforts have yielded mixed results, with the group demonstrating resilience despite tactical setbacks. The African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM), transitioned to the African Union Transition Mission in Somalia (ATMIS) in 2022, deployed over 20,000 troops at its peak to support Somali forces in reclaiming territory, contributing to Al-Shabaab's loss of major urban centers like Mogadishu by 2011 and further gains in central regions during Somali-led offensives starting in 2022.[2] However, as ATMIS phases out bases by 2024â€“2025, Al-Shabaab has exploited the resulting security vacuums, regaining rural strongholds and launching intensified attacks, underscoring the missions' failure to build sustainable Somali National Army (SNA) capacity amid clan rivalries and desertions.[108][28] U.S. airstrikes and special operations, numbering over 250 since 2017, have inflicted significant casualties on Al-Shabaab, including the confirmed killing of senior leader Mohamed Ali between 2020 and 2025 and dozens of fighters in targeted raids, disrupting command structures and logistics.[109][62] Despite this, the group's estimated 7,000â€“12,000 fighters persist, adapting through guerrilla tactics and extortion revenues exceeding $100 million annually, as strikes often fail to address underlying governance deficits that fuel recruitment.[2] Civilian casualties from U.S. operations, such as those documented in 2020â€“2025 raids, have eroded local support for counterterrorism partners, with AFRICOM acknowledging errors but rejecting reparations, further complicating alliances.[110][111] Broader limitations stem from fragmented Somali state-building, where international aidâ€”totaling over $1.3 billion from the U.S. alone since 2020â€”prioritizes centralized federal structures over clan-based legitimacy, enabling Al-Shabaab to position itself as an alternative governance provider in ungoverned areas.[112] UN sanctions and designations since 2010 have isolated financiers but not curtailed diaspora funding or local taxation, while Kenyan and Ethiopian border operations have contained spillovers yet provoked retaliatory attacks, such as the 2013 Westgate Mall siege.[2] Analysts attribute stalled progress to insufficient integration of counterinsurgency with development, as Al-Shabaab leverages food insecurity and clan grievancesâ€”exacerbated by climate shocksâ€”for sustained operations, outpacing kinetic measures.[113][114] Overall, while international efforts have prevented Al-Shabaab from regaining pre-2011 dominance, the group's evolution into a hybrid insurgent-terror network indicates that without addressing causal factors like corruption and weak institutions, responses risk perpetuating a cycle of temporary gains followed by resurgence.[115]

Other entities

Additional militant groups

The Islamic State â€“ Somalia Province (ISSP), also referred to as ISIS-Somalia or Abnaa ul-Calipha, formed as a splinter from Al-Shabaab in October 2015 when its leader, Abdul Qadir Mumin, and an estimated 300 fighters in Puntland declared allegiance to the Islamic State's caliphate.[116] Operating mainly in Puntland's mountainous areas, ISSP has carried out attacks on Somali government forces, African Union troops, and Al-Shabaab rivals, including a 2016 offensive in Qandala that briefly captured the port town before Somali and Puntland forces, with U.S. support, retook it in December 2016.[44] By 2025, ISSP numbered fewer than 500 fighters, significantly smaller than Al-Shabaab, and has faced repeated defeats from Somali offensives, such as those in 2024-2025 that targeted its strongholds.[117] ISSP sustains operations through extortion, smuggling, and international recruitment, drawing foreign fighters from Kenya, Tanzania, and beyond, while sending funds to Islamic State core networks.[116] Ideological tensions with Al-Shabaab, rooted in loyalty disputes between al-Qaeda and Islamic State affiliations, have led to inter-group violence, with Al-Shabaab launching assaults to suppress ISSP's presence, as seen in clashes near Bosaso in 2018 and ongoing skirmishes.[44] Despite U.S. designations as a foreign terrorist organization since 2016 and drone strikes eliminating key figures, ISSP persists as a localized threat, though its global ambitions remain limited by territorial constraints and resource shortages.[116] Other jihadist entities in Somalia remain marginal, often absorbing into Al-Shabaab or ISSP; for instance, small cells linked to al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb have occasionally operated but lack independent structure.[44] No major additional groups rival the dominance of these two, with clan-based militias like Ahlu Sunna Waljama'a functioning primarily as anti-jihadist forces rather than ideological peers.[117]

Sports clubs and teams

Al-Shabab Club, based in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, is a multi-sport organization founded in 1947 that fields teams in disciplines including football, handball, basketball, taekwondo, karate, and athletics.[118] Its professional football team competes in the Saudi Pro League, one of the kingdom's top-tier competitions, and plays home matches at Al-Shabab Club Stadium.[119] The club maintains academies for youth development in football (established 2014), taekwondo (2017), and karate (2015).[118] In Bahrain, Al-Shabab Club is a professional football club headquartered in Jidhafs, Manama, that participates in the Bahraini Premier League and uses Bahrain National Stadium as its home ground.[120] [121] Al-Shabab SC, located in Seeb, Oman, operates a football section in the Oman Professional League, with Al-Seeb Stadium as its venue; the club qualified for the 2025/2026 AFC Challenge League West Zone Group A.[122] [123]
