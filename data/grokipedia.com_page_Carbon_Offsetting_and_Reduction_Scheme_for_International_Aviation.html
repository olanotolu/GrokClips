Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation

The Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) is a global market-based measure adopted by the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) in 2016, designed to cap net CO₂ emissions from international aviation at approximately 2020 levels by requiring operators to offset any growth above a sectoral baseline through the purchase and retirement of eligible emissions units.[1][2] Implemented in phases, with voluntary participation from 2021 to 2023 and mandatory compliance for larger operators (emitting over 10,000 tonnes of CO₂ annually) from 2024 onward, CORSIA applies to most international flights excluding those to least developed countries, landlocked developing countries, and small island developing states.[3] The baseline for offsetting was initially set based on 2019-2020 emissions but adjusted to 85% of 2019 levels from 2024 to 2035 in response to the COVID-19 pandemic's impact on aviation demand.[2] CORSIA complements technological advancements in fuel efficiency and sustainable aviation fuels by addressing residual emissions growth, with projections indicating it could avert an increase from around 600 million tonnes of CO₂ annually to 900 million tonnes by 2035 without intervention.[2] As of 2025, 129 states representing over 75% of international aviation emissions participate, though actual offset volumes remain limited due to post-pandemic recovery and reliance on eligible units from sectors like forestry and renewables.[2] The scheme mandates annual emissions reporting and verification, fostering transparency, but its effectiveness hinges on the quality of offsets used.[4] Despite these aims, CORSIA has faced criticism for lacking ambition, as it permits continued absolute emissions growth from aviation while displacing reductions to other sectors via offsets, which often suffer from issues of additionality, permanence, and over-crediting.[5] Peer-reviewed analyses highlight that without stricter eligibility criteria for offsets—many of which carry high risks of non-additionality—the scheme may fail to deliver genuine net-zero contributions, potentially undermining global climate goals under the Paris Agreement.[6][7] Furthermore, by excluding domestic flights (about 40% of aviation emissions), CORSIA covers only a portion of the sector's total impact, prompting calls for integration with regional systems like the EU ETS to enhance environmental integrity.[8]

Origins and Development

Historical Context of Aviation Emissions Regulation

The regulation of aviation emissions originated with the establishment of the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) in 1944 under the Chicago Convention, which laid the groundwork for international standards on safety and environmental protection, though initial focus was on operational uniformity rather than emissions. Early environmental efforts centered on aircraft noise, with ICAO adopting the first Standards and Recommended Practices (SARPs) in Annex 16, Volume I, on April 2, 1971, effective August 2, 1971, and applicable from January 6, 1972; these addressed community noise impacts from jet aircraft.[9] By 1981, Annex 16, Volume II introduced SARPs for non-volatile particulate matter (smoke) and gaseous emissions (hydrocarbons, carbon monoxide, and nitrogen oxides) from aircraft engines, effective March 15, 1983, targeting local air quality around airports rather than global climate effects. The shift toward regulating greenhouse gas emissions, particularly carbon dioxide (CO2) from international aviation, gained prominence in the 1990s amid growing awareness of climate change, but international aviation was deliberately excluded from national emission inventories under the 1997 Kyoto Protocol. Article 2.2 of the Protocol directed parties to pursue limitation or reduction of emissions from international aviation "working through the International Civil Aviation Organization," assigning ICAO primary responsibility while exempting these "bunker fuels" from Annex I countries' quantified reduction targets.[10] This exclusion stemmed from challenges in attributing emissions to specific nations due to the extraterritorial nature of international flights, leaving a regulatory gap that ICAO was tasked to fill, though no binding global CO2 standards emerged immediately.[11] Domestic aviation emissions, by contrast, fell under national obligations, highlighting the Protocol's bifurcated approach.[12] ICAO's response intensified post-Kyoto, with the 1998 formation of the Committee on Aviation Environmental Protection (CAEP) accelerating studies on CO2 certification, though progress on a global standard stalled until later decades. In 2001, the 32nd ICAO Assembly adopted Resolution A32-22, committing to work on limiting or reducing emissions through technological and operational improvements. By 2004, ICAO's Group on International Aviation and Climate Change (GIACC) recommended exploring market-based measures (MBMs) like emissions trading or levies, but implementation faced resistance over sovereignty and economic competitiveness concerns. The 35th Assembly in 2007 saw airlines voluntarily commit to improving fuel efficiency by 25% by 2020 and stabilizing emissions at 2005 levels until 2020 via a "basket of measures," marking the first industry-led aspirational goal for carbon-neutral growth beyond that point, without mandatory enforcement.[13] Negotiations advanced unevenly through the 2000s, with expert groups evaluating MBM options—including global emissions levies, cap-and-trade systems, and offsetting—amid calls for a single global framework to avoid fragmented unilateral actions, such as the European Union's 2008 inclusion of aviation in its Emissions Trading System, which provoked international backlash and partial exemptions.[13] The 37th Assembly in 2010 achieved a consensus to develop a comprehensive framework for MBMs aimed at capping net CO2 emissions from international aviation at 2020 levels, setting the stage for further technical work on monitoring, verification, and offsetting mechanisms, though binding agreement remained elusive until subsequent sessions.[13] This period underscored ICAO's incremental approach, prioritizing consensus among 193 member states over rapid mandates, with emissions continuing to rise—international aviation CO2 output grew from about 500 million tonnes in 2000 to over 900 million tonnes by 2019—driven by traffic expansion outpacing efficiency gains.[14]

ICAO's Role and Negotiations Leading to CORSIA

The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO), a specialized agency of the United Nations established in 1944 under the Chicago Convention, holds primary responsibility for developing international standards and recommended practices (SARPs) for civil aviation, including environmental protection measures to address CO2 emissions from international flights. ICAO's Committee on Aviation Environmental Protection (CAEP) and the Group on International Aviation and Climate Change (GIACC) have coordinated technical assessments and policy development, ensuring measures respect the principle of non-discrimination among states while promoting collective action over unilateral initiatives. This role stems from ICAO's mandate to foster safe, orderly, and sustainable global air transport, preventing fragmented regulations that could distort competition.[15] Efforts to regulate aviation emissions evolved over decades, initially focusing on noise and local air quality before shifting to CO2 in the 1990s amid growing climate concerns. By 1998, at its 32nd Assembly, ICAO began studying market-based measures (MBMs) such as levies, but subsequent assemblies, including the 35th in 2004, rejected global emission charges in favor of exploring emissions trading systems (ETS) while urging states to avoid unilateral actions.[16] The 36th Assembly in 2007 marked a pivot, establishing GIACC and introducing carbon offsetting as a viable MBM option through Resolution A36-22, which emphasized technology, operations, and economic incentives alongside offsets.[17] Negotiations intensified post-2009, when GIACC's high-level meeting declaration called for a global framework. The 37th Assembly in 2010 adopted Resolution A37-19, setting an aspirational 2% annual fuel efficiency improvement and the carbon-neutral growth (CNG) goal from 2020 levels (CNG2020), while directing further MBM development. Challenges emerged from divergent state positions: developed nations and airlines favored comprehensive MBMs, while developing countries, citing UNFCCC principles of common but differentiated responsibilities, resisted measures imposing disproportionate costs or infringing sovereignty, leading to opposition against proposals like the EU's extension of its ETS to non-European flights.[16] At the 38th Assembly in October 2013, Resolution A38-18 committed ICAO to finalize a single global MBM scheme by 2016 to achieve CNG2020, prioritizing an offsetting mechanism over cap-and-trade or revenue-generating levies to address equity concerns and minimize administrative burdens. Between 2013 and 2016, GIACC and CAEP refined options, with industry groups like IATA advocating a simple offsetting system using eligible emission units, while environmental organizations pushed for stronger baselines and verification.[2] These talks balanced technical feasibility—drawing from CAEP's CO2 trends assessment showing aviation's projected 500-700% emissions growth without intervention—with political consensus, culminating in the 39th Assembly's adoption of Resolution A39-2 on October 7, 2016, which established CORSIA as a mandatory offsetting scheme post-voluntary phases, applicable to international flights over 10,000 km initially. This agreement, the first sector-specific global MBM, reflected ICAO's success in forging compromise amid resistance to alternatives like global taxes, though critics noted its reliance on offsets deferred absolute reductions.[18]

Adoption and Legal Framework

2016 ICAO Assembly Decision

The 39th Session of the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) Assembly, held from 27 September to 7 October 2016 in Montreal, Quebec, Canada, adopted Resolution A39-3, which established the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) as the global market-based measure (MBM) applicable to CO₂ emissions from international aviation.[19][20] This decision built on prior ICAO commitments, reconfirming the sector's aspirational goals of carbon-neutral growth from 2020 levels and a 2 percent annual improvement in fuel efficiency, with CORSIA designed to offset emissions growth beyond those levels as a supplement to technological, operational, and infrastructure measures.[19][21] Resolution A39-3 determined that CORSIA would serve as the exclusive MBM for such emissions, emphasizing principles of universality, gradual implementation, transparency, cost-effectiveness, and minimal market distortion to ensure equitable application across states without favoring specific operators or regions.[19][20] It directed the ICAO Council, with technical input from the Committee on Aviation Environmental Protection (CAEP), to develop and adopt by the 40th Assembly in 2019 the necessary Standards and Recommended Practices (SARPs), including requirements for monitoring, reporting, verification (MRV), and record-keeping, with MRV obligations commencing in 2019.[19][20] The resolution outlined a phased approach to implementation: a voluntary pilot phase from 2021 to 2023, a first voluntary phase from 2024 to 2026 open to all states except least developed countries (LDCs), landlocked developing countries (LLDCs), and small island developing states (SIDS), and a mandatory phase from 2027 to 2035 for states representing at least 90 percent of international revenue ton-kilometers (RTKs) in 2018, subject to the same exemptions and socio-economic considerations.[20] Offsetting requirements would apply to emissions exceeding a baseline of 2019 levels for the pilot phase and 85 percent of 2019 levels for 2024–2035, accounting for projected efficiency gains.[20] To align with broader climate frameworks, the decision ensured CORSIA's compatibility with the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and the Paris Agreement, permitting the use of emissions units generated under those mechanisms once eligibility criteria were established by a Technical Advisory Body (TAB).[19][20] ICAO projected that CORSIA would result in offsetting approximately 2.5 billion tonnes of CO₂ from 2021 to 2035, based on CAEP analyses.[20] The framework prioritized state-level participation, with operators subject to national implementation, and included provisions for review and adjustment at future assemblies to address implementation challenges.[19][20]

Core Principles and Standards

The Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) operates on the principle of achieving carbon-neutral growth from 2020 onward by requiring aircraft operators to offset CO2 emissions from international flights that exceed a sectoral baseline, thereby stabilizing net emissions at 2020 levels without imposing absolute reduction targets on individual operators.[3] This approach prioritizes offsetting over direct mitigation within aviation, leveraging global market-based mechanisms to address the sector's projected emissions growth, which empirical data from ICAO indicate could reach 2-3% annually absent intervention.[22] The baseline is calculated as a multi-year average of historical emissions, initially set at the 2019-2020 average for the pilot phase and adjusted to 85% of the 2019-2023 average for subsequent voluntary phases to account for pandemic-related disruptions.[20] Standards and Recommended Practices (SARPs) for CORSIA, codified in ICAO Annex 16, Volume IV (Second Edition, applicable from January 1, 2024), establish mandatory requirements for states to implement monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) of operators' emissions, ensuring transparency and compliance through independent third-party verification.[23] Operators must report annual CO2 emissions using ICAO-approved methodologies, with offsetting obligations fulfilled via eligible emissions units (EEUs) or CORSIA-eligible fuels that demonstrate verifiable reductions elsewhere.[4] The scheme's design emphasizes international equity, applying uniformly to flights between participating states while exempting small operators (under 10,000 tonnes CO2 annually) and certain routes to minimize administrative burdens on developing economies.[24] Eligibility criteria for EEUs mandate that units represent additional, real, and permanent emissions reductions or removals, quantified conservatively with no leakage or double counting, and verified ex-post by accredited bodies to prevent over-crediting common in less rigorous offset programs.[25] Programs supplying EEUs must maintain secure registries, disclose methodologies publicly, and incorporate safeguards against environmental and social risks, with ICAO's Technical Advisory Body periodically reviewing and approving standards such as those from the Gold Standard or Verra for compliance.[26] Similarly, CORSIA-eligible fuels, including sustainable aviation fuels and lower carbon aviation fuels, must achieve at least a 10% net life-cycle GHG emissions reduction relative to conventional jet fuel baselines, certified under approved sustainability schemes that enforce feedstock restrictions to avoid indirect land-use change effects.[27] These standards aim to ensure offsets contribute genuine atmospheric benefits, though critics note reliance on external credits may delay aviation-specific technological reductions.[28]

Operational Mechanism

Baseline Calculation and Offsetting Obligations

The baseline in CORSIA serves as the reference point for measuring emissions growth that triggers offsetting requirements, aiming to achieve carbon-neutral growth from stabilized levels. For the pilot phase (2021–2023), the baseline is defined as the total CO₂ emissions from all covered international aviation flights in 2019, reflecting an adjustment from the original 2019–2020 average due to the severe drop in 2020 emissions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic.[29] [30] From 2024 onward, encompassing the voluntary first phase (2024–2026) and mandatory phase (2027–2035), the baseline is set at 85% of 2019 total CO₂ emissions, as established by ICAO Assembly Resolution A41-22 in October 2022 to account for pandemic disruptions while maintaining the scheme's environmental integrity.[29] [3] This fixed sectoral baseline applies globally to participating operators and states, with ICAO aggregating reported data to determine annual sectoral totals. Offsetting obligations are calculated using a sectoral approach, where individual operator requirements are derived from the collective growth in international aviation CO₂ emissions above the baseline. Each covered aeroplane operator—those with annual emissions exceeding 10,000 tonnes of CO₂ from international flights—must monitor and report fuel use and CO₂ emissions annually via ICAO's standardized tools, such as the three-step Emissions Monitoring Plan and the CO₂ Emissions Reporting Tool (CERT), converting fuel consumption to emissions using default or operator-specific factors.[1] [31] The operator's offsetting requirement (ER) for year Y is then:
ER = Operator's CO₂ emissions in Y × [1 – (Baseline sectoral emissions / Sectoral CO₂ emissions in Y)].
This equates to offsetting the operator's proportional share of the sector's excess emissions, with the sectoral growth factor [1 – (baseline / sectoral emissions in Y)] published annually by ICAO based on aggregated state-submitted data.[32] [33] Emissions reductions from eligible sustainable aviation fuels (SAFs) or emissions units claimed in prior years can reduce the effective emissions base before applying the growth factor.[4] Compliance occurs over triennial cycles (e.g., 2024–2026), with offsetting due by 45 months after the cycle's end—such as December 31, 2029, for the 2024–2026 period—allowing operators to surrender eligible emissions units verified by accredited bodies.[20] States oversee operator compliance, reviewing reports and ensuring unit cancellations, while ICAO coordinates global consistency through Standards and Recommended Practices (SARPs) in Annex 16, Volume IV.[1] Failure to meet obligations may result in state-imposed penalties, though enforcement varies by jurisdiction.[34] This methodology incentivizes efficiency improvements and fuel uptake but relies on accurate sectoral data aggregation, with periodic ICAO reviews potentially introducing individual baselines post-2035.[4]

Eligible Emissions Units and Monitoring

CORSIA Eligible Emissions Units (EEUs) are carbon credits generated by approved offset programs, each representing one tonne of carbon dioxide equivalent (tCO₂e) in emissions reductions, avoidance, or removals that aircraft operators may use to fulfill offsetting requirements.[35] These units must originate from programs assessed and approved by the ICAO Technical Advisory Body (TAB) against the Emissions Unit Eligibility Criteria (EUC), which ensure environmental integrity through requirements such as additionality (reductions beyond business-as-usual scenarios), permanence (long-term storage or avoidance of emissions), no leakage (emissions displacement to other areas), accurate quantification, robust monitoring and verification, and avoidance of double counting or environmental harm.[25] The ICAO Council makes final approval decisions based on TAB recommendations, with programs required to demonstrate compliance via independent third-party audits and adherence to governance standards like transparency and public registries.[26] Approved programs include legacy mechanisms like the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) from the Kyoto Protocol, alongside voluntary standards such as the Gold Standard, American Carbon Registry, and Verified Carbon Standard, with eligibility varying by phase— for instance, as of October 2024, multiple programs were authorized for the 2021–2023 period, subject to serial number lists excluding units from projects linked to human rights concerns or non-additional activities.[35] Operators must acquire EEUs from these programs, retire them via cancellation in designated registries to prevent reuse, and report details including serial numbers and vintage years, ensuring units postdate the scheme's baseline to align with CORSIA's growth-based offsetting.[26] Exclusions apply to units from forestry projects with high reversal risks or those failing integrity thresholds, reflecting ICAO's emphasis on verifiable, high-quality offsets amid criticisms that some voluntary credits may overestimate reductions due to baseline inflation or unverifiable additionality.[25] Emissions monitoring under CORSIA requires aircraft operators to track CO₂ emissions from eligible international flights using standardized methodologies outlined in ICAO Annex 16, Volume IV, primarily based on fuel uplift and consumption data derived from aircraft technical logs, fuel invoices, or on-board recording systems.[36] Monitoring commenced January 1, 2019, for all operators regardless of phase participation, employing Tier 1 default emission factors (e.g., 3.16 kg CO₂ per kg of fuel for jet kerosene) for smaller operators or Tier 3 fuel-specific measurements for precision in larger fleets, with adjustments for biogenic fuels like sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) to claim zero lifecycle emissions.[37] Operators maintain records for at least five years, including flight details, fuel quantities, and payload data, to enable annual emissions calculations against sector baselines set at 85% of 2019–2020 averages for post-2020 phases.[36] Verification involves independent accredited verifiers reviewing monitoring plans, data accuracy, and compliance with ICAO standards, culminating in a verification report submitted alongside emissions reports to national authorities by May 31 annually.[37] States aggregate operator data for ICAO submission, fostering transparency through a centralized repository, though challenges persist in data consistency across jurisdictions and verifier qualifications, with ICAO providing guidance tools like the CO₂ emissions calculator software to standardize processes.[38] This MRV framework underpins offsetting obligations by ensuring emissions data integrity, enabling ICAO to adjust baselines dynamically based on verified sector growth and participation rates exceeding 80% of international aviation activity.[37]

Implementation Phases

Pilot Phase (2021)

The Pilot Phase of the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) commenced on January 1, 2021, as a voluntary three-year period (2021–2023) designed to test the scheme's operational mechanisms, including monitoring, reporting, verification, and offsetting requirements for eligible international flights.[1] Participation was limited to routes between states that voluntarily notified the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) of their intent to join, with operators of aircraft emitting more than 10,000 tonnes of CO₂ annually subject to compliance for those covered flights.[3] By July 2021, over 100 states had voluntarily participated, covering a significant portion of international aviation routes, though participation remained optional for states during this phase.[39] The baseline for emissions growth was established using 2019 sector-wide CO₂ emissions, against which any increase in an operator's emissions from covered flights would trigger offsetting obligations.[21] However, due to the sharp decline in global air traffic caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, the ICAO-determined annual sector's growth factor for 2021 resulted in no offsetting requirements accruing for that year, as actual emissions fell below the baseline.[3] Operators were nonetheless required to monitor fuel use and calculate CO₂ emissions for eligible flights using ICAO-approved methodologies, with initial reporting deadlines set for May 2022 covering 2021 data, to build administrative capacity and verify compliance frameworks.[1] Eligible offsetting units included credits from approved programs, such as the Verified Carbon Standard, which met ICAO criteria for additionality, permanence, and avoidance of double-counting, though no units were surrendered in 2021 given the absence of obligations.[40] This phase emphasized capacity-building among states and operators, with ICAO providing technical support for emissions inventories and verification by accredited bodies, while exemptions applied to least-developed countries, small island states, and operators below the emissions threshold.[3] The lack of offsetting in 2021 highlighted the scheme's sensitivity to external disruptions but also demonstrated its baseline mechanism's role in deferring costs during low-emission periods, with data from the year informing adjustments for subsequent phases.[1]

First Voluntary Phase (2024-2026)

The First Voluntary Phase of CORSIA operates from January 1, 2024, to December 31, 2026, allowing states to opt in voluntarily, after which their aircraft operators must comply with offsetting requirements for eligible international flights.[1] Once a state elects to participate, all operators under its jurisdiction with annual emissions exceeding 10,000 tonnes of CO₂ from international aviation are obligated to monitor, report, and verify emissions, then offset any amounts surpassing the global sectoral baseline.[3] This phase builds on the Pilot Phase by expanding potential coverage, with the baseline calculated as 85% of 2019 industry-wide CO₂ emissions to account for post-2020 recovery from the COVID-19 downturn, excluding 2020 data entirely.[41] As of October 2025, 130 states have committed to participation, covering approximately 85% of international aviation emissions and encompassing routes between participating states, though flights to or from non-participating states remain exempt unless bilateral agreements apply.[42] Additional states, including Comoros, Mauritania, and Saint Lucia, joined effective January 1, 2025, incrementally broadening the scheme's scope without mandatory enforcement for non-opt-in countries.[3] Operators submit emissions reports annually, with the first compliance cycle for 2024 data commencing in 2025, requiring offsetting via ICAO-approved Eligible Emissions Units (EEUs) such as those from the American Carbon Registry, Verra's Verified Carbon Standard, and Gold Standard, which underwent expanded approvals in 2024-2025 to meet CORSIA criteria for additionality, permanence, and avoidance of double-counting.[35] Projections indicate demand for EEUs in this phase ranging from 107 million to higher volumes depending on traffic growth and fuel efficiency gains, with ICAO planning a baseline review in September 2025 to incorporate updated emissions data and assess scheme efficacy.[3] Participation remains state-driven, potentially limiting universal coverage compared to the upcoming mandatory phase, as smaller emitters or non-aligned nations can opt out, though major economies like the United States and European Union states have opted in, ensuring substantial sectoral involvement.[1] Initial implementation has focused on refining monitoring tools and credit eligibility, with no reported widespread non-compliance as of mid-2025, though airlines face costs estimated at $1-5 per tonne of CO₂ offset based on prevailing carbon market prices.[43]

Mandatory Phase (2027 Onward)

The Mandatory Phase of the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA), effective from January 1, 2027, to December 31, 2035, shifts participation from voluntary to compulsory for all ICAO Contracting States, excluding those qualifying for exemptions based on economic and emissions criteria.[44][3] This phase aims to stabilize CO2 emissions from international aviation at 2020 levels by requiring operators to offset emissions growth above a sectoral baseline, calculated as the average of 2019 and 2020 emissions, with adjustments applied to account for the COVID-19 pandemic's impact—specifically, an 85% baseline factor for the years 2024 through 2030, transitioning to 100% thereafter.[45][4] Participation exemptions in this phase are granted to Least Developed Countries (LDCs), Small Island Developing States (SIDS), and Landlocked Developing Countries (LLDCs), as well as states where international aviation revenue constitutes less than 1% of total national revenue and the state's share of total revenue ton-kilometers (RTKs) from international flights is below 0.5% of global RTKs.[44] Flights between two participating states trigger offsetting obligations for the operator, regardless of the operator's home state, ensuring broad coverage of international routes; estimates indicate this phase will encompass over 85% of global international aviation emissions, up from voluntary phases due to enforced state-level implementation.[46][47] Operators subject to the scheme must monitor and report CO2 emissions from eligible flights using ICAO-approved methodologies, with exemptions for those emitting 10,000 tonnes or less annually from international operations; however, any calculated offsetting requirement below 3,000 tonnes CO2 is waived.[4][48] Offsetting occurs through the cancellation of eligible emissions units, verified under ICAO standards, with compliance demonstrated every three years via state authorities; non-compliance may result in sanctions under national laws, though ICAO lacks direct enforcement powers.[3][20] This phase builds on prior voluntary implementation without altering core mechanisms, focusing instead on universal adherence to achieve carbon-neutral growth from 2020 levels.[45]

Scope and Participation

Operator and State Participation Requirements

State participation in the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) is structured across phases, with voluntary enrollment in the pilot phase (2021–2023) and first voluntary phase (2024–2026), requiring notification to the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) by June 30 of the preceding year for a state to apply offsetting requirements to relevant routes in the following calendar year.[20] From the mandatory phase onward (2027–2035), participation is compulsory for states whose individual share of 2018 international aviation revenue tonne-kilometres (RTKs) exceeds 0.5%, or for groups of states whose combined share surpasses 0.5% if they elect collective participation, aiming to encompass states representing at least 90% of total international RTKs; exemptions apply to least developed countries (LDCs), small island developing states (SIDS), and landlocked developing countries (LLDCs), though these may opt in voluntarily.[20] [42] As of 2026, 130 states are set to participate voluntarily in offsetting requirements, demonstrating broad but incomplete global uptake prior to mandatory enforcement.[41] Aeroplane operators face offsetting obligations for CO2 emissions from international flights connecting two participating states (known as Chapter 3 state pairs), irrespective of the operator's state of registry, meaning operators based in non-participating states must still comply for emissions on such routes.[20] Participation thresholds exclude operators whose annual CO2 emissions from international flights total less than 10,000 tonnes or who operate solely aeroplanes with a maximum take-off mass (MTOM) below 5,700 kilograms; monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) requirements apply universally to operators exceeding the 10,000-tonne threshold since 2019, even if their state does not join offsetting phases.[20] [32] New entrant operators are exempt from offsetting for their first three years of operation or until their emissions surpass 0.1% of 2019 total international aviation emissions, whichever occurs first, to accommodate market entry without immediate burden.[20] States of the operator bear responsibility for enforcing compliance, including submission of emissions reports and unit cancellations every three-year period, with ICAO's Central Registry facilitating data exchange and verification.[1]

Exemptions, Thresholds, and Coverage Limitations

The Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) applies primarily to aeroplane operators conducting international flights, with exemptions and thresholds designed to focus regulatory burden on larger emitters while excluding small-scale or specialized operations. Operators whose annual CO₂ emissions from international flights do not exceed 10,000 metric tonnes are exempt from monitoring, reporting, verification (MRV), and offsetting requirements, as specified in ICAO Annex 16, Volume IV. This threshold ensures that small operators, which collectively represent a minor share of emissions, are not subject to compliance costs disproportionate to their impact. Operators exceeding this level must implement an Emissions Monitoring Plan and report emissions annually, with offsetting obligations applying only to emissions on covered routes.[20][49] Coverage is limited to international flights between states participating in CORSIA's offsetting requirements for the relevant compliance period, known as Chapter 3 State Pairs; flights where either the departure or destination state is non-participating or exempt do not trigger offsetting, though reporting may still apply for qualifying operators. Domestic flights are entirely excluded from the scheme's scope, as CORSIA targets only international aviation to avoid overlap with national measures. Additionally, new entrants to international routes receive a temporary exemption from offsetting for up to three years or until their emissions surpass 0.1% of 2019's total international aviation CO₂ emissions, whichever occurs first, to accommodate market growth without immediate penalties.[50][20] Specific operational exemptions include aircraft with a maximum take-off mass of 5,700 kg or less, which are outside the scheme due to their negligible emissions profile, as well as helicopters, which fall under separate ICAO standards. State-operated flights, such as those by military, customs, or police entities, are excluded to preserve sovereignty in non-commercial operations. Humanitarian, medical, and firefighting missions, along with associated repositioning flights, receive exemptions to prioritize emergency response without administrative hurdles. States themselves face exemptions from mandatory participation starting in 2027 if classified as Least Developed Countries (LDCs), Small Island Developing States (SIDS), Landlocked Developing Countries (LLDCs), or those with less than 0.5% of 2018 international revenue ton-kilometers (RTK) or outside the top 90% of states by RTK share, thereby limiting coverage to approximately 85-90% of global international aviation emissions while shielding vulnerable economies.[20][50] These provisions balance environmental coverage with practical implementation, though they result in exclusions estimated to leave out 10-15% of emissions, particularly from exempt states and small operators. For instance, routes involving exempted states do not count toward an operator's offsetting baseline, potentially reducing the scheme's stringency for carriers serving those markets. Operators transitioning across thresholds, such as dropping below 10,000 tonnes, must notify their state and may revert to exemption status, with historical data retained for potential re-entry.[20][50]

Environmental and Technical Assessments

Projected and Actual Emission Reductions

The Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) is designed to achieve carbon-neutral growth by requiring operators to offset CO₂ emissions exceeding a sectoral baseline, rather than mandating direct reductions in aviation fuel use or flight volumes. Projections for offsetting requirements, which represent the volume of emissions units airlines must retire to comply, vary by traffic growth scenarios. According to ICAO analyses, cumulative offsetting obligations could reach approximately 150 million tonnes of CO₂ through 2026, with total demand across 2024–2035 potentially ranging from 980 million to 1.5 billion tonnes, depending on aviation demand forecasts and participation rates.[51] IATA estimates for the first voluntary phase (2024–2026) place offsetting needs between 146 and 246 million tonnes of CO₂, reflecting expected post-pandemic recovery and baseline set at 85% of 2019 emissions levels.[52] These figures assume offsets from eligible units deliver equivalent atmospheric CO₂ removal or avoidance elsewhere, though the scheme's reliance on external credits raises questions about net global reductions given potential issues with credit quality and double-counting. In the pilot phase (2021–2023), no offsetting requirements materialized, as global international aviation CO₂ emissions remained below the 2019 baseline due to COVID-19 travel restrictions, resulting in a sectoral growth factor of zero as calculated by ICAO.[3] Emissions reports submitted by participating states confirmed this, with operators facing no compliance obligations despite monitoring and reporting mandates.[53] Limited actual reductions occurred via CORSIA-eligible fuels (CEF), totaling about 30,000 tonnes of CO₂ across the phase, primarily from early adoption of lower-carbon fuels by select operators.[54] This outcome highlights the scheme's growth-offsetting mechanism's dependency on exceeding baselines, which did not happen amid depressed demand. For the first voluntary phase (2024–2026), offsetting obligations commenced in 2024 as emissions surpassed the adjusted baseline, with 126 states initially participating, rising to 129 by January 2025.[3] ICAO's 2024 sectoral growth factor indicated positive requirements, prompting airlines to acquire eligible emissions units, though full compliance data for 2024 emissions—due by July 2025—remains pending verification.[53] As of mid-2025, actual offsets retired are minimal relative to projections, with market analyses estimating initial demand fulfillment through pre-approved credits from programs like the American Carbon Registry, but no comprehensive ICAO-verified reduction totals have been published.[55] The phase's outcomes will inform the 2025 periodic review, potentially adjusting baselines or unit criteria to align with empirical emission trends.[29]

Integration with Technological and Fuel Innovations

CORSIA complements in-sector emissions reductions achieved through technological advancements in aircraft design, such as improved aerodynamics, lightweight materials, and more efficient engines, which lower fuel consumption and thus the CO2 emissions subject to offsetting. These innovations reduce operators' actual emissions below the scheme's sectoral growth baseline—set at 85% of 2019 levels for the 2024-2026 voluntary phase—thereby decreasing the volume of offsets required for compliance.[1][54] For instance, historical aviation fuel efficiency improvements, averaging around 2% per year since the 1990s, directly contribute to such reductions without altering CORSIA's offsetting mechanism. The scheme integrates fuel innovations primarily through provisions for CORSIA-eligible fuels, which allow operators to claim emissions reductions in their compliance calculations. Sustainable aviation fuels (SAF), derived from renewable or waste feedstocks and meeting ICAO's sustainability criteria—including at least a 50% lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions savings compared to conventional jet fuel—can reduce offsetting obligations when certified and reported.[56] Lower carbon aviation fuels (LCAF), fossil-based alternatives with a minimum 10% lifecycle emissions reduction relative to the baseline, are also eligible, broadening access to lower-emission options amid limited SAF supply.[57][56] To facilitate adoption, CORSIA employs a book-and-claim system for SAF, enabling producers to certify environmental attributes separately from physical delivery, which airlines can then claim to offset emissions even if the fuel is not directly used in their operations. This mechanism, introduced to accelerate SAF deployment, relies on third-party certification schemes like ISCC CORSIA, ensuring verifiable lifecycle emissions data.[58][59] ICAO's methodology for actual lifecycle emissions calculations, updated as of June 2025, standardizes assessments for co-processed and standalone SAF production, accounting for feedstock sourcing, processing, and distribution to prevent over-crediting.[60] While CORSIA prioritizes offsetting over direct incentives for innovation, resolutions such as A42-22 emphasize a preference for in-sector measures like advanced technologies and SAF before relying on emissions units, fostering long-term decarbonization.[61] However, critics note that the offset-focused design may dilute urgency for rapid technological breakthroughs, as airlines can comply via cheaper credits rather than investing in costly innovations, though actual emissions data from efficient fleets still confer competitive advantages.[62]

Economic Impacts

Costs to Airlines and Passengers

Airlines bear direct costs under CORSIA for emissions monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV), which involve implementing systems to track fuel use and CO2 emissions on international flights, as well as acquiring and retiring eligible emissions units (EEUs) to offset excesses over the baseline.[1] In the Pilot Phase (2021-2023), no final offsetting requirements accrued due to pandemic-related emission reductions below the 2019 baseline, limiting costs primarily to MRV setup estimated in the low millions per major operator.[3] For the First Voluntary Phase (2024-2026), with a baseline at 85% of 2019 emissions, industry offset demand is projected at 106-137 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent, with prices for Phase I-eligible credits rising from around USD 11 per tonne in early 2024 to USD 20 by December 2024 and potentially USD 18-51 per tonne overall, yielding total costs of USD 1.9-7.0 billion.[63] [64] These offset expenses equate to 0.07-0.15% of airlines' total annual operating costs during this phase.[21] From the Mandatory Phase in 2027, costs escalate with projected demand of 502-1,299 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent and prices up to USD 91 per tonne in later years, potentially driven by credit supply constraints.[63] Airlines face strategic decisions on cost allocation, either absorbing offsets into operating margins—reducing profits by up to 4% in Phase I scenarios—or passing them through via surcharges or embedded fare adjustments.[63] If fully passed on, Phase I costs could raise average international ticket prices by 0.5-1.0%, or USD 0.3-2.0 per passenger depending on route length and load factors.[63] More conservative estimates project smaller per-ticket add-ons, such as USD 0.40 for a 2030 Brussels-New York flight under indicative pricing.[65] By 2035, offsetting may comprise 0.4-1.4% of projected international aviation revenues.[66] As of October 2025, no empirical data confirms direct fare hikes from CORSIA compliance, though anticipated credit shortages could amplify future passenger burdens if demand outstrips supply of high-integrity units.[67] UK government analysis anticipates only marginal airfare impacts overall, potentially influencing demand minimally.[68]

Market Effects on Aviation Growth

CORSIA facilitates carbon-neutral growth in aviation by requiring operators to offset CO₂ emissions exceeding a baseline of 85% of 2019 levels from 2024 onward, allowing revenue passenger kilometers (RPKs) to expand without corresponding net emission increases. This structure imposes variable costs on airlines for emission growth, with collective offsetting expenses projected at $1.9–7.0 billion during the voluntary Phase I (2024–2026), driven by credit purchases at prices potentially rising to $25–60 per tonne by the late 2020s.[69][70] These costs, representing a small fraction of total operating expenses (where fuel alone accounts for 20–30%), are anticipated to translate into modest fare increases of 0.5–1.0% for international tickets, as airlines pass through marginal expenses to maintain profitability.[71] Air travel demand exhibits low price elasticity, typically estimated at -0.4 to -1.0 overall, with even lower responsiveness for business and long-haul segments central to international aviation. Studies on carbon pricing shocks, such as EU ETS implementations, show flight volume reductions of 5–15% for domestic and short-haul routes per 10–20% cost increases, but negligible impacts on extra-EU international traffic due to fewer substitutes and higher income inelasticity.[72][73] Consequently, CORSIA's cost mechanism is unlikely to materially dampen global passenger growth, which rebounded to over 90% of pre-2019 levels by 2023 and is forecasted to average 4% annually through 2040 under baseline scenarios.[14] The scheme's design incentivizes efficiency gains—such as fleet modernization and sustainable aviation fuel adoption—over demand suppression, as offsets enable unlimited physical expansion at a price. Game-theoretic models indicate that while emission fees elevate fares and could reduce demand by 1–3% in competitive markets if fully passed through, airlines often absorb portions to preserve market share, minimizing growth inhibition.[74][62] Potential credit supply shortages, however, risk amplifying prices and compliance burdens, indirectly curbing marginal route development in cost-sensitive regions, though ICAO adjustments aim to balance this by preserving growth viability.[1] Overall, empirical projections affirm CORSIA's role in decoupling traffic expansion from emission rises without imposing hard caps on sector output.

Criticisms and Debates

Shortcomings of Offset-Only Approach

The Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) relies exclusively on offsetting mechanisms to achieve its goal of carbon-neutral growth from 2020 levels onward, requiring participating airlines to purchase and retire emissions units equivalent to their sectoral emissions exceeding a baseline derived from 2019 fuel use data (adjusted to 85% for the 2024-2035 period).[22] This approach caps neither absolute emissions nor flight volumes, allowing aviation CO2 output—which reached approximately 920 million tonnes in 2019—to continue expanding as long as offsets are procured, thereby failing to curb the sector's projected doubling by 2050 under business-as-usual scenarios.[75] A primary shortcoming is the absence of direct incentives for in-sector emission reductions, as offsetting external projects—such as forestry or renewable energy—proves more cost-effective for airlines than investing in fuel-efficient aircraft, sustainable aviation fuels (SAF), or operational efficiencies beyond the baseline trajectory, which already incorporates assumed technological improvements.[76] Analysis indicates that offset prices under CORSIA, typically ranging from $5-15 per tonne of CO2, remain far below the marginal abatement costs for aviation-specific measures like SAF, which exceed $500 per tonne, thus prioritizing financial compliance over technological innovation.[76] This structure has contributed to limited uptake of low-carbon alternatives, with SAF comprising less than 0.1% of aviation fuel in 2023 despite CORSIA's implementation.[77] Empirically, the offset-only model has not stabilized aviation's environmental footprint, as post-2020 emissions rebounded sharply—surpassing 2019 levels by 2023—necessitating offsets for growth without reducing flight-related CO2 releases, which offsets merely claim to neutralize elsewhere through unproven sequestration or avoidance.[78] Studies highlight a rebound effect, where perceived carbon neutrality from offsets may encourage increased air travel demand, further elevating gross emissions; for instance, econometric modeling shows that offset availability correlates with 5-10% higher flight volumes in participating markets.[79] Moreover, the scheme overlooks non-CO2 impacts like contrails and nitrogen oxides, which amplify aviation's warming by up to three times CO2 effects, receiving no offsetting mandate.[80] Offset integrity compounds these issues, as CORSIA-eligible credits often derive from projects with questionable additionality—meaning reductions might occur absent purchases—and impermanence, particularly forestry offsets prone to reversal from fires or land-use changes, undermining causal equivalence to aviation's persistent atmospheric CO2. Historical data from similar schemes, like the Clean Development Mechanism, reveal over-crediting by 20-50% in renewable projects due to baseline inflation, a risk unmitigated in CORSIA despite eligibility criteria.[81] The European Commission has noted these inadequacies, stating in 2024 assessments that CORSIA delivers negligible tangible reductions, prioritizing industry accommodation over rigorous climate mitigation.[78]

Concerns Over Credit Integrity and Additionality

Critics have raised significant concerns regarding the additionality of carbon credits used under the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA), arguing that many eligible emission units represent reductions that would have occurred regardless of offset funding. Additionality requires that credited emission reductions exceed what would happen under a business-as-usual scenario without the financial incentive from carbon markets; however, analyses of CORSIA-eligible projects indicate widespread deficiencies in this area. For instance, a rating of 27 CORSIA-eligible projects found that 67% exhibited significant additionality risk, primarily due to limited dependence on carbon finance for project viability, with project ratings ranging from 'BBB' (moderate delivery likelihood) to 'C' (low).[82] Similarly, eligible credits from programs like the Verified Carbon Standard (VCS) have faced scrutiny for incorporating methodologies prone to non-additionality, such as renewable energy projects that align with host country policies independent of offset revenues.[7] Credit integrity is further compromised by CORSIA's eligibility criteria, which permit units issued since 2016, potentially including credits from projects predating the scheme's incentives and thus lacking genuine additionality. The Öko-Institut has highlighted that current criteria provide only basic assurances of additionality, with inconsistent application across approved programs, recommending stricter limits to project types with proven high additionality and independent expert validation to address these gaps.[80] Quantifying reductions also poses integrity risks, as baselines in some methodologies may overestimate avoided emissions, leading to over-crediting; this issue persists despite ICAO's safeguards, as evidenced by broader evaluations showing systematic overestimation in similar offset programs used for CORSIA compliance.[83] Permanence concerns intersect with additionality, as CORSIA's monitoring of reversals (e.g., in forestry projects) is limited to approximately 20 years, far short of the millennia-scale atmospheric impact of aviation CO2, raising doubts about the long-term integrity of credited reductions. Critics contend that these flaws enable "hot air" credits—those yielding no net environmental benefit—undermining CORSIA's goal of stabilizing international aviation emissions, with early issuances under the scheme reportedly delivering negligible actual reductions due to inherent non-additionality in sourced credits like those from the Clean Development Mechanism.[84] While ICAO mandates criteria such as corresponding adjustments to avoid double-counting, independent assessments suggest these measures insufficiently mitigate pervasive additionality and integrity risks, potentially allowing airlines to claim compliance without substantive emission cuts.[80][7]

Equity Issues and Non-Participation Risks

Developing countries have raised concerns that CORSIA disproportionately burdens their aviation sectors, which are essential for economic growth and connectivity, without adequately accounting for historical emissions primarily from developed nations. For instance, under common equity principles such as historical responsibility or ability to pay, developing states argue they should bear less offsetting obligation compared to CORSIA's sector-wide baseline approach, which applies uniformly regardless of per-capita or cumulative emissions.[85] A United Nations perspective has described CORSIA as falling short of fairness, asserting that developing countries should not be held liable for emissions accumulated through industrialization processes largely absent in their histories. China's delegation at ICAO has criticized the scheme's pathways for lacking equitable standards, particularly in enforcing carbon-neutral growth from 2020 without sufficient negotiation or differentiation for emerging markets.[86][87] These equity tensions manifest in the scheme's phased implementation, where voluntary participation in the initial phases (2021–2023 pilot and 2024–2026 first phase) allows developing and low-emission states to opt out, but at the cost of forgoing influence over offset standards and baselines. Critics contend this structure perpetuates inequities, as wealthier nations and carriers can more readily absorb compliance costs—estimated at $1–5 per ton of CO2 initially—while smaller operators in the Global South face administrative and financial strains that could stifle route expansion.[46] Proponents, including ICAO, counter that exemptions for least developed countries, small island states, and landlocked nations from 2027 onward, alongside revenue-neutral offsetting, mitigate burdens, though empirical data on differential impacts remains limited post-pilot phase.[22] Non-participation risks undermine CORSIA's global efficacy, as the scheme only mandates offsetting for flights between participating states during voluntary phases, potentially covering as little as 70–80% of international emissions if major emitters abstain. Notable non-participants in the first phase include China and Russia, whose airlines account for substantial traffic volumes, leading to uneven compliance burdens where operators flying mixed routes must monitor and report emissions selectively.[88] This opt-out flexibility, derived from the Chicago Convention's Annex provisions, enables states to avoid offsetting but risks carbon leakage, where airlines might reroute via non-participating hubs to evade requirements, though no large-scale evidence of such shifts has materialized as of 2025.[85] Furthermore, incomplete participation erodes additionality in offset markets, as reduced demand from non-compliant flights diminishes incentives for high-integrity credits, potentially inflating prices or diluting environmental outcomes for participating entities.[45] ICAO's encouragement for universal voluntary uptake has yielded 126 states in the first phase, yet sustained opt-outs by high-growth markets like China—cited for concerns over forced goals without equity—could delay the scheme's transition to mandatory coverage in 2027, prolonging fragmented global regulation.[89][48]

Recent Developments and Future Outlook

Periodic Reviews and Adjustments (2022-2025)

The first periodic review of CORSIA, conducted by the ICAO Council in 2022 as mandated by Assembly Resolution A40-19, assessed the scheme's implementation during the pilot phase (2021-2023) and recommended adjustments to address disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic.[29][90] Key outcomes included revising the sectoral emissions baseline for the first and second implementation phases (2024-2035) to 85% of 2019 CO2 emissions levels, rather than the original average of 2019 and 2020 data, to account for the sharp decline in aviation activity during 2020.[91][8] This adjustment aimed to stabilize offsetting requirements amid uneven post-pandemic recovery, with the change formalized in updates to ICAO Annex 16, Volume IV.[4] Subsequent refinements in 2023 incorporated the 2022 review findings into operational guidelines, including a second edition of Annex 16, Volume IV published in July 2023, which clarified monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) processes and eligibility criteria for emissions units.[4][92] ICAO also approved additional independent standards for CORSIA-eligible emissions units, expanding supply options for the pilot and first phases by granting full approval to four new methodologies, though these were limited in project volume compared to established standards.[93] In preparation for the 2025 periodic review, the ICAO Council in March 2023 tasked the Committee on Aviation Environmental Protection (CAEP) with analyzing supply, demand, and pricing dynamics for CORSIA-eligible fuels and emissions units, building on 2022 assessments of scheme costs and implementation.[29][94] Interim technical assessments released in early 2025 highlighted potential supply constraints, with only about 15 million eligible credits available for the first phase (2024-2026), prompting evaluations of additionality and integrity criteria.[95] Sustainability criteria for eligible fuels were updated in June 2025 to require at least 10% net GHG emissions reductions over the lifecycle baseline, emphasizing empirical verification of feedstock sourcing and emissions avoidance.[27] The 2025 review's outcomes, deliberated at the ICAO Council's 235th session from June 9 to July 4, 2025, focused on backward-looking evaluations of pilot phase performance and forward projections, including interim demand estimates indicating rising offsetting obligations from 2027 onward due to traffic growth exceeding the adjusted baseline.[96][41] While final adjustments remained under consideration as of late 2025, preliminary findings underscored the need for enhanced unit supply to avoid price volatility, with 130 states committing to voluntary participation starting 2026, potentially amplifying demand pressures.[41][97] These reviews have iteratively refined CORSIA's mechanisms without altering its core offsetting-only structure, prioritizing data-driven responses to real-world emission trajectories over aspirational targets.

Potential Expansions or Revisions Post-2027

The second phase of CORSIA, spanning 2027 to 2035, introduces mandatory offsetting requirements for international aviation emissions above an 85% baseline of 2019 levels, applicable to all ICAO member states except least developed countries, small island developing states, landlocked developing countries, and those with international revenue ton-kilometers below 0.1% or total RTKs under 0.5% of global figures.[3][98] This phase expands coverage to virtually all international flights between participating states, projected to encompass over 80% of sector emissions, compared to voluntary participation in prior phases.[46] Offsetting obligations will rely on eligible emissions units vetted by ICAO's Technical Advisory Body (TAB), with cumulative demand potentially exceeding 1 billion tonnes of CO2 equivalent by 2035 under baseline growth scenarios.[51] Revisions within this phase may arise from ongoing TAB reassessments of emissions unit eligibility, scheduled for sub-periods such as 2027-2029, to ensure credits meet criteria for additionality, permanence, and avoidance of double-counting; four additional standards received approval in 2024, potentially broadening supply but subject to future scrutiny amid concerns over credit quality.[93] The ICAO Council and Committee on Aviation Environmental Protection (CAEP) conduct periodic reviews, which could adjust baselines, methodologies, or exemptions based on updated emissions inventories and technological feasibility, as demonstrated by the 2022 baseline revision to account for COVID-19 impacts.[99] Projections indicate offsetting costs could rise significantly, with airlines facing collective liabilities of hundreds of millions of tonnes annually by the late 2020s, prompting potential refinements to mitigate economic burdens without undermining environmental integrity.[70] Looking beyond 2035, ICAO has outlined a special review by the end of 2032 to evaluate scheme continuation, possible enhancements in stringency, or integration with emerging measures like sustainable aviation fuels mandates or global carbon pricing to align with the sector's net-zero by 2050 aspirations.[75] Discussions at the 42nd Assembly in 2025 highlighted extension prospects, driven by the interim nature of offsetting amid persistent growth in aviation demand and limited progress on in-sector reductions.[100] Absent such revisions, the scheme's termination could leave a gap, as current frameworks prioritize stabilization over absolute cuts, with no formalized post-2035 plans despite member states' expectations for CORSIA's role in long-term decarbonization.[101] Potential expansions might include voluntary domestic aviation inclusion or linkages to regional systems like the EU ETS, though geopolitical tensions and varying national capacities pose implementation hurdles.[102]
