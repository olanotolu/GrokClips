Al-Quds (disambiguation)

Al-Quds (Arabic: القدس, romanized: al-Quds, lit. 'the Holy') is the Arabic name for Jerusalem, an ancient city sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam due to sites like the Temple Mount, Church of the Holy Sepulchre, and Al-Aqsa Mosque.[1][2] The term derives from the Semitic root q-d-š signifying holiness, reflecting the city's religious centrality across millennia.[3] Beyond the city, Al-Quds designates key Palestinian institutions, including Al-Quds University, a major collegiate research institution with its primary campus in Abu Dis near Jerusalem offering over 120 programs; the Al-Quds newspaper, a prominent Arabic-language daily founded in 1951 and based in Jerusalem; and Saraya al-Quds (Jerusalem Brigades), the paramilitary arm of Palestinian Islamic Jihad responsible for rocket attacks and other operations against Israel.[4][5][6] These usages underscore the term's enduring cultural and political resonance in Arab and Palestinian contexts.

Etymology and Primary Reference

Linguistic Origins and Meaning

"Al-Quds" (Arabic: القدس) derives from the Arabic noun quds, rooted in the Semitic triliteral q-d-š, which conveys concepts of holiness, sanctity, and purity across languages like Arabic, Hebrew (qādôš), and Aramaic.[3] This etymological foundation underscores a shared Semitic linguistic heritage emphasizing sacred consecration, independent of specific religious doctrines.[3] The definite article al- ("the") prefixes quds to form "the holy" or "the sanctity," a designation evoking ritual purity and divine separation from the profane.[1][2] Linguistically, it abbreviates fuller historical Arabic phrases such as madīnat al-quds ("city of holiness") or bayt al-muqaddas ("house of the sanctified"), reflecting adaptive naming conventions in Islamic and Arabic textual traditions for revered locales.[3] In broader usage, quds appears in Quranic and classical Arabic contexts to denote holiness (taqaddus), as in divine glorification (subḥāna llāhi wa-taʿālā), but al-Quds as a proper noun crystallized post-Islamic conquests to designate Jerusalem, prioritizing its perceived sacred status over pre-Islamic Semitic toponyms like Yerushalayim.[1] This semantic shift highlights how linguistic evolution in Arabic favored abstract sanctity descriptors for geographic referents, contrasting with descriptive Hebrew etymologies linking the city to peace (shalom) or vision (yir'eh).[3]

Historical Association with Jerusalem

"Al-Quds," meaning "the Holy" in Arabic, derives from the triliteral root q-d-š, connoting sanctity, and has been historically linked to Jerusalem's religious preeminence in Islam since the 7th century CE. This designation underscores the city's status as home to the Al-Aqsa Mosque, referenced in the Qur'an as al-Masjid al-Aqṣā (Surah 17:1), tied to the Prophet Muhammad's Israʾ (night journey) around 621 CE, which established it as the initial qibla for prayer before redirection to Mecca.[7] The formal Islamic association intensified with the conquest of Jerusalem in 637–638 CE by Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab, who accepted the city's surrender from Patriarch Sophronius and issued a covenant guaranteeing protections to its inhabitants. At this juncture, the site was designated Bayt al-Maqdis ("House of the Holy"), a term appearing in Umar's era inscriptions on the Temple Mount, such as one dedicating the area for Muslim worship while acknowledging its prior Jewish temple associations.[8][9] Bayt al-Maqdis reflected the site's holiness rooted in Abrahamic traditions, paralleling Hebrew Beit HaMikdash for the Temple, and served as the precursor to Al-Quds as a concise evocation of sanctity.[10] By the medieval period, Al-Quds gained prominence in Islamic scholarship and texts, as evidenced in Qadi Iyad's Ash-Shifa bi taʿrif huquq al-Mustafa (d. 1149 CE), which affirms Jerusalem's nomenclature through its Qur'anic holiness, and later works like Mujir al-Din al-Hanbali's al-Uns al-jalil (1496 CE), which detail its enduring Muslim identity. This evolution solidified Al-Quds as the standard Arabic toponym, emphasizing causal ties to Islamic eschatology, pilgrimage, and governance over the unified sacred precinct known as the Haram al-Sharif.[7]

Geographical and Administrative Uses

Quds Governorate

The Quds Governorate (Arabic: محافظة القدس, Muḥāfaẓat al-Quds), also known as the Jerusalem Governorate, is one of the 16 governorates established by the Palestinian Authority in 1995 as administrative divisions of the Palestinian territories.[11][12] Located in the central West Bank, it centers on East Jerusalem (al-Quds), which the Palestinian Authority designates as its capital and administrative hub. The governorate spans 344 square kilometers, encompassing urban, suburban, and rural areas including parts of the Jerusalem municipal boundary as defined by the PA.[13] Its territory includes sites of historical and religious significance, such as the Old City of Jerusalem, though effective PA governance is constrained by Israeli security and administrative control over East Jerusalem since Israel's capture of the area in the 1967 Six-Day War and subsequent annexation via the 1980 Jerusalem Law—a move deemed invalid under international law by the United Nations Security Council.[14] Demographically, the governorate is predominantly Palestinian Arab, with population figures compiled by the Palestinian Central Bureau of Statistics (PCBS) focusing on residents under PA jurisdiction or in PA-recognized localities. PCBS projections estimate the mid-year population at 471,834 in 2021, reflecting a density of over 1,300 persons per square kilometer driven by urban concentration in East Jerusalem and surrounding villages.[15] These statistics exclude Israeli settlers in the area, estimated separately by Israeli authorities at around 200,000 in East Jerusalem neighborhoods as of recent years; the PA does not include them in governorate totals, viewing settlements as violations of international law. Growth rates align with broader West Bank trends, influenced by natural increase and limited migration due to restrictions on movement and residency permits imposed by Israel.[16] Administratively, the governorate operates under the PA's Ministry of Local Government, with a governor appointed directly by the PA president to oversee local councils, services, and coordination with civil society. The current governor, Adnan Ghaith, has issued statements condemning Israeli actions such as home raids and excavation works near religious sites like Al-Aqsa Mosque, citing risks to infrastructure stability as of October 2023.[17][18] PA control is nominal in core East Jerusalem areas, where Israeli municipal services predominate, leading to fragmented authority: the governorate manages some 30 localities, but Israeli barriers and checkpoints limit connectivity and development. Economically, it relies on tourism, trade, and remittances, hampered by the separation barrier constructed by Israel in the early 2000s, which encircles much of the governorate and restricts access for non-residents.[11]

Media Outlets

Al-Quds (Contemporary Palestinian Newspaper)

Al-Quds is a Palestinian Arabic-language daily newspaper published in broadsheet format and based in East Jerusalem. It was established in 1968 through the merger of the pre-existing dailies Al-Difaa and Al-Jihad after Palestinian newspapers ceased operations during the 1967 Six-Day War; founder Mahmoud Abu-Zalaf secured an Israeli license to resume publication that year.[19][20] The paper maintains editorial offices in East Jerusalem despite ongoing political restrictions on media operations there.[21] Owned by the Abu-Zalaf family—descendants of Mahmoud Abu-Zalaf, who served as editor-in-chief from 1968 until his death in 2005—the newspaper positions itself as independent within the Palestinian media landscape.[21] Current owners include Walid and Ziad Abu-Zalaf, who have overseen expansions such as the launch of a Hebrew-language edition in April 2023 aimed at reaching Israeli audiences.[22] With an estimated circulation of 20,000 copies as of the mid-2000s, Al-Quds holds the largest readership among Palestinian dailies and covers politics, society, and regional affairs from a Palestinian perspective.[21] It has faced operational challenges, including a one-day shutdown in July 2019 due to a workers' strike over unpaid wages.[23] The outlet operates a website at alquds.com, providing both Arabic and English content.[24]

Al-Quds Al-Arabi

Al-Quds Al-Arabi is an Arabic-language daily newspaper established in London in April 1989 by Palestinian expatriates, with printing operations initially centered in the United Kingdom and later expanded to New York and Frankfurt.[25] The publication positions itself as an independent political daily, emphasizing coverage of Arab world affairs, international relations, and Palestinian issues, while maintaining a pan-Arabist editorial line that critiques authoritarian regimes and supports resistance narratives.[26] From its inception until July 2013, Abdel Bari Atwan, a Palestinian journalist born in a Gaza refugee camp, served as editor-in-chief, during which the paper gained prominence for its outspoken stance against Western interventions in the Middle East and for hosting dissenting voices from Arab opposition figures.[27] Under Atwan's leadership, Al-Quds Al-Arabi distinguished itself by publishing exclusive content from Islamist groups, including full transcripts of messages from Osama bin Laden, whom Atwan interviewed in 1996 in Afghanistan; this access elevated the newspaper's profile but drew accusations of sympathy toward al-Qaeda from critics in Western and some Arab media outlets.[26] The paper's commitment to "uncompromising integrity," as Atwan described it, led to bans in several Arab countries, such as Algeria and Libya, due to its reporting on political repression and regional conflicts that challenged official narratives.[28] Circulation figures have not been publicly detailed in recent years, but the newspaper has sustained operations through print and digital formats, distributing content across Europe and the United States. Following Atwan's departure in 2013 to found the online outlet Rai al-Youm, subsequent editors have continued the tradition of independent journalism, though the publication has faced scrutiny over potential funding ties to Qatari interests via intermediaries, which some pro-Israel monitoring groups cite as influencing its pro-Palestinian and anti-Israel bias in coverage.[29] As of 2025, Al-Quds Al-Arabi remains active, issuing daily editions available via digital subscriptions and focusing on ongoing conflicts in Gaza, Syrian developments, and intra-Arab diplomacy, with recent articles critiquing U.S. policy in the region and highlighting settler violence in the West Bank.[30] Its role in Arabic media underscores a niche for expatriate-led outlets that prioritize pan-Arab solidarity over state-aligned reporting, though this has occasionally amplified unverified claims from militant sources without rigorous fact-checking.[26]

Al-Quds (Ottoman-Era Newspaper)

Al-Quds was an Arabic-language daily newspaper published in Jerusalem from 1908 to 1914, representing the first privately owned public daily in Palestine during the late Ottoman era. Founded by Jurji Habib Hanania (1864–1920), a journalist and pioneer of the Palestinian press, the newspaper emerged in the wake of the 1908 Ottoman Constitutional Revolution, which expanded press freedoms and enabled private publications beyond official gazettes. Hanania, who owned, published, and managed the paper, launched its inaugural issue on 18 September 1908, printing dates in Julian solar, Hijri, and Gregorian calendars to reflect its broad appeal.[31][32] The publication covered social, literary, and political matters, serving as a platform for local discourse on modernization, education, and regional events in the Jerusalem mutasarrifate. Sheikh Ali al-Rimawi acted as editor-in-chief, contributing to its content under Hanania's oversight. As the earliest independent Arabic daily in the region, Al-Quds tested the limits of the new constitutional liberties while documenting early 20th-century shifts, including infrastructure developments and cultural debates, before ceasing operations amid World War I disruptions in 1914.[33][34][35]

Military and Paramilitary Entities

Al-Quds Brigades

The Al-Quds Brigades (Arabic: سرايا القدس, Saraya al-Quds, meaning "Jerusalem Brigades") is the military wing of the Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), a Sunni Islamist militant organization dedicated to the destruction of Israel and the establishment of an Islamist state in historic Palestine.[36] Formed as PIJ's armed component, it conducts guerrilla warfare, rocket attacks, and other asymmetric operations primarily against Israeli military and civilian targets.[6] The group operates mainly from the Gaza Strip, where it ranks as the second-largest Palestinian armed faction after Hamas's Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades, but maintains cells in the West Bank and elsewhere.[6] PIJ, and by extension its brigades, receives funding, training, and weaponry from Iran, enabling sustained operations despite resource constraints.[37] The Al-Quds Brigades were established in 1992 amid escalating Palestinian militancy during the First Intifada, building on PIJ's foundational armed activities that trace back to the group's inception in 1981 under founder Fathi Shaqaqi.[38] Shaqaqi, a Gaza-born physician influenced by Egyptian Islamist thought, split from the Muslim Brotherhood to prioritize violent jihad over political engagement, forming PIJ in Gaza with initial support from Syria.[36] The brigades formalized PIJ's paramilitary structure, claiming responsibility for early attacks such as the first suicide bombing attributed to the group against an Israeli military target at the outset of the Second Intifada in 2000.[39] Over time, the organization has evolved into a decentralized network of local battalions, with operations coordinated from Gaza leadership councils.[6] Leadership of the Al-Quds Brigades falls under PIJ's political command, with military operations directed by field commanders who often operate pseudonymously to evade targeting. Ziyad al-Nakhalah has served as PIJ secretary-general since 2018, overseeing strategic decisions from exile in Damascus, Syria.[36] Notable past Gaza commanders include Baha Abu al-Ata, killed by Israeli forces in an airstrike on November 1, 2019, which PIJ described as retaliation for prior rocket barrages.[6] The group's spokesman, known as Abu Hamza, frequently issues statements claiming attacks and eulogizing fallen fighters.[6] Iranian Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps advisors have reportedly influenced tactical training, enhancing capabilities in rocket production and tunnel warfare.[40] The brigades' operations encompass suicide bombings, shootings, and indiscriminate rocket fire into Israel, often in coordination with Hamas or other factions during escalations.[36] Examples include joint rocket salvos during the 2014 Gaza conflict and multiple barrages in 2023 that prompted Israeli counterstrikes.[41] Since the 1990s, these activities have resulted in hundreds of Israeli casualties, with the group rejecting ceasefires that do not advance its ideological aims.[36] In the West Bank, smaller cells have conducted stabbing and shooting attacks, collaborating with groups like the al-Aqsa Martyrs' Brigades.[6] The Al-Quds Brigades, as part of PIJ, is designated a terrorist organization by the United States (Foreign Terrorist Organization since October 8, 1997), the European Union, Australia, Canada, and others, due to its deliberate targeting of civilians and rejection of peaceful resolutions. U.S. sanctions target PIJ leaders and financiers for enabling these acts, emphasizing the group's role in perpetuating violence over governance or diplomacy.[42] Despite such measures, Iranian backing sustains its arsenal, including short-range rockets and anti-tank weapons.[37]

Quds Force

The Quds Force is the external operations branch of Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), tasked with conducting unconventional warfare, military intelligence, and support for proxy militias abroad to advance Tehran's regional influence and export its Islamic Revolution.[43] It emerged in the 1980s during the Iran-Iraq War as the IRGC's expeditionary arm, expanding its role post-2003 in Iraq to arm Shiite insurgents against U.S. forces and after 2011 in Syria to bolster Bashar al-Assad's regime.[43] The force reports directly to Iran's Supreme Leader and operates with significant autonomy, providing training, funding, weapons, and logistical aid to groups including Hezbollah in Lebanon, Hamas in Gaza, Houthis in Yemen, and various Shiite militias in Iraq and Syria.[44] Leadership of the Quds Force has centered on key IRGC figures, with Qasem Soleimani serving as commander from the late 1990s until his death in a U.S. drone strike on January 3, 2020, alongside Iraqi militia leader Abu Mahdi al-Muhandis; Soleimani was succeeded by Brigadier General Esmail Qaani.[44] [43] Under these commanders, the force has directed operations such as the deployment of 20,000–30,000 regional proxies in Syria, the creation of 82 militia units totaling around 70,000 fighters there since 2011, and the arming of Iraqi groups responsible for roadside bomb attacks that killed 603 U.S. service members between 2003 and 2011.[44] [45] [46] Internally, the Quds Force is organized into specialized units, including Unit 190 for financing operations, Unit 400 for logistics, and Unit 1500 for counterintelligence, facilitating activities like militia recruitment, weapons smuggling via Syria to Lebanon, and irregular warfare along the Golan Heights.[45] Its operations have included supporting Hezbollah's arsenal buildup since the 1980s, enabling Houthi missile strikes on Saudi Arabia, and coordinating Iran's April 2024 drone and missile barrage against Israel.[44] [43] In Syria, commanders such as Khalil Zahedi have overseen efforts to protect Shi'a holy sites, establish a land corridor to the Mediterranean, and counter Israeli airstrikes, though the force has suffered setbacks including the loss of field commanders to targeted killings.[45] The United States designated the IRGC, including the Quds Force, as a Foreign Terrorist Organization on April 15, 2019, citing its institutional role in global terrorism, including the 1996 Khobar Towers bombing, foiled plots in Europe and the U.S., and proxy attacks killing U.S. personnel and civilians.[46] This designation highlights the Quds Force's foundational support for groups like Hezbollah and Hamas, which have conducted attacks on Israel and Western targets, as well as its orchestration of militia networks that have destabilized Iraq and Yemen.[46]

Educational Institutions

Al-Quds University

Al-Quds University (Arabic: جامعة القدس) is a public research university operating in the Jerusalem Governorate of Palestine, serving as the principal Palestinian institution of higher education in the Jerusalem area. Established in 1977 to fulfill a longstanding Arab initiative for an educational center in the city, it remains the only such university with a presence in Jerusalem proper. The main campus is located in Abu Dis, east of Jerusalem, with additional facilities in East Jerusalem suburbs including Sheikh Jarrah and Beit Hanina, and in al-Bireh near Ramallah. These sites accommodate a distributed model necessitated by regional geography and access constraints.[47][48] The university enrolls around 12,000 full-time students, comprising 10,623 undergraduates and 1,705 postgraduates, supported by over 700 faculty members. Undergraduates are 68.3% female, reflecting a notable gender imbalance in enrollment. It offers 55 undergraduate programs and 45 graduate programs across 15 faculties, spanning disciplines such as medicine, engineering and technology, law, business and economics, arts, and sciences. To date, Al-Quds has graduated 48,584 students and allocates $9.8 million annually in financial aid to 47% of its undergraduates, aiding accessibility amid economic pressures in the region.[47] Operations occur amid geopolitical tensions, with documented instances of campus disruptions, including evacuations of over 12,000 students in 2014 due to nearby military actions and periodic raids by Israeli forces citing security issues. Such events, reported primarily by Palestinian-affiliated sources, highlight vulnerabilities tied to the university's location in contested territories, though accounts vary by outlet and lack uniform international corroboration. The institution maintains partnerships and programs aimed at regional development, including community service initiatives, despite funding shortages exacerbated by movement restrictions.[49][50][51]

Political and Commemorative Events

Quds Day

Quds Day, also known as International Quds Day, is an annual event observed on the last Friday of Ramadan, designated by Iran's Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini on August 7, 1979, shortly after the Islamic Revolution, as a day of global Muslim solidarity against Israeli sovereignty over Jerusalem (Al-Quds in Arabic).[52][53] Khomeini framed it as a counter to perceived distortions of history by the "Zionist regime" and a call for Muslims to unite in support of Palestinian claims to the city, explicitly urging demonstrations to liberate Al-Quds from Israeli control.[54] The inaugural observance occurred in 1980, coinciding with the first Ramadan after its proclamation, and has since been institutionalized in Iran as a state-sponsored national holiday involving mass rallies in Tehran and other cities, often drawing hundreds of thousands of participants chanting anti-Israel slogans such as "Death to Israel" and burning Israeli flags.[1][55] The event's significance lies in its role as a platform for Iran's ideological opposition to Israel's existence, promoting the narrative of Jerusalem's occupation as a central injustice warranting armed resistance and the eventual elimination of the Israeli state, aligned with Khomeini's vision of exporting the Islamic Revolution.[52][56] It extends beyond Iran through affiliated groups like Hezbollah in Lebanon and Hamas supporters, with rallies held in countries including Iraq, Yemen, Pakistan, and Western cities such as London, where demonstrations have persisted for over 40 years despite counter-protests.[57][58] In 2024, for instance, events on April 5 drew global attention amid ongoing Israel-Hamas conflict, with Iranian state media reporting widespread participation emphasizing resistance to Israeli actions in Gaza.[1] Organizers, including Iran's government, position it as a symbol of unity against oppression, but participation has varied; in Iran, attendance has reportedly declined in recent years due to domestic economic concerns overshadowing foreign policy rallies.[59] Critics, including organizations tracking extremism, describe Quds Day as a mechanism for fostering antisemitism and radicalization, normalizing calls for violence against Jews and Israel while serving Iran's strategic interests in undermining Western alliances and supporting proxy militias.[56][55] Events have occasionally been repurposed for intra-Muslim dissent, such as 2009 Iranian protests against election fraud during Quds Day marches, highlighting tensions between its revolutionary intent and local grievances.[1] Despite official promotion, empirical indicators like reduced turnout in Tehran suggest waning public enthusiasm in Iran, where state coercion plays a role in mobilization.[59]

International Organizations and Initiatives

Al-Quds Committee

The Al-Quds Committee is a permanent standing committee of the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC), established in July 1975 pursuant to Resolution No. 1/6-P adopted by the 6th Islamic Conference of Foreign Ministers.[60][61] It operates as an Arab-Islamic institution dedicated to addressing issues in Jerusalem (Al-Quds), including the preservation of its religious, cultural, and humanitarian heritage amid the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.[61] The committee's primary mandate involves monitoring Israeli occupation activities, such as settlement expansion, Judaization policies, and forced displacement, while formulating political, diplomatic, and practical responses to protect the city's status and Islamic sanctities, particularly Al-Aqsa Mosque.[60][62] Its efforts encompass two main tracks: diplomatic advocacy through OIC coordination and resolutions urging member states to influence international bodies, and on-the-ground initiatives via the Bayt Mal Al-Quds Asharif Agency, established in 1998 as its executive arm to fund restoration projects, support Palestinian residents, and counter urban changes altering the city's Arab-Islamic character.[61][63] Chairmanship of the committee has been held by the King of Morocco since its inception, beginning with King Hassan II and continuing under King Mohammed VI, who oversees sessions emphasizing unified OIC action against perceived threats to Jerusalem's legal and historical status.[64] Recent OIC meetings, including those in 2025, have reaffirmed its centrality in confronting policies aimed at demographic shifts or site alterations in Al-Quds, with endorsements from Arab-Islamic summits for Morocco's leading role.[65][66] The committee convenes periodically, as in its twentieth session documented in 2019, to review developments and mobilize resources, though its effectiveness is constrained by reliance on OIC member contributions and diplomatic leverage rather than enforceable mechanisms.[67]

COMCEC Al-Quds Program

The COMCEC Al-Quds Program is a technical and financial assistance initiative launched in 2020 by the Standing Committee for Economic and Commercial Cooperation (COMCEC) of the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC), aimed at bolstering Palestinian efforts in the cultural and tourism sectors of Al-Quds (Jerusalem).[68] It serves as an expression of solidarity with Palestinians amid ongoing disputes over the city's status, focusing on areas such as heritage preservation and community development.[69] The program's primary objectives include aiding restoration projects for historical sites, enhancing civil society participation, and reinforcing the Palestinian-Arab and Islamic identity of Al-Quds.[68] By 2025, it had funded at least eight projects, including youth empowerment initiatives like "Al-Quds Youth," which provides training in career awareness and negotiation skills to young Palestinians, conducted through partners such as ACT for Conflict Resolution.[70][71] Other efforts encompass cultural programs, such as "The Artists' Identity Path: Tracing Heritage in Al-Quds" and "Jerusalemites: Because We Are Here," designed to promote local artistic expression and historical awareness.[72] Implementation occurs via COMCEC's project funding mechanism, with sessions integrated into broader OIC economic forums, such as the 41st Follow-Up Committee Meeting in May 2025, where progress on Al-Quds-related activities was reviewed.[73] These initiatives prioritize tangible support over political advocacy, though they align with OIC resolutions emphasizing Palestinian sovereignty claims in the region.[74]
