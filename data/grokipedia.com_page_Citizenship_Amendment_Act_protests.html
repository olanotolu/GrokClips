Citizenship Amendment Act protests

The Citizenship Amendment Act protests consisted of mass demonstrations across India from December 2019 through early 2020, triggered by the enactment of the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019, which modified the Citizenship Act of 1955 to grant expedited citizenship to non-Muslim immigrants—specifically Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians—from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India on or before December 31, 2014, aiming to provide refuge to religious minorities persecuted in those Muslim-majority nations.[1][2][3] Opposition arose primarily from concerns that the religion-based criteria violated India's secular constitution and, when paired with the proposed National Register of Citizens, could systematically disenfranchise Muslims, though the government asserted the law targeted only illegal migrants from specified countries and preserved citizenship for all Indians regardless of faith.[4][5] The movement originated in Assam on December 4, 2019, amid fears of demographic shifts from Bengali-speaking migrants, before spreading to major cities with nonviolent sit-ins such as the 101-day Shaheen Bagh blockade in Delhi, student-led actions at universities like Jamia Millia Islamia, and nationwide strikes, but devolved into violence in states including Uttar Pradesh and Uttar Pradesh, where arson, stone-pelting, and police responses resulted in at least 25 deaths in Uttar Pradesh alone and over 50 nationwide by early 2020.[5][6][7] Clashes peaked with the February 2020 Delhi riots, linked to escalating tensions around the protests, claiming 53 lives mostly in communal violence, underscoring deep societal divisions over immigration, identity, and secularism.[8][9] Authorities restored order through deployments and court interventions, clearing sites like Shaheen Bagh in March 2020 amid the COVID-19 lockdown, while the Supreme Court deferred CAA implementation amid over 200 petitions; rules were finally notified in March 2024, eliciting subdued responses compared to the initial uproar.[8][10]

Background

Provisions of the Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019

The Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019, amends the Citizenship Act, 1955, by inserting a proviso to section 2(1)(b), which defines illegal migrants as foreigners entering India without valid documents or overstaying permitted periods.[11] This proviso exempts from the illegal migrant classification any person belonging to the Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, or Christian communities from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, or Pakistan who entered India on or before December 31, 2014, provided they have not been exempted under specific passport or foreigners laws but are otherwise covered by the exemption.[12][13] Under amended section 3, such exempted individuals are eligible for registration as Indian citizens, bypassing standard naturalization requirements related to illegal entry status.[11] Section 5 is also amended to deem citizens of the specified countries from these communities as Indian citizens if they entered India as minors before the cutoff date and resided continuously for six months, with parental eligibility extending the provision.[12] For naturalization under section 6(1), the aggregate residency period is reduced from eleven years to five years (including one year of immediate prior residence), excluding any period of illegal stay from the calculation for these applicants.[11][13] The Act explicitly excludes its application from the tribal areas of Assam, Meghalaya, Mizoram, and Tripura as specified in the Sixth Schedule to the Constitution, as well as areas in those states and Nagaland where the Inner Line Permit regime applies under the Bengal Eastern Frontier Regulations, 1873.[11] Additionally, it inserts section 7B into the 1955 Act, allowing cancellation of Overseas Citizen of India (OCI) cardholder registration for violations of any Indian law notified by the central government, expanding grounds beyond previous provisions for fraud, disloyalty, or imprisonment exceeding two years.[12] The amendments took effect on January 10, 2020, following presidential assent on December 12, 2019.[13]

Rationale and empirical basis for CAA

The Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019 (CAA) amends the Citizenship Act, 1955, to grant Indian citizenship on an expedited basis to Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian migrants from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India on or before December 31, 2014, provided they were not granted citizenship in any other country and faced religious persecution in their origin states.[14] The Indian government's stated rationale is to extend refuge to these non-Muslim minorities, who constitute vulnerable groups in neighboring Islamic republics where state policies and societal pressures rooted in Islamic governance systematically target them for discrimination, violence, and marginalization, contrasting with protections unavailable to Muslim migrants presumed not to face equivalent religious persecution as the demographic majority.[15] This approach aligns with India's post-Partition historical role in absorbing displaced non-Muslims, reflecting a targeted humanitarian response rather than blanket immigration policy, as the Act reduces the residency requirement for naturalization from 11 years to 5 for qualifying individuals while excluding economic or undocumented entrants irrespective of religion. Empirical evidence supporting the persecution basis includes stark demographic declines among these minorities, driven by documented patterns of forced conversions, temple desecrations, blasphemy accusations, and mob violence. In Bangladesh, the Hindu population share fell from 22% in 1951 to 8.5% by 2011, with census data and migration studies attributing over 90% of the absolute decline—approximately 12 million individuals—to emigration amid recurrent anti-Hindu riots, land grabs under the Enemy Property Act (rebranded as Vested Property Act), and targeted killings, as reported in post-independence pogroms like those in 1964 and 1971.[16] In Pakistan, Hindus declined from 14.6% of the population in 1941 to 1.6% by 2017, correlating with blasphemy laws disproportionately enforced against minorities (over 1,500 cases since 1987, with Hindus comprising a significant share despite their small numbers), forced marriages of Hindu girls (estimated 1,000 annually), and systemic discrimination under Sharia-influenced statutes.[17] Afghanistan's Hindu and Sikh communities, numbering around 200,000 in the 1970s, dwindled to fewer than 1,000 by 2023 following Taliban resurgence, with U.S. State Department reports documenting executions, property seizures, and fatwas mandating conversion or exile under de facto Sharia rule.[18] These trends are corroborated by international assessments of religious freedom, including U.S. Commission on International Religious Freedom designations of Pakistan and Afghanistan as "Countries of Particular Concern" for severe violations against non-Muslims, such as kidnappings and honor killings in Pakistan (over 300 Hindu and Christian girls affected yearly) and the near-eradication of non-Muslim practices in Afghanistan post-2021. India's policy thus addresses causal factors of state-sponsored or tolerated Islamism—evident in constitutional Islamization in Pakistan (Article 2 declaring Islam the state religion) and Bangladesh's historical alignment with Islamist parties—without extending to Muslim migrants, whose influx (e.g., over 4 million excluded in Assam's 2019 National Register of Citizens, predominantly Muslim Bangladeshis) is largely economic or infiltrative rather than persecution-driven, as no parallel population collapse occurs for Muslims in these contexts.[19] This distinction underscores the Act's empirical grounding in verifiable refugee flows tied to religious causality, fulfilling a first-order obligation to verifiable victims over undifferentiated asylum claims.

Context of illegal immigration and NRC

India has faced substantial inflows of illegal immigrants from Bangladesh since the 1950s, with acceleration following the 1971 Bangladesh Liberation War, leading to demographic pressures particularly in border states like Assam.[20] Estimates of the total number of illegal Bangladeshi migrants in India vary widely due to the clandestine nature of entries, but government assessments have cited figures around 20 million, contributing to security, economic, and cultural strains.[21] In Assam, this migration has altered population composition, with the Muslim share rising from 24.68% in 1951 to 34.22% in 2011 per census data, fueling local anxieties over resource competition, unemployment, and loss of indigenous identity.[22] These dynamics precipitated the Assam Movement (1979–1985), a mass agitation by the All Assam Students' Union demanding detection, disenfranchisement, and deportation of post-1966 immigrants, which culminated in the 1985 Assam Accord setting March 25, 1971, as the citizenship cut-off date for those entering without valid documents.[23] The National Register of Citizens (NRC) in Assam served as the primary mechanism to operationalize the Accord by verifying residents' citizenship status against the 1951 NRC and pre-1971 electoral rolls.[24] Launched in 2013 under Supreme Court supervision, the process required applicants to prove ancestry or arrival before the cut-off, aiming to exclude illegal entrants amid ongoing border incursions—Indian authorities apprehended thousands annually along the Indo-Bangladesh frontier, with over 2,000 pushed back in early 2025 alone.[25] The final NRC draft published on August 31, 2019, included 31.1 million people while excluding approximately 1.9 million from 33 million applicants, reflecting challenges in documentation rather than targeting any single group.[26] Subsequent disclosures indicated that among exclusions, around 700,000 were Muslims and 500,000 Bengali Hindus, underscoring that Hindus faced significant denials despite later CAA provisions.[27] Illegal immigration's broader repercussions included heightened insurgency risks, as unchecked inflows enabled external influences and communal tensions, exemplified by the 1983 Nellie massacre where over 2,000 were killed amid fears of migrant dominance.[23][22] Proposals for a nationwide NRC gained traction post-Assam, driven by persistent infiltration—Ministry of Home Affairs data show continuous detections, with over 14,000 Bangladeshi nationals deported since 2019—yet implementation stalled amid concerns over administrative feasibility and potential statelessness without safeguards like CAA for persecuted non-Muslims.[28] In Assam, NRC exclusions triggered Foreigners Tribunals for appeals, but deportation remained limited due to Bangladesh's refusal to accept undocumented returns, leaving many in limbo and highlighting enforcement gaps.[29] This context framed anti-CAA protests, where critics conflated NRC's citizen verification with discriminatory intent, despite empirical evidence of multi-faith exclusions and the immigration crisis's root causes in porous borders and economic pull factors.

Chronology of Anti-CAA Protests

December 2019 outbreak

Protests against the Citizenship Amendment Act erupted in India's northeastern states immediately following its passage by Parliament on December 11, 2019, with Assam serving as the epicenter due to longstanding anxieties over illegal immigration from Bangladesh altering the region's demographic balance.[9] Demonstrators, including members of indigenous groups and student organizations, contended that the law would undermine the National Register of Citizens (NRC) exercise in Assam, which had identified nearly 1.9 million individuals as potential non-citizens in August 2019, by granting a pathway to citizenship for non-Muslim migrants who entered before December 31, 2014.[30] On December 10, as the bill cleared the Lok Sabha just after midnight, an 11-hour shutdown was observed across the Northeast, leading to clashes in Guwahati where police used batons and tear gas to disperse crowds burning tires and blocking roads.[31][30] Violence escalated in Assam over the ensuing days, with security forces firing on protesters in multiple locations including Guwahati, Tezpur, and Dibrugarh. By December 16, at least four individuals had been killed in police firing during confrontations that involved arson and attacks on government property, prompting the arrest of 175 people and detention of over 1,400 others.[32] Overall, five deaths were reported in Assam from such incidents between December 10 and 16, alongside the imposition of curfews and deployment of the army in sensitive areas to restore order.[33] Protesters in the region emphasized protecting indigenous culture and land rights, viewing the CAA as a concession to Bengali-speaking Hindu migrants excluded from the NRC, rather than solely a religious discrimination issue.[34] By mid-December, the unrest spread to university campuses in northern India, particularly Aligarh Muslim University (AMU) and Jamia Millia Islamia in New Delhi. On December 15, over 2,000 Jamia students marched peacefully against the CAA but faced a heavy police response, including entry into the campus where tear gas, rubber bullets, and stun grenades were used, injuring more than 100 students and leading to dozens of arrests.[35] Similar police action occurred at AMU on the same day, with forces firing tear gas and rubber bullets inside the premises amid protests that had begun earlier in the week.[36] These campus clashes, captured in viral videos, drew national attention and solidarity from other institutions, framing the opposition as a defense of secularism and constitutional values against perceived religious favoritism in the law.[37]

January 2020 escalation

On January 5, 2020, a group of over 50 masked individuals armed with rods, sticks, and possibly acid entered the Jawaharlal Nehru University (JNU) campus in New Delhi and assaulted students and faculty during a student union meeting opposing the CAA, NRC, and a proposed fee hike. Approximately 30 people were injured, including severe head injuries requiring hospitalization.[38] The attackers were filmed vandalizing property and chanting slogans, prompting immediate allegations from students and left-leaning groups that the assailants were affiliated with the Akhil Bharatiya Vidyarthi Parishad (ABVP), the student wing of the BJP; however, Delhi police investigations later identified internal student clashes between leftist and right-wing groups as a factor, with arrests made primarily of students from the protesting side.[38] [39] This incident triggered solidarity protests at over 20 universities across India, including Jamia Millia Islamia, Aligarh Muslim University, and IITs, amplifying the anti-CAA movement and drawing criticism of government inaction on campus safety.[38] The Shaheen Bagh sit-in, initiated by Muslim women on December 15, 2019, to block a major arterial road in southeast Delhi, intensified in January as it entered its second month, symbolizing non-violent resistance and attracting global media coverage. Protesters, predominantly women and elderly "dadis" (grandmothers), maintained round-the-clock vigils with constitutional recitations and cultural performances, despite harsh winter conditions and traffic disruptions affecting thousands daily.[40] Authorities attempted negotiations to clear the site, but demonstrators refused to vacate until the CAA's repeal, framing the law as discriminatory against Muslims when paired with NRC.[41] The protest inspired similar women-led occupations in Lucknow, Kolkata, and other cities, shifting the movement's image toward peaceful, inclusive civil disobedience.[42] On January 10, 2020, the Ministry of Home Affairs notified the CAA rules, bringing the act into force and enabling fast-track citizenship applications for eligible non-Muslim migrants from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India before December 31, 2014.[43] This implementation fueled immediate backlash, with protests erupting in southern states like Kerala and Tamil Nadu, where tens of thousands marched on January 4 demanding withdrawal, citing fears of exclusionary policies.[44] In Assam, the All Assam Students' Union (AASU) planned demonstrations against Prime Minister Modi's visit on January 7, reflecting Northeast concerns over demographic changes despite CAA exemptions for tribal areas.[45] Throughout January, protests saw sporadic violence and police interventions, including lathi charges and tear gas in Uttar Pradesh and Delhi, with reports of over 1,000 arrests nationwide by mid-month.[9] On January 26, Republic Day, hundreds of thousands participated in marches across cities like Mumbai, Hyderabad, and Kolkata, waving tricolors and chanting against the law's perceived unconstitutionality, though some events faced counter-mobilization and isolated clashes.[46] Cumulative deaths from protest-related violence reached dozens by month's end, primarily from earlier December unrest but with ongoing tensions in Muslim-majority areas.[9] The Supreme Court began hearing challenges to the CAA, scheduling further arguments for January 22 without granting interim stays.[9]

February-March 2020 peak and Delhi riots

Protests against the Citizenship Amendment Act reached their most intense phase in February 2020, marked by widespread road blockades and clashes between demonstrators and counter-protesters. In Delhi, anti-CAA activists established new sit-in sites, including at Jaffrabad on February 22, mirroring the Shaheen Bagh model by blocking a major highway, which prompted local residents and pro-CAA groups to demand clearance due to disruptions in daily life and commerce.[47][48] This escalation intertwined with ongoing university mobilizations, where student groups from institutions like Jawaharlal Nehru University and Jamia Millia Islamia continued anti-CAA campaigns, often facing accusations of leftist and Islamist influence in organizing violent disruptions.[49] The peak violence erupted into the 2020 Delhi riots from February 23 to 26, triggered by confrontations in northeast Delhi neighborhoods such as Jaffrabad, Maujpur, and Shiv Vihar. Initial clashes occurred when pro-CAA demonstrators, including Hindu nationalist groups, marched against the blockades, leading to stone-throwing and arson; reports indicate that anti-CAA protesters, predominantly from Muslim communities fearing disenfranchisement under a potential National Register of Citizens, initiated attacks with petrol bombs and firearms targeting opponents and police.[50][51] The riots spread rapidly, resulting in 53 deaths—approximately 40 Muslims and 13 Hindus—over 700 injuries, and extensive property damage, including the burning of shops, vehicles, and religious sites, with initial violence disproportionately directed against Hindu properties and individuals before retaliatory actions.[52][53] Police investigations later uncovered evidence of premeditation, including conspiracy charges against anti-CAA organizers like student activist Umar Khalid and AAP councilor Tahir Hussain, who allegedly coordinated from rooftops to incite mobs using weapons and incendiaries.[54] Causal analysis from ground reports and charge sheets highlights how radicalized elements within the anti-CAA movement, fueled by narratives equating the law with anti-Muslim persecution, exploited protest sites for mobilization, leading to one-sided aggression against perceived CAA supporters.[51][54] Delhi Police filed over 758 FIRs, with convictions in several cases affirming rioters' roles in targeted communal attacks, though acquittals in others stemmed from evidentiary issues rather than exoneration of broader instigation patterns.[55] By March, the protest momentum waned amid the COVID-19 lockdown, with the Shaheen Bagh sit-in dismantled on March 23 by authorities citing health risks, effectively curtailing the peak phase.[56] This period underscored the protests' shift from peaceful dissent to violent confrontation, exacerbating communal tensions without altering the CAA's implementation trajectory.[57]

Postponement and lull (2020-2023)

The nationwide protests against the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) began to subside following the Delhi riots in February 2020, with the COVID-19 pandemic accelerating the decline. India's first confirmed case appeared on January 30, 2020, but the situation escalated rapidly by March, prompting Prime Minister Narendra Modi to announce a 21-day nationwide lockdown starting March 25, 2020, which restricted public gatherings and effectively halted large-scale demonstrations.[58][59] This lockdown, extended multiple times until May 2020, shifted public focus to health and economic survival, diminishing the momentum of anti-CAA mobilizations that had persisted in sites like Shaheen Bagh until early March.[60] The government's delay in notifying the CAA rules further contributed to the postponement of implementation, preventing the Act from becoming operational despite its passage in December 2019. Rules were not framed until March 11, 2024, a delay attributed to the scale of initial protests and the disruptions from the pandemic, which included over 1,500 days of inaction by the Ministry of Home Affairs.[61][62] During 2020-2023, no nationwide NRC update was pursued, and the absence of rules meant no applications for fast-tracked citizenship under CAA were processed, effectively suspending the policy's effects amid ongoing legal challenges in the Supreme Court.[63] Sporadic opposition activities occurred, such as small demonstrations by parties like Congress in 2021 demanding repeal, but these lacked the mass participation of earlier phases and faced restrictions under pandemic protocols.[63] Arrests of alleged protest organizers continued into the lockdown period, with Delhi Police charging over 700 individuals linked to earlier events, though street protests remained minimal due to enforcement of social distancing and sedition cases.[60] This period marked a strategic lull for both protesters, constrained by health crises, and the government, which avoided reigniting tensions while preparing for eventual rollout.[64]

2024 implementation and sporadic renewals

On March 11, 2024, the Ministry of Home Affairs notified the Citizenship (Amendment) Rules, 2024, operationalizing the Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019, after a delay of more than four years since its parliamentary passage on December 11, 2019.[65][2] The rules established procedures for eligible non-Muslim migrants from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan—specifically Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians who entered India on or before December 31, 2014—to apply for citizenship, requiring documentation such as proof of entry date and religious persecution.[66] This implementation addressed a backlog of approximately 31,000 applications from qualifying individuals already processed under prior discretionary provisions.[67] The notification prompted sporadic protests in select locations, including student demonstrations at Jamia Millia Islamia University in New Delhi, where police used tear gas to disperse crowds chanting against the law on March 12, 2024.[68] In Assam, regional groups organized strikes and rallies, citing fears of demographic changes due to Bengali-origin migrants, with heightened security deployed to contain gatherings.[69] Similar isolated actions occurred in Kerala and West Bengal, led by opposition parties and Muslim organizations alleging religious discrimination, though these lacked the nationwide coordination and intensity of the 2019-2020 mobilizations.[70] No large-scale violence or road blockades materialized, and protests subsided within days amid government assurances that the law targeted only foreign persecuted minorities without impacting Indian citizens.[71] International observers, including Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International, condemned the rules as undermining secular principles by excluding Muslims, claims echoed in protests but contested by supporters emphasizing the Act's focus on verifiable persecution from theocratic regimes rather than domestic citizenship revocation.[17][72] Through late 2024, no further significant renewals of protest activity were reported, with implementation proceeding via an online portal handling initial applications quietly.[14] In September 2025, the government extended procedural deadlines to December 31, 2024, to facilitate more applications from eligible minorities, but this adjustment did not trigger notable public opposition.[73]

2025 developments

In May 2025, the Indian government granted the first set of citizenship certificates under the Citizenship Amendment Act to qualifying non-Muslim migrants from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who had entered India before December 31, 2014.[74] This step advanced the law's operationalization, following the notification of implementation rules in March 2024, amid ongoing legal challenges in the Supreme Court regarding its compatibility with constitutional equality provisions. On September 3, 2025, the Assam Jatiya Parishad, a regional political outfit advocating for Assamese indigenous interests, staged protests in Assam against the central government's extension of the CAA application deadline.[75] Demonstrators contended that the extension undermined efforts to protect the state's demographic balance against influxes from Bangladesh, accusing the Bharatiya Janata Party-led administration of subordinating native concerns to electoral calculations in other regions. The action highlighted persistent Northeast opposition to the Act's provisions, which protesters viewed as diluting safeguards under the Assam Accord of 1985 limiting citizenship to those residing before March 24, 1971. These events represented limited, region-specific mobilization rather than the nationwide upheaval seen in prior years, with no reports of widespread violence or blockades. Legal proceedings involving anti-CAA activists from earlier phases, such as Sharjeel Imam's ongoing incarceration since 2020 on sedition charges related to protest speeches, continued to draw commentary but did not spur fresh demonstrations.[76]

Pro-CAA Counter-Mobilization

Domestic rallies and campaigns

The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) launched domestic counter-mobilization efforts shortly after the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) passed in December 2019, organizing rallies and awareness campaigns to defend the law's aim of expediting citizenship for persecuted non-Muslim minorities from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan while refuting claims of discrimination. These activities sought to engage supporters, clarify provisions excluding Muslims from eligibility, and contrast the Act with opposition narratives linking it to a nationwide National Register of Citizens (NRC).[77] On December 30, 2019, the BJP's Karimnagar district unit in Telangana held a large procession supporting both the CAA and NRC, with participants marching to affirm the measures' role in safeguarding indigenous populations from illegal immigration.[78] From January 5 to 15, 2020, BJP president Amit Shah spearheaded a nationwide door-to-door campaign targeting 30 million families to distribute literature explaining the CAA's humanitarian intent and counter misinformation. Shah personally canvassed homes in New Delhi's Lajpat Nagar neighborhood, emphasizing that the law provided refuge without revoking any Indian citizenship.[77] Public rallies reinforced these messages, including Shah's January 21 address in Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh, where he declared the CAA irreversible amid protests and urged public discourse on its merits.[79] A BJP-led pro-CAA rally in Delhi on January 27, 2020, attended by supporters including minister Anurag Thakur, highlighted solidarity but drew scrutiny for Thakur's call to "shoot the traitors" targeting anti-CAA demonstrators.[80] Similar demonstrations occurred in Madhya Pradesh, Jharkhand, and Maharashtra, where BJP affiliates rallied to frame the CAA as protective of Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian refugees fleeing religious persecution, thereby aiding the party's electoral positioning in contested areas.[80] After CAA rules were notified on March 11, 2024, BJP intensified application-assistance campaigns in immigrant-heavy regions. In West Bengal, the party organized over 1,000 camps starting late October 2024, primarily in Bangladesh-border districts like Malda and Murshidabad, to help eligible Hindu refugees file claims and leverage the law for outreach to Hindu voters ahead of 2026 state polls.[81] These efforts, coordinated with affiliates, processed initial applications from communities citing post-partition and recent migrations.[82]

Government and BJP responses

The Indian government, led by Prime Minister Narendra Modi and Home Minister Amit Shah, consistently defended the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) as a humanitarian measure to grant citizenship to persecuted religious minorities—Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians—from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India before December 31, 2014, without revoking citizenship from any Indian nationals.[83] In parliamentary debates following the Act's passage on December 11, 2019, Shah emphasized that the legislation addressed historical injustices faced by these groups due to religious persecution in Muslim-majority neighboring countries, reducing the residency requirement for naturalization from 11 years to 5 years for eligible applicants.[84] The government rejected opposition claims that the CAA discriminated against Muslims, asserting it was not linked to the National Register of Citizens (NRC) and contained no provisions to strip citizenship from any community.[85] In response to widespread protests erupting in December 2019, Modi addressed the Rajya Sabha on February 6, 2020, refuting allegations of unconstitutionality and linking the CAA to the visions of India's independence leaders, including Mahatma Gandhi and Sardar Patel, who advocated sheltering partitioned minorities.[83] He criticized opposition parties for spreading misinformation and inciting violence, noting that the Act fulfilled long-standing promises to protect refugees while maintaining India's secular framework.[2] Shah echoed this in multiple statements, declaring on March 14, 2024—after notifying implementation rules—that the CAA was a sovereign right and would never be repealed, with 85% of potential applicants already possessing required documents like passports or voter IDs.[86][87] The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) mobilized counter-campaigns, including rallies and public outreach to clarify the Act's scope. On December 30, 2019, BJP units in Telangana organized processions supporting CAA and NRC, framing them as tools to safeguard indigenous populations from illegal immigration.[78] Amid violence during protests, such as the February 2020 Delhi riots, Shah announced on March 12, 2020, that special investigation teams (SITs) under senior officers would probe 50 serious cases, vowing no leniency for perpetrators regardless of affiliation and crediting police for containing damage to 4% of Delhi's area.[4] The government notified CAA rules on March 11, 2024, via the Ministry of Home Affairs, establishing an online portal for applications despite ongoing legal challenges and sporadic protests, prioritizing retrospective citizenship grants from December 31, 2014.[65][14]

Overseas support

Members of the Indian diaspora in the United States organized several rallies in December 2019 to express support for the Citizenship Amendment Act, framing it as a humanitarian measure to aid persecuted religious minorities from neighboring countries.[88] On December 20, a gathering occurred in front of the Indian Consulate in Houston, Texas, drawing significant participation from community groups emphasizing the Act's role in providing refuge without affecting Indian citizens' rights.[88] Additional events followed in cities including Seattle on December 21, Austin and Dublin, Ohio, on December 22, and Raleigh, North Carolina, on December 22, where over 75 doctors and leaders attended to counter perceived misinformation about the legislation.[88] In New York, supporters held demonstrations at Times Square on December 29, organized by the Overseas Friends of BJP-USA, with participants displaying placards stating "CAA is about human rights" and "NRIs support CAA," alongside chants endorsing Prime Minister Narendra Modi.[89] A related event took place in Long Island on December 28, led by the American India Public Affairs Committee, where organizers described the Act as a "historic decision" and act of humanitarianism.[89] These gatherings, involving groups like the Friends of India Society International, aimed to highlight the Act's focus on non-Muslim refugees from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan, while planning further events in cities such as Dallas, Chicago, and San Francisco.[88] Advocacy organizations provided ongoing backing, with the Hindu American Foundation issuing statements in 2019 and 2020 asserting that the CAA addressed long-standing persecution of minorities without altering citizenship criteria for India's Muslim population or imposing a National Register of Citizens.[90] The group submitted testimony to the U.S. Commission on International Religious Freedom, criticizing narratives of discrimination and emphasizing the Act's necessity for refugees facing religious violence.[91] In March 2024, following notification of the CAA rules, the Foundation and other Indian-American bodies reiterated approval, calling it "long overdue" for expediting citizenship for eligible persecuted groups.[92][93] Comparable organized pro-CAA demonstrations were less documented in the United Kingdom, Canada, or Australia during the peak protest period, where Indian diaspora activities abroad more prominently featured opposition events.[89] U.S.-based efforts thus represented the primary locus of overseas counter-mobilization, coordinated through diaspora networks aligned with the Act's proponents.[88]

Locations and Regional Dynamics

Northeast India (Assam focus)

Protests against the Citizenship (Amendment) Act (CAA) in Northeast India, with a primary focus on Assam, began on December 4, 2019, immediately following the bill's introduction in the Lok Sabha, reflecting longstanding regional anxieties over illegal immigration from Bangladesh and its impact on indigenous populations. Organizations such as the All Assam Students' Union (AASU) and the Asom Jatiyatabadi Yuba Chatra Parishad (AJYCP) spearheaded the opposition, arguing that the CAA contravened the 1985 Assam Accord, which aimed to detect and deport post-1971 illegal migrants, and threatened the cultural, linguistic, and demographic integrity of Assamese society by potentially granting citizenship to non-Muslim migrants from neighboring countries.[57][94] Unlike protests elsewhere emphasizing religious discrimination, those in Assam united Hindus, Muslims, and tribal groups against perceived threats to native land rights and resources, exacerbated by the recent National Register of Citizens (NRC) update that excluded nearly 1.9 million residents.[95] The agitation intensified with statewide bandhs (shutdowns) and mass rallies, including an 11-hour regional shutdown on December 10, 2019, which saw hundreds blocking streets in Guwahati and other cities. Violence erupted on December 10-13, 2019, as protesters clashed with police, leading to arson of vehicles and government buildings; authorities responded with curfews, internet suspensions in multiple districts, and deployment of the army. Five individuals—Sam Stafford, Dipanjal Das, Ishwar Nayak, Abdul Alim, and Dwijendra Panging—were killed, primarily in police firing during demonstrations in Guwahati and nearby areas like Sribhumi and Lakhipur.[31][30][96] While the CAA exempted tribal autonomous districts under the Sixth Schedule, protesters demanded a blanket exclusion for the entire Northeast to prevent any migrant influx, viewing the law as incompatible with Article 5 of the Indian Constitution and regional safeguards. In neighboring states like Tripura and Meghalaya, similar demonstrations occurred, but Assam witnessed the most sustained mobilization, with AASU coordinating torchlight marches and cultural events to sustain pressure. The unrest subsided after Supreme Court interventions and assurances of review, though underlying fears persisted, resurfacing in limited protests following the CAA rules' notification in 2024.[97][98]

Uttar Pradesh

Protests against the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) in Uttar Pradesh began in mid-December 2019, shortly after the bill's passage by Parliament on December 11 and its assent by President Ram Nath Kovind on December 12.[9] Demonstrations initially occurred in university campuses, such as Aligarh Muslim University (AMU), where students held marches and sit-ins opposing the law's exclusion of Muslims from fast-track citizenship for certain refugees.[99] By December 15, protests spread to cities including Lucknow, Kanpur, and Prayagraj, with participants blocking roads and chanting slogans against the CAA and the proposed National Register of Citizens (NRC).[100] Violence escalated on December 19–21, 2019, in multiple districts with significant Muslim populations, including Sambhal, Moradabad, Meerut, Firozabad, and Kanpur.[101] In Sambhal, clashes began on December 19 when protesters attempted to march toward a temple, leading to stone-pelting, arson of vehicles and shops, and police deployment of tear gas and plastic bullets; two individuals died from gunshot wounds the following day.[102] Meerut saw intense rioting on December 20, with reports of mobs setting fire to a police post and vehicles, prompting police firing that killed at least five Muslim men, according to families and eyewitness accounts; no FIRs were filed against officers a year later.[103] Firozabad recorded six deaths from police action during similar unrest, including an 8-year-old boy.[100] Uttar Pradesh police reported 19 total deaths across the state since December 10, attributing them to riot-related injuries and firing in self-defense against mobs armed with stones, petrol bombs, and firearms.[101] Independent investigations and rights groups, however, documented 23 deaths, nearly all Muslims, with 22 resulting from police bullets, highlighting patterns of disproportionate force in Muslim-majority areas.[104] [105] Law enforcement under Chief Minister Yogi Adityanath's administration responded aggressively, arresting 1,113 individuals and placing 5,558 in preventive detention by late December.[101] Authorities demolished or issued notices for damages to over 200 properties linked to alleged rioters, recovering approximately INR 45 million (USD 630,000) in claims for public losses from arson and vandalism.[106] Critics, including human rights organizations, alleged targeted demolitions of Muslim-owned structures and denial of medical aid to injured protesters, while government officials maintained that many "protesters" were infiltrators inciting violence to destabilize the BJP-ruled state.[49] [105] Protests subsided by early 2020 amid curfews and internet shutdowns in affected districts, though sporadic arrests continued into 2020, including high-profile cases like that of activist Kafeel Khan, detained under the National Security Act for inflammatory speeches.[107] The Uttar Pradesh dynamics reflected broader tensions in a state with a 19% Muslim population, where opposition parties like the Samajwadi Party framed the CAA as discriminatory, fueling localized mobilizations that often transitioned from peaceful assemblies to riots involving property destruction estimated in hundreds of crores.[99]

Delhi (Shaheen Bagh and riots)

The Shaheen Bagh sit-in began on December 15, 2019, in a Muslim-majority neighborhood of southeast Delhi, following clashes between police and students at nearby Jamia Millia Islamia university protesting the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA). Primarily organized by local women, the demonstration involved blocking both carriageways of a key stretch of National Highway 24 (Mathura Road), a vital artery connecting Delhi to Noida and Faridabad, with tents, barricades, and protest stages. Participants, numbering in the thousands at peak, demanded repeal of the CAA and opposition to a nationwide National Register of Citizens (NRC), arguing the laws discriminated against Muslims by excluding them from fast-tracked citizenship for non-Muslim refugees from neighboring countries.[108][109][110] The blockade persisted for 101 days until March 24, 2020, when authorities cleared the site citing COVID-19 lockdown enforcement, despite initial reluctance due to the site's designation as a public space. It caused severe traffic congestion, diverting thousands of daily commuters and affecting access for over 500,000 residents in adjoining areas, including emergencies like ambulances delayed by up to hours. Local Hindu and non-protester residents petitioned courts for relief, highlighting economic losses from disrupted commutes and businesses, while the Supreme Court in February 2020 appointed negotiators to urge de-escalation, later ruling in October that indefinite occupation of public roads violated others' rights to movement under Article 19(1)(d) of the Constitution.[111][112][113] Tensions from Shaheen Bagh and related CAA demonstrations escalated into riots in northeast Delhi from February 23 to 26, 2020, amid clashes between pro-CAA and anti-CAA groups in areas like Jaffrabad and Chand Bagh, where protesters had similarly blocked roads. Sparked by a February 23 speech from BJP leader Kapil Mishra calling for clearance of blockades if not removed peacefully, violence involved arson, stone-pelting, and targeted attacks on religious sites and properties, resulting in 53 deaths (including 36 Muslims, 15 Hindus, and 2 unidentified), over 200 injuries, and widespread destruction of shops and homes. Delhi Police imposed curfews and deployed forces, arresting over 2,600 individuals, but faced accusations of delayed response in Muslim areas and bias in investigations, with courts later acquitting dozens in cases citing insufficient evidence or fabricated charges.[52][114][115] Official probes, including chargesheets exceeding 17,000 pages, attributed much violence to organized mobs opposing CAA, with patterns of premeditated rioting like acid attacks and petrol bombs, though human rights groups alleged disproportionate arrests of Muslim protesters. The riots, occurring weeks after Shaheen Bagh's peak, underscored fault lines between sustained non-violent blockades and sporadic escalations into communal unrest, with economic damages estimated in crores from looted businesses predominantly owned by Hindus in affected lanes.[116][117][118]

West Bengal and other states

In West Bengal, Chief Minister Mamata Banerjee led opposition to the Citizenship Amendment Act, participating in a protest march in Kolkata on December 18, 2019, from Jadavpur to Jadu Babu's Bazar, and calling for a statewide mega rally against the law, which she described as unconstitutional changes to citizenship rules.[35][119] On December 19, a rally in Kolkata drew approximately 60,000 participants from diverse social, economic, cultural, and religious backgrounds, marking a broad coalition against the Act.[120] Further actions included a rally on December 21 attended by workers and youth, as well as localized blockades, such as protesters burning tires to obstruct National Highway 60 in Birbhum district on December 16.[121][122] Demonstrations in the state were predominantly peaceful, with Banerjee publicly discouraging violence and stating that political leaders should act responsibly during protests.[123] The Trinamool Congress government declared it would not implement the CAA, framing resistance as protection against perceived discrimination.[124] In Kerala, protests featured coordinated actions by ruling and opposition fronts, including a statewide hartal on December 17, 2019, and joint sit-ins by leaders from the Left Democratic Front and United Democratic Front.[125][126] The People's Long March took place in Ernakulam on December 23, with hundreds, including filmstars and theatre artists, marching from Kaloor Stadium to Cochin Shipyard against the CAA and National Register of Citizens.[127][128] In January 2020, several million people formed a 620-kilometer human chain spanning the state to demand repeal of the law.[129] In Karnataka, protests in Mangaluru on December 19, 2019, involved thousands demonstrating against the Act but turned violent, with stone-pelting, arson on vehicles and a police vehicle, prompting police to fire on the crowd, killing two individuals.[130][131] Authorities responded by suspending mobile internet, imposing curfews, and later inquiring into the incidents, with some footage suggesting premeditated elements among agitators.[132] In Maharashtra, approximately 60,000 people rallied in Malegaon on December 19.[133] Tamil Nadu witnessed demonstrations, including thousands gathering in Chennai's Washermanpet area to oppose the CAA, NRC, and National Population Register.[134]

Violence, Casualties, and Law Enforcement

Fatalities and injuries data

During the initial wave of protests in December 2019, at least 25 individuals were killed across multiple states, with Uttar Pradesh accounting for 23 deaths—predominantly Muslims succumbing to gunshot wounds amid clashes and alleged police firing on December 20-21.[6] [135] In Assam, five protesters died during violent demonstrations, including incidents involving gunfire and mob clashes.[96] Isolated fatalities occurred elsewhere, such as two in Karnataka from bullet injuries during a protest on December 19.[136] The February 23-26, 2020, riots in northeast Delhi, erupting from tensions surrounding CAA demonstrations, resulted in 53 confirmed deaths—40 Muslims and 13 Hindus—along with over 200 injuries from stabbings, arson, and shootings.[137] [138] Police reported more than 100 injuries among their ranks in these events, amid accusations of both mob violence and inadequate response.[49] Injuries nationwide exceeded 1,000, including over 260 police personnel in the December clashes alone, often from stone-pelting and confrontations.[7] Uttar Pradesh saw hundreds hospitalized from lathi charges, bullets, and beatings, though exact figures remain disputed due to varying attributions between protester actions and security forces.[139] Overall totals for fatalities hover around 80 when including Delhi, though some human rights reports emphasize higher Muslim casualties in government-controlled areas, potentially reflecting selective enforcement patterns.[140]

Patterns of rioting and property damage

Violence during the Citizenship Amendment Act protests occasionally escalated from demonstrations into riots characterized by arson, stone-pelting, and targeted destruction of property, primarily in Uttar Pradesh in December 2019 and Delhi in February 2020. These incidents typically began with gatherings defying administrative bans under Section 144 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, progressing to attacks on police and public infrastructure before spreading to private assets. In Uttar Pradesh, riots on December 19-20, 2019, in districts including Lucknow, Kanpur, and Aligarh involved mobs setting fire to over 100 buses, train coaches, and police vehicles, alongside damage to district magistrate offices and railway stations. The state government estimated public property losses exceeding Rs 200 crore, with specific incidents like the burning of 24 new buses in Lucknow valued at Rs 12 crore. Authorities issued recovery notices to identified participants, totaling Rs 3.35 crore across multiple districts, though Supreme Court interventions later scrutinized the process for procedural lapses.[141][142] The February 23-26, 2020, riots in northeast Delhi exhibited more communal dimensions, with mobs using petrol bombs and firearms to torch over 300 vehicles, loot shops, and raze buildings in areas like Shiv Vihar and Karawal Nagar. Affected parties claimed damages surpassing Rs 153 crore, encompassing structural losses of Rs 250 crore and broader economic impacts exceeding Rs 1,000 crore from disrupted businesses. Patterns included sequential arson waves starting from contested protest sites like Jaffrabad, escalating into block-level attacks on commercial hubs and residences, often amid chants opposing the CAA. Over 535 FIRs were filed for property offenses, highlighting the scale of coordinated destruction.[143][144][145] Across both regions, rioting followed a recurring sequence: initial defiance of dispersal orders led to clashes with law enforcement, followed by retaliatory arson focused on high-value, easily ignitable targets like vehicles and markets. While some reports attributed damage to counter-mob actions, primary investigations pointed to anti-CAA groups initiating much of the violence, with public transport and government symbols bearing the brunt early on.[146]

Police actions and controversies

During the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) protests from December 2019 onward, Indian police forces deployed tear gas, lathi charges, rubber bullets, and water cannons to disperse crowds in multiple locations, including university campuses and urban streets. In Uttar Pradesh, where protests turned violent with incidents of arson and stone-pelting, police resorted to live firing in self-defense, resulting in at least 15 deaths reported by state authorities between December 19 and 21, 2019, primarily from gunshot wounds amid clashes in districts like Kanpur, Firozabad, and Sambhal. Hospitals documented up to 11 fatalities in UP alone during this period, though police disputed the figures, attributing some deaths to cross-firing by protesters rather than official gunfire. Nationwide, at least 25 protesters were killed in clashes with law enforcement, with the majority occurring in BJP-ruled states like Uttar Pradesh, where Human Rights Watch documented patterns of excessive force but noted the protests often involved attacks on police stations and public property.[147] At Jamia Millia Islamia University in New Delhi on December 15, 2019, police entered the campus following stone-throwing and protests spilling onto roads, using tear gas inside libraries and classrooms, which injured dozens of students and sparked widespread video evidence of baton charges against allegedly non-violent demonstrators hiding in reading rooms. Authorities justified the incursion as pursuit of rioters who had vandalized vehicles and pelted officers, denying unprovoked brutality, while student accounts and footage highlighted disproportionate aggression against unarmed individuals, leading to calls for judicial probes rejected by India's Supreme Court. Similar actions occurred at Aligarh Muslim University, where police fired tear gas and plastic bullets, injuring over 50 students. Controversies intensified with allegations of targeted harassment of Muslim protesters, including arbitrary arrests under sedition laws—over 1,000 detentions nationwide—and selective enforcement, as reported by activists, though police cited evidence of coordinated violence, including petrol bombs and firearms recovered from crowds.[148][149] In the February 2020 Delhi riots, which killed 53 people amid clashes between pro- and anti-CAA groups, police faced accusations of delayed response and complicity with Hindu nationalist mobs in Muslim-majority areas like Northeast Delhi, with Amnesty International citing instances of officers allegedly standing by or joining attackers, contributing to 38 Muslim deaths. However, investigations revealed intelligence lapses and attacks on police—resulting in injuries to over 150 officers—along with evidence of premeditated violence by Islamist groups, including beheadings and arson, prompting Delhi Police to file charges against rioters while denying bias. A high court judge's criticism of police inaction led to his abrupt transfer, fueling claims of institutional pressure, though subsequent probes, including by the BBC, noted weakening cases against some accused due to evidentiary issues rather than proven fabrication. These events underscored broader debates on police restraint versus necessity, with empirical data showing protester-initiated violence—such as bus burnings and assaults on stations—preceding many escalations, contrasting narratives from human rights groups often aligned against the ruling government.[150][151]

Protest Methods and Tactics

Non-violent demonstrations

Non-violent demonstrations against the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) encompassed marches, rallies, and campus gatherings held across India from December 2019 onward, often emphasizing constitutional concerns and secular principles. These events drew participants from student groups, civil society, and diverse communities, contrasting with instances where protests escalated into confrontations.[119] On December 16, 2019, tens of thousands of students and faculty participated in peaceful marches on university campuses nationwide, protesting the CAA's perceived incompatibility with India's secular framework.[119] Similar gatherings occurred at institutions in Bengaluru, Kolkata, Mumbai, and other cities, with organizers calling for the law's repeal without reported disruptions to public order in many cases.[152] In Bengaluru, a major rally on December 23, 2019, organized by the Joint Action Committee, attracted an estimated 80,000 to 100,000 participants who marched to Freedom Park, concluding without incidents of violence.[153][154] Police facilitated the event by managing traffic, highlighting its orderly nature. Kerala witnessed coordinated non-violent actions, including the People's Long March on December 23, 2019, in Kochi, where hundreds of students, activists, actors, and filmmakers marched against the CAA and proposed National Register of Citizens (NRC).[155][127] This was followed by a statewide human chain on January 30, 2020, spanning 620 kilometers and involving millions, symbolizing unified opposition through passive assembly.[129] Such demonstrations underscored regional mobilization, with participants framing the CAA as discriminatory based on religion, though government defenders argued it addressed refugee persecution without affecting citizens.[15]

Disruptive actions and blockades

Protesters at Shaheen Bagh in Delhi initiated a sit-in on December 15, 2019, blocking a key arterial road connecting south Delhi to Noida and thereby disrupting vehicular traffic for commuters, ambulances, and public transport.[156] The blockade persisted for approximately 69 days until its clearance by police on February 21, 2020, amid Supreme Court observations questioning the legality of indefinite road occupations for protests.[157] This action, primarily led by local Muslim women, extended into over 100 days in adjacent protest sites, forcing alternative routes and contributing to daily traffic snarls affecting thousands.[158] In West Bengal, anti-CAA demonstrators blockaded roads and railway stations, leading to the torching of buses and empty trains on December 14, 2019, which halted rail services and public mobility in multiple districts.[159] Similar tactics involved squatting on railway tracks to enforce bandhs, as seen in Bihar on December 21, 2019, where protesters disrupted train movements and blockaded bus terminuses across cities like Patna and Muzaffarpur.[160] During the People's Long March in Kerala on December 23, 2019, participants established road blockades and halted trains to protest the CAA and NRC, affecting connectivity in Ernakulam and surrounding areas.[161] In Assam, opposition groups coordinated blockades on highways and rail lines starting early December 2019, exacerbating transport disruptions in the Northeast amid fears over immigration policies.[161] These actions often coincided with calls for statewide shutdowns, prioritizing demonstration over minimal public inconvenience.[162]

Symbolic and media strategies

Protesters against the Citizenship Amendment Act utilized symbolic art forms such as posters, graffiti, murals, and banners to visually contest the legislation and associated National Register of Citizens. At Shaheen Bagh in Delhi, the sit-in site transformed into an open-air gallery where participants created hand-drawn illustrations, photography installations, and wall art emphasizing themes of constitutional equality and resistance to perceived discrimination.[163][164] These visuals often depicted women protesters, police actions, and slogans invoking India's secular ethos, with over 100 such posters documented by early 2020.[165] Similar artistic expressions emerged at university campuses, including Jamia Millia Islamia, where students painted ambitious murals on roads and walls amid clashes with police on December 15, 2019, symbolizing solidarity against violence and the CAA.[166] Banners and graffiti, such as those reading "No CAA, No NRC" on overbridges and barricades, served to disrupt public spaces and reinforce the narrative of disenfranchisement fears, particularly among Muslim communities.[167] While these efforts aimed to foster public discourse on citizenship, some posters incorporated religious imagery that critics argued promoted sectarian divides rather than universal secularism.[168] Media strategies involved amplifying these symbols through social media campaigns on platforms like Twitter and Instagram, where hashtags such as #IndiaAgainstCAA and #CitizenshipAmendmentAct garnered millions of engagements by January 2020, enabling rapid dissemination of protest art and live updates.[169][170] Organizers shared digital versions of posters and filters for broader participation, turning individual expressions into viral counter-narratives against government messaging.[171] This online mobilization drew international attention, with outlets like Al Jazeera highlighting artist contributions, though domestic coverage varied due to partisan media landscapes.[167] Such tactics not only sustained domestic momentum but also pressured policymakers by projecting the protests as a grassroots defense of pluralism.[172]

Participants and Motivations

Key organizers and student groups

Students at Jamia Millia Islamia University in New Delhi initiated prominent anti-CAA demonstrations on December 13, 2019, with gatherings escalating into clashes by December 15, prompting police entry onto campus and use of tear gas, resulting in over 200 injuries.[173] The Jamia Students' Union and affiliated groups coordinated these campus-based protests, which drew national attention and inspired solidarity actions elsewhere.[174] At Aligarh Muslim University (AMU), student-led protests began on December 13, 2019, involving marches and effigy burnings of Prime Minister Narendra Modi, leading to police deployment and reports of violence that injured dozens.[175] AMU's student union played a central role in mobilizing participants, framing the CAA as discriminatory against Muslims when paired with the National Register of Citizens (NRC).[176] Jawaharlal Nehru University (JNU) students, often aligned with left-wing organizations like the Students' Federation of India (SFI) and All India Students' Association (AISA), organized solidarity marches and seminars against the CAA starting mid-December 2019, emphasizing constitutional concerns over secularism.[177] Key figures such as Umar Khalid, a former JNU student politician, and Sharjeel Imam, a JNU alumnus, emerged as vocal organizers, with Imam advocating "chakka jam" road blockades in speeches that later led to sedition charges.[173] In Assam, the All Assam Students' Union (AASU), representing indigenous interests, led widespread protests against the CAA from December 2019 onward, including torchlight marches and shutdowns, opposing the law's provisions for granting citizenship to non-Muslim migrants from neighboring countries, which they viewed as undermining efforts to detect and deport illegal immigrants.[178] AASU's campaigns, involving thousands of members, highlighted regional fears of demographic changes rather than religious discrimination.[179]

Demographic and ideological drivers

The protests against the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) of 2019 primarily drew participants from Muslim communities, particularly in urban centers like Delhi, where events such as the Shaheen Bagh sit-in were led by women from Muslim-majority neighborhoods including Jamia Nagar, Okhla, and surrounding areas.[180] [181] These demonstrations featured extended participation by local residents, with women and families forming the visible core, often including children, in a locality with a demographic composition exceeding 90% Muslim as per local estimates. Student groups from institutions with significant Muslim enrollments, such as Jamia Millia Islamia and Aligarh Muslim University, were prominent in initial clashes and sustained mobilizations, alongside left-leaning students from Jawaharlal Nehru University.[182] Arrest records from Delhi during the protests and associated February 2020 riots reflect a demographic skew, with police detentions and convictions disproportionately involving Muslims; for instance, of the over 2,000 arrests in northeast Delhi violence—linked by authorities to anti-CAA agitation—courts sentenced multiple Muslim individuals to terms of up to 10 years, while Hindu arrests were fewer and often resulted in acquittals or lighter outcomes.[149] [183] Nationwide, participation extended to urban middle-class professionals and academics, but empirical data from protest sites and casualty figures—such as the 53 deaths in Delhi riots, with over 40 Muslim fatalities—underscore the concentration in Muslim-populated areas.[149] In contrast, northeastern protests, especially in Assam, involved indigenous ethnic groups like Assamese and tribal communities, motivated less by religious identity and more by demographic preservation against perceived Bengali migrant influxes.[170] Ideologically, the protests were propelled by assertions of secular constitutionalism, with participants framing the CAA as a breach of India's equality principles under Article 14 by explicitly excluding Muslims from fast-tracked citizenship for persecuted minorities from neighboring countries.[59] Opponents, including student activists and civil society groups, argued it presaged a religiously discriminatory regime when paired with the National Register of Citizens (NRC), potentially rendering Indian Muslims vulnerable to statelessness despite official clarifications that the Act targeted only pre-2014 immigrants and preserved existing citizenship rights.[184] This narrative resonated among left-liberal intellectuals and minority rights advocates, who positioned the movement as resistance to Hindu-majoritarian policies under the BJP government, invoking broader critiques of nationalism as exclusionary.[185] In Assam and the northeast, ideological drivers centered on ethno-cultural autonomy, rejecting the CAA's provisions as enabling unchecked demographic shifts that threatened indigenous land rights and linguistic identities, independent of religious framing.[170] While some analyses attribute participation to anti-BJP polarization, empirical patterns reveal layered motivations, with secular-left coalitions amplifying minority fears amid unsubstantiated claims of mass disenfranchisement.[186]

Claims of foreign funding or coordination

The Enforcement Directorate (ED) of India alleged in January 2020 that the Popular Front of India (PFI), a now-banned Islamist organization, channeled funds to affiliates fueling anti-CAA protests and violence, particularly in Uttar Pradesh, with investigations uncovering Rs 120.5 crore credited to related bank accounts suspected of being used for protest logistics and incitement.[187][188] The ED's findings linked these transactions to coordinated efforts by PFI-linked entities to organize demonstrations and procure materials for unrest, prompting referrals to the Ministry of Home Affairs for further scrutiny of potential terror financing under the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act.[188] PFI rejected these accusations as baseless, asserting that the funds were for legitimate welfare activities and not protest-related.[189] Indian intelligence agencies reported evidence of Pakistani involvement in the protests, including electronic intercepts from December 2019 to early 2020 indicating operatives directing anti-CAA activities to exacerbate communal tensions and destabilize the government.[190] These claims pointed to cross-border coordination, with funds allegedly routed through proxies to sustain prolonged sit-ins and riots, aligning with Pakistan's historical pattern of supporting insurgent narratives against Indian policies.[191] BJP leaders, including parliamentarians, echoed these assertions, attributing protest persistence to financing by entities opposed to India's sovereignty, such as terrorist organizations.[192] In the context of the February 2020 Delhi riots—stemming from anti-CAA agitations—police investigations uncovered remittances from Oman totaling significant sums received by rioters' associates, used for acquiring weapons and logistics, as detailed in chargesheets and verified through bank trails.[193] Separate probes into the NewsClick media outlet, raided in 2021 and charged in 2023, revealed that Chinese entities funneled approximately Rs 38 crore between 2018 and 2021, part of which supported anti-CAA narratives, protest coordination, and procurement of arms for violence, according to witness statements and financial audits submitted to courts.[194] Critics of these allegations, including affected groups and opposition figures, have dismissed them as politically motivated attempts to discredit grassroots dissent, though judicial proceedings have upheld the probes' evidentiary basis without conclusive foreign state sponsorship proven in public records.[189] No independent international verification has confirmed direct governmental foreign funding, but the patterns of hawala transfers and offshore links raised by agencies underscore suspicions of external amplification beyond domestic opposition.[187][190]

Economic and Societal Impacts

Disruptions to infrastructure and commerce

The Shaheen Bagh sit-in, a prominent anti-CAA demonstration in southeastern Delhi, involved protesters occupying and blocking the arterial road linking Delhi to Noida and Faridabad from December 15, 2019, until its clearance on March 24, 2020.[113][195] This blockade spanned over three months, forcing commuters to navigate extensive detours via alternative routes, which added significant time to daily travel for residents and workers in the National Capital Region.[158] The Supreme Court of India acknowledged the resulting public inconvenience, stating that genuine protests must not obstruct public roads indefinitely.[196] Emergency services faced critical delays due to the road occupation, with reports of ambulances being obstructed or slowed by protesters. On January 29, 2020, an ambulance transporting a woman in critical condition requiring ventilator support was reportedly denied prompt passage through the site, exacerbating her health risks.[197] Authorities permitted limited access for emergency vehicles and school buses, but persistent blockages undermined these measures, highlighting vulnerabilities in infrastructure access during prolonged demonstrations.[198] Local commerce suffered substantially from the disruptions, as the blockade led to the closure of over 250 shops in Shaheen Bagh and adjacent areas, resulting in job losses for numerous workers dependent on foot traffic and supply chains.[199] Business owners reported diminished customer access and halted operations, with aggregate losses estimated at ₹150 crore by February 2020, attributed to severed connectivity and reduced economic activity in the vicinity.[199] Beyond Delhi, anti-CAA protests in regions like Uttar Pradesh and Assam prompted government-imposed internet shutdowns and curfews to contain unrest, indirectly amplifying commercial disruptions. These measures, enacted in December 2019 and early 2020, impeded e-commerce deliveries, digital transactions, and remote work, leaving small vendors and laborers without income during peak shutdown periods.[200][201] In Assam, widespread bandhs and road occupations further stalled trade and transport, compounding infrastructural strain in a state already sensitive to migration-related economic pressures.[202]

Communal polarization effects

The Citizenship Amendment Act protests, often concentrated in Muslim-majority areas and framed by critics as discriminatory against Muslims, elicited significant backlash from Hindu nationalist groups and the general Hindu populace, who viewed the demonstrations as obstructive and ideologically motivated opposition to a policy aiding persecuted non-Muslim minorities from Islamic neighboring countries.[203] This perception fueled counter-protests and rallies supporting the CAA, exacerbating divisions along religious lines as pro-CAA gatherings emphasized national security and historical injustices against Hindus, Sikhs, and others fleeing persecution.[49] Prolonged blockades, such as the 101-day sit-in at Shaheen Bagh in Delhi from December 2019 to March 2020, disrupted daily life for thousands, particularly affecting Hindu commuters and residents, which amplified grievances and narratives portraying the protests as prioritizing minority appeasement over public convenience.[204] These tactics contributed to a hardening of positions, with social media and public discourse amplifying claims of anti-Hindu bias in the protests, leading to increased communal rhetoric and sporadic clashes between demonstrators and supporters of the law.[205] The peak of this polarization manifested in the February 2020 Northeast Delhi riots, triggered by tensions over an anti-CAA road blockade in Jaffrabad, where clashes between pro- and anti-CAA groups escalated into widespread violence from February 23 to 26.[206] The riots resulted in 53 deaths—36 Muslims, 15 Hindus, and 2 unidentified—along with over 200 injuries and extensive property damage targeting mosques and homes in Muslim areas.[204] Official investigations attributed the violence to premeditated elements on both sides, but the events reinforced mutual distrust, with Hindu communities citing prior protest disruptions as provocation and Muslim groups alleging targeted pogroms.[207] Post-riots data indicated a surge in communal incidents, with over 210 recorded clashes between Hindus and Muslims during the BJP's second term, the deadliest being the Delhi events linked to CAA disputes.[205] This escalation entrenched polarized identities, as evidenced by heightened vigilance in mixed neighborhoods and electoral shifts favoring parties emphasizing Hindu unity in response to perceived threats from Islamist mobilization.[203] While some analyses attribute the divides to underlying Hindu nationalism, empirical patterns of protest tactics and retaliatory violence suggest causal links to the confrontational nature of the demonstrations themselves.[49]

Long-term policy deterrence

The Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) rules, essential for its operationalization, were notified by the Government of India on March 11, 2024, over four years after the Act's passage on December 11, 2019.[15] This delay followed nationwide protests that began immediately after enactment, encompassing non-violent sit-ins, road blockades, and episodes of violence resulting in over 100 deaths, including the February 2020 Delhi riots.[208] Critics, including opposition parties and human rights groups, attributed the postponement to the scale of public backlash, which highlighted fears of religious discrimination and linkage to a National Register of Citizens (NRC).[72] However, government officials maintained that the timing reflected administrative and legal deliberations, not capitulation to unrest.[2] Implementation proceeded post-notification, with the Ministry of Home Affairs reporting that thousands of eligible non-Muslim migrants from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan—primarily Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians who entered India before December 31, 2014—received citizenship by April 2, 2025.[209] Union Home Minister Amit Shah reiterated in March 2024 that the CAA would not be repealed, emphasizing its focus on protecting persecuted minorities without impacting Indian Muslims or requiring any citizen to prove citizenship.[84] This persistence suggests the protests exerted short-term pressure via legal challenges in the Supreme Court but failed to achieve long-term reversal of the core policy, as the Act's provisions took effect without further concessions.[15] In parallel, the protests amplified concerns over a nationwide NRC, envisioned as a comprehensive citizen verification exercise akin to Assam's 2019 update, which excluded 1.9 million residents pending appeals. Although Shah asserted in December 2019 that a pan-India NRC would occur independently of the CAA and exclude no Indian citizen regardless of religion, no such rollout has materialized as of October 2025.[210] Analysts link this inaction to the demonstrated potential for mass mobilization and communal tensions during the protests, which framed NRC-CAA as a discriminatory tandem targeting Muslims, thereby raising political costs for the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) ahead of elections.[211] The government's focus has shifted to related measures like the National Population Register (NPR) updates tied to the deferred 2021 census, now slated for 2026-27, without committing to NRC linkage.[212] Overall, while the protests did not deter CAA enactment or its eventual execution, they appear to have induced caution on expansive citizenship exercises like nationwide NRC, preserving the policy's selective application to Assam amid ongoing litigation and public scrutiny.[211] This outcome reflects a balance where empirical risks of unrest—evident in economic disruptions and polarization—tempered broader ambitions without altering the government's stated objective of addressing illegal migration from neighboring countries.[213]

Legal and Judicial Proceedings

Supreme Court challenges

Petitions challenging the constitutionality of the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019 (CAA), were filed in the Supreme Court of India shortly after its passage by Parliament on December 11, 2019, and presidential assent on December 12, 2019.[214] The Indian Union Muslim League (IUML) submitted the first writ petition under Article 32 of the Constitution on December 19, 2019, arguing that the CAA violates Articles 14 (equality before the law), 15 (prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion), and 21 (right to life and personal liberty) by excluding Muslims from expedited citizenship pathways for migrants from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India before December 31, 2014.[215] Subsequent petitions, totaling over 200 by 2024, echoed these claims, contending that the religion-based criteria render the law discriminatory and under-inclusive, potentially facilitating exclusion when linked to the National Register of Citizens (NRC).[216][217] In early hearings, a Constitution Bench declined to grant interim stays on the CAA's implementation. On January 22, 2020, the Court, comprising Justices S.A. Bobde, B.R. Gavai, and Surya Kant, heard arguments from 60 consolidated petitions and refused to halt the law, observing that it did not appear ex facie unconstitutional and directing the Union government to respond to specific queries on its scope and interaction with existing citizenship frameworks. The cases faced prolonged delays amid the COVID-19 pandemic and judicial backlog, with limited substantive progress until the notification of CAA rules on March 11, 2024, which operationalized the amendment by establishing procedures for eligible applicants.[2] Renewed urgency followed the rules' notification, prompting fresh pleas for stays. On March 15, 2024, the Supreme Court agreed to examine interim relief applications, listing them for hearing on March 19, 2024, before a bench led by Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud, along with Justices J.B. Pardiwala and Manoj Misra.[218] During the March 19 hearing on 237 petitions, the bench rejected requests to suspend the rules, emphasizing the need for balanced consideration of persecution faced by targeted minorities and directing the Centre to file counter-affidavits within three weeks, with petitioners' rejoinders due shortly thereafter; the next listing was scheduled post-April 9, 2024.[219][220] As of October 2025, the petitions remain pending without a final verdict, with the Court yet to rule on the merits despite arguments from petitioners highlighting religious discrimination and from the government defending the CAA as a humanitarian measure for non-Muslim refugees fleeing state-sponsored persecution, without impacting Indian citizens' rights or the NRC process.[214][17] The extended litigation reflects broader debates on the Act's alignment with secular constitutional principles, though the absence of stays has enabled rule implementation, including initial citizenship grants to eligible applicants in 2024.[221]

Interim stays and ongoing status

The Supreme Court of India refused to grant an interim stay on the implementation of the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019, in its initial hearings following the law's passage in December 2019, despite petitions seeking urgent suspension on grounds of alleged unconstitutionality.[222] In December 2019, the court declined requests for an early hearing or stay, bundling over 200 petitions for consideration by a constitution bench, citing the need for deliberate review rather than precipitous action.[217] Following the notification of the Citizenship (Amendment) Rules, 2024, on March 11, 2024, which enabled the law's operationalization, petitioners renewed applications for an interim stay, arguing potential irreparable harm to secular principles and equality under Articles 14 and 15 of the Constitution. On March 19, 2024, a bench led by Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud rejected the stay, emphasizing that the rules' enforcement did not preclude judicial scrutiny and directing the Union government to file responses within three weeks, with hearings deferred to April.[223] This refusal allowed the government to proceed with a dedicated online portal for processing applications from eligible non-Muslim migrants from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India before December 31, 2014.[224] As of October 2025, the constitutional challenges to the CAA remain pending before the Supreme Court, with no final verdict delivered despite multiple adjournments and referrals to larger benches for resolving preliminary issues like the need for a five-judge constitution bench.[214] The court has continued to list the matters for hearing, including a scheduled consideration on October 31 for potential reference to a three-judge bench, but implementation of the rules has not been halted, reflecting the judiciary's reluctance to intervene absent prima facie invalidity. Over 237 petitions, including those from political parties, activists, and citizens, contest provisions like Section 2(1)(b), which fast-tracks citizenship for specified religious minorities, yet the absence of a stay has enabled administrative processing of claims, with relaxed documentation requirements noted in government clarifications by May 2025.[225]

Related litigation on NRC

The Supreme Court of India has overseen the National Register of Citizens (NRC) process primarily in Assam, directing its updating in 2013 following a public interest litigation filed in 2009 by Sarbananda Sonowal challenging illegal immigration.[226] The final NRC list, published on August 31, 2019, excluded approximately 1.9 million individuals, prompting multiple petitions alleging procedural errors, arbitrary exclusions, and lack of due process in verification.[2] These challenges intensified amid the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) protests, as critics argued that NRC exclusions, combined with CAA's religious criteria, could systematically disenfranchise Muslims without providing them equivalent citizenship pathways.[222] In response to claims of "large-scale errors" in the Assam NRC, including mismatched documents and unaddressed discrepancies, the Supreme Court issued notices to the central government and Assam authorities on a September 2025 plea seeking time-bound reverification of the list.[227] Earlier, in August 2025, the Court admitted a writ petition filed by Hitesh Dev Sarma, the former state NRC coordinator, demanding comprehensive revision of the draft NRC and re-verification of at least 20% of entries to rectify alleged inclusions of foreigners and exclusions of genuine citizens.[228] Sarma's petition highlighted forensic audits revealing potential infiltration in the data, though Assam's government has opposed blanket re-verification due to logistical burdens and past expenditures exceeding ₹1,600 crore.[229] No final orders on these reverification pleas have been issued as of October 2025, with proceedings ongoing. Related foundational litigation includes challenges to Section 6A of the Citizenship Act, 1955, which implements the 1985 Assam Accord by setting a March 25, 1971, cut-off date for citizenship in the state, underpinning the NRC's eligibility criteria. On October 17, 2024, a five-judge Constitution Bench upheld Section 6A's validity in a 4:1 majority, rejecting arguments that it violated equality principles or enabled demographic changes through immigration.[230] Justice Bhushan R Gavai's majority opinion affirmed the provision's alignment with federalism and the Accord's protective intent for indigenous Assamese, while Justice Sanjay Kishan Kaul dissented, citing risks of arbitrary application.[226] This ruling indirectly bolsters the Assam NRC framework but has fueled ongoing debates in CAA-NRC linked challenges, where petitioners contend it entrenches discriminatory cut-offs when paired with CAA exclusions for Muslims.[231] Petitions have also targeted ancillary NRC aspects, such as detention camps for declared foreigners excluded from the list. In one case, activist Harsh Mander challenged the conditions in Assam's camps as inhuman, seeking their closure or reform, though the Court has deferred broader relief pending Foreigners Tribunals' adjudication of exclusions.[232] Nationwide NRC proposals, referenced in CAA protest narratives as a threat, have not advanced to implementation, with the government stating in 2023 no such exercise is underway, averting direct Supreme Court litigation but leaving Assam-specific disputes as the primary judicial battleground.[2]

Broader Reactions and Debates

Domestic political and civil society views

The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP)-led National Democratic Alliance government enacted the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) on December 11, 2019, defending it as a humanitarian provision to grant expedited citizenship to persecuted non-Muslim minorities—Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians—from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India before December 31, 2014, without revoking rights of Indian citizens.[233] Prime Minister Narendra Modi accused opposition parties of disseminating misinformation, asserting the law addressed historical injustices against religious minorities in Islamic-majority neighbors while excluding Muslims due to their ability to seek refuge in those countries.[233] Regional allies like the Shiromani Akali Dal and Shiv Sena (at the time) endorsed the measure, viewing it as consistent with protecting co-religionists abroad.[234] Opposition parties, including the Indian National Congress, Trinamool Congress, and Communist parties, condemned the CAA as religiously discriminatory and a departure from India's secular constitutional framework, organizing a nationwide bandh on December 19, 2019, to protest its passage.[235] Critics argued it, when paired with the National Register of Citizens (NRC), could systematically marginalize India's Muslim population by creating a religion-based citizenship pathway absent for Muslims fleeing the same regions.[236] Public opinion surveys reflected polarization: a Mood of the Nation poll in August 2020 found 50% of respondents supported the CAA, with only 15% deeming it discriminatory, while a January 2020 IANS-C Voter survey showed 70% approval in northern India but lower support in the Northeast.[237][238] Civil society responses diverged regionally and ideologically. In the Northeast, groups like the All Assam Students' Union opposed the CAA not primarily on religious grounds but due to apprehensions of increased immigration altering indigenous demographics and cultures, leading to violent clashes in Assam and exclusion clauses for tribal areas.[186] Nationwide, student unions at universities like Jawaharlal Nehru University and Jamia Millia Islamia, alongside Muslim-led initiatives such as the Shaheen Bagh sit-in, framed protests as resistance to authoritarianism and minority exclusion, drawing participation from feminists, Dalits, and secular activists.[186] Conversely, pro-CAA civil groups, including Hindu advocacy organizations, rallied in support, emphasizing the act's focus on verifiable persecution data—such as the decline of Hindus in Pakistan from 23% in 1947 to 2% by 2019—and decrying protest tactics like road blockades as disruptive to public life.[234] A January 2020 Mood of the Nation poll indicated 43% viewed CAA-NRC linkage as a distraction from economic woes, underscoring broader societal fatigue with sustained unrest.[239]

International responses and critiques

The United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights, Michelle Bachelet, expressed concern over the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) in December 2019, urging Indian authorities to respect the right to peaceful assembly during protests and adhere to international standards on the use of force.[240] In March 2020, the UN High Commissioner's office sought to intervene in Supreme Court proceedings challenging the CAA, arguing it violated India's obligations under international human rights law, including the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, by introducing religious criteria for citizenship.[241] The UN reiterated in March 2024 that the CAA remained "fundamentally discriminatory" in nature, particularly when paired with the National Register of Citizens, and called for its repeal while condemning the government's response to 2019-2020 protests, which included arbitrary detentions and excessive force.[242] The United States government voiced concerns about the CAA shortly after its passage in December 2019, with the State Department stating it was monitoring the law's implementation for potential impacts on religious freedom and equal treatment under the law.[243] In March 2024, following rules for CAA implementation, the US reiterated its position, describing the Act as discriminatory and emphasizing close monitoring, prompting India to dismiss the remarks as "misplaced, misinformed, and unwarranted," asserting the law addressed persecution of minorities from neighboring countries without affecting Indian citizens.[242][244] The US Commission on International Religious Freedom (USCIRF) highlighted the CAA in its 2023 annual report as contributing to systematic violations, recommending India's designation as a Country of Particular Concern, though this reflected the commission's advisory role rather than official policy.[245] The European Parliament drafted multiple resolutions in January 2020 criticizing the CAA for its selective exclusion of Muslims and urging EU member states to raise the issue in bilateral talks with India, though votes on these were postponed amid diplomatic sensitivities.[246][247] India rejected these interventions as interference in internal matters, emphasizing the CAA's focus on granting refuge to non-Muslim minorities fleeing religious persecution in Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan, without revoking citizenship from any Indian nationals.[248] Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International, prominent non-governmental organizations, condemned the CAA as discriminatory and a threat to secularism, linking it to protest suppressions that resulted in dozens of deaths, though these groups' assessments have faced criticism for overlooking the Act's targeted relief for verified persecuted groups amid regional Islamist extremism.[17][72]

Fact-checking common protest narratives

Claim that the CAA-NRC combination would strip Indian Muslims of citizenship. The Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019, amends the Citizenship Act, 1955, to provide an accelerated path to citizenship for non-Muslim religious minorities—Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians—from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India before December 31, 2014, and faced persecution on religious grounds.[14] It does not alter the citizenship status of any existing Indian citizen, regardless of religion, nor does it mandate exclusion based on faith for those already holding citizenship documents.[249] The National Register of Citizens (NRC), implemented only in Assam as of 2019, requires residents to prove citizenship via documents predating March 24, 1971, and excluded approximately 1.9 million people, including both Hindus and Muslims, without any religious criterion.[15] Nationwide NRC rollout has not occurred, and the CAA explicitly relaxes residency requirements for eligible non-Muslims in potential NRC contexts, serving as a safeguard rather than a tool for disenfranchisement. Critics' assertions of targeted statelessness for Muslims lack empirical basis, as neither law retroactively revokes citizenship, and Muslims remain eligible for naturalization through standard processes without religious bar.[250] Claim that anti-CAA protests were entirely peaceful and met only with disproportionate police violence. While some demonstrations, such as the Shaheen Bagh sit-in, remained non-violent for extended periods, multiple anti-CAA protests from December 2019 to March 2020 involved significant violence initiated by protesters, including arson, stone-pelting, and clashes resulting in deaths and property damage. In Assam, protests turned violent shortly after the bill's passage on December 9, 2019, with attacks on government offices and vehicles.[9] The February 2020 Delhi riots, linked to anti-CAA tensions, saw 53 fatalities—predominantly Muslims—and over 200 injuries, with investigations attributing much of the violence to mobs targeting properties and police, including documented cases of protesters firing on forces and setting fire to buildings.[58] Over 30 deaths occurred nationwide during protest-related unrest, alongside arrests for rioting in Uttar Pradesh and elsewhere, contradicting narratives of unilateral state aggression. Police responses included tear gas and lathi charges, but reports confirm protester-initiated attacks in several instances.[49] Claim that the CAA is inherently discriminatory against Muslims by excluding them from eligibility. The Act targets non-Muslims from three specified Muslim-majority countries where they constitute religious minorities subject to documented persecution, such as forced conversions and blasphemy laws disproportionately affecting Hindus in Pakistan (where their population declined from 23% in 1947 to 2% by 2019) and similar patterns for others in Bangladesh and Afghanistan.[15] Muslims are excluded from this fast-track not due to animus but because, as the majority faith in those nations, they do not qualify as persecuted minorities fleeing religious oppression; however, Muslims from any country, including these three, can apply for Indian citizenship via the standard 11-year residency naturalization route without religious restriction.[2] This distinction aligns with India's historical policy of sheltering Partition-era refugees and others from religious strife, while secular constitutional provisions for equal treatment do not preclude affirmative relief for unequally persecuted groups. Challenges to the Act's constitutionality remain pending before the Supreme Court of India as of October 2025, with no final verdict invalidating it.[1]
