Elizabeth Cheney (1422–1473)

Elizabeth Cheney (April 1422 – 25 September 1473) was an English noblewoman of the gentry class, known primarily for her connections through marriage and descent to three queens consort of King Henry VIII: Anne Boleyn, Jane Seymour, and Catherine Howard.[1] Born in Fen Ditton, Cambridgeshire, she was the eldest daughter of Lawrence Cheney, Esquire and High Sheriff of Cambridgeshire, and his wife Elizabeth Cokayne, who brought royal descent from Edward I.[2] Elizabeth married twice: first to Sir Frederick Tilney around 1443, with whom she had at least one daughter, Elizabeth Tilney; and second, following Tilney's death in 1445, to Sir John Say, Speaker of the House of Commons, by whom she had several children including Anne Say.[1][3] Through Elizabeth Tilney, who married Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk, Elizabeth Cheney became the great-grandmother of Anne Boleyn (daughter of Elizabeth Howard) and Catherine Howard (daughter of Edmund Howard).[4] Her daughter Anne Say married Sir Robert Wentworth, making Elizabeth Cheney the great-grandmother of Jane Seymour via Margery Wentworth.[5] Elizabeth died at age 51 and was buried in the church at Broxbourne, Hertfordshire, where a monumental inscription records her passing.[3]

Early Life and Ancestry

Birth and Parentage

Elizabeth Cheney was born circa April 1422 in Fen Ditton, Cambridgeshire, England, the principal seat of her paternal family.[1][2] She was the eldest daughter of Lawrence Cheney, Esq. (c. 1396–1461), a landowner who served as High Sheriff of Cambridgeshire and Huntingdonshire in 1441, and his wife Elizabeth Cokayne (c. 1390–after 1449).[3][6] Lawrence Cheney inherited estates in Fen Ditton and surrounding areas through his lineage, which traced back to earlier Cheney holders in the region.[7] Elizabeth Cokayne was the daughter of John Cokayne (d. 1438), a justice of the peace in Derbyshire, and his wife Ida (or Elizabeth) Grey, heiress to properties including Ashbourne Hall.[8] The marriage between Lawrence Cheney and Elizabeth Cokayne likely occurred around 1421, as evidenced by their joint acquisition of lands shortly thereafter, strengthening the family's holdings in eastern England.[9] Elizabeth Cheney had several siblings, including brothers John and William, though records of the full sibling order remain incomplete due to the scarcity of contemporary parish registers from the period.[10]

Paternal and Maternal Lineage

Elizabeth Cheney's paternal lineage derived from the gentry Cheney family, centered in Fen Ditton, Cambridgeshire, with earlier ties to Buckinghamshire manors such as Drayton Parslow.[6] Her father, Lawrence Cheney (c. 1396–1461), served as escheator of Bedfordshire and Buckinghamshire and sheriff of Cambridgeshire and Huntingdonshire; he inherited estates including the manor of Mochettes in Fen Ditton.[11] [12] Lawrence was the son of William Cheney (c. 1368–c. 1399), who received settlement of the Fen Ditton manor in 1377, and Katherine Pabenham (c. 1372–1436), daughter of Lawrence Pabenham of Pabenham, Bedfordshire.[13] [6] William Cheney descended from Henry Cheney, who held lands in the region during the late 14th century and transferred property to his son before 1377.[12] Her maternal ancestry linked to the Cokayne family, Derbyshire landowners who acquired Ashbourne manor by the 1340s through royal grants and service, establishing themselves as local lords until the late 17th century.[14] Elizabeth Cokayne, her mother (c. 1404–after 1461), was the daughter of John Cokayne (c. 1370–1429), appointed Chief Baron of the Exchequer in 1422 and buried at Ashbourne, and Edith (Ida) de Grey (c. 1368–1426).[15] [16] Edith de Grey was the daughter of Reginald Grey, 2nd Baron Grey de Ruthyn (d. 1388), a marcher lord with holdings in Denbighshire, Wales, and Eleanor le Strange; this connection elevated the Cokayne line through baronial associations in the Welsh borders.[17] John Cokayne himself stemmed from earlier generations of the family at Ashbourne, including his father John Cokayne, who expanded estates in Derbyshire and Bedfordshire via purchases such as Bury Hatley.[15]

Marriages

Marriage to Frederick Tilney

Elizabeth Cheney married Frederick Tilney, esquire, of Ashwellthorpe, Norfolk, and Boston, Lincolnshire, sometime before 1445, as evidenced by the birth of their daughter that year or earlier.[3] Tilney, born around 1420, was the son and heir of Philip Tilney, esquire, and his wife Isabel, daughter of Robert Thorpe, esquire, thereby linking Cheney's Cambridgeshire lineage to longstanding Norfolk gentry interests in land and local administration.[2][18] The marriage, documented indirectly through subsequent deeds and heirship records, produced no recorded sons, positioning their sole daughter as primary beneficiary of Tilney's modest but strategic manors centered on Ashwellthorpe Hall. Sir Frederick Tilney died in 1445, leaving Cheney widowed at approximately age 23 and tasked with safeguarding family estates amid the uncertainties of mid-century feudal obligations.[1] His passing, confirmed by inheritance proceedings, transferred holdings to the infant heiress under Cheney's guardianship, underscoring the era's patrilineal constraints where female inheritance often invited later disputes or strategic remarriages.[19] No contemporary accounts detail the marriage's personal dynamics, but it exemplifies typical alliances among the English gentry for consolidating regional influence without evident ties to royal or magnate factions during Henry VI's reign.

Marriage to John Say

Elizabeth Cheney, widowed from her first marriage to Sir Frederick Tilney (d. c.1445), married Sir John Say as his first wife circa November 1446.[20] This union is corroborated by a royal grant dated 11 November 1446 awarding John Say and "Elizabeth his wife" an annuity of £32 10s, indicating the marriage had occurred by that point or was formalized in close proximity.[3] Sir John Say (c.1425–1478), of Poddington, Bedfordshire, and Broxbourne, Hertfordshire, was a member of the gentry whose career advanced through service to the crown; he later served as Speaker of the House of Commons (1449–1450), Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster (1453–1455), and Under Treasurer of England (1460).[20] The marriage aligned with patterns of 15th-century gentry alliances, merging Cheney's Cambridgeshire estates with Say's Hertfordshire and Bedfordshire holdings, though no surviving contemporary records detail specific dowry or jointure arrangements beyond the annuity grant.[3] Elizabeth, as heiress to her father Lawrence Cheney (c.1396–1461), Sheriff of Cambridgeshire and Huntingdonshire, contributed to the couple's economic standing, enabling Say's political ascent during the turbulent mid-century reigns of Henry VI.[2] Genealogical reconstructions, drawing from inquisitions post mortem and heraldic visitations, confirm the partnership's legitimacy without noted disputes over its validity.[20]

Family and Issue

Children from First Marriage

Elizabeth Cheney and her first husband, Frederick Tilney (died before November 1446), had one daughter, Elizabeth Tilney.[3] Elizabeth Tilney was born before 1445 at Ashwellthorpe Hall, Norfolk, and died on 4 April 1497.[19][1] Elizabeth Tilney married firstly circa 1466 Sir Humphrey Bourchier (c. 1435–1471), son of John Bourchier, 1st Baron Berners, and Marjorie Bernard, with whom she had three children: John Bourchier, 2nd Baron Berners (c. 1467–1533); a son Humphrey Bourchier (died young); and possibly another issue, though records primarily confirm the heir John.[3] Following Bourchier's death at the Battle of Barnet on 14 April 1471, she married secondly by 30 April 1472 Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk (1443–1524), bearing him at least seven children, including Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk (1478–1554); Edward Howard (c. 1478–1513); and Elizabeth Howard (c. 1480–1538), who became mother to Anne Boleyn and Catherine Howard.[19] As sole heiress to her father's Tilney estates, Elizabeth Tilney inherited significant lands in Norfolk and Lincolnshire, which passed through her descendants.[3]

Children from Second Marriage

Elizabeth Cheney and Sir John Say had eight children, of whom seven survived childhood: three sons and four daughters.[21] Their eldest son, Sir William Say (c. 1452–1529), was knighted and served as Sheriff of Essex and Hertfordshire in 1489 and 1494, as well as Constable of Colchester Castle; he married Elizabeth Fray, daughter of Sir John Fray, and inherited the family estates at Broxbourne, Hertfordshire.[21][22] The second son, Thomas Say, is noted in contemporary pedigrees but left limited records of his life or issue.[3] The youngest son, Leonard Say, pursued a clerical career, serving as Rector of Spaxton, Somerset.[3] Among the daughters, Anne Say (d. before 22 October 1484) married Sir Henry Wentworth of Nettlestead, Suffolk, KG (c. 1448–1501), by whom she had issue including Margaret Wentworth (c. 1478–1550), who married Sir John Seymour and was the mother of Queen Jane Seymour, third wife of Henry VIII.[21][23] Elizabeth Say married Thomas Sampson, Esq., of Kersey, Suffolk.[3] Katherine Say wed Robert Wenlock, Esq.[3] The youngest daughter, Mary Say, entered religious life as a nun.[3] These offspring linked the Cheney-Say lineage to prominent Tudor families through strategic marriages, enhancing the family's influence in late medieval England.[21]

Landholdings and Economic Role

Inherited and Acquired Estates

Elizabeth Cheney inherited estates primarily from her father, Laurence Cheney, esquire of Fen Ditton, Cambridgeshire, who died on 31 December 1461.[24] His holdings centered on Fen Ditton and extended to lands in Cambridgeshire, Bedfordshire, and Northamptonshire.[3] She further inherited substantial properties following the death of her brother, John Cheney, with the inquisition post mortem valuing her share at £271 12s. 8d. annually.[25] Through her first marriage to Sir Frederick Tilney, who died in 1445, Cheney acquired dower rights to portions of the Tilney estates, including lands in Suffolk provided in full satisfaction of her dower.[26] Prior to her second marriage to Sir John Say in 1446, her father settled additional lands yielding fifty marks clear per annum on the couple and their issue, effective before Candlemas 1453. This settlement augmented her holdings in support of the marriage. Via the Say union, she gained access to jointure arrangements from Say's properties, which included manors in Hertfordshire such as Broxbourne and Sawbridgeworth in Essex, where Say held seisin at his death in 1478.[27][28] These acquisitions through marital settlements and dower rights significantly expanded her economic base beyond paternal inheritance.

Management and Inheritance Disputes

Following the death of her first husband, Sir Frederick Tilney, in or before 1447, Elizabeth Cheney took charge of the guardianship of their only child and heir, Elizabeth Tilney (c.1445–1497), thereby gaining administrative control over the Tilney estates, primarily Ashwellthorpe Hall and associated manors in Norfolk and Lincolnshire..htm) As the minor heir's custodian, Cheney directed the day-to-day operations, including agricultural output and tenant relations, to preserve the value of holdings valued at several hundred pounds annually in rents, preventing depletion during the approximately 30-year minority period until full livery of seisin was granted to her daughter around 1475..htm) Her remarriage to Sir John Say by early 1449 integrated Cheney's inherited Cheney family lands in Fen Ditton, Cambridgeshire, and Northamptonshire with Say's properties in Broxbourne, Hertfordshire, and Essex, creating a consolidated portfolio exceeding 20 manors across eastern England.[29] Jointly, they employed common law devices like the levying of fines and common recoveries to disentail and re-entail properties, directing succession to male heirs from their union—such as sons Frederick, John, and William Say—while protecting dower rights and barring claims from female lines or collateral relatives that could fragment the estates under gavelkind or primogeniture customs..htm) These legal maneuvers, documented in court rolls from the period, resolved latent tensions over Tilney collateral branches' potential reversionary interests by affirming the direct line's priority. No protracted litigation over core holdings is attested during Cheney's tenure, though routine disputes with tenants over customary rents and services arose, as typical for 15th-century gentry management; she enforced obligations through local manorial courts, recovering arrears equivalent to 10-20% of annual yields in some years.[29] Upon her death on 25 September 1473, the estates devolved smoothly to her children per prior settlements, with Say continuing oversight until his own death in 1478, after which minor adjustments occurred via his will favoring Cheney-issue sons.[29] This stability underscores Cheney's strategic use of widowhood rights and marital alliances to safeguard familial wealth amid the Wars of the Roses' uncertainties.

Connections to Later Historical Events

Influence via Descendants in the Tudor Era

Elizabeth Cheney's descendants exerted considerable influence during the Tudor period (1485–1603) through strategic marriages into noble families and direct ties to the royal court, particularly under Henry VIII (r. 1509–1547). Her daughter from her first marriage, Elizabeth Tilney (c. 1445–1497), wed Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk (1443–1524), linking the Cheney line to the powerful Howard dynasty. Their son, Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk (1473–1554), rose to prominence as a key advisor, serving as Lord High Treasurer from 1523 to 1546 and Earl Marshal, roles that positioned him at the center of military campaigns, such as the victory at Flodden in 1513 (under his father's command, though he succeeded in influence), and domestic intrigues including the orchestration of Anne Boleyn's marriage to Henry VIII in 1533 and her subsequent trial in 1536.[2][30] Norfolk's maneuvers helped shape the Henrician Reformation and court factions, though his ambitions led to imprisonment in 1546–1553. Tilney and Howard's daughter, Elizabeth Howard (c. 1480–1513), married Thomas Boleyn, 1st Earl of Wiltshire (c. 1477–1539), producing Anne Boleyn (c. 1501–1536), who became Henry VIII's second queen consort in 1533, mother to Elizabeth I (r. 1558–1603), and a catalyst for England's break from the Catholic Church via the Act of Supremacy in 1534. Another child of Tilney and Howard, Edmund Howard (c. 1478–1539), fathered Catherine Howard (c. 1523–1542), Henry VIII's fifth queen in 1540, whose brief reign underscored Howard familial leverage before her execution for adultery and treason. These connections elevated the Howards as a counterweight to rivals like the Seymours, influencing succession politics and religious policy.[2] From her second marriage to John Say (d. 1478), Cheney's daughter Mary Say (c. 1450–c. 1470) married William Bourchier (d. 1484), leading to descendants including Elizabeth Bourchier (c. 1472–1555), who wed Robert Wentworth (d. 1528); their daughter Margaret Wentworth (c. 1478–1551) married Henry Seymour of Wolf Hall (d. 1510), producing Jane Seymour (c. 1508–1537), Henry VIII's third queen in 1536 and mother of Edward VI (1537–1553). Jane's union shifted alliances post-Anne Boleyn's fall, stabilizing the succession with a male heir and advancing Protestant leanings. Her brother Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset (c. 1500–1552), leveraged this kinship to become Lord Protector from 1547 to 1549, directing regency policies like the dissolution of chantries in 1547 and early Reformation reforms before his execution for treason.[2][31] Cheney's multifaceted lineage thus intersected with three of Henry VIII's consorts, amplifying noble influence on monarchical decisions, religious upheaval, and dynastic continuity.

Genealogical Ties to English Monarchy

Elizabeth Cheney's genealogical connections to the English monarchy stem primarily from her female descendants, who married into prominent families and produced queens consort to Henry VIII, thereby linking her lineage to Tudor sovereigns. Through her first marriage to Sir Frederick Tilney (d. 1445), she was the mother of Elizabeth Tilney (c. 1445–1495), who wed Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk (1443–1524). This union yielded Elizabeth Howard (c. 1480–1538), who married Thomas Boleyn, 1st Earl of Wiltshire (c. 1477–1539), and bore Anne Boleyn (c. 1501–1536), Henry VIII's second wife; Anne's execution in 1536 followed the birth of their daughter, Elizabeth I (1533–1603), making Cheney the great-great-grandmother of the Virgin Queen.[1][2] The same Howard-Tilney line also produced Edmund Howard (c. 1478–1539), father of Catherine Howard (c. 1523–1542), Henry VIII's fifth wife, reinforcing Cheney's proximity to the throne through multiple consorts, though Catherine bore no children. Her second marriage to Sir John Say (d. 1478), Speaker of the House of Commons, produced Anne Say (d. c. 1484), who married Sir Henry Wentworth (c. 1448–1501); their daughter, Margaret Wentworth (c. 1478–1550), wed Sir John Seymour (c. 1476–1536) and gave birth to Jane Seymour (c. 1508–1537), Henry VIII's third wife. Jane died shortly after delivering Edward VI (1537–1553), rendering Cheney the great-great-grandmother of this king, whose brief reign (1547–1553) marked the final Tudor male succession.[2][1] These descent lines, documented in medieval pedigrees and escheator records, positioned Cheney as a common ancestress to three of Henry VIII's queens—Anne Boleyn, Jane Seymour, and Catherine Howard—whose relationships as second cousins underscored the interconnected gentry networks facilitating royal alliances.[3] No direct evidence links Cheney's own ancestry to prior English monarchs; her parents, Lawrence Cheyne (c. 1394–1436) and Elizabeth Cokayne (d. after 1461), traced to knightly Shropshire and Derbyshire lines without royal intermarriage noted in contemporary inquisitions post mortem.[3] Her ties thus represent downward mobility from gentry to royal progenitors, influencing Tudor legitimacy claims amid the dynasty's reliance on Howard and Seymour affinities for political stability post-Bosworth.[2]

Death and Legacy

Final Years and Death

Elizabeth Cheney died on 25 September 1473, at the age of 51.[2][3][1] Her death occurred during a period of political instability in England amid the Wars of the Roses, though no records indicate her direct involvement in contemporary events.[3] She was buried in the Church of St Augustine at Broxbourne, Hertfordshire, a location associated with her second husband Sir John Say's family estates.[3][2] A monumental inscription at the site commemorates her as "dame Elizabeth Say late wyf to Syr John Say knight," noting her decease on that date and invoking prayers for her soul.[3] No testament or inquisition post mortem for Cheney herself survives in readily accessible records, limiting details on her final personal affairs or estate dispositions.[3] Sir John Say, who had served as Speaker of the House of Commons and held roles under both Lancastrian and Yorkist regimes, outlived her by nearly five years, remarrying Agnes Danvers before October 1474 and dying on 12 April 1478.[2][3]

Burial and Posthumous Recognition

Elizabeth Cheney died on 25 September 1473 and was buried in St Augustine's Church, Broxbourne, Hertfordshire.[3][1][2] A monumental brass in the church commemorates her alongside her second husband, Sir John Say, who survived her and was later buried there as well; the figures depict the couple in period attire, with Elizabeth shown in a heraldic mantle.[32][33] The accompanying inscription identifies her parentage, marriages, and death: "Here lyth dame Elizabeth wyf to John Say squyer for body & c to Kyng Edward the iiij dame Elizabeth was dowther to Sir Lawrence Cheyne knyght & to Elizabeth hys wyf dowther to Sr John Cokayne knyght the sayd Elizabeth Saye decessed the xxv day of September the yere of our lorde Mccccc lxxiij on whos soule god have mercy. Amen."[3] Posthumous recognition of Cheney derives chiefly from her genealogical connections, as the mother of Elizabeth Tilney (who married Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk) and thus great-grandmother to Anne Boleyn and Catherine Howard, both queens consort to Henry VIII.[2][1] The Broxbourne brass has endured as a historical artifact, inspiring later reproductions such as 19th- and 20th-century rubbings and colored oil paintings based on it, held in collections like those of the National Trust.[34][35] No evidence exists of formal honors or widespread contemporary commemoration beyond these familial and monumental elements.
