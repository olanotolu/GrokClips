New Murabba

New Murabba is a 19-square-kilometer mixed-use real estate development under construction in northwestern Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, launched in 2023 by Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman to establish the world's largest modern downtown district.[1][2] The project spans over 25 million square meters of floor area, incorporating 104,000 residential units, 9,000 hotel rooms, 300,000 square meters of retail space, and 80 entertainment venues, with the Mukaab serving as its iconic centerpiece—a 400-meter cubic structure equipped with holographic displays and immersive technologies for retail, cultural, and recreational purposes.[1] Aligned with Saudi Vision 2030, New Murabba aims to contribute more than $47 billion to non-oil GDP through economic diversification, featuring sustainable elements such as 25% green space, renewable energy systems, and a 15-minute city layout where amenities are accessible by foot, alongside AI-driven infrastructure inspired by local heritage.[1][2] Key components include four mixed-use corner towers and the New Murabba Stadium with 45,000 capacity, positioning the development as a hub for innovation, tourism, and urban living with projected completion phases advancing through 2030.[2][1]

Background and Development

Announcement and Strategic Context

The New Murabba project was announced on February 16, 2023, by Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, who launched the New Murabba Development Company (NMDC), a subsidiary of the Public Investment Fund (PIF), to oversee its execution.[3][4] The initiative aims to construct the world's largest modern downtown district in Riyadh, covering 19 square kilometers adjacent to the historic al-Murabba neighborhood, with an estimated investment of SAR 500 billion.[1][5] This development is positioned as a transformative urban project intended to integrate residential, commercial, cultural, and entertainment spaces within a compact, walkable 15-minute radius, supported by an internal transportation system.[3] Strategically, New Murabba aligns with Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 program, launched in 2016 to diversify the economy away from oil dependency through non-oil sector growth, tourism enhancement, and urban modernization.[1] The project is projected to generate 334,000 direct and indirect jobs and contribute SAR 180 billion annually to the non-oil gross domestic product by 2030, fostering economic resilience and positioning Riyadh as a global hub for innovation and lifestyle.[3] It supports Vision 2030's pillars of economic diversification, job creation, and sustainable urban development, including preparations for Expo 2030 in Riyadh, by emphasizing mixed-use districts that promote live-work-play integration and reduce reliance on traditional commuting.[1][6] The announcement underscores the Saudi government's commitment to giga-projects under PIF stewardship, drawing on historical precedents like the original al-Murabba district—Riyadh's foundational quarter established in the early 20th century—to blend heritage with futuristic urbanism.[3] While Vision 2030 initiatives have accelerated infrastructure investment, the project's scale has prompted scrutiny over feasibility and funding amid global economic pressures, though official projections emphasize its role in elevating Saudi Arabia's international profile.[5]

Location and Physical Scale

New Murabba is situated in northwestern central Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, at the intersection of King Salman Road and King Khalid Road.[3] This positioning integrates the development into the city's urban fabric while extending toward less developed northern areas.[7] The project encompasses 19 square kilometers of land area, equivalent to approximately 7.3 square miles.[1] [7] Within this footprint, it plans for over 25 million square meters of total floor space, supporting mixed-use development including residential, commercial, and cultural facilities.[1] [7] Physically, New Murabba draws inspiration from the historic al-Murabba district, aiming to create a modern downtown extension that balances density with open spaces.[3] The scale is designed to accommodate up to 1.4 million residents and visitors daily, emphasizing vertical and horizontal expansion to optimize land use in Riyadh's arid environment.[7] Infrastructure integration includes enhanced road networks and proximity to existing transport hubs, facilitating connectivity across the capital.[3]

Funding and Governance

The New Murabba Development Company (NMDC), established in 2023, serves as the primary entity overseeing the project and is wholly owned by Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF), the kingdom's sovereign wealth fund managing assets exceeding $900 billion as of 2024.[3][8][9] Funding for New Murabba originates predominantly from PIF allocations, supporting the estimated $500 billion total investment required to realize the development's 7.5 square mile urban expanse by 2030.[10][11] This state-backed financing aligns with PIF's mandate to drive Vision 2030 diversification, with the project projected to contribute SAR 180 billion to non-oil GDP through real estate, technology, and construction sectors.[3][12] Supplementary opportunities for private and institutional investment are being pursued, including partnerships like the 2024 collaboration with the Tourism Development Fund to enhance hospitality components.[13] Governance of New Murabba falls under NMDC's board of directors, chaired by Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman bin Abdulaziz Al Saud, who also serves as prime minister and PIF governor, ensuring strategic alignment with national priorities.[3][14] Board members include PIF representatives, providing oversight on operational, financial, and sustainability aspects, such as the 2024 launch of environmental, social, and governance (ESG) frameworks.[14][15] The CEO, appointed to lead execution, reports to the board amid efforts to attract global expertise in project delivery.[12]

Architectural and Urban Design

Core Landmark: The Mukaab

The Mukaab is a monumental cube-shaped skyscraper designed as the iconic centerpiece of the New Murabba urban development in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia. Measuring 400 meters in height, width, and length, it will encompass 2 million square meters of floor space, positioning it as the world's largest building by area and the tallest structure in Riyadh.[16] The design fuses elements of traditional Najdi architecture with contemporary innovation, aiming to create a mixed-use landmark that integrates cultural, commercial, and experiential functions.[16] [17] Announced on February 16, 2023, by Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman as part of the Public Investment Fund-backed New Murabba project, the Mukaab supports Saudi Vision 2030 goals by fostering advanced urban living and entertainment.[17] [2] Construction began with groundbreaking in October 2024, with the structure projected for completion by 2030 at an estimated cost of $50 billion.[18] [19] [20] The interior emphasizes immersive technologies, featuring over 10 key attractions with multi-layered sensory experiences that blend sight, sound, and touch through 3D hologram shows, advanced digital displays, state-of-the-art lighting, and high-end audio systems.[16] [21] These elements enable interactive environments transporting visitors to virtual worlds, alongside premium hospitality, retail, cultural venues, and public art programs.[21] The Mukaab's volumetric displays are planned to surpass the scale of those in Dubai Mall, enhancing its role as a hub for entertainment and technological demonstration.[22]

Sustainability and Technology Integration

New Murabba incorporates sustainability as a core principle, aiming for net-zero emissions by 2060 through decarbonization strategies that prioritize environmental impact minimization, resource efficiency, and alignment with Saudi Green Initiatives.[23][24] The project dedicates 25% of its 19 square kilometers to green spaces, equivalent to approximately 4.75 square kilometers of parks, gardens, and natural areas designed to enhance biodiversity and urban cooling via passive architecture techniques such as shading and natural ventilation.[25][26] Another 25% of the land is allocated to mobility infrastructure supporting low-emission transport, including extensive pedestrian and cycling networks to reduce reliance on vehicles.[26] Renewable energy integration features prominently, with solar panels planned across buildings and infrastructure to generate clean power and offset operational demands, complemented by energy-efficient systems in construction materials and building designs certified under green building standards.[23][27] Water management employs advanced recycling and conservation technologies to achieve sustainable usage in this arid region, while waste reduction targets circular economy principles through on-site processing and minimal landfill contributions.[24] These efforts position New Murabba as a model for low-carbon urban development, with environmental, social, and governance (ESG) frameworks outlined in December 2024 to guide operations toward Vision 2030's sustainability pillars.[15] Technology integration transforms New Murabba into a smart city, leveraging Internet of Things (IoT) sensors, artificial intelligence (AI), and data analytics for real-time urban management, including traffic optimization, energy distribution, and public safety.[28] Strategic partnerships enhance this, such as the June 2025 alliance with NAVER Cloud for cloud-based smart infrastructure supporting predictive maintenance and resident services, and a November 2024 agreement with stc Group for advanced information and communications technology (ICT) deployment.[29][30] The 15-minute city model is enabled by these technologies, ensuring most daily needs—work, leisure, and services—are accessible within a short walk or cycle, reducing emissions and promoting livable urban density.[31][27]

Pedestrian-Oriented Urban Layout

New Murabba's urban layout emphasizes pedestrian accessibility and walkability, integrating extensive networks of pathways, green corridors, and mixed-use zones to minimize reliance on vehicular transport. The development allocates 25% of its land to mobility infrastructure, including an 11-kilometer urban loop dedicated to walking, cycling, and non-motorized activities, fostering a seamless pedestrian experience across the 19-square-kilometer site.[26] This design prioritizes human-scale navigation, with vibrant plazas, landscaped wadis, and integrated art installations that guide foot traffic through distinct experiential zones known as Wahaa.[32] Central to the layout is the "15-minute city" model, ensuring residents can reach essential amenities—such as shops, schools, healthcare facilities, parks, and mosques—within a 15-minute walk from any residential point, promoting reduced congestion and enhanced quality of life.[2] Pedestrian-friendly features include dedicated walking and cycling paths weaving through green spaces that occupy another 25% of the area, alongside urban parkways that connect residential, commercial, and recreational districts.[3] These elements draw from sustainability principles, aiming to encourage active lifestyles while integrating technology for wayfinding and lighting to create safe, memorable journeys.[27] The pedestrian orientation extends around key landmarks like the Mukaab, where surrounding residential, office, and entertainment areas form a walkable core that discourages car dominance through car-free zones and proximity-based planning.[33] This approach aligns with broader goals of urban vitality, though implementation details remain subject to ongoing construction phases initiated in 2023.[9]

Key Project Components

Residential and Workforce Housing

The New Murabba project incorporates over 104,000 residential units within its 19 square kilometer footprint, aimed at accommodating around 420,000 residents in a modern downtown setting.[1][34] These units form a core element of the mixed-use development, launched by Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman in February 2023, and are projected to integrate with over 25 million square meters of total floor area including commercial and leisure spaces.[35] Housing design prioritizes accessibility and livability, adhering to a 15-minute city model where residences are within walking distance of shops, parks, schools, healthcare facilities, and mosques, supported by pedestrian-oriented infrastructure and internal transport systems.[2] Sustainability features include low-carbon construction materials, renewable energy integration, and allocation of 25% of the site to green spaces to enhance urban ecosystems and resident well-being.[2] Unit types range from apartments suited for young professionals to family homes, fostering a diverse demographic while aligning with the project's emphasis on healthy, immersive living environments.[36] Workforce housing is embedded within the broader residential framework, providing options proximate to employment hubs in office and retail districts to support the estimated 440,000 direct and indirect jobs generated by the development.[1] While specific allocations for affordable or dedicated workforce units remain limited in public disclosures—such as a noted reservation of 169 apartments for affordability in select components—no large-scale separate accommodations for construction or transient labor are outlined, reflecting the focus on permanent urban residency.[37] This integration aims to reduce commute times and promote economic vitality, though feasibility depends on broader Riyadh housing market dynamics.[2]

Commercial and Retail Districts

The commercial and retail components of New Murabba are integrated into a mixed-use urban framework spanning over 19 square kilometers, emphasizing accessibility and sustainability over traditional segregated zoning. The project allocates more than 980,000 square meters to retail space and 1.4 million square meters to office space, distributed across neighborhoods to support a "live-work-play" model where essential amenities, including shops and employment hubs, lie within a 15-minute walking radius of residences.[3][32] Retail facilities feature avenues lined with premium outlets, local shops, and corner stores embedded in mixed-use areas, alongside high-end shopping integrated into the Mukaab structure, which combines retail with cultural and tourist attractions. These spaces prioritize pedestrian connectivity, linking to public transport corridors and enhanced by urban elements such as plazas, wadis, and landscaping to foster vibrant, experiential shopping environments. Primary retail zones are strategically positioned near mobility loops and green corridors, promoting seamless integration with leisure assets totaling 620,000 square meters, including over 80 entertainment venues.[3][32] Commercial districts focus on Grade-A office developments and distributed employment hubs aligned with major transport routes, such as King Salman and King Khalid roads, to facilitate efficient commuting and reduce reliance on centralized business areas. This approach supports economic diversification under Saudi Vision 2030 by attracting private sector investment in mixed-use real estate, with opportunities for developers in retail-hospitality synergies around the Mukaab. The design incorporates sustainable features like bioswale drainage and biodiversity zones to mitigate urban heat and enhance long-term viability of these workspaces.[3][32][38]

Entertainment, Culture, and Recreation Facilities

New Murabba incorporates over 80 state-of-the-art entertainment and cultural venues designed to foster a vibrant district within Riyadh.[1][39] These facilities emphasize immersive experiences, including a multipurpose immersive theater and an iconic museum, aimed at attracting tourists and residents alike.[40][9] The central Mukaab structure will host advanced entertainment features such as 3D hologram shows and a dedicated entertainment center, leveraging modern lighting and holographic technology for visitor engagement.[21] A key component is the New Murabba Stadium, a 45,000-seat multi-purpose venue capable of hosting diverse sporting events, concerts, and other entertainment activities through its flexible, adaptable spaces.[41][42] Positioned to enhance community interaction, the stadium integrates with surrounding areas to support long-term usability beyond initial events.[43] Recreational amenities prioritize accessibility, with the project structured as a "15-minute city" where residences are within walking distance of parks, green spaces, and community facilities spanning approximately 1.8 million square meters.[2][44] These elements include extensive green areas and leisure zones intended to improve quality of life and promote outdoor activities amid the urban layout.[45]

Economic and Strategic Impacts

Alignment with Saudi Vision 2030

New Murabba is listed among the strategic projects under Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030, which seeks to foster a thriving economy through diversification, enhanced quality of life, and sustainable urban development.[1] The initiative aims to create a 19 km² mixed-use district in Riyadh, integrating residential, commercial, and entertainment spaces to redefine the city's urban landscape and support national goals of economic resilience beyond oil revenues.[1] [6] In terms of economic diversification, New Murabba is projected to contribute over $47 billion to non-oil GDP by developing 14 million m² of office space and 980,000 m² of retail, attracting international investment and fostering sectors like technology, tourism, and logistics.[1] [6] [3] This aligns with Vision 2030's thriving economy pillar by reducing oil dependency through innovative urban projects that promote private sector growth and global partnerships.[6] Additionally, it is expected to generate 334,000 direct and indirect jobs, emphasizing opportunities in construction, sustainability, and digital innovation to build a skilled national workforce.[3] [39] The project's sustainability efforts further support Vision 2030's ambitious nation pillar and the Saudi Green Initiative, incorporating ESG frameworks focused on decarbonization, renewable energy adoption, energy efficiency, and low-carbon materials to achieve carbon neutrality by 2060.[24] Features such as urban greenery, car-free zones, and a 15-minute city design minimize environmental impact while enhancing livability, directly addressing climate action and resource management objectives.[6] [24] Social components, including 104,000 residential units, 9,000 hotel rooms, and 80 entertainment venues, promote a vibrant society by improving access to culture, recreation, and efficient internal transport systems.[1]

Investment Opportunities and Job Creation

The New Murabba project is anticipated to generate 334,000 direct and indirect jobs by 2030, contributing to workforce development in construction, technology, real estate, and related services as part of Riyadh's urban expansion.[3][2] This employment forecast aligns with the initiative's scale, encompassing over 100,000 residential units, commercial districts, and entertainment facilities, which will require sustained labor input during the construction phase and ongoing operations post-completion in 2030.[46] The Public Investment Fund (PIF), which owns the New Murabba Development Company, has emphasized these job outcomes as a mechanism to bolster Saudi Arabia's non-oil economy, with roles spanning skilled trades, engineering, and digital innovation.[3] Investment opportunities within New Murabba target sectors such as technology, real estate, and construction, with the CEO highlighting potential for private sector participation to drive urban growth and innovation.[47] The project has actively promoted these avenues at events like the PIF Private Forum and the Future Investment Initiative, seeking international partnerships to fund infrastructure, smart city technologies, and commercial developments.[48][49] Real estate brokers and investment partners can register for exclusive access to property and development deals, while vendor collaborations in areas like hospitality and retail are encouraged through dedicated portals.[50] Overall, these opportunities are projected to channel investments toward a SAR 180 billion addition to non-oil GDP, fostering long-term economic multipliers beyond initial construction.[3][51]

Diversification from Oil Dependency

New Murabba aligns with Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 initiative to reduce oil dependency, which has historically accounted for approximately 40% of the kingdom's GDP and over two-thirds of fiscal revenues.[52] The project emphasizes development in non-oil sectors such as tourism, entertainment, retail, and technology, fostering a shift toward a knowledge-based economy less vulnerable to global oil price fluctuations.[1] By 2030, it is projected to contribute SAR 180 billion (approximately $48 billion) to non-oil GDP, supporting broader goals to triple non-oil contributions through investments in urban innovation hubs.[3][6] The development's mixed-use design, encompassing 104 square kilometers of residential, commercial, and cultural districts, is expected to generate 334,000 direct and indirect jobs primarily in service-oriented industries.[3] These roles span entertainment facilities, immersive retail spaces, and workforce housing, which prioritize high-skill employment in hospitality, digital services, and creative industries over extractive activities.[53] Such job creation counters oil sector volatility, as evidenced by non-oil GDP growth accelerating to 4.93% in the first half of 2023 from 1.82% in 2016.[54] Foreign direct investment is anticipated to surge due to New Murabba's appeal as a global destination, with incentives for tech firms and tourism operators to establish operations in Riyadh.[53] This influx targets diversification into renewables-adjacent tech and experiential economies, potentially offsetting oil revenue dips—such as those from production cuts under OPEC+ agreements—by enhancing private sector resilience.[55] Official projections indicate the project's completion by 2030 will integrate with giga-projects like NEOM and Qiddiya, amplifying cumulative non-oil economic multipliers across the kingdom.[46]

Controversies and Criticisms

Religious and Cultural Objections to the Mukaab Design

The Mukaab's cubic design, measuring 400 meters on each side, has drawn religious objections primarily for its visual resemblance to the Kaaba, the cube-shaped structure in Mecca toward which Muslims direct their prayers and which holds unparalleled sanctity in Islam. Critics argue that replicating this form for a secular entertainment and commercial complex risks trivializing or mocking a symbol central to tawhid (the oneness of God), with some invoking Islamic prohibitions against imitating or embellishing representations of the Kaaba, as noted by historical scholars who deem such acts haram (forbidden).[56][57] Upon the project's announcement on February 19, 2023, Muslims on social media platforms expressed widespread anger, labeling it a "new Kaaba" or an act of blasphemy, with hashtags and posts amplifying fears that the structure could confuse the faithful or dilute the Kaaba's uniqueness.[58][59] Objections from self-identified Muslim scholars and commentators worldwide highlighted this as part of broader concerns over Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 initiatives, which they view as prioritizing entertainment over religious preservation, potentially eroding Islamic identity in the kingdom.[60] Culturally, the design has been criticized for clashing with Saudi Arabia's Wahhabi-influenced heritage, where ostentatious mega-projects are seen by traditionalists as Western-inspired excesses that overshadow modest Islamic architectural principles, such as those in the original Murabba Palace from which the project claims indirect inspiration.[61] Saudi officials have countered that the Mukaab draws from pre-Islamic cubic motifs in Najdi architecture rather than the Kaaba, emphasizing its role in economic diversification without religious intent.[62] Despite these assurances, the controversy persists, reflecting tensions between modernization and cultural conservatism in the kingdom.[63]

Skepticism on Feasibility and Financial Viability

Critics have questioned the financial viability of the New Murabba project, particularly the Mukaab centerpiece, estimated at $50 billion in construction costs alone, amid Saudi Arabia's reliance on oil revenues that have fallen short of fiscal needs.[64] The kingdom requires an oil price of approximately $98 per barrel to balance its budget, but prices have hovered around $73 per barrel, straining funding for megaprojects and prompting potential budget cuts for initiatives like the Mukaab.[65] Saudi Finance Minister Mohammed Al-Jadaan has acknowledged delays or extensions in such projects as beneficial for the economy, reflecting broader fiscal pressures including government deficits projected at 3.5% of GDP in 2025.[65][66] Princeton University professor Bernard Haykel has argued that Saudi Arabia lacks sufficient funds due to insufficient oil prices, echoing concerns over foreign investment shortfalls and parallels with scaled-back projects like NEOM, which faced a 20% budget reduction and $8 billion in impairments in 2024.[65] Literature on megaprojects highlights frequent cost overruns and economic viability doubts, with Saudi giga-projects contributing to rising fiscal risks as spending mounts despite efforts to diversify from oil.[67][68] Engineering feasibility poses additional challenges for the 400-meter cube, which demands unprecedented structural solutions for its flat roof, high load-bearing requirements across floors, and immense energy needs for climate control in Riyadh's desert environment.[61] New Murabba's CEO has described the Mukaab as the most challenging engineering project in modern construction history, raising doubts about practicality and livability within such a hyper-dense structure.[69] Critics point to the financial and technical hurdles of scaling to fit 20 Empire State Buildings inside, including ventilation, transportation, and resource demands that could exacerbate overruns seen in similar ambitious builds.[70][71]

Environmental and Social Displacement Concerns

The New Murabba project, encompassing 19 square kilometers in northern Riyadh, has prompted scrutiny over its environmental sustainability amid Saudi Arabia's arid climate and Vision 2030 net-zero ambitions by 2060. Critics argue that the scale of the development, including the 2-million-square-meter Mukaab structure—equivalent in floor area to 20 Empire State Buildings—exacerbates resource demands in a water-scarce region, necessitating vast quantities of water for construction and operations despite official pledges for a closed-loop system recycling 95% of wastewater.[72][73] Construction activities have already displaced over 10 million cubic meters of earth, generating significant waste and contributing to high carbon emissions from material transport and on-site processes.[74][72] Energy demands pose further challenges, as the project's massive enclosed spaces require continuous cooling and lighting in Riyadh's extreme heat, potentially straining the kingdom's electricity grid reliant on fossil fuels, even with commitments to 100% electric public transport and solar integration.[72][23] Habitat disruption is another concern, with the large footprint likely impacting local desert ecosystems, including potential destruction of sparse native flora and fauna habitats, though project proponents emphasize creating a "biodiversity oasis" with 25% green coverage.[72][11] These issues reflect broader skepticism toward megaprojects' alignment with climate goals, where promotional sustainability claims may overlook the inherent environmental costs of rapid desert urbanization.[72] Regarding social displacement, no verified reports indicate forced relocations or evictions of existing residents, as the site appears to be largely undeveloped peripheral land rather than established communities.[2] The project plans for 104,000 residential units to house up to 420,000 people, potentially easing housing pressures in Riyadh without direct displacement, though indirect effects such as rising regional land values or infrastructure strain on nearby areas could influence local populations over time.[75][51] Official ESG strategies highlight community-focused design, but independent assessments of long-term social impacts remain limited.[24]

Construction and Timeline

Major Milestones and Progress

The New Murabba project was publicly announced on February 16, 2023, by Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, envisioning a 19-square-kilometer urban district in northern Riyadh as a flagship initiative under Vision 2030.[76] Early planning phases focused on design conceptualization, including the Mukaab—a 400-meter cubic structure intended as the project's centerpiece—with architectural competitions and feasibility studies completed by mid-2023.[2] Groundbreaking for site preparation and excavation commenced in late 2023, transitioning into full-scale earthworks by early 2024.[77] Excavation at the Mukaab and surrounding podium sites advanced rapidly, reaching 86% completion by October 15, 2024, with over 10 million cubic meters of earth relocated and more than 3 million safe work hours logged, emphasizing stringent safety protocols.[77] By October 16, 2025, excavation was fully completed, totaling over 14 million cubic meters of material moved, marking a transition to foundational infrastructure and structural works.[78] This phase aligned with procurement milestones, including a $1 billion steel supply contract awarded in September 2025 to support the Mukaab's massive framework, capable of housing the equivalent of 20 Empire State Buildings by volume.[20] Additional progress in 2025 included strategic partnerships, such as an August memorandum of understanding with Alat to integrate advanced manufacturing technologies into the Mukaab's construction and operations.[79] Corporate infrastructure development advanced concurrently, with office spaces and vendor onboarding initiated to support on-site operations.[2] The project remains on track for phased openings starting in 2030, though full completion of core elements like the Mukaab is targeted for that year, amid ongoing monitoring of logistical and supply chain efficiencies.[80]

International Partnerships and Recent Advancements

New Murabba Development Company, a subsidiary of Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, has pursued multiple international partnerships to integrate advanced technologies and designs into the project. In June 2025, it signed an agreement with South Korea's NAVER Cloud to collaborate on robotics, autonomous vehicles, and smart city infrastructure, aiming to enhance urban innovation aligned with Saudi Vision 2030.[29] Similarly, a memorandum of understanding (MoU) with Honeywell, a U.S.-based multinational in automation and energy solutions, was established to bolster digital infrastructure and building controls within the development.[81] Architectural and experiential collaborations have also expanded globally. In July 2025, New Murabba entered an MoU with Korea's Heerim Architects & Planners to explore innovative urban designs, signed during a visit to Seoul by CEO Michael Dyke.[82] This was followed in August 2025 by a long-term strategic agreement with Falcon's Creative Group, an international firm specializing in themed entertainment, to develop AI-powered immersive experiences for The Mukaab, the project's central cube structure.[83] Additional ties include cooperation with South Korea's Hanam City on sustainable urban planning and infrastructure sharing best practices.[84] Recent advancements include heightened international engagement through events and investment outreach. In September 2025, New Murabba showcased its vision at Expo 2025 Osaka, where CEO Dyke delivered a keynote on integrating design, technology, and community value, while inviting global innovators for potential collaborations.[85] By October 2025, the company announced plans for investment opportunities in technology, real estate, and construction sectors to attract foreign capital, as stated by Dyke amid participation in forums like TOURISE 2025 and the Cityscape series.[12] Construction progress has advanced from initial Mukaab excavation to infrastructure development and establishment of corporate offices, supporting the 2030 completion target.[2]
