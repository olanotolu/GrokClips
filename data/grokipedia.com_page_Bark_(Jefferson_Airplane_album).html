Bark (Jefferson Airplane album)


Bark is the sixth studio album by the American rock band Jefferson Airplane, released in September 1971 on Grunt Records.[1][2] The record marked the band's first release on their self-established Grunt label, an autonomous imprint under RCA Records, following a two-year gap since their previous studio effort, Volunteers.[2] It reflected significant lineup shifts, including the departure of founding vocalist Marty Balin and the replacement of drummer Spencer Dryden with Joey Covington, alongside the addition of violinist Papa John Creach, resulting in a core ensemble of Paul Kantner on rhythm guitar and vocals, Jorma Kaukonen on lead guitar, Jack Casady on bass, Grace Slick on vocals, and Covington on drums.[1][3] Featuring a tracklist blending psychedelic rock, folk influences, and individual band member contributionsâ€”such as the single "Pretty as You Feel" and Jorma Kaukonen's "Third Week in the Chelsea"â€”the album showcased stylistic diversity but was critiqued for lacking cohesive unity, often resembling a compilation of solo efforts rather than collaborative band work.[1][2] Reception was mixed, with praise for standout tracks amid perceptions of transitional fragmentation during the band's evolving late-period phase.[1]

Background and Context

Band's Mid-Career Transition

Jefferson Airplane reached its commercial and artistic zenith in the late 1960s as a leading exponent of San Francisco's psychedelic rock scene, propelled by albums like Surrealistic Pillow (1967), which topped charts with singles "Somebody to Love" and "White Rabbit," and the experimental After Bathing at Baxter's (1967), followed by Crown of Creation (1968) and the politically charged Volunteers (November 1969). These releases solidified the band's core lineupâ€”Marty Balin, Paul Kantner, Grace Slick, Jorma Kaukonen, Jack Casady, and Spencer Drydenâ€”as innovators blending folk, blues, and acid rock, amid the counterculture's peak at events like Monterey Pop (1967) and Woodstock (1969).[4][5] By 1970, however, empirical signs of decline emerged, including a two-year gap in studio albums after Volunteers, attributable to internal fractures and diverging priorities rather than external success metrics alone. Drummer Spencer Dryden exited in February 1970, succeeded by Joey Covington after a transitional overlap, while Kaukonen and Casady devoted substantial time to their blues-oriented side project Hot Tuna, launched in 1970, which diluted the Airplane's unified creative output. Founder Marty Balin, increasingly marginalized, formally departed in April 1971, exacerbating tensions between the Kantner-Slick faction and the rhythm section.[6][7][8] The band's mid-career pivot coincided with broader causal shifts: the counterculture's erosion post-Altamont Speedway concert on December 6, 1969, where Balin was punched unconscious by Hells Angels security during the set, underscoring violence's intrusion on utopian ideals and hastening psychedelia's fade amid rising hard rock dominance by acts like Led Zeppelin. Recording sessions for Bark spanned late 1970 to July 1971 at Wally Heider Studios, overlapping the formation of Grunt Records on August 7, 1971â€”a self-managed RCA subsidiary enabling autonomy but reflecting fragmented momentum over innovative renewal.[9][10][11]

Lineup Instability and Internal Conflicts

The dismissal of drummer Spencer Dryden in early 1970 disrupted Jefferson Airplane's cohesion as Bark's sessions approached, with Dryden citing disillusionment after the band's experiences at the Altamont Speedway concert on December 6, 1969. Dryden had anchored the rhythm section since 1966, but his exit reflected mounting interpersonal strains and a transitional phase where both he and incoming drummer Joey Covington performed with the group in late 1969. Covington, who officially replaced Dryden, brought a bluesier influence but highlighted the band's shifting dynamics.[12][13] Founding vocalist Marty Balin's departure in November 1970 further destabilized the lineup, occurring after initial February sessions but before completion, restricting his input to co-writing one track, "Pretty as You Feel." The onstage assault on Balin by Hells Angels security at Altamont had already sown seeds of discord, eroding his commitment and amplifying ego clashes within the group. Balin's reduced role underscored how personal resentments and creative divergences hampered unified production efforts.[14] Parallel pursuits of solo and side projects exacerbated tensions, as guitarist Paul Kantner released his sci-fi concept album Blows Against the Empire in 1970 under the Jefferson Starship moniker, while vocalist Grace Slick explored individual endeavors amid the band's inactivity. Financial acrimony with RCA Records, including disputes over lyrical content and distribution control, culminated in the August 1971 formation of Grunt Records as a subsidiary for greater autonomy, directly influencing Bark's independent release. These fracturesâ€”personnel turnover, side ventures, and label strifeâ€”manifested in the album's stylistic inconsistencies, as members prioritized personal agendas over collective vision.[15]

Production Process

Recording Sessions and Locations

The recording sessions for Bark took place primarily at Wally Heider Studios in San Francisco, with documented work on key tracks occurring from December 1970 to January 1971.[11] These sessions were characterized by logistical challenges stemming from the band's lineup instability, including the departure of co-founder Marty Balin in late 1970, which led to intermittent attendance and the integration of new members such as drummer Joey Covington and violinist Papa John Creach.[2] The self-produced effort by Jefferson Airplane lacked a centralized external overseer, contributing to a fragmented process as members balanced personal commitments and internal tensions.[2] To align with the launch of the band's independent Grunt Records label, final mixing and mastering were expedited, enabling a September 1971 release as the imprint's debut.[10] This timeline compressed post-production despite the extended tracking phase, reflecting the practical demands of transitioning from RCA distribution to self-managed output amid ongoing personnel flux.[16]

Song Development and Contributions

The development of songs for Bark highlighted the band's fragmentation, as individual and subgroup efforts dominated over collective composition, resulting in an eclectic mix that underscored diverging creative priorities rather than unified evolution. Paul Kantner penned the opening track "When the Earth Moves Again" as a solo effort, incorporating his characteristic science fiction motifs of cosmic upheaval and renewal, which he had explored in prior works like those on Blows Against the Empire.[17] Grace Slick contributed "Law Man," a pointed critique of authority drawn from her personal songwriting, performed with her lead vocals and piano, reflecting her increasing independence amid band tensions.[18] Jorma Kaukonen's "Third Week in the Chelsea" originated from informal jams tied to his Hot Tuna side project with bassist Jack Casady, capturing experiences at New York City's Chelsea Hotel during a period of band relocation and strain in early 1971; its acoustic folk-blues style prefigured Hot Tuna's separation from Airplane dynamics and contributed to the album's uneven cohesion.[19] Similarly, drummer Joey Covington's "Pretty as You Feel," co-written with Casady and Kaukonen, emerged from subgroup sessions emphasizing rhythmic grooves, marking Covington's integration after prior lineup shifts and serving as one of the few forward-looking tracks amid the prevailing discord. These contributions, often rooted in solo or duo explorations rather than full-band rehearsals, evidenced causal drivers like Kaukonen's prioritization of Hot Tuna, which strained Airplane unity and yielded a patchwork of styles without overarching direction.[20][18]

Musical Composition

Styles and Instrumentation

Bark exhibits a hybrid sonic palette that marks a departure from Jefferson Airplane's earlier acid rock and psychedelic dominance, incorporating blues rock influences derived from guitarist Jorma Kaukonen and bassist Jack Casady's parallel work in Hot Tuna, alongside more accessible pop-rock and jam-oriented structures. Tracks like "Feel So Good" showcase funky hard rock grooves with prominent lead guitar, reflecting this blues-infused shift, while the instrumental "Wild Turkey" blends extended guitar improvisation with violin, evoking Hot Tuna's raw, unpolished edge rather than the band's prior orchestrated psychedelia.[1][21][22] Instrumentation underscores this transitional dilution of the Airplane's signature experimental sound, with the addition of drummer Joey Covington introducing layered percussion and a steadier rhythmic drive absent in earlier lineups reliant on Spencer Dryden's more fluid style. Violinist Papa John Creach contributes folk-tinged string textures, particularly on "Wild Turkey" and the Grunt Records single "Pretty as You Feel," where his playing intersects with Kaukonen's guitar jams to add organic warmth over psychedelic remnants like spacey, feedback-adjacent guitar excursions.[23][1][24] Compared to the acid-drenched feedback and modal explorations of albums like After Bathing at Baxter's (1967), Bark features reduced sonic experimentation, prioritizing band members' individual contributions in a looser, less unified framework akin to a collaborative solo showcase.[1][22] This stylistic evolution yields mixed results, as evidenced by AllMusic's assessment of the album's uneven cohesion and rating of 6.9 out of 10, highlighting the tension between lingering psychedelic traces and the band's pivot toward bluesy accessibility without fully reinventing their core identity.[1]

Lyrical Themes and Content

The lyrics on Bark predominantly explore motifs of impending catastrophe, defiance against authority, and fleeting sensory indulgence, reflecting the band's shift from the overt revolutionary fervor of their 1969 album Volunteers toward more insular, fatalistic expressions amid the waning countercultural optimism of the early 1970s. Paul Kantner's "When the Earth Moves Again," which opens the album, evokes apocalyptic imagery through references to ancient conquests like Hannibal's march on Rome and cosmic upheavalsâ€”"when the earth moves again"â€”suggesting environmental or existential collapse, with lines urging survival by seeking "new ground" and transcending earthly limits via space travel: "If you've only lived on earth / You've never seen the sun / Or the sky."[25] This track's sci-fi undertones align with Kantner's recurring interest in interstellar escape as a response to terrestrial decay, yet it prioritizes personal detachment over collective action.[17] Grace Slick's "Law Man" channels raw antagonism toward legal enforcers, portraying a domestic standoff where the narrator warns an intruding officer of lethal consequencesâ€”"Law man, I'm afraid you just walked in here at the wrong time / You know my old man's gun has never been fired / But there's a first time"â€”framing rebellion as immediate, self-defensive violence against perceived generational oppression.[26] Such direct confrontation echoes the band's earlier anti-establishment edge but manifests here as isolated confrontation rather than organized resistance, mirroring real-life tensions from the era's drug raids and police clashes that plagued the San Francisco scene. In contrast, Joey Covington's "Pretty as You Feel" embraces hedonistic ephemerality, with its bluesy exhortation to revel in superficial allureâ€”"If you feel pretty, you are / It's a state of mind"â€”highlighting transient pleasures over enduring ideals, a theme rooted in the band's documented immersion in substance-fueled lifestyles that prioritized immediate gratification.[27] These themes causally stem from the Airplane's internal dissolution, including rampant drug use and relational strifeâ€”exemplified by drummer Joey Covington's replacement of the increasingly erratic Spencer Dryden amid escalating heroin and psychedelic dependenciesâ€”which infused lyrics with escapist fatalism but eroded the sharp, mobilizing rhetoric of prior works.[14] Slick's contributions, such as "Law Man," retain her signature biting edge, delivering social critique through sardonic threats rather than the politicized anthems of the 1960s, signaling a creative pivot toward personal cynicism as band cohesion frayed under excess.[28] Unlike Volunteers' calls for systemic upheaval, Bark's content underscores hedonism's inherent constraints, where individual rebellion and doom-laden visions offer catharsis but fail to transcend the very indulgences fueling the disillusionment.[29]

Release Details

Label Formation and Initial Formats

Grunt Records was founded in August 1971 by Jefferson Airplane members, including Paul Kantner and Grace Slick, as a vanity label operating as a subsidiary of RCA Records, which provided distribution and manufacturing support.[10][30] This structure emerged from negotiations led by band manager Bill Thompson, securing the group enhanced control over recording, design, and business decisions while leveraging RCA's infrastructure, reflecting a pragmatic bid for autonomy amid prior label tensions.[31][15] Bark marked Grunt's debut release, launched in September 1971 under catalog number FTR-1001.[1][21] The initial format consisted of a vinyl LP housed in a unique brown paper bag outer sleeve that unfolded into a gatefold display incorporating band photographs, abstract artwork, credits, and a fold-out lyric sheet.[32][33] No singles were prioritized for immediate extraction, underscoring the album's role in establishing the label's emphasis on full-length artistic statements tied to the band's independence ethos.[2]

Promotion and Packaging

Bark was promoted primarily through limited live performances following its September 1971 release on the band's independent Grunt Records label, which constrained access to major label marketing machinery.[2] Internal lineup disputes and personal commitments, including the recent birth of Grace Slick and Paul Kantner's daughter, further restricted extensive touring, resulting in sporadic appearances such as the August 15, 1971, concert at Wall Stadium in Wall Township, New Jersey.[34] These efforts paled in comparison to the band's earlier, more vigorous promotional campaigns during their peak in the late 1960s. The rollout lacked a prominent single push or widespread media advertising, with subsequent attention shifting to the 1972 album Long John Silver, which benefited from a dedicated two-month U.S. tour commencing in July.[35] Promotional copies of Bark were distributed in a special printed craft-paper bag, underscoring the DIY ethos of Grunt's operations.[36] Packaging emphasized unconventional presentation, featuring a brown kraft paper outer bag mimicking a grocery sack, shrink-wrapped around a die-cut cardboard inner sleeve with pasted artwork and a fold-out double-sided lyric insert containing credits and a poem.[33] Acy Lehman provided the package concept and design, while Gary Blackman handled art direction and contributed the bag poem, aiming for a tactile, unrefined format reflective of the band's evolving, fragmented identity.[37] This grocery bag enclosure was included in initial pressings, with later reissues adopting standard sleeves.[38]

Commercial Outcomes

Chart Performance

Bark reached a peak position of No. 11 on the Billboard 200 chart in 1971.[39] This represented a commercial decline compared to the band's breakthrough album Surrealistic Pillow, which peaked at No. 3 in 1967.[40] The album's chart run lasted around 21 weeks.[41] Internationally, Bark entered the UK Albums Chart at a modest No. 43, reflecting limited crossover appeal beyond the U.S. market. No singles from the album cracked the upper echelons of major charts; the lead single "Pretty as You Feel" stalled at No. 60 on the Billboard Hot 100.[42] The tempered performance aligned with broader shifts following the 1969 Altamont Speedway concert, which eroded the psychedelic rock scene's unified momentum, compounded by lineup instabilityâ€”including Spencer Dryden's departureâ€”and the proliferation of competing subgenres like progressive and hard rock that fragmented audience attention.

Sales and Certifications

Bark achieved gold certification from the Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA) in September 1971, reflecting U.S. shipments of at least 500,000 units.[43] This milestone aligned with contemporaneous press reports of strong initial sales momentum, though the certification predated formal RIAA shipment thresholds codified in later years.[44] Estimated U.S. sales for the album totaled approximately 500,000 copies, establishing it as a moderate commercial success within the band's discography but falling short of the one million units required for platinum status.[45] Unlike predecessors such as Surrealistic Pillow and Volunteers, which also earned gold but benefited from higher peak sales driven by hit singles, Bark lacked equivalent breakout tracks to propel it beyond this threshold. No further RIAA awards, including multi-platinum recognitions, were issued for the album on a standalone basis, even as Jefferson Airplane's overall catalog sales exceeded 4.5 million units across seven albums by later estimates.[45] Grunt Records' distribution arrangement with RCA Victor facilitated the album's market entry as the imprint's debut release but constrained broader penetration relative to the band's prior direct-label efforts, owing to the vanity label's narrower promotional infrastructure.[44] Subsequent catalog boosts from 1990s compilations enhanced the band's enduring revenue streams without retroactively elevating Bark's individual certifications.[45]

Reception Analysis

Initial Critical Responses

Upon its release in September 1971, Bark received mixed to negative reviews from contemporary critics, who frequently highlighted the album's inconsistency and departure from the band's earlier psychedelic cohesion. Greil Marcus, writing in Rolling Stone on November 11, 1971, described the record as provoking "yawns even among some Airplane cognoscenti," noting its "fractured" quality amid a shift toward bluesier, looser elements, though he praised "Pretty as You Feel" as a "fine, loose jam" suitable for loud playback.[46] He critiqued tracks like "Law Man" as a "stiff, preachy mess" and "Wild Turkey" as a bore, reflecting a broader perception of uneven execution following Marty Balin's departure and the integration of new members.[46] Robert Christgau, in his Village Voice consumer guide for 1971, rated the album C+, acknowledging it was "not as bad as the faithless claim" compared to prior efforts but dismissing it as "a collection of weirdnesses rather than songs," underscoring a lack of focused songcraft.[47] Such assessments aligned with period press consensus, averaging around 3 out of 5 in evaluative terms, signaling the band's real-time erosion from peak psychedelic form amid internal changes and stylistic experimentation.[46][47] Critics implied sloppiness in the production and arrangements, attributing it to the group's evolving dynamics rather than deliberate innovation.

Long-Term Evaluations and Critiques

Retrospective assessments of Bark portray it as a product of Jefferson Airplane's internal fragmentation following Marty Balin's 1970 departure, with band members pursuing divergent interests that diluted collective focus. AllMusic rates the album 3 out of 5 stars, characterizing it as uneven and transitional, marked by jam-session origins for several tracks that prioritize individual contributions over cohesive innovation.[1] Prog Archives users assign an average of 3.15 out of 5 based on 57 ratings, often critiquing its shift from psychedelic rigor to folk and country-inflected indulgence, viewing it as the band's lowest ebb amid ego clashes between Grace Slick and Paul Kantner.[22] Critiques emphasize Bark's embodiment of early 1970s rock's self-indulgent tendencies, where personal experimentation supplanted disciplined songcraft, in contrast to contemporaries like Led Zeppelin who maintained structural intensity through rigorous rehearsal. A 2011 analysis describes the album as a "sprawling mess" derived from ad-hoc sessions involving subsets of the group, underscoring how Hot Tuna's rising prominence for Jorma Kaukonen and Jack Casady foreshadowed the Airplane's creative splintering.[48] Similarly, a 2009 review labels it filler material even for fans, highlighting tracks like "Pretty as You Feel" as salvageable bluesy outliers that align more with Hot Tuna's improvisational ethos than the Airplane's fading psychedelic core.[20] Later re-evaluations in the 2010s reinforce Bark as evidence of the band's post-peak decline, with Rate Your Music aggregating user scores around 3.2 out of 5 from over 1,000 ratings, faulting its uninspired execution and lack of group enthusiasm amid countercultural burnout.[49] Discogs community averages hover at 3.8 out of 5 across thousands of ratings, yet comments frequently note its "decent but not essential" status, debunking revisionist nostalgia by attributing strengths to isolated Hot Tuna-adjacent elements rather than Airplane-wide vitality.[2] These views collectively position Bark as a artifact of causal disintegration in the San Francisco scene, where ideological excesses yielded fragmentation over enduring artistic rigor.

Personnel and Credits

Core Musicians

The core musicians on Bark comprised vocalist and keyboardist Grace Slick, guitarist and vocalist Paul Kantner, guitarist and vocalist Jorma Kaukonen, bassist Jack Casady, drummer and vocalist Joey Covington, and violinist Papa John Creach.[2] Slick provided primary lead vocals across multiple tracks, while Kantner and Kaukonen handled rhythm and lead guitar duties, respectively, contributing to the album's guitar-driven psychedelic rock foundation. Casady's bass work anchored the rhythm section, and Covington's drumming marked his debut as the band's full-time percussionist, replacing Spencer Dryden.[3][22] Creach's violin added a distinctive string element to select tracks, such as "When the Earth Moves Again" and "Wild Turkey," signaling his integration as a de facto core member despite originating from Kantner's side projects.[2][22] This personnel reflected lineup flux following Marty Balin's departure and Dryden's exit, with Covington and Creach's arrivals fostering an ad-hoc ensemble dynamic evident in the album's diverse instrumental textures and contributor credits.[22]

Additional Contributors

Papa John Creach contributed violin across multiple tracks, introducing a folk-inflected string element that distinguished Bark from prior Airplane releases and positioned him as a transitional figure toward the Jefferson Starship configuration, where he remained a core violinist through subsequent albums.[2] Guest vocalist Bill Laudner provided backing on "War Movie," augmenting the band's vocal resources during a period of internal flux.[1] Uncredited appearances included Carlos Santana on guitar for "Pretty as You Feel," enhancing the track's rhythmic drive with his signature Latin-infused style, and Will Scarlett on harmonica for "Third Week in the Chelsea," adding bluesy accents amid the band's acoustic leanings.[3] These selective external inputs addressed specific sonic gaps without overhauling the core ensemble. Technical support came from engineers Allen Zentz, Pat Ieraci (credited as Masterful Maurice for 16-track operations), George Engfer, and Bob Matthews, who handled recording at Wally Heider Studios in San Francisco.[2] The album's production was overseen entirely by Jefferson Airplane members, eschewing prominent outside producers in favor of internal arrangements that prioritized cost efficiency on their newly launched Grunt Records imprint.[1] This self-reliant approach, leveraging associate engineers over high-profile studio veterans, yielded a characteristically raw and uneven sound quality reflective of the band's transitional phase and budgetary constraints post-RCA.[22]

Enduring Impact

Legacy in Rock History

Bark's position in rock history underscores a transitional but ultimately marginal role for Jefferson Airplane, bridging the band's psychedelic origins with the more commercial orientations that would define Jefferson Starship, yet failing to catalyze broader innovation or enduring stylistic influence. Released amid the band's fracturing dynamics, the album incorporated eclectic elements like funk-infused tracks ("Pretty as You Feel") and country-tinged experimentation ("Feel So Good"), prefiguring the pop-rock evolution of Starship's later hits, but critics and historians note it as a diluted endpoint rather than a pivotal advancement. Unlike contemporaries such as the Who's Who's Next or Led Zeppelin's Led Zeppelin IV, both from 1971 and credited with refining hard rock paradigms, Bark did not spawn replicable sonic templates or inspire widespread emulation in subsequent psychedelic or jam-oriented acts.[50] Empirical indicators of influence remain sparse, with Bark infrequently cited in retrospective "best of 1971" compilations and overshadowed by the Airplane's 1960s catalog, such as Surrealistic Pillow (1967), which defined the San Francisco sound's acid-rock zenith. User-curated aggregators rank it modestlyâ€”486th among 1971 releases on one database and outside top psychedelic albums of the year on anotherâ€”reflecting its niche appeal rather than canonical status, often romanticized in counterculture nostalgia but lacking the paradigm-shifting impact of peers. Song covers are limited primarily to intra-band extensions, such as Jefferson Starship's renditions of "Law Man" (a Grace Slick-penned critique of authority) in live sets during the 1970s, alongside sporadic fan or niche tributes like Royal Trux's raw reinterpretation or acoustic homages, indicating appreciation within Airplane/Starship orbits but minimal crossover adoption.[51][21] Causally, Bark's subdued legacy stems from internal band deteriorationâ€”exacerbated by escalating drug use, interpersonal egos, and key departures like Marty Balin's exit prior to recordingâ€”over external market shifts, marking the effective close of the Airplane's creative peak without external scapegoating for the decline. Drummer Joey Covington's addition and Skip Spence's sidelining due to substance issues fragmented cohesion, yielding a patchwork record that, while commercially viable, forfeited the unified vision of earlier works and failed to counter the "San Francisco sound's" overromanticized persistence in historiography, where empirical output metrics prioritize the band's pre-1970 contributions. This internal rot, documented in member accounts of rampant psychedelics and alcohol eroding reliability, precluded the kind of resilient evolution seen in bands like the Grateful Dead, consigning Bark to a footnote in the psychedelic-to-arena rock continuum.[14][52]

Reissues and Modern Availability

The album Bark was first reissued on compact disc in 1996 by RCA Records as a remastered edition, reproducing the original 1971 tracklist of ten songs.[53] In 2015, Culture Factory USA released a high-fidelity CD edition designed to replicate the components of the original LP packaging in compact-disc format.[54] The same year, it appeared in the limited-edition Jefferson Airplane CD Vinyl Replica Collection, a nine-disc box set limited to 1,000 copies worldwide, which included Bark alongside other band albums in 12-inch square replicas of their vinyl sleeves.[55] A 2019 expanded CD reissue by Iconoclassic Records added two bonus tracks: an extended jam version of "Feel So Good" and the single edit of "Pretty as You Feel," increasing the total runtime beyond the original 44 minutes.[56] Vinyl reissues have primarily consisted of represses from the 1970s and 1980s on labels such as Grunt and RCA, with availability today largely confined to secondary markets for collectors rather than new remastered pressings.[2] In terms of modern access, Bark has been available on digital streaming services like Spotify since the early 2010s, offering both the standard eleven-track edition (incorporating outtakes like "Third Week in the Chelsea" from some early pressings) and versions with additional bonuses.[57] [58] Official full-album audio streams were uploaded to YouTube by the band's representatives on August 12, 2025, marking the first complete digital upload of the release to the platform.[59] Physical copies, including CDs and occasional vinyl, remain in print through catalog distributors like Amazon, often bundled in Jefferson Airplane compilations or box sets for archival rather than standalone appeal.[60] As of October 2025, no significant new editions or enhancements have been announced.[2]
