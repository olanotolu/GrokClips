Mont Blanc Tunnel fire

The Mont Blanc Tunnel fire occurred on 24 March 1999 within the 11.6-kilometre bidirectional road tunnel piercing the Mont Blanc massif and connecting Chamonix-Mont-Blanc in France to Courmayeur in Italy, when a Belgian-registered freight truck carrying approximately 20 tonnes of margarine and flour suffered a mechanical failure leading to ignition of its cargo.[1][2] The resulting conflagration rapidly intensified as the margarine liquefied and burned with high calorific value, spreading to over 30 vehicles over a 500-metre section and generating temperatures exceeding 1,000Â°C for up to 53 hours, ultimately killing 39 peopleâ€”primarily through asphyxiation from toxic smoke and carbon monoxide rather than direct thermal injury.[3][1] Inadequate initial detection, ventilation systems that inadvertently fanned the flames by supplying fresh air instead of extracting smoke, and delayed coordinated response between French and Italian authorities exacerbated the disaster, trapping victims in unlit, smoke-filled conditions with limited escape options due to the tunnel's single-bore design and lack of intermediate refuges.[4] Empirical post-incident analyses, including the joint French-Italian inquiry led by DuffÃ© and Marec, revealed systemic safety lapses such as permitting high-fire-load hazardous goods without sufficient segregation or suppression capabilities, contributing to the fire's unchecked propagation and the fusing of vehicles into molten debris.[1] The event prompted the tunnel's indefinite closure for reconstruction starting in 1999, reopening only in March 2002 after â‚¬300 million in upgrades including enhanced fireproofing, bidirectional traffic separation, advanced detection sensors, and mandatory hazardous cargo inspections; it also spurred EU-wide directives mandating rigorous tunnel risk assessments and operational reforms to mitigate similar causal chains in enclosed linear infrastructure.[5][3]

Tunnel Background

Construction and Design

The Mont Blanc Tunnel, connecting Chamonix in France to Courmayeur in Italy, was built from 1957 to 1965, spanning 11.6 kilometers beneath Mont Blanc to facilitate cross-Alpine vehicular transport.[6][7] Drilling operations began in 1959 and achieved breakthrough in 1962, after which lining and fitting-out work enabled the tunnel's opening to traffic on July 16, 1965.[8][9] The project reflected post-World War II priorities for economic integration, establishing a direct route for inter-regional exchange between the two nations.[7] Engineered as a single-tube structure, the tunnel supported bi-directional traffic flow on two lanes within a 8.6-meter-wide cross-section, with a maximum gradient of 4.5% and overburden depths exceeding 1,200 meters at points.[10] Ventilation was provided via longitudinal systems and access shafts to adjacent galleries, allowing fresh air intake and exhaust, though the setup relied primarily on natural and mechanical extraction without transverse barriers.[11] Initial safety provisions emphasized structural stability and basic operational controls over comprehensive fire mitigation, lacking dedicated suppression systems like sprinklers or pressurized refuge chambers along the length.[12] Hazardous materials transport was permitted under regulatory oversight with required documentation, aligning with the era's focus on enabling heavy goods vehicle passage to support trade volumes exceeding expectations shortly after opening.[13]

Pre-Incident Operations and Safety Protocols

The Mont Blanc Tunnel was jointly managed by the French concessionaire Autoroutes et Tunnel du Mont-Blanc (ATMB) and the Italian concessionaire SocietÃ  Italiana per il Traforo del Monte Bianco (SITMB), two semi-public entities sharing equal operational responsibilities across the 11.6 km bidirectional roadway linking Chamonix, France, to Courmayeur, Italy.[14] Daily traffic volumes averaged over 4,000 vehicles, encompassing passenger cars, buses, and heavy goods vehicles (HGVs), with the latter permitted only under strict bilateral restrictions on hazardous or flammable cargo to mitigate risks in the confined alpine environment.[13] Toll revenues, derived from these volumes exceeding 2 million annual crossings in the late 1990s, funded routine maintenance but also incentivized high throughput, with checks focused on permit compliance rather than exhaustive cargo inspections.[15] Pre-incident safety protocols relied on foundational infrastructure from the tunnel's 1965 opening, including transverse ventilation systems with axial fans to dilute exhaust fumes and smoke, supplemented by manual patrols conducted at intervals by on-duty personnel.[16] Emergency provisions encompassed 17 pressurized refuges spaced approximately every 500 meters, intended to shelter occupants during incidents for up to two hours, alongside basic communication links and fire extinguishers accessible via niches.[17] Franco-Italian bilateral agreements governed cross-border coordination, mandating synchronized shift handovers and joint drills, yet enforcement emphasized operational continuity over proactive hazard modeling. Absent were automated fire detection sensors or comprehensive real-time closed-circuit television (CCTV) coverage, with monitoring limited to periodic visual inspections and airflow gauges rather than integrated alarm systems.[18] Operational complacencies emerged from unaddressed precursors, including at least 16 prior truck fires over the tunnel's first 35 years, each contained through manual intervention without prompting systemic upgrades to detection or residue management.[19] Accumulated oil spills and combustible deposits from vehicle leaks had built up along the roadway, noted in internal logs but not rigorously remediated, as maintenance prioritized traffic flow amid revenue-dependent budgets.[10] A pre-1999 assessment by Haute-Savoie fire services had flagged ventilation inadequacies for large-scale events, yet recommendations for enhanced extraction capacity were deferred in favor of incremental 1990s modernizations that fell short of contemporary standards for long tunnels.[20] These gaps reflected a regulatory framework anchored in mid-20th-century engineering norms, with bilateral oversight insufficient to enforce stricter preload inspections or residue protocols despite evident patterns from minor incidents.[16]

The Incident

Initial Trigger

On March 24, 1999, shortly after 10:45 a.m. CET, Gilbert Degrave, a Belgian truck driver, entered the Mont Blanc Tunnel from the French entrance in a Volvo FH12 semi-trailer registered in Belgium and loaded with approximately 9 tons of margarine and 12 tons of flour.[3] [21] About 6.5 kilometers into the 11.6-kilometer tunnel, Degrave detected smoke emerging from the vehicle's cab, prompting him to halt the truck near the French-side kilometer marker.[3] [22] The blaze originated in the engine compartment, where an accidental leakage of lubricating oil reportedly contacted hot surfaces such as exhaust components, leading to rapid ignition.[23] [24] Although the precise ignition mechanism has been contested in subsequent inquiries, with no definitive mechanical failure identified in the truck's design, the oil leak scenario aligns with forensic evidence from the vehicle's remains and manufacturer analysis.[23] [25] Degrave exited the cab as flames intensified within seconds, enveloping the trailer and cargo.[21] [26]

Fire Progression and Containment Attempts

The fire began at approximately 10:30 a.m. on March 24, 1999, when a Belgian-registered truck carrying margarine and other flammable materials ignited roughly 2.6 km from the French entrance. Initial flames quickly engulfed the trailer, with temperatures surging beyond 1,000Â°C due to the combustion of liquid margarine and vehicle combustibles, producing intense radiant heat that melted nearby metal structures.[1] [12] Burning fuel cascaded along the roadway, igniting tires and fuel tanks of adjacent vehicles, leading to explosive involvement of at least 20 trucks and cars within the first hour.[27] [1] This rapid escalation over roughly 90 minutes transformed a single-vehicle incident into a multi-vehicle inferno spanning over 300 meters, with peak heat release rates estimated in the tens of megawatts from heavy goods vehicle trailers.[28] Tunnel ventilation systems, activated to extract smoke, were erroneously configured by operators, drawing fresh air from the Italian side and inadvertently fanning the flames while propelling dense, toxic smokeâ€”laden with carbon monoxide and particulatesâ€”toward the Italian portal.[1] [29] This pushed visibility to effectively zero across large sections, exacerbating disorientation amid the confined space and accelerating smoke propagation along the 11.6 km length.[30] The imbalance in airflow, compounded by the fire's position closer to France, created a chimney effect that sustained high velocities of hot gases, preventing effective smoke layering or clearance.[31] Early containment efforts faltered due to reliance on manual interventions without sufficient protective gear; the truck driver attempted to extinguish the blaze using an onboard extinguisher shortly after ignition, but the operation failed against the overwhelming heat and fuel load.[19] Fixed suppression systems, including manual deluge nozzles, were not promptly engaged, allowing the fire to gain momentum before water application proved inadequate against temperatures exceeding 1,000Â°C, which vaporized sprays and offered minimal cooling.[32] These deficiencies permitted unchecked progression until professional firefighting resources could intervene hours later, by which point the blaze had persisted for over 50 hours overall.[3]

On-Site Emergency Response

French firefighters from the Chamonix side initiated on-site response shortly after the fire alarm at approximately 10:52 AM on March 24, 1999, with the first light fire engine entering by 10:57 AM and the public brigade arriving at 11:10 AM; they advanced to within 2.7 km of the blaze by 11:15 AM but were forced to retreat due to overwhelming toxic smoke and heat.[10][1] Italian firefighters entered from the Courmayeur side around 11:00 AM, reaching within 300 meters of the fire by 11:20 AM before taking refuge in side chambers as conditions deteriorated rapidly.[1] These initial entries exposed responders to dense, acrid fumes from burning margarine and flour, compounded by inadequate ventilation that pushed smoke toward the French portal.[10] Coordination between the French and Italian teams was severely impaired by the absence of direct communication links between control rooms and incompatible emergency protocols, preventing shared situational awareness and joint tactical decisions; for instance, Italian crews proceeded without full knowledge of French ventilation actions, which inadvertently worsened smoke conditions on their side.[1][33] Responders relied on standard hoses connected to tunnel hydrants and breathing apparatus for some teams, but initial French patrol efforts used only handheld extinguishers, limiting effective suppression amid zero visibility and temperatures exceeding survivable limits.[10] These equipment constraints, combined with the fire's rapid spread, led to high risks for first responders, including entrapment and exposure without sufficient protective measures. Evacuation of trapped drivers proved extraordinarily challenging, as over 50 individuals were caught behind the conflagration in vehicles or on foot; many sought shelter in tunnel alcoves and refuge chambers designed for emergencies, but these offered inadequate protection against penetrating heat, carbon monoxide, and other toxic gases, resulting in numerous asphyxiations despite prolonged waits for rescue.[1][10] Rescue operations via fresh-air access points began around 11:54 AM but extended over six to seven hours for some survivors, with 12 people ultimately evacuated to the Italian side from deeper refuges; first-responder fatalities included one French firefighter from smoke inhalation during shelter evacuation and the Italian commanding officer, who died in hospital after leading trapped crews.[1][10] The response highlighted acute dangers to personnel, with Italian teams' 15 members requiring extraction after being isolated by fumes.[1]

Immediate Consequences

Human Casualties

The Mont Blanc Tunnel fire on March 24, 1999, resulted in 39 fatalities.[25][1] Victims comprised primarily truck drivers whose vehicles were overtaken by the blaze, including the Belgian operator of the originating truck; passengers in tourist vehicles, such as a French family of five and an Italian family of four; and five firefighters engaged in the initial response.[34] Nationalities among the deceased included French, Italian, Belgian, and others from various European countries.[34] Autopsies and forensic analyses indicated that the predominant cause of death was acute respiratory failure from inhaling toxic smoke, rich in carbon monoxide, cyanide, and other combustion byproducts, leading to rapid asphyxiation.[1][34] Most victims perished within 15 minutes of ignition due to the swift accumulation of dense, low-oxygen fumes, with a smaller number succumbing to thermal burns or hyperthermia.[1] At least one French firefighter died specifically from smoke inhalation during early containment efforts.[34] Approximately 34 bodies were recovered from the French side of the tunnel, where smoke had propagated extensively.[34] Among survivors, around 26 individualsâ€”including 12 civilians and 14 firefightersâ€”sustained primarily respiratory injuries from smoke exposure, treated with oxygen therapy and monitoring for pulmonary damage.[1] No significant traumatic injuries resulted from structural collapses or explosions, as the incident involved sustained combustion rather than detonations.[1]

Physical Damage to Infrastructure and Vehicles

The fire destroyed or severely damaged 34 vehicles, including the originating truck transporting margarine and flour, which was reduced to molten slag amid temperatures reaching 1,300Â°C.[35][36] Twenty-six vehicles on the French side, comprising cars, trucks, and a motorcycle, along with eight heavy goods vehicles on the Italian side, ignited sequentially as the blaze propagated.[36] Structural damage to the tunnel's concrete lining was extensive, with explosive spalling occurring due to thermal shock and moisture vaporization, affecting depths up to 40 cm in the 45 cm-thick initial sections near the fire's origin.[37][38] This degradation spanned several hundred meters, primarily around the 1.6 km mark from the French entrance, where intense heat caused surface cracking and material loss without leading to overall tunnel collapse.[12] Thermal expansion induced additional fissures in the lining and surrounding rock, compromising integrity and requiring reinforcement.[39] Ventilation systems, including extractor fans and ducts, were rendered inoperable by melted components and warped structures, while electrical cabling and control systems suffered total burnout, precipitating a prolonged blackout of lighting and signaling.[12] Ancillary infrastructure such as emergency doors and signage also sustained irreparable harm from soot accumulation and heat distortion. Total material damages were estimated at approximately â‚¬392 million, encompassing vehicle losses, structural repairs, and system replacements.[40]

Causal Analysis

Direct Causes from Vehicle and Driver

The fire in the Mont Blanc Tunnel on March 24, 1999, originated in the engine compartment of a Belgian-registered Volvo FH12 tractor-trailer driven by Gilbert Degrave, which was transporting approximately 8 tons of margarine and 12 tons of flour.[21] Investigations by Volvo and forensic analysis identified the ignition source as an accidental oil leakage from the engine, which likely contacted hot exhaust components, rather than a manufacturing defect.[23] Poor maintenance of the vehicle contributed to this failure, as evidenced by the driver's prior observations of smoke and the truck's operational history, though Degrave did not conduct adequate pre-trip inspections or halt for repairs.[22] Degrave first noticed smoke emanating from the truck shortly after entering the tunnel from the French side but dismissed it as minor and continued driving despite flashing headlights from oncoming vehicles signaling the issue.[1] By the time he stopped approximately 6.5 km into obstruction.[21] This delay in response, stemming from inadequate driver training on emergency protocols for mechanical faults in confined spaces, allowed the blaze to escalate unchecked initially.[22] The cargo's composition exacerbated the vehicle's role in the fire's severity, as the margarine liquefied under heat, creating a highly flammable pool equivalent in energy release to thousands of liters of fuel, while the flour contributed to rapid combustion propagation.[41] Forensic examination confirmed that the truck's excessive diesel fuel loadâ€”over 145 gallonsâ€”combined with leaking engine oil, provided initial accelerants, underscoring violations of standard load security practices that could have contained leaks.[17] Degrave's decision to proceed with the journey Investigations

French Technical Inquiry

The French administrative technical inquiry, established immediately following the 24 March 1999 fire, was tasked with reconstructing events and analyzing technical factors contributing to the disaster's severity. Led by experts under ministerial authority, the investigation emphasized empirical data from witness accounts, video footage, and physical evidence, concluding that inadequate initial detection and ventilation exacerbated the fire's rapid escalation.[43] Smoke toxicity emerged as a dominant lethality factor, with carbon monoxide and other combustion byproducts from the burning margarine-laden truck asphyxiating 34 victims trapped in vehicles within minutes of exposure, preventing escape amid dense, unstratified fumes. The inquiry's timeline reconstruction highlighted response delays, noting that while opacimeters detected smoke at approximately 10:52 and tunnel access was closed by 10:55â€“10:56, the first French fire response vehicle did not depart until 11:02, compounded by ventilation systems initially propagating smoke toward the French side rather than containing it. These delays, rooted in fragmented alerting protocols between French and Italian operators, allowed the fireâ€”originating in a truck stopped near garage 21â€”to spread to 24 trucks, 9 cars, and 1 motorcycle between garages 19 and 23, generating temperatures exceeding 1,000Â°C and toxic plumes that overwhelmed occupants before organized evacuation could commence.[43] Fire modeling simulations, informed by ventilation data and temperature measurements, underscored the need for revised cargo regulations; the inquiry recommended classifying high-calorific-value substances like those in the index truck (margarine and flour derivatives) as hazardous goods under ADR standards, subjecting them to tunnel-specific bans or escorts to mitigate ignition risks from refrigeration systems and fuel loads. Additional proposals included upgrading ventilation to achieve 110 mÂ³/s extraction over 600-meter sections with ceiling vents every 100 meters, limiting longitudinal airflow to preserve smoke layering, and expanding fire-resistant refuges to 36 units (each at least 25 mÂ², equipped with independent ventilation, water, and communications) capable of withstanding two hours of heat exposure. These data-driven measures aimed to address systemic vulnerabilities exposed by the 53-hour conflagration, which claimed 39 lives (with up to 45 possible including a firefighter).[43][44]

Italian and Bilateral Reviews

The Italian magistrate's inquiry into the Mont Blanc Tunnel fire scrutinized operational decisions during the incident, particularly highlighting ventilation maneuvers on the French side that directed smoke and flames toward the Italian portal, thereby intensifying smoke accumulation and complicating evacuations in the Italian section. This assessment diverged from French emphases on the initial fire ignition and contrasted with bilateral findings by underscoring unilateral errors in cross-border airflow management as a primary exacerbating factor.[45] A dedicated Italian commission of inquiry, presided over by engineer Pasquale, produced the "Rapporto sull'incendio del Monte Bianco," which examined pre-fire management lapses, inadequate monitoring systems, and the need for unified protocols in the binational tunnel. The report stressed shared infrastructural vulnerabilities but prioritized critiques of divergent national practices in emergency response, informing subsequent Italian policy discussions on alpine transport safety without leading to major domestic prosecutions.[46] The bilateral Franco-Italian task force, established immediately after the March 24, 1999, fire, issued its joint technical investigation report on June 30, 1999, under leads Pierre DuffÃ© and Michel Marec. This review identified critical communication breakdowns between the tunnel's independent French (ATMB) and Italian (SITMB) control rooms, which operated without real-time integrated data sharing, delaying coordinated closure and ventilation shutdowns. Proponents of rail shifts, including Italian transport advocates, cited the fire as evidence of insufficient modal diversification, though French counterparts defended road infrastructure investments as economically vital; these contentions persisted into the early 2000s without resolving into unified transport reforms.[49]

Legal Accountability

Manslaughter Prosecutions

In the aftermath of the Mont Blanc Tunnel fire on March 24, 1999, French judicial authorities initiated criminal proceedings for homicides involontaires (involuntary manslaughter) against individuals and entities responsible for tunnel oversight and the originating vehicle, emphasizing failures in safety protocols and risk management. The case centered on charges that negligence in maintenance, monitoring, and emergency preparedness contributed causally to the 39 deaths, with prosecutors arguing that preventable lapses allowed the fire to escalate uncontrollably.[22][50] The primary trial unfolded from January 31, 2005, at the Tribunal Correctionnel in Bonneville, Haute-Savoie, involving 16 defendants comprising 12 individuals and four companies, each facing potential sentences of up to three years imprisonment and fines. These documents, sourced from internal records and whistleblower accounts, underscored individual accountability for prioritizing operational continuity over rigorous compliance, with testimony revealing deliberate underreporting to evade costly upgrades.[54][55]

Verdicts, Appeals, and Sentences

In July 2005, a French court in Bonneville convicted 13 defendants, including tunnel officials and the truck driver, of involuntary manslaughter in connection with the fire, while acquitting three others, including truck manufacturer Volvo, due to insufficient evidence of direct negligence or intent.[56][50] The convictions centered on failures such as inadequate response protocols, poor training, and ventilation issues that exacerbated the blaze, though prosecutors could not prove deliberate misconduct sufficient for harsher charges.[49] Key sentences included six months' imprisonment (to be served) plus two years suspended for GÃ©rard Roncoli, the tunnel's French security chief, for procedural lapses and delayed activation of fire suppression systems; Reforms and Aftermath

Tunnel Closure and Reopening

The Mont Blanc Tunnel was immediately closed to all traffic following the fire on March 24, 1999, with the shutdown extending for nearly three years until its full reopening on March 9, 2002.[62][63] The closure halted the primary road link between France and Italy under Mont Blanc, forcing heavy vehicle and freight traffic to reroute via alternative Alpine passes such as the FrÃ©jus Tunnel, where heavy-duty vehicle volumes increased by 75 percent.[64] This diversion exacerbated congestion on secondary roads and rail networks, contributing to substantial economic disruptions for regional trade, including delays in transporting 13 million tons of annual freight that previously utilized the tunnel.[65] Repair and renovation efforts, which addressed extensive structural damage from the prolonged blaze, totaled approximately â‚¬350 million.[66] These works focused on restoring the tunnel's integrity while preparing for enhanced operational protocols, though specific safety upgrades implemented during this period are detailed elsewhere. Prior to resuming public access, the reopening process incorporated phased testing, including full-scale fire simulations in January 2002 to verify ventilation and suppression systems, followed by emergency evacuation drills.[18] Initial operations emphasized unidirectional traffic for light vehicles, with unmanned trial runs and progressive monitoring to ensure stability before allowing bidirectional heavy goods flow.[15][67] This cautious approach mitigated risks identified from the incident, enabling controlled resumption of service without immediate recurrence of hazards.

Policy and Regulatory Changes

In response to the 1999 Mont Blanc Tunnel fire, the European Union adopted Directive 2004/54/EC on 29 April 2004, establishing minimum safety requirements for road tunnels longer than 500 meters within the Trans-European Road Network, including trans-Alpine crossings.[72] This legislation mandated periodic safety and risk assessments, equipment standards for fire detection and suppression, and operational protocols such as traffic restrictions during emergencies, directly informed by analyses of the Mont Blanc incident and contemporaneous fires like Tauern.[1] Member states were required to transpose the directive into national law by 30 April 2007, with full compliance for existing tunnels by 30 April 2014, leading to widespread audits that identified deficiencies in ventilation, signage, and emergency access across EU tunnels.[73] Specific to the Mont Blanc Tunnel, post-fire regulations imposed a permanent ban on heavy goods vehicles (HGVs) transporting flammable or dangerous materials classified under the European Agreement concerning the International Carriage of Dangerous Goods by Road (ADR), prohibiting substances like margarine, fuel derivatives, and other combustibles that contributed to the rapid fire spread in 1999.[74] This measure, enforced bilaterally by French and Italian authorities, eliminated high-risk cargoes to mitigate ignition and propagation risks, with empirical data post-reopening in 2002 showing no recurrence of large-scale HGV fires attributable to banned goods.[17] France and Italy established a joint steering committee immediately following the disaster to revise operational coordination, standardizing emergency response protocols and command structures across the border-spanning tunnel, which addressed prior disjointed efforts that delayed evacuation and firefighting.[13] These bilateral adjustments influenced the World Road Association (PIARC) guidelines on tunnel fire management, incorporating lessons from Mont Blanc into recommendations for phased evacuation, smoke control, and cross-jurisdictional training, as detailed in PIARC reports emphasizing unified incident command to reduce response times.[69] Proposals for incentivizing modal shifts to rail freight were debated in policy circles, citing the fire's role in highlighting road vulnerabilities, yet implementation remained limited, with HGV traffic resuming under stricter volume controls and escorts, resulting in sustained reductions in peak throughput compared to pre-1999 levels.[49] Overall, these changes prioritized risk mitigation through prohibition and assessment over wholesale transport reconfiguration, correlating with fewer reported fire incidents in audited EU tunnels thereafter.[75]

Long-Term Impact

Influence on Global Tunnel Safety Standards

The Mont Blanc Tunnel fire of March 24, 1999, acted as a catalyst for integrating empirical lessons into international tunnel safety frameworks, notably through the World Road Association (PIARC), which disseminated analyses emphasizing enhanced fire ventilation, detection systems, and cross-border coordination to mitigate smoke propagation and evacuation delays observed in the incident.[76][69] PIARC's recommendations, drawn from the fire's 39 fatalities and structural damage spanning over 2 km, influenced the European Union's Directive 2004/54/EC on minimum safety requirements for trans-European road network tunnels, promoting standardized risk assessments over uniform prohibitions on hazardous cargo.[77][78] In the United States, the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) referenced the event's fire spread dynamicsâ€”where a single truck fire escalated due to combustible linings and inadequate suppressionâ€”in updating its 2009 Technical Manual for Road Tunnel Design and fixed firefighting guidelines, incorporating probabilistic modeling of heavy vehicle ignition risks.[79][80] These adaptations extended to enhanced heat release rate (HRR) simulations for predicting fire loads up to 300 MW, as evidenced in post-1999 FHWA-supported research on tunnel linings.[81] The incident's legacy fostered a global shift toward risk-based design paradigms, with PIARC advocating quantitative evaluations of fire scenarios over blanket restrictions, a principle adopted in non-European codes to balance infrastructure demands with safety.[82] European audits post-retrofitting, including those under the EU directive, report no catastrophic fires equivalent to Mont Blanc's scale in compliant tunnels through 2021, attributing this to implemented HRR-informed ventilation and suppression upgrades.[69]
