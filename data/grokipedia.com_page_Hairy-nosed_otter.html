Hairy-nosed otter

The hairy-nosed otter (Lutra sumatrana) is a semiaquatic mammal native to Southeast Asia and one of the rarest and least known otter species. It is threatened by loss of natural resources and poaching.

Description

The hairy-nosed otter has a short brown fur that becomes paler on the belly. It has a distinguished rhinarium which is fully covered with short dark hair "from the upper edge of the nostrils". Its upper lip and chin are whitish. Some individuals are reddish-chestnut in colour. Its body is long, its tail slender, and its fully webbed paws have prominent claws. Head-to-body length ranges from 57.5 to 82.6 cm (22.6 to 32.5 in), tail length from 35 to 50.9 cm (13.8 to 20.0 in), and weight from 5 to 8 kg (11 to 18 lb).[3] Its skull is flatter than that of smooth-coated otter, and it has smaller teeth.[4] Its forepaws are 5.8 cm (2.3 in) wide and smaller than the hind paws with about 6.6 cm (2.6 in).[5]

Distribution and habitat

The hairy-nosed otter occurs in Southeast Asia from southern Thailand, Cambodia, southern Vietnam, Peninsular Malaysia, and possibly Laos, to Sumatra and Borneo. It is locally extinct in India, Singapore, and Myanmar, and possibly also in Brunei.[1] As of the 2024 IUCN assessment, the species remains endangered across its range.[1] In Thailand, it was recorded in the Pru Toa Daeng peat swamp forest and in areas around the Bang Nara river.[5] It mainly inhabits lowland flooded forests with climax vegetation in three levels: a primary forest zone, a secondary forest zone made up of Melaleuca cajuputi, and a third zone of grasslands. These tiers make the habitat hard to penetrate, providing protection from human disturbance and cover from predators. The Bang Nara river habitat, where communities have been discovered, is tidal. The two reserves in Vietnam are both peat swamp forests, surrounded by 15 m high Melaleuca cajuputi, covered in dense lianas such as Stenochlaena palustris in its primary zone, and a second zone of meadows made up of Eleocharis dulcis. These two Vietnamese reserves contain many canals and floating aquatic plants like Eichhornia crassipes, Pistia stratiotes, Salvinia cucullata and Ipomoea aquatica to hunt and play in, with surrounding rice paddies as a third buffer zone.[3] In Cambodia, it was recorded in lowland flooded forest around Tonle Sap Lake.[9] In Vietnam, it was sighted and recorded by camera traps in U Minh Thuong National Park in 2000, where also spraints were found with fish scales and remains of crabs.[10] In 2008, it was also recorded in U Minh Ha National Park.[11] In Sumatra, a hairy-nosed otter was killed on a road next to the Musi River in 2005.[12] Recent observations include a 2024 record in South Sumatra.[13] In Borneo, 2023 records confirm presence in the Mangun River area, Central Kalimantan, Indonesia, where it shares habitat with other otter species.[14] In Sabah, historic records date to the late 19th century. In 2010, one individual was recorded for the first time by a camera trap in Deramakot Forest Reserve incidentally. In 2016, several individuals were sighted in Tabin Wildlife Reserve.[15]

Ecology and behavior

The hairy-nosed otter occurs in coastal areas and on larger inland rivers, solitary or in groups of up to four.[10] Its diet includes fish, such as broadhead catfish, snakeheads, and climbing perch, and water snakes, mollusks, and crustaceans.[1][10][3] During the dry season, individuals forage in drainage canals and ponds.[3] Pairing of a male and a female may be limited to the breeding period. The contact call between otters is a single-syllabic chirp. Adult females call to pups with a staccato chatter.[4] Populations in Cambodia breed between November and March. The gestation period lasts around two months. A family of both parents and cubs were sighted between December and February.[1]

Threats to the Hairy-Nosed Otter

Threats

The hairy-nosed otter faces multiple threats across its range in Southeast Asia. The primary threat is habitat loss and degradation due to deforestation, conversion of wetlands to agriculture (particularly oil palm plantations), logging, and human settlement expansion.[1] Water pollution from agricultural runoff, industrial waste, and domestic sewage degrades aquatic habitats and reduces prey availability. Poaching for its fur, meat, and use in traditional medicine, as well as illegal trade for the pet market, continues to impact populations. Other threats include incidental capture in fishing gear, road kills, and depletion of fish stocks due to overfishing. These factors have led to a decreasing population trend, contributing to the species' Endangered status.[1]

Conservation

The hairy-nosed otter is listed as Endangered on the IUCN Red List since 2021, with a decreasing population trend.[1] It is considered one of the rarest otters in Asia, with only scattered populations remaining across Southeast Asia. Recent records include sightings in Sumatra (2024) and Sabah, Malaysia (2025), indicating persistence in some areas but ongoing decline due to habitat loss and other pressures detailed in the threats section.[1][13][16] Conservation efforts include surveys and habitat protection initiatives. In 2024, a small grant was awarded for research and conservation in Indonesia to assess population status and threats.[ ][17] The species is protected under CITES Appendix II, regulating international trade.

In captivity

Few hairy-nosed otters have been held in captivity, with no established breeding programs as of 2025. Historical rescues include individuals at Phnom Tamao Wildlife Rescue Centre in Cambodia in 2008 and 2010, though the latter died shortly after.[3] More recently, in 2023, a young otter named Oreo was rescued and is being cared for at the Wildlife Friends Foundation Thailand (WFFT) sanctuary, one of only two known individuals in rescue centers worldwide.[18] Efforts focus on rehabilitation and release where possible, given the species' rarity.
