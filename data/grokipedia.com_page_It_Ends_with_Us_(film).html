It Ends with Us (film)

It Ends with Us is a 2024 American romantic drama film written by Christy Hall and directed by Justin Baldoni, adapting Colleen Hoover's 2016 novel of the same name.[1] The story centers on Lily Bloom, portrayed by Blake Lively, a young woman who relocates to Boston to pursue her dream of opening a florist shop, only to become entangled in a tumultuous relationship with neurosurgeon Ryle Kincaid (Baldoni), marked by escalating abuse, while her first love Atlas Corrigan (Brandon Sklenar) reemerges.[2] Baldoni also produced the film through his Wayfarer Studios, with Sony Pictures Releasing handling distribution following its premiere on August 6, 2024, and wide theatrical release in the United States on August 9.[1] Produced on a $25 million budget, the film achieved substantial commercial success, grossing $148.5 million in the United States and Canada and $351.4 million worldwide, driven by strong opening weekend performance and appeal to audiences familiar with the source material.[3] Critically, it garnered mixed reviews, with a 54% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes from 197 critics, praised for Lively's earnest performance and the adaptation's fidelity to the novel's exploration of domestic violence and intergenerational trauma, but faulted for clunky dialogue and superficial handling of heavy themes.[2] Audience reception was more favorable, reflected in a 6.3/10 IMDb user rating from over 93,000 votes, highlighting its emotional resonance despite narrative shortcomings.[1] The production drew significant attention due to off-screen tensions between Lively and Baldoni, culminating in Lively filing a lawsuit in December 2024 against Baldoni, his production company, and others, alleging sexual harassment during filmingâ€”including exposure to explicit content and improvised intimate scenes without consentâ€”and subsequent retaliation through a smear campaign to undermine her reputation.[4] Baldoni has denied the claims, with his team framing the dispute as arising from creative differences over the film's tone and marketing, particularly Lively's push for a lighter promotional approach amid the story's dark subject matter; the legal battle, ongoing as of early 2025, has amplified scrutiny of the film's themes of abuse and power dynamics in relationships.[5]

Source material

Novel origins and publication

It Ends with Us originated from Colleen Hoover's personal family history, specifically her mother's experience of domestic abuse inflicted by Hoover's father during her childhood.[6][7] Hoover has described the novel's central themes of intergenerational cycles of abuse as directly informed by these events, aiming to explore the complexities of breaking such patterns without romanticizing violence.[6] The book was first published in hardcover by Atria Books, an imprint of Simon & Schuster, on August 2, 2016.[8] It spanned 384 pages and marked Hoover's sixth novel, following her earlier works in the romance and new adult genres.[8] Atria Books handled the initial print and distribution, positioning it as contemporary women's fiction addressing emotional and relational trauma.[9]

Book's commercial success and cultural impact

"It Ends with Us," published by Atria Books on August 2, 2016, experienced delayed but explosive commercial success, initially self-published before gaining mainstream traction. By September 2024, the novel had sold more than 10 million copies worldwide in print, audio, and ebook formats.[10] In 2022, it ranked as the best-selling book in the United States, moving over 2.7 million copies that year alone, amid author Colleen Hoover's dominance of bestseller lists with eight titles among the top 25 print books sold.[11][12] The book's sustained sales, including topping Amazon's fiction charts in 2024 despite its age, were amplified by the 2024 film adaptation, which reignited demand.[13] Its rise was propelled by viral engagement on TikTok's BookTok community, where user-generated content and reviews drove a surge in popularity starting around 2020, transforming it from a niche romance into a publishing phenomenon.[14] Hoover's titles, including this one, accounted for 15 spots on USA Today's bestseller list in August 2022, underscoring the novel's role in elevating contemporary romance sales during the platform's peak influence.[15] Culturally, "It Ends with Us" has shaped conversations around domestic violence, cycles of abuse, and personal agency in relationships, resonating with readers through its semi-autobiographical elements drawn from Hoover's family experiences.[16] The story's exploration of breaking intergenerational trauma garnered nearly 2 million five-star ratings on platforms like Goodreads, fostering a dedicated fanbase that engaged in book clubs, fan art, and social media analyses.[14] However, its impact includes polarized discourse, with critics arguing it inadvertently normalizes abusive dynamics by framing them within romantic narratives, as noted in analyses questioning its influence on young readers' perceptions of toxicity in partnerships.[16] This tension has amplified its visibility, positioning it as a flashpoint in broader debates on romance genre conventions and the responsibilities of popular fiction in addressing real-world social issues.[17]

Criticisms and defenses of the novel's themes

Critics have accused the novel of romanticizing domestic abuse by humanizing the abuser, Ryle Kincaid, as a "complicated" and "damaged" neurosurgeon whose violence stems from childhood trauma, thereby excusing or softening his calculated cruelty, including physical assaults causing bruises, concussions, and a premeditated sexual assault involving choking and restraint.[18][16] This portrayal, detractors argue, reinforces toxic masculinity tropes and normalizes red flags in relationships, potentially misleading young readersâ€”its primary audience of 18- to 25-year-oldsâ€”into viewing abuse as a forgivable flaw in otherwise charismatic partners.[16] Furthermore, the resolution draws ire for Lily Bloom's decision to co-parent their daughter with Ryle post-divorce without pursuing protective legal measures, such as supervised visitation under laws like Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 208 Section 31A, which critics say endangers the child and undermines the narrative's anti-abuse message.[18] Defenders, including the author Colleen Hoover, counter that the novel deliberately depicts the gradual escalation of abuse and Ryle's manipulative charm to mirror real dynamics, educating readers on why victims often remain in relationships rather than endorsing them.[19] Hoover has stated the story draws from her mother's experience with domestic violence, emphasizing Lily's choice to leave Ryle and break the intergenerational cycleâ€”symbolized by the titleâ€”as an act of empowerment, not reconciliation.[16] They argue criticisms overlook readers' ability to recognize Ryle as a villain through the shocking reveals and Lily's internal conflict, while the absence of a traditional "happily ever after" with him distinguishes it from pure romance genres.[20] Domestic violence survivors have echoed this, praising the unvarnished accuracy of elements like love-bombing and psychological entrapment, which align with empirical patterns where abusers alternate tenderness and violence to maintain control.[21]

Plot

Synopsis


It Ends with Us centers on Lily Bloom, a recent college graduate who relocates to Boston to pursue her dream of opening a flower shop, determined to escape the shadow of her traumatic childhood marked by her father's abuse toward her mother.[22][23] Soon after arriving, Lily encounters Ryle Kincaid, a successful and charismatic neurosurgeon, during a rooftop incident, leading to an intense and rapid romantic connection between them.[1][2] As Lily and Ryle's relationship deepens, including marriage and the anticipation of parenthood, her past resurfaces when she reunites with Atlas Corrigan, her first love from adolescence who had provided her solace amid family turmoil.[2][24] This rekindling forces Lily to grapple with unresolved emotions and patterns of behavior echoing her upbringing, ultimately compelling her to confront the realities of her current circumstances and decide her path forward independently.[1][25]

Cast

Principal performers

Blake Lively stars as Lily Bloom, the film's protagonist, a recent college graduate who relocates to Boston to pursue her dream of owning a flower shop while grappling with past trauma and a complicated romance.[1] Lively's casting in the lead role was announced on January 23, 2023, alongside Justin Baldoni.[26] Her performance drew mixed responses, with some critics praising her emotional depth in portraying domestic abuse cycles, while others noted the age discrepancy between her 36 years at filming and the character's early-20s depiction in the source novel.[2][1] Justin Baldoni portrays Ryle Kincaid, a charismatic neurosurgeon who enters a passionate but increasingly abusive relationship with Lily.[1] Baldoni, who also directed the film, was cast in the role concurrently with Lively in January 2023.[26] His dual involvement in acting and directing sparked discussions on set dynamics, particularly amid reported tensions with Lively during post-production.[27] Brandon Sklenar plays Atlas Corrigan, Lily's first love from her teenage years who reenters her life as a successful restaurateur, complicating her current entanglement.[1] Sklenar joined the cast in a key supporting lead role, with his involvement confirmed prior to principal photography in 2023.[28] The character's flashbacks and present-day arcs highlight themes of enduring connection amid adversity.[29]

Supporting roles

Jenny Slate portrays Allysa, Ryle's spirited sister and Lily's loyal best friend, who co-manages the flower shop with Lily and provides comic relief amid the drama.[30] Brandon Sklenar plays Atlas Corrigan, the homeless teenager who forms a deep bond with young Lily and later reappears as a successful chef, representing an idealized alternative to Ryle.[26][30] Hasan Minhaj appears as Marshall, Allysa's optimistic husband and father, whose enthusiastic personality contrasts the film's heavier themes.[30] Kevin McKidd depicts Andrew Bloom, Lily's abusive father whose past violence shapes her family dynamics and informs her adult relationships.[28] Amy Morton plays Jenny Bloom, Lily's mother, who endured domestic abuse from Andrew and later reflects on breaking the cycle with her daughter.[30] Additional supporting performers include Alex Neustaedter as young Ryle Kincaid and Isabela Ferrer as young Lily Bloom, illustrating the protagonists' formative years through flashbacks.[31]

Production

Development and pre-production

In July 2019, Justin Baldoni announced via Instagram that he had acquired the film rights to Colleen Hoover's 2016 novel It Ends with Us through his production company, Wayfarer Studios, with plans to direct the adaptation himself.[32][33] Baldoni, who had recently directed the romantic drama Five Feet Apart (2019), bonded with Hoover over shared interests in storytelling about resilience and trauma, leading her to entrust him with the project before the novel's sales surged to over 20 million copies amid TikTok-driven popularity in 2021.[34] Pre-production advanced in early 2023, with screenwriter Christy Hall adapting Hoover's novel into a screenplay that emphasized the protagonist Lily Bloom's internal conflicts while incorporating consultations with Hoover to maintain fidelity to the source material's themes of generational abuse cycles.[35] On January 26, 2023, Sony Pictures confirmed its distribution partnership with Wayfarer Studios, announcing Blake Lively as Lily Bloom and Baldoni as Ryle Kincaid, alongside Baldoni's directing role; Lively also joined as a producer.[36] The casting drew fan criticism for age discrepanciesâ€”Lively was 35 portraying a 23-year-old, and Baldoni 39 as a 30-year-oldâ€”but Hoover publicly endorsed both selections, citing Lively's emotional depth as aligning with the character's arc.[37] Pre-production in Los Angeles from February to April 2023 focused on script revisions, intimacy coordination protocols given the story's domestic violence elements, and logistical planning for principal photography, which was later delayed by the 2023 Writers Guild of America and SAG-AFTRA strikes.[38]

Filming process

Principal photography for It Ends with Us began on May 23, 2023, in Hoboken, New Jersey, with initial scenes shot at a vacant storefront transformed into protagonist Lily Bloom's flower shop.[39][40] Production halted indefinitely shortly after due to the Writers Guild of America strike that commenced on May 2, 2023, and was compounded by the SAG-AFTRA strike starting July 14, 2023, which prevented further work involving actors.[39][40] Filming resumed in January 2024 following the resolution of both strikes, with principal photography wrapping by the end of that month.[41][42] The schedule's interruptions required adjustments to maintain continuity, including reshoots and set preparations during the downtime. Although set in Boston, Massachusetts, and featuring some establishing shots of the city's skyline, the majority of the film was lensed in northern New Jersey for cost efficiency and to evoke a similar urban New England aesthetic. Key locations included Jersey City for romantic street scenes on Newark Avenue, the South House restaurant (doubling as Atlas Corrigan's eatery Root), and Van Vorst Park for a farmers' market sequence; Plainfield for Lily's childhood home in Maine; Weehawken for Ryle Kincaid's loft apartment in a Veris Residential building; and Hoboken for additional storefront and neighborhood shots. A pivotal rooftop meeting between Lily and Ryle was filmed in Los Angeles owing to inclement winter weather in New Jersey.[43] Production designer Russell Barnes emphasized recreating authentic contrasts between settings of affection and coercion, with meticulous, collaborative builds for interiors like the flower shop and Ryle's modern apartment to reflect character dynamics and the novel's themes.[44][45]

Soundtrack

Original score and songs

The original score for It Ends with Us was composed by Rob Simonsen and Duncan Blickenstaff.[46] Simonsen, known for prior collaborations with lead actress Blake Lively on The Age of Adaline, co-wrote the music to underscore the film's themes of romance, trauma, and cycles of abuse.[47] The score emphasizes emotional piano motifs and subtle orchestral swells, recorded with a focus on intimacy rather than bombast.[48] Madison Gate Records released the official soundtrack album, It Ends with Us (Original Motion Picture Soundtrack), digitally on August 9, 2024, coinciding with the film's theatrical debut.[46] The album comprises 22 instrumental tracks totaling 40:45, including cues such as "Opening" (2:21), "It Was Nice Meeting You" (1:38), and "It Ends With Us" (the title track at varying lengths across versions).[49] Season Kent supervised the music integration, blending the score with licensed contemporary tracks to enhance narrative tension.[50] No original songs were composed specifically for the film; instead, it features pre-existing licensed tracks for key scenes, such as Ethel Cain's "Strangers" during introspective moments, Taylor Swift's "my tears ricochet" for emotional peaks, and Lewis Capaldi's "Love the Hell Out of You" in promotional tie-ins.[51] Other notable inclusions are Rhye's "Hymn" and Chyvonne Scott's "Everybody Needs A Friend," selected to evoke vulnerability and relational complexity without altering the source material's tone.[52]

Marketing and release

Promotional strategies

The promotional campaign for It Ends with Us centered on leveraging the popularity of Colleen Hoover's 2016 novel among young adult readers, particularly through social media platforms like TikTok and Instagram, where "BookTok" communities had driven book sales exceeding 20 million copies globally. Trailers were released strategically to build anticipation, with the first official teaser debuting on May 16, 2024, highlighting romantic elements and Blake Lively's portrayal of Lily Bloom, followed by a second, more emotionally intense trailer on July 19, 2024. These trailers emphasized visual motifs of flowers and empowerment, aligning with the story's floral symbolism, while downplaying the narrative's depiction of domestic violence to appeal to a broader romance audience.[53][54][55] Blake Lively spearheaded much of the star-driven promotion, conducting press tours and appearances in floral-patterned outfits, such as a $19,000 pair of jeans and a Versace dress previously worn by Britney Spears, to evoke the film's aesthetic of beauty and resilience. She integrated personal brand endorsements, including her haircare line Blake Brown and Betty Buzz sparkling drinks, into events like themed cocktail gatherings tied to the movie's release, framing the film as a celebratory "girls' night out" experience. This approach extended to social media posts encouraging group viewings with phrases like "grab your friends," which echoed marketing from prior films but was criticized for trivializing the subject matter.[56][57][58] The campaign avoided explicit references to domestic abuse in advertisements and interviews, positioning the film as a story of love, self-discovery, and breaking cycles rather than trauma, which targeted a demographic skewing female, under 35, and digitally engaged. No major corporate partnerships were prominently featured, though tie-ins with Hoover's book series and fan events amplified word-of-mouth among existing readers. This selective thematic focus, while boosting initial buzz from the novel's fanbase, later contributed to public scrutiny over mismatched messaging.[55][59][60]

Theatrical rollout

The film premiered theatrically in select international markets on August 7, 2024, including Belgium, Finland, Iceland, the Philippines, and Sweden.[61] In the United States, it opened wide on August 9, 2024, across 3,839 theaters, distributed by Sony Pictures.[3] The rollout followed delays from an initial February 9, 2024, target date announced in July 2023, with a further postponement in April 2024 from June 21 to August 9 to allow additional post-production time.[62][63] Expansion continued into additional territories shortly after, with strong early openings in markets such as Mexico, Brazil, the United Kingdom, South Korea, and France contributing to a projected global debut exceeding $70 million.[64] China approved a separate theatrical release on August 30, 2024, following regulatory clearance for imported films.[65] The strategy emphasized a simultaneous North American and key international launch to capitalize on the source novel's fanbase, without noted emphasis on premium formats like IMAX in initial announcements.[66]

Home media and streaming availability

The film became available for digital purchase and rental on September 24, 2024, through platforms including Amazon Prime Video and Apple TV.[67][68] Sony Pictures Home Entertainment released the film on Blu-ray and DVD on November 5, 2024, with the Blu-ray edition including bonus features such as deleted scenes, behind-the-scenes footage, and commentary tracks.[69][70][71] In the United States, It Ends with Us began streaming exclusively on Netflix on December 9, 2024, under a licensing agreement with Sony Pictures, making it accessible to subscribers without additional rental fees.[72][73] As of October 2025, it remains available for streaming on Netflix in select regions, while digital rental and purchase options persist on Amazon Prime Video, Apple TV, and Google Play.[73][74] Availability on other platforms, such as Max (formerly HBO Max), has been limited and region-dependent, with no widespread U.S. streaming confirmed beyond Netflix.[75] International streaming rights vary, with the film accessible on Netflix in countries including the UK and Australia by early 2025.[73]

Commercial performance

Box office earnings

It Ends with Us premiered in the United States on August 9, 2024, earning $7 million from Thursday previews, the strongest such performance for a romantic drama since Fifty Shades of Grey in 2015.[66] The film opened domestically to $50 million over the three-day weekend, surpassing initial projections of $30â€“40 million and securing the top spot at the North American box office.[76] Domestically, the film accumulated $148,518,266 in ticket sales across 3,839 theaters, ranking 15th among 2024 releases.[77] Internationally, it generated $201,204,091, with significant contributions from markets including the United Kingdom ($28.3 million) and Germany.[66] The worldwide gross reached $349,722,357, placing it 20th globally for 2024.[78] Despite a reported production budget of around $25 million, the earnings reflected strong word-of-mouth driven by the source novel's fanbase, though performance tapered after the opening amid mixed reviews.[66]

Financial profitability analysis

The film was produced on a budget of $25 million.[66] Worldwide theatrical gross reached $351 million, with $148.5 million from the United States and Canada and $202.5 million from international markets.[66] Accounting for typical industry splits where studios retain approximately 50% of box office revenue after theater shares, the theatrical earnings alone generated substantial returns exceeding production costs several times over.[79] Total worldwide expenses, including marketing, prints, advertising, and distribution, amounted to $153 million, per industry estimates.[79] Net profit, incorporating box office, home video, streaming, and ancillary revenues, was calculated at $207 million, ranking the film as the sixth most profitable blockbuster of 2024.[79] This figure reflects Sony's 50% co-financing stake and efficient production amid 2023 strikes, underscoring the adaptation's commercial viability driven by source material popularity rather than high upfront investment.[79]

Reception

Critical evaluations

The film received mixed reviews from critics, earning a 54% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 197 reviews, with the site's consensus describing it as "earnestly performed if marred by clunky dialogue" and noting its more graceful handling of sensitive material despite overall flaws.[2] On Metacritic, it scored 52 out of 100 from 50 critics, indicating "mixed or average" reception.[80] Reviewers frequently praised the lead performances, particularly Blake Lively's portrayal of Lily Bloom as emotionally nuanced and committed, with Variety's Owen Gleiberman calling the adaptation "affecting" and crediting Lively and co-star Justin Baldoni for maintaining conviction amid the story's tonal shifts from romance to darker themes.[81] Critics often highlighted the film's strengths in depicting psychological elements of abusive relationships, such as love-bombing and the cycle of trauma, which some experts and reviewers found authentic based on real-world patterns of interpersonal violence.[82] The Hollywood Reporter commended Baldoni's direction for building emotional stakes in Lily's navigation of romance and past abuse, emphasizing the earnestness of the cast's efforts to convey intergenerational trauma.[83] However, common criticisms centered on the screenplay's melodramatic excess and inconsistent tone, with The New York Times' Manohla Dargis labeling it "fitfully diverting, at times touching, often ridiculous," faulting oversimplifications that risked diluting the severity of domestic violence by prioritizing romantic allure.[84] Several outlets questioned the film's balance in portraying abuse, arguing it occasionally glamorized the initial romance or attributed Ryle's violence primarily to his childhood trauma without sufficient emphasis on perpetrator accountability, potentially misleading viewers about the unidirectional causality of such behaviors.[85] [86] This approach drew ire for echoing patterns in popular media where abuse is romanticized, though defenders noted the story's intent to illustrate breaking cycles rather than excusing them.[16] Pacing issues and contrived plot elements, including abrupt escalations, further undermined credibility for detractors, contributing to the film's middling aggregate scores despite commercial success.[82]

Audience and fan responses

The film garnered strong audience approval, evidenced by an A- CinemaScore from opening weekend polls of over 1,000 viewers and an 88% audience score on Rotten Tomatoes derived from more than 5,000 verified ratings as of late 2024.[87][2] These metrics reflect broad appeal, particularly among female demographics aged 18-34, who comprised the majority of attendees and rated the film highly for its emotional resonance.[88] Rotten Tomatoes audience consensus characterized the adaptation as "a faithful and well-done" rendering of Colleen Hoover's novel, emphasizing its relatable exploration of flawed relationships and capacity to evoke strong emotions, with many reviewers noting tearful theater experiences.[2] Book fans, including those active in online communities like BookTok, frequently praised the retention of pivotal scenes and performances by Blake Lively and Justin Baldoni, crediting the film with capturing the story's heartfelt core despite production constraints.[89][90] Nevertheless, a subset of Hoover's readership voiced dissatisfaction, primarily alleging that the screenplay diluted the novel's stark depiction of domestic violence cycles, rendering the abuse less visceral and potentially romanticized through glossy visuals and marketing emphasis on romance over trauma.[85][86] Pre-release fan petitions and online discourse also targeted casting decisions, arguing that actors' ages deviated from book descriptionsâ€”Lively at 36 portraying a mid-20s protagonistâ€”and risked undermining character authenticity.[91] Author Colleen Hoover acknowledged such critiques but affirmed her approval of the final product, attributing fan intensity to deep investment in the source material's themes drawn from her personal experiences.[90][91] This polarization underscores a divergence wherein general audiences valued the film's accessibility and cathartic elements, contrasting with select fans' expectations for unvarnished fidelity to the book's unflinching realism on intergenerational abuse.[85][2]

Controversies

On-set conflicts between key figures

During the principal photography of It Ends with Us in early 2023, tensions arose between lead actress Blake Lively, who portrayed Lily Bloom, and director-producer Justin Baldoni, who played Ryle Kincaid, primarily stemming from creative differences over the film's tone and handling of intimate scenes.[92] Reports indicated disagreements on script revisions, including input from Lively's husband Ryan Reynolds, which prompted Baldoni to leave a 2 a.m. voice message in April 2023 apologizing for his reaction to one such rewrite.[4] Lively later alleged in a December 2024 complaint to the California Civil Rights Department that Baldoni created a hostile work environment on set through repeated inappropriate sexual discussions, such as inquiring about her personal sex life with her husband and demonstrating explicit acts like simulated oral sex during rehearsals without prior consent or intimacy coordinator involvement.[93] She further claimed Baldoni added unscripted sex scenes and made her recount graphic details of a birth scene, contributing to discomfort during filming in May 2023, including a behind-the-scenes slow dance sequence she described as unwelcome.[4] Baldoni has denied these accusations, asserting that all scenes were collaboratively discussed and that Lively's involvement in production decisions, including hiring an intimacy coordinator, ensured professional boundaries.[5] Crew accounts corroborated a strained set atmosphere, with multiple members noting Lively's frequent scheduling conflicts due to external commitments, leading to her perceived limited presence and a "closed off" demeanor in the final weeks of production.[94] Baldoni's body double reported observing interpersonal friction that impacted the overall vibe, though specifics on direct confrontations remained anecdotal.[95] Production was further disrupted by the WGA strike in June 2023, halting filming amid existing interpersonal strains, but no evidence links the strike directly to the Lively-Baldoni dynamic.[96] These on-set issues escalated into broader legal disputes post-release, with both parties attributing the root causes to differing visions for the adaptation of Colleen Hoover's novel.[34]

Marketing and thematic representation issues

The marketing campaign for It Ends with Us emphasized romantic elements, featuring trailers that highlighted the initial attraction between protagonists Lily Bloom (Blake Lively) and Ryle Kincaid (Justin Baldoni), while downplaying the film's depiction of domestic abuse until late in the narrative.[97][85] This approach drew criticism for glamorizing intimate partner violence by presenting it within a glossy, aspirational aesthetic akin to romantic comedies, potentially misleading audiences about the story's gravity.[98][99] Blake Lively's press tour amplified these concerns, as her promotional activities focused on fashion, beauty products, and personal anecdotesâ€”such as sharing a hair care routine on The Today Show on August 7, 2024â€”rather than engaging substantively with the theme of abuse.[100][17] Critics argued this tone-deafness undermined the film's intent to address intergenerational trauma and cycles of violence, with social media users and commentators labeling it as insensitive to survivors.[16][21] Lively later stated in a December 2024 interview that the surrounding scandal had "traumatized" her children, attributing it to public misinterpretation of the film's content.[101] Thematically, the film faced accusations of romanticizing abuse by casting an conventionally attractive actor as the perpetrator and framing early interactions as passionate rather than indicative of coercive control, which some analysts contended perpetuated myths that abusers are identifiable monsters rather than everyday partners.[85][16] Domestic violence advocacy groups, including RAINN, noted that while the story aimed to illustrate breaking cyclesâ€”inspired by author Colleen Hoover's real-life observations of her parents' relationshipâ€”it simplified victim experiences by depicting escape as relatively straightforward, culminating in Lily retaining custody of her child conceived through rape without depicted long-term repercussions.[102] Survivors interviewed post-release expressed divided views: some praised the visibility for sparking conversations, but others criticized the "happy ever after" undertones and lack of emphasis on systemic barriers to leaving, arguing it set unrealistic expectations.[21][103] These representational choices echoed broader debates on whether popular media inadvertently normalizes abuse by prioritizing emotional catharsis over clinical realism.[104]

Legal actions and resolutions

In December 2024, Blake Lively filed a complaint with the California Civil Rights Department against Justin Baldoni, Wayfarer Studios, and associated publicists, alleging sexual harassment on the set of It Ends with Us, including improvised intimate scenes without consent and unscripted additions of nudity, as well as retaliatory actions such as a coordinated smear campaign following her objections during production and the press tour.[105][106] Lively claimed this campaign, involving media leaks and negative narratives, led to reputational damage and a 56-78% drop in sales for her hair-care product line.[106] She formalized the complaint into a federal lawsuit in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York on December 31, 2024, seeking remedies under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act for a hostile work environment and retaliation.[105] Baldoni responded with legal actions of his own, filing a $250 million libel suit against The New York Times on December 31, 2024, over its reporting on Lively's allegations, which he described as false and damaging to his reputation.[106] On January 16, 2025, Baldoni, Wayfarer Studios, and others countersued Lively, her husband Ryan Reynolds, and her publicist Leslie Sloane for $400 million, accusing them of defamation through public statements and extortion via threats to disclose private text messages unless Baldoni met certain demands regarding the film's narrative and promotion.[105][106] Lively amended her complaint on February 18, 2025, incorporating additional evidence and a defamation counterclaim, while the court consolidated the related suits on January 30, 2025, and set a trial date for March 9, 2026.[105] Several claims were resolved through dismissals in mid-2025. On June 9, 2025, a federal judge dismissed Baldoni's $400 million suit against Lively and Reynolds, citing insufficient grounds for the defamation and extortion allegations.[105][106] Baldoni's libel action against The New York Times was also dismissed in June 2025, prompting the newspaper to countersue on September 30, 2025, for $150,000 in legal fees under California's anti-SLAPP statute.[106] Lively voluntarily withdrew her emotional distress claim on June 3, 2025, and Baldoni declined to amend his dismissed claims by June 24, 2025, opting instead for alternative legal strategies.[105][106] As of October 2025, Lively's core lawsuit against Baldoni and Wayfarer remains active, with discovery proceedings ongoing, including Lively's deposition beginning July 31, 2025.[106] Recent filings by Lively's team allege that Baldoni and associates used auto-deleting Signal messages and voice memos to conceal evidence of the purported smear campaign, in violation of court preservation orders, prompting requests for sanctions and adverse inferences against the defendants.[107] Related peripheral suits, such as one by publicist Jed Wallace against Lively for $7 million in defamation, continue alongside the main action, with no full resolution achieved to date.[106]

Sequel prospects

Rights and planning status

Wayfarer Studios, co-founded by Justin Baldoni, acquired the film rights to Colleen Hoover's It Ends with Us and its sequel novel It Starts with Us in 2019.[108][109] The studio retains primary control over adaptation decisions for the sequel, including potential creative involvement from Baldoni as director or producer.[110][111] Sony Pictures, distributor of the 2024 It Ends with Us film, holds first-look distribution rights for any It Starts with Us adaptation, granting the studio priority in handling theatrical release if production advances.[109] This arrangement stems from the original financing and distribution deal for the first film, ensuring continuity in market strategy.[108] No formal development, scripting, or pre-production announcements for an It Starts with Us film have been made public as of late 2024, with Wayfarer Studios indicating that sequel progression depends on multiple factors including cast availability and studio greenlighting.[110][112] Hoover, the author, has confirmed she lacks direct rights to the sequel adaptation, deferring authority to Wayfarer.[113] Planning remains exploratory, with no confirmed timeline or budget allocations reported.[114]

Challenges and uncertainties

The principal challenge to developing a sequel film adaptation of It Starts with Us, the 2022 novel by Colleen Hoover that continues the story from It Ends with Us, stems from the protracted public and legal feud between lead actress and producer Blake Lively and director and producer Justin Baldoni.[108][109] Sources close to the production have indicated that the acrimony, which escalated during post-production and promotion of the 2024 film, has rendered future collaboration improbable, with one insider stating, "There's probably no world where they work together again."[108] Baldoni's production company, Wayfarer Studios, holds the adaptation rights to It Starts with Us, which introduces additional contractual complexities, as any sequel would likely require Lively's involvement given her central role as Lily Bloom and her producer credits through her company, Blake Lively Productions.[109] Ongoing litigation, including a breach-of-contract lawsuit filed by Wayfarer against Lively's representatives in early 2025 and counter-allegations of misconduct, has further stalled progress, with depositions extending into September 2025 involving third parties such as Taylor Swift.[115][116] Uncertainties persist regarding key creative roles, including directorial leadership; Baldoni publicly suggested in August 2024 that Lively could direct the sequel herself, citing her readiness, but this proposal has not advanced amid mutual accusations of creative overreach and workplace disputes during the original film's production.[117] As of October 2025, Sony Pictures, the distributor of It Ends with Us, has made no official announcement for a sequel despite the first film's global box office exceeding $350 million, leaving open questions about studio commitment, recasting possibilities, and resolution of the underlying conflicts.[114][118]
