Les Carlson

Leslie Merle Carlson (February 24, 1933 – May 3, 2014) was an American actor, who spent most of his professional life in Canada. He is known to film audiences for his work with directors David Cronenberg and Bob Clark, and was also a prolific stage actor.[1] He was nominated for the Genie Award for Best Performance by an Actor in a Supporting Role for his performance in Cronenberg's Videodrome (1983)[2], and won a Dora Award for his performance in the Athol Fugard play Valley Song (1996).[1]

Early life

Carlson was born in Mitchell, South Dakota, in 1933. He was a multi-sport athlete in his youth, playing football, basketball, and track and field.[1] During the Korean War, he served in the United States Air Force. He earned both a BFA and a MA from the University of South Dakota,[1] which he attended in the 1950s on the G.I. Bill.

Career

Carlson began his acting career performing in several stage plays in both the U.S. and England. He immigrated to Canada in the late 1960s and began acting in many films, TV shows, and stage productions in the early '70s. His most memorable film roles were in the horror classic Black Christmas and Deranged , both in 1974 and as a pushy Christmas tree salesman in A Christmas Story in 1983. Carlson appeared in four movies from director David Cronenberg, Videodrome (1983), The Dead Zone (1983), The Fly (1986), and Camera (2000). His portrayal of the antagonist Barry Convex in Videodrome earned him a Genie Award nomination for Best Supporting Actor. His TV appearances include The X-Files , Friday the 13th: The Series (for which he won an ACTRA Award for his role in Raku Fire on CBC), 21 Jump Street and Road to Avonlea. His stage performances included the premiere of Leaving Home, Valley Song (1996, for which he won a Dora Award), A Walk in the Woods in 1991 at the Gateway Theatre, Death and the Maiden in 1993 with the Vancouver Playhouse Theatre Company, the musical Jekyll & Hyde in 1996 for the Canadian Stage Company, Glengarry Glen Ross , Hamlet , and All My Sons.[3] He played notable stints with the Tarragon Theatre, the Stratford Festival, the Shaw Festival, and the Theatre Calgary.[1]

Personal life

Carlson was first married to dancer Donna Grasso in his early 20s; the marriage ended in divorce. He then married actress Patricia Hamilton in 1967; they had one child together, actor Ben Carlson (born c. 1969), and separated amicably around 1971. His third marriage was to Joan Warren in 1983, with whom he had another son, Edmund Carlson, born in 1988.[4]

Death

Carlson died of cancer in Toronto, Ontario, aged 81, on 3 May 2014.[4]
