Barmaley Fountain

The Barmaley Fountain, informally named after the villainous character from Korney Chukovsky's children's poem and officially known as the Children's Round Dance (Детский хоровод), is a pre-World War II sculptural fountain originally installed in Railway Station Square in Stalingrad (present-day Volgograd), Russia, consisting of bronze figures of six children dancing in a circle around a central crocodile that functioned as the water jet.[1][2] Created by sculptor Olga Dmitrievna Manuilova in the 1930s, it symbolized youthful joy and was a prominent urban feature before the German invasion.[3] The fountain gained enduring fame through photographs taken by Soviet war correspondent Emmanuil Evzerikhin on August 23, 1942, depicting its damaged yet standing form amid the rubble following intense Luftwaffe bombing during the early stages of the Battle of Stalingrad, thereby embodying civilian resilience against Nazi aggression.[4][1] Heavily damaged during the prolonged urban combat of the battle, the structure was partially restored by Soviet forces for the 1945 Victory Day celebrations but was ultimately dismantled in the 1950s as part of postwar urban redevelopment; replicas have since been erected near the original site and in other locations such as Voronezh to preserve its memory.[5][2]

Design and Symbolism

Architectural and Sculptural Features

The Barmaley Fountain features a central sculptural composition depicting six children—three boys and three girls—holding hands in a traditional Russian khorovod (round dance) encircling a crocodile figure.[6] The children are portrayed in dynamic, playful poses, emphasizing movement and joy, with the group mounted on a pedestal within a circular basin from which water jets emanate.[7] The crocodile, positioned at the core, functions as a water-spouting element, contributing to the fountain's functional and aesthetic design.[8] Sculpted by Romuald Iodko in the early 1930s, the bronze figures exemplify Soviet-era public art characterized by figurative realism and thematic optimism.[2] The overall structure includes ornate detailing around the basin edges, integrating the sculpture with surrounding water features to create an interactive urban element intended for pedestrian spaces.[9] This design draws from standardized fountain motifs produced in workshops, such as those in Kharkov, where the components were fabricated before assembly.[9]

Inspiration from Literature and Folklore

The informal name of the Barmaley Fountain derives from the eponymous 1925 fairy-tale poem Barmaley by Soviet children's author Korney Chukovsky (1882–1969), in which the titular character is a cannibalistic pirate who captures and threatens children in a fantastical African setting but is ultimately thwarted by rescuers, embodying themes of peril overcome by ingenuity and communal spirit.[2][10] The poem's narrative of childhood vulnerability amid exotic dangers resonated in Soviet cultural motifs of innocence prevailing against chaos, influencing the fountain's allegorical depiction of playful defiance.[11] The fountain's central motif—a circle of six children performing a khorovod (traditional Slavic round dance) around a crocodile—further echoes Chukovsky's broader literary universe, particularly his 1916 poem Crocodile, which portrays a mischievous reptile's adventures in Russia involving child protagonists in whimsical, folkloric escapades blending anthropomorphic animals with human folly.[4] This integration of literary fantasy with khorovod, a ritualistic folk dance rooted in pre-Christian Slavic traditions symbolizing seasonal cycles and communal harmony, underscores the sculpture's evocation of unspoiled youth engaging harmoniously with the wild or threatening unknown.[12] Chukovsky's works, drawing on Russian oral folklore's anthropomorphic beasts and moral fables while incorporating nonsense verse for didactic playfulness, provided a Soviet-era template for public art celebrating proletarian optimism; the fountain's designer adapted these elements to visualize collective joy as a bulwark against adversity, though the crocodile's spouting water evoked both nurturing abundance and latent menace akin to the poem's predatory undertones.[1][13]

Historical Construction and Early Years

Installation in Stalingrad

The Barmaley Fountain was installed in 1930 on Privokzalnaya Square (Railway Station Square) in Stalingrad, directly in front of the Museum of Defense of Tsaritsyn.[2][14][15] This placement coincided with the reconstruction of the Stalingrad railway station, reflecting the Soviet regime's push in the early 1930s to enhance urban public spaces with decorative outdoor architecture and sculptural elements.[15][16] Designed by Soviet sculptor Romuald Iodko, the fountain consisted of a circular basin with a central crocodile figure spouting water, encircled by six bronze children—three boys and three girls—depicted holding hands in a round dance.[8][17] Iodko, known for similar whimsical public fountains, drew from motifs evoking childhood play, aligning with the era's emphasis on monumental yet accessible socialist realism in civic art.[16] The structure stood approximately 2 meters in height, with the children's figures cast in bronze and the basin in concrete, integrated into the square's pedestrian layout to serve as a focal point for passersby.[14] No formal opening ceremony is documented in available records, but the fountain quickly became a local landmark, operational by mid-1930 and contributing to Stalingrad's pre-war aesthetic of proletarian optimism through public amenities.[16][15] Its installation exemplified the broader Soviet policy under the first Five-Year Plan to proliferate such installations across industrial cities, with comparable fountains appearing in places like Dnipropetrovsk and Artemovsk.

Pre-War Cultural Role

The Barmaley Fountain, formally known as the "Children's Round Dance," was installed in 1935 on the square in front of the Stalingrad railway station, featuring a bronze sculptural ensemble of six children holding hands in a circular dance around a crocodile spouting water.[8] This design drew direct inspiration from Korney Chukovsky's 1925 children's poem "Barmaley," which depicts playful children encountering an initially menacing crocodile that ultimately aids their rescue from the poem's titular villain, evoking themes of innocence prevailing over threat.[18] In the Soviet cultural context of the 1930s, such public monuments aligned with socialist realism's promotion of joyful collectivism and protected childhood, integrating folklore adaptations into urban spaces to foster communal harmony and ideological optimism.[18] Prior to the German invasion in 1941, the fountain functioned as a prominent landmark in Stalingrad's central district, embodying the era's emphasis on carefree youth amid rapid industrialization.[5] It symbolized vitality and untroubled daily life, serving as a visual anchor for residents in a city undergoing transformation under Stalin's Five-Year Plans, where public art reinforced narratives of progress and security for the proletariat's offspring.[19] Similar installations in other Soviet cities, such as Voronezh and Orenburg, underscored its role within a broader network of standardized cultural motifs, but in Stalingrad, its placement near the transport hub amplified its accessibility as a site for informal gatherings and aesthetic appreciation.[20] The fountain's pre-war prominence reflected Soviet priorities in children's literature and public pedagogy, with Chukovsky's works—despite occasional censorship—widely disseminated to instill moral resilience and communal spirit.[18] By juxtaposing childlike exuberance against the crocodile's reformed guardianship, it mirrored state messaging on harmony between nature, society, and authority, contributing to the cultural landscape of a frontier industrial hub aspiring to metropolitan sophistication.[19] No records indicate formal events tied exclusively to the fountain, yet its enduring local recognition prior to hostilities highlights its integration into everyday civic identity.[5]

Role in World War II

Survival Amid the Battle of Stalingrad

The Barmaley Fountain, located on Privokzalnaya Square adjacent to Stalingrad's railway station, endured the initial German Luftwaffe bombardment that marked the start of the Battle of Stalingrad on August 23, 1942.[21] This aerial assault devastated the city center, yet photographs taken that same day by Soviet war correspondent Emmanuil Evzerikhin captured the fountain's central crocodile and surrounding children's figures standing amid the rubble and fires, with the sculptures appearing largely intact despite surrounding destruction.[4][22] Throughout the ensuing urban combat from late August 1942 to February 1943, the railway station area became a focal point of fierce fighting, with German and Soviet forces repeatedly contesting positions in close-quarters battles.[1] The fountain, situated in this heavily shelled and assaulted zone, withstood artillery barrages, infantry assaults, and structural collapses nearby, as evidenced by its presence in wartime imagery symbolizing civilian innocence amid warfare's horror.[23] By the Soviet victory on February 2, 1943, the fountain had sustained damage but retained its core sculptural elements, avoiding total obliteration unlike much of the surrounding architecture.[1] This resilience allowed for its documentation in post-battle scenes and facilitated early restoration efforts, underscoring its role as a rare surviving pre-war landmark in the city's ruins.[21]

Iconic Wartime Photography

The Barmaley Fountain gained international recognition through photographs captured by Soviet war photographer Emmanuil Evzerikhin on August 23, 1942, immediately following the initial Luftwaffe bombing raids that initiated the Battle of Stalingrad.[4][23] These images depicted the fountain's bronze statues of six children dancing in a circle around a crocodile, partially damaged by shrapnel yet standing amid widespread rubble and devastation in the city's central square near the railway station.[4][24] Evzerikhin's photographs, taken as German forces launched their aerial assault that reduced much of Stalingrad to ruins, juxtaposed the playful, intact forms of the child figures against the backdrop of collapsed buildings and debris, symbolizing the abrupt intrusion of war upon civilian life and innocence.[23][2] The fountain, which had miraculously avoided total destruction despite the intense bombardment that killed thousands and leveled surrounding structures, became a poignant emblem of resilience in Soviet wartime imagery.[25] These images circulated widely during and after the war, contributing to the global perception of the Battle of Stalingrad's ferocity, with the fountain's survival highlighting the selective mercy of destruction in the urban inferno.[4][1] Soviet propaganda leveraged the photographs to underscore themes of cultural endurance, though their raw depiction of ruin also conveyed unvarnished evidence of Axis aggression's toll on non-combatants.[23] The photographs' enduring legacy stems from their stark visual contrast, influencing subsequent historical documentation of the Eastern Front's urban warfare.[24]

Post-War History and Reconstructions

Immediate Aftermath and Restoration Efforts

Following the Red Army's victory in the Battle of Stalingrad on February 2, 1943, the Barmaley Fountain stood amid the rubble as one of the few surviving pre-war landmarks near the central railway station, symbolizing improbable resilience despite extensive wartime destruction. The sculptural ensemble had endured months of aerial bombings, artillery fire, and urban combat, resulting in fragmentation of the concrete basin and figures, with several child statues decapitated or toppled, though the overall composition remained recognizable.[26][2] In the ensuing months, as Soviet forces secured the area and began initial clearance operations, the damaged fountain featured in wartime documentation, including newsreels and photographs that underscored the human cost of the conflict while highlighting civilian structures' tenacity. By 1943, it appeared in footage for Soviet films depicting the liberation, with fragments collected but no comprehensive repairs undertaken amid ongoing national reconstruction priorities and the continuation of hostilities elsewhere in Europe.[26] Full-scale restoration efforts materialized after the European theater of World War II concluded on May 8, 1945, as part of broader initiatives to rehabilitate Stalingrad's infrastructure under the Soviet government's post-war recovery programs. Workers reassembled and recast damaged elements, restoring the fountain's basin and child figures to operational condition by the late 1940s, enabling it to function once more as a public water feature and cultural site before urban planning changes led to its disassembly in the mid-1950s.[2][4][27]

Removal and Urban Redevelopment

The original Barmaley Fountain, having survived the Battle of Stalingrad and been restored shortly after the war's end, was dismantled in the 1950s as part of Volgograd's (then Stalingrad) extensive post-war urban reconstruction.[28][2] The city's infrastructure had been devastated, with over 90% of buildings destroyed during the 1942–1943 battle, necessitating a Soviet master plan for rebuilding that emphasized grand, neoclassical-style public spaces, wide boulevards, and monumental architecture to symbolize resilience and Soviet triumph.[29] This redevelopment prioritized functional urban layouts over preserving individual wartime relics, leading to the removal of structures like the fountain to accommodate new infrastructure, such as expanded squares and roadways.[28] The precise date and mechanism of the fountain's removal remain undocumented in available records, with its post-dismantlement location and fate unknown; components were likely melted down or repurposed amid material shortages during reconstruction.[7] Urban planning under architects like Lev Rudnev and others focused on ideological reconstruction, integrating propaganda elements into the cityscape while clearing debris and wartime anomalies, which included the fountain's site near central areas repurposed for modern utilities and traffic flow.[29] By the mid-1950s, under early Khrushchev-era shifts, such efforts accelerated de-Stalinization in nomenclature but continued the physical overhaul, rendering pre-war and wartime features like the Barmaley secondary to the new "hero city" aesthetic.[29] No contemporary records indicate ideological opposition to the fountain's theme—drawn from Korney Chukovsky's fairy tale—as a factor; removal aligned with pragmatic engineering demands rather than cultural erasure.[28]

Modern Replicas and Preservation

In 2013, two replicas of the Barmaley Fountain were erected in Volgograd to commemorate the fountain's historical legacy and the 71st anniversary of the Battle of Stalingrad's onset. One replica, portraying the original intact design of children dancing in a khorovod around a crocodile, was installed on Privokzalnaya Square opposite the railway station.[18] The unveiling of this replica took place on August 23, 2013, with Russian President Vladimir Putin in attendance, highlighting its role as a symbol of the city's wartime endurance.[5] A second replica, configured to replicate the fountain's damaged appearance as documented in 1942 photographs amid the ruins, was positioned near Gerhardt's Mill, a preserved Battle of Stalingrad site.[30] These modern installations utilize durable composite materials rather than the original concrete, with child figures scaled larger to suit contemporary urban proportions.[31] The replicas represent ongoing preservation efforts focused on cultural memory rather than physical restoration of the lost original, which was dismantled in the 1950s during post-war urban reconstruction.[4] By reinstating visual echoes of the fountain at key historical loci, Volgograd authorities and local initiatives, including contributions from groups like the Night Wolves motorcycle club, aim to sustain public awareness of its symbolism without altering the authentic wartime landscape.[32]

Cultural and Historical Significance

Symbolism of Resilience and Childhood Innocence

![Barmaley Fountain after German air raid in Stalingrad][float-right] The Barmaley Fountain, officially known as the Children's Khorovod, features bronze sculptures of six children engaged in a traditional Russian round dance encircling a crocodile, evoking themes of playful innocence and communal joy associated with childhood.[5] This depiction draws from Soviet-era cultural motifs celebrating youth and vitality, contrasting sharply with the surrounding urban environment in pre-war Stalingrad.[21] During the Battle of Stalingrad from August 1942 to February 1943, the fountain endured intense Luftwaffe bombings that reduced much of the city to rubble, remaining remarkably intact and thereby symbolizing the unyielding spirit of innocence amid unparalleled destruction.[1] Iconic photographs taken by Soviet war correspondent Emmanuil Evzerikhin on August 23, 1942, captured the undamaged figures standing defiant against a backdrop of devastation, reinforcing its role as an emblem of resilience where fragile human ideals persisted through total war.[4] The sculpture's survival, despite direct hits nearby, has been interpreted as a testament to the durability of childhood's purity and the human capacity to reclaim joy post-trauma, influencing its post-war cultural resonance as a marker of endurance rather than mere victimhood.[28] This symbolism underscores causal links between the fountain's physical fortitude—owing to its sturdy construction and fortuitous positioning—and broader narratives of societal recovery, without reliance on ideological overlays.[2]

Use in Soviet Propaganda and National Narrative

![Barmaley Fountain after German bombing][float-right] The Barmaley Fountain's survival amid the intense German bombardment during the Battle of Stalingrad in August 1942 was captured in photographs by Soviet photographer Emmanuil Yevzerikhin, who worked for the state news agency TASS.[23] These images, depicting the intact statue of children dancing around a crocodile surrounded by rubble and, in one notable instance, a dead German soldier slumped nearby, were disseminated through Soviet media to highlight the contrast between the joyful symbol of childhood innocence and the destruction wrought by Nazi forces.[4] [33] In wartime propaganda, the fountain served as a potent emblem of Soviet resilience, illustrating that even in the epicenter of devastation near the city's railway station, symbols of peaceful Soviet life and the future generation endured unscathed by enemy attacks.[5] Official narratives portrayed its preservation as evidence of the defenders' unyielding spirit, reinforcing morale among troops and civilians by evoking the protection of the motherland's children against fascist barbarism.[28] Post-war, the fountain was woven into the Soviet national narrative of the Great Patriotic War, commemorating Stalingrad's pivotal role in turning the tide against Germany. Restored initially after the victory, its imagery persisted in memorials and publications as a testament to collective heroism and moral superiority, underscoring themes of sacrifice for the coming generation's peace.[5] This usage aligned with broader state efforts to mythologize the battle, emphasizing endurance over annihilation to foster national unity and ideological commitment.[28]

Criticisms and Alternative Interpretations

Some art historians and critics have characterized the Barmaley Fountain as an exemplar of socialist realist kitsch, a style decried by Clement Greenberg in his 1939 essay "Avant-Garde and Kitsch" for its mass-produced sentimentality that fosters emotional conformity and serves totalitarian regimes by substituting vicarious experience for authentic engagement with reality.[34] In this view, the fountain's depiction of joyful children amid urban planning promotes an idealized vision of Soviet childhood innocence, masking the regime's coercive social engineering and the brutal realities of collectivization and purges preceding World War II.[35] The fountain's informal name derives from Korney Chukovsky's 1925 children's poem "Barmaley," which portrays Africa as a perilous realm inhabited by cannibals, sharks, and gorillas, reinforcing early Soviet literary stereotypes of the continent as savage and inhospitable—a trope critiqued in analyses of Russian children's literature for echoing colonialist anxieties despite the USSR's anti-imperial rhetoric.[11] Scholars note that while the poem entertains through fantasy, its exoticized dangers, including the pirate Barmaley's abduction of children, perpetuate a "dark Africa" narrative that prioritizes avoidance over nuance, potentially influencing cultural perceptions embedded in monuments like the fountain.[36] Alternative interpretations question the fountain's role in post-war Soviet historiography, arguing that its survival and photographic iconography, such as Emmanuil Evzerikhin's 1942 images, were leveraged to symbolize unyielding civilian spirit while eliding Soviet military miscalculations and the high cost of urban defense strategies that exposed non-combatants to devastation.[1] This selective emphasis, per some cultural memory studies, aligns with state narratives that elevated the Battle of Stalingrad as a moral crusade, downplaying Stalin-era policies contributing to pre-war vulnerabilities like industrial overreach and famine.[37]

Name and Etymology

Origin of the Name "Barmaley"

The informal name "Barmaley" for the fountain derives from the titular character in Korney Chukovsky's children's poem Barmaley, first published in 1925, which depicts a cannibalistic pirate who kidnaps two children, Tanechka and Vanechka, in Africa and threatens to feed them to crocodiles before falling ill, summoning Doctor Aybolit for treatment, reforming, and ultimately expressing remorse through telegrams of affection.[38][39] The poem's narrative of childhood peril resolved through reconciliation and whimsy, intertwined with Chukovsky's broader cycle featuring benevolent crocodiles in works like Doctor Aybolit, resonated with the fountain's sculptural motif of six bronze children dancing in a ring around a central crocodile figure spouting water, symbolizing innocence and joy prevailing over menace.[40] Designed by Soviet-Polish architect Romuald Iodko and installed in 1935 in front of the Museum of the Defense of Tsaritsyn-Stalingrad, the fountain's official designation was "Det skiy Khorovod" (Children's Round Dance), emphasizing the choreographed figures rather than the literary antagonist.[40] However, the evocative parallel to Chukovsky's tale—where the children's eventual harmony echoes the post-reform idyll—prompted locals to adopt "Barmaley" as the colloquial moniker, alongside alternatives like "Krokodil" (Crocodile) or "Tantsuyushchiye Deti" (Dancing Children), reflecting the design's folkloric inspiration amid Stalin-era cultural motifs blending whimsy with ideological optimism.) This naming convention underscores how Soviet public art often drew from accessible children's literature to evoke resilience and communal harmony, though the villainous connotation of "Barmaley" later acquired ironic layers in wartime contexts.[14]

Alternative Names and Linguistic Context

The Barmaley Fountain bears the official designation Fountain "Children's Khorovod", where khorovod denotes a traditional Slavic folk dance executed in a circular chain, with participants linking hands and advancing in rhythmic steps to accompany songs or chants.[41] This name reflects the sculpture's central motif of six bronze children clasping hands in a ring around a crocodile figure, evoking communal joy and innocence.[17] Colloquial alternatives include "Dancing Children" (Tantsuyushchiye deti), "Crocodile" (Krokodil), and "Children and Crocodile", alluding directly to the sculptural elements and drawing from associations in Soviet-era children's literature by Korney Chukovsky, whose tales featured anthropomorphic reptiles and child protagonists.[17] [41] The nickname Barmaley, though informal and not reflective of the design's ostensible theme of harmony, gained prevalence post-World War II through wartime photographs and oral tradition, linking the fountain to Chukovsky's monstrous character despite the crocodile's benign pose.[42] Linguistically, Barmaley (Бармалей) functions as a nonce word in Russian, invented by Chukovsky for his 1925 poem to conjure an exotic, barbaric menace, with phonetic echoes possibly from Turkic-Mongol onomatopoeia for rumbling or exotic names like Bayram-Ali, though Chukovsky reportedly drew inspiration from a St. Petersburg street name without deeper etymological intent.[43] This contrasts with the fountain's Slavic-rooted khorovod, rooted in Proto-Slavic xorovodъ, emphasizing collective ritual over individual threat.[41]
