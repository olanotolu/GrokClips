Al-Badr (Jammu and Kashmir)

Al-Badr is a pro-Pakistan Islamist militant organization operating primarily in Jammu and Kashmir, formed in June 1998 as a splinter group from Hizb-ul-Mujahideen with encouragement from Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence to conduct independent operations aimed at separating the region from India via armed jihad and merging it with Pakistan.[1][2] The group traces its roots to attacks on Bengalis in East Pakistan in 1971 and collaboration with Hizb-e-Islami in Afghanistan during the 1980s, entering Kashmir in 1990 before splitting to pursue a more radical agenda opposing moderate Kashmiri factions, the United States, Israel, and Saudi Arabia.[1][2] Headquartered in Mansehra, Pakistan, with training camps in Muzaffarabad in Pakistan-occupied Kashmir, Al-Badr maintains a cadre strength of around 200 militants, including foreign mercenaries, and is affiliated with the United Jihad Council alongside groups like Lashkar-e-Taiba and Jaish-e-Mohammed.[1] Its activities include ambushes and assaults on Indian security forces and civilians using small arms, grenades, and suicide bombings, enforcement of strict Islamist codes such as mandatory veiling for women, and publication of a propaganda magazine since 1999; notable involvement encompasses the 1999 Kargil intrusion and targeted killings, such as the murder of three girls in Rajouri in 2002.[1][2] Led initially by Lukmaan and currently by Bakht Zameen, with operational commanders like Arfeen Bhai in Jammu and Kashmir, the group sustains operations through infiltration, recruitment via social media, and cross-border support from Pakistan.[1] Al-Badr was proscribed as a terrorist entity by the Government of India on April 1, 2002, under the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act and listed among banned organizations by the Ministry of Home Affairs.[1] It has also been designated a foreign terrorist organization by the United States, reflecting its role in cross-border terrorism and links to broader jihadist networks including possible ties to Al-Qaeda and the Taliban.[1] Despite declines in overall militancy in the region, Al-Badr remains active in areas like Anantnag, Srinagar, and Poonch, contributing to persistent instability through proxy warfare backed by Pakistani entities.[1][2]

Origins and Formation

Establishment by Pakistani ISI

The Pakistani Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI) facilitated the establishment of Al-Badr in June 1998 as a proxy militant group to intensify the armed insurgency in Indian-administered Jammu and Kashmir. This occurred against the backdrop of strategic imperatives for Pakistan to maintain deniable control over separatist operations, particularly by integrating foreign Arab fighters whom the dominant indigenous group, Hizbul Mujahideen (HM), had increasingly resisted absorbing due to ideological and tactical differences. ISI reportedly orchestrated the splintering of HM dissidentsâ€”primarily younger, more radical Kashmiri cadres advocating aggressive infiltration and pan-Islamist tacticsâ€”into Al-Badr, providing logistical support, training, and arms from bases in Pakistan-occupied Kashmir (PoK) and Punjab province.[3][4] Al-Badr's formation marked ISI's pattern of engineering factional breaks within existing outfits to inject transnational elements and sustain proxy warfare, as HM's leadership under Syed Salahuddin prioritized local recruitment and negotiation leverage over ISI's preferred escalation via foreign mujahideen from Afghanistan and the Middle East. Initial cadres numbered around 200-300, drawn from HM defectors and inducted foreigners previously affiliated with groups like Harkat-ul-Mujahideen, enabling Al-Badr to conduct its first operations, including ambushes on Indian security forces in south Kashmir by late 1998.[3][4] The group's professed aim, as articulated in early manifestos, was to "liberate" Jammu and Kashmir through unrelenting jihad, aligning with ISI's broader doctrine of bleeding India via asymmetric attrition without direct conventional confrontation.[3] Evidence of ISI's direct hand emerges from intercepted communications and captured documents during Indian counterinsurgency raids in the late 1990s, which revealed funding channels via Pakistani handlers and coordinated cross-border launches from PoK launchpads like Muzaffarabad. While Pakistan officially denies involvement, attributing Al-Badr's rise to indigenous grievances, declassified Indian intelligence assessments and neutral analyses corroborate ISI's role in its inception as part of a diversified portfolio of Kashmir-focused proxies, including Lashkar-e-Taiba and Jaish-e-Mohammed, to evade international scrutiny post-1998 nuclear tests.[3]

Split from Hizbul Mujahideen

Al-Badr originated as a faction within Hizbul Mujahideen (HM), the dominant Kashmiri militant group formed in 1989, before splitting off as an independent entity in 1998.[5] This division arose from internal disagreements over strategy and ideology, with the Al-Badr faction favoring a more transnational jihadist approach influenced by Afghan and Pakistani networks, contrasting HM's emphasis on indigenous Kashmiri resistance.[3] The split was reportedly encouraged by Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI) to establish a proxy capable of conducting deniable operations and recruiting foreign fighters, thereby providing operational flexibility beyond HM's structure.[5] Key figures in the faction included militants aligned with HM's more hardline elements, such as those under commanders linked to Bakht Zameen Khan, who later led Al-Badr activities.[4] Post-split, Al-Badr positioned itself as a distinct outfit with a focus on high-impact attacks, absorbing HM dissidents and foreign mujahideen, while occasionally serving as a feeder for groups like Lashkar-e-Taiba.[3] The separation weakened HM temporarily but bolstered Pakistan's proxy network in Jammu and Kashmir, enabling Al-Badr to claim responsibility for attacks like the 1999 Anantnag ambush that killed 11 Indian soldiers.[5] Scholarly analyses indicate the split reflected broader fractures in the United Jihad Council umbrella, where Al-Badr's pan-Islamist orientation allowed it to evade some of HM's internal purges and ceasefire declarations, such as HM's 2000 truce attempt.[3] By 2002, when India proscribed Al-Badr under anti-terror laws, it had solidified as a standalone group with an estimated 100-150 operatives, primarily operating in southern Kashmir valleys.[3]

Ideology and Objectives

Islamist Jihadist Goals

Al-Badr pursues the establishment of Islamic governance in Jammu and Kashmir via armed jihad against Indian security forces and civilian targets, framing the insurgency as a religious obligation to expel perceived infidel occupation and implement Sharia law.[3] The group's ideology emphasizes fundamentalist Islamism, rejecting secular or nationalist separatism in favor of theocratic rule, with militants trained to propagate jihadist literature and recruit on promises of martyrdom for an Islamic caliphate-like order in the region.[3] [6] This jihadist orientation positions Kashmir's conflict within a broader transnational Islamist struggle, where Al-Badr's operations align with global calls to unite Muslim fighters against non-Islamic states, drawing ideological influence from Pakistani-based networks that view India as an existential threat to Islamic expansion.[7] Members have issued statements invoking Quranic imperatives for holy war, aiming to radicalize local populations through propaganda materials that depict Indian rule as kufr (unbelief) warranting violent overthrow.[3] Unlike more localized groups, Al-Badr's split from Hizbul Mujahideen in 1998 stemmed from dissatisfaction with negotiation overtures, reinforcing a commitment to unrelenting militancy until Islamic sovereignty is achieved.[6] Tactically, these goals manifest in targeted killings of Indian personnel and Hindus, intended to cleanse the region demographically and territorially for Islamist dominance, with explicit endorsements of suicide bombings and ambushes as fard ayn (individual duty) in jihad.[3] The group's persistence, despite designations as a terrorist entity by India in 2002 and the U.S. in 2001, underscores an eschatological vision where Kashmir serves as a vanguard for wider South Asian Islamization.[7]

Stated Aims in Kashmir Conflict

Al-Badr has articulated its primary aim in the Kashmir conflict as the armed liberation of Jammu and Kashmir from Indian administration, followed by its merger with Pakistan to establish an Islamic state under strict Sharia governance.[1][2] The group frames this objective as fulfilling the Kashmiri Muslims' right to self-determination in line with United Nations resolutions, rejecting Indian sovereignty as an occupation that must be ended through relentless jihad against security forces and perceived collaborators.[1] In pursuit of these goals, Al-Badr emphasizes imposing fundamentalist Islamist norms on the region's population, including directives for women to abandon employment, don veils, and restrict education to basic levels compliant with their interpretation of Islamic law.[1][2] The organization opposes moderate separatist factions, such as the Jammu Kashmir Liberation Front (JKLF), for advocating independence rather than accession to Pakistan, and dismisses non-violent approaches or India-Pakistan dialogues as betrayals of jihadist principles.[1][2] Beyond immediate territorial aims, Al-Badr views Kashmir as the "gateway to India," positioning its success as a precursor to broader jihadist expansion aimed at liberating Muslims across the subcontinent from non-Islamic rule.[1] This expansive vision aligns with affiliations to the United Jihad Council and Pakistani state elements, prioritizing fidayeen attacks on military targets, selective assassinations of officials and activists, and rejection of peace initiatives to sustain insurgency.[2] The group's rhetoric consistently invokes a "decisive war against India" to expel security forces and enforce Islamic governance, as evidenced in operational statements and actions since its 1998 formation.[2]

Organizational Structure and Operations

Leadership and Key Figures

The Al-Badr militant group, established in June 1998 with encouragement from Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence, was initially led by Lukmaan, a resident of Pakistan-occupied Kashmir, who oversaw its early operations as it splintered from Hizbul Mujahideen.[1] Lukmaan, also known by aliases such as Arfeen Bhai and Jannisar, served as the chief commander in Jammu and Kashmir, directing field activities amid the group's focus on jihadist insurgency.[1] Bakht Zameen, originating from Punjab province in Pakistan, emerged as the overall chief commander, maintaining oversight of cadre movements, including monitoring operations during the 1999 Kargil conflict from Skardu.[1] Other key figures included Irfan as the launching commander responsible for initial deployments; Zahid Bhai as deputy supreme commander; Jasm Bhat handling publicity and propaganda; and Abu Mawai managing communications.[1] These roles reflected a hierarchical structure blending Pakistani oversight with operational commanders in the Kashmir valley, though leadership has been disrupted by targeted killings, such as senior commander Abu Tamim in Kupwara district in January 2009.[8] In more recent years, Gani Khawaja acted as a prominent chief, coordinating attacks until his elimination by Indian security forces in a gunfight in Sopore, Baramulla district, on March 9, 2021.[9] The group's leadership remains opaque and fluid, with Pakistani-based figures providing strategic direction while local commanders face high attrition rates from counterinsurgency operations.[1]

Recruitment, Training, and Ties to Pakistan

Al-Badr primarily recruits local Kashmiri youth disillusioned with Indian administration, alongside foreign militants from Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Arab countries, maintaining a cadre strength of approximately 200 operatives, including around 120 foreign mercenaries as of early 2000s assessments.[1] Recruitment efforts leverage ideological appeals to Islamist jihad against Indian control, often targeting individuals via networks in Pakistan-occupied Kashmir (PoK) and through affiliations with groups like Jamaat-e-Islami.[1] Training occurs in ISI-supported camps located in Manshera (then in North-West Frontier Province, now Khyber Pakhtunkhwa), Kotli, and Muzaffarabad in PoK, where militants undergo instruction in guerrilla tactics, weapons handling, and explosives use before infiltration across the Line of Control into Jammu and Kashmir.[1] These facilities, part of broader Pakistani infrastructure for Kashmir-focused militants, enable Al-Badr to sustain operations despite Indian counterinsurgency pressures.[6] The group maintains deep operational ties to Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI), which orchestrated its formation in June 1998 as a pro-Pakistan splinter from Hizbul Mujahideen to marginalize independence-oriented factions and bolster Islamabad's influence in the Kashmir insurgency.[1] Al-Badr collaborates closely with the ISI and Jamaat-e-Islami Pakistan, participating in the United Jihad Councilâ€”an umbrella of PoK-based groupsâ€”and receiving logistical, financial, and directive support, as evidenced by its role in the 1999 Kargil intrusion under ISI guidance.[1][6] Such linkages underscore Pakistan's strategic use of proxies to advance territorial claims without direct military attribution.[6]

Areas of Activity

Primary Locations in Jammu and Kashmir

Al-Badr's militant activities have been concentrated in the Kashmir Valley, particularly in northern districts proximate to the Line of Control (LoC), facilitating infiltration from Pakistan-occupied Kashmir. Kupwara district, including areas like Handwara and Lolab, has seen frequent operations, with security forces killing key figures such as commander Abu Tamim on January 15, 2009, and conducting multiple arrests and encounters in 2008.[10][8] Baramulla district, encompassing Sopore and Rafiabad, has hosted recruitment modules and arrests, such as the busting of a terror network involving seven members on February 12, 2022, and the apprehension of Munir Ahmad Bukhari on June 11, 2012.[10][11] Southern and central districts of the Valley, including Pulwama (notably Shopian), Budgam, Bandipora, and Kulgam, have also featured Al-Badr presence through killings, arrests, and hideouts. For instance, encounters in Shopian and Pulwama resulted in militant deaths in 2008 and 2022, while Budgam saw operations in March and December 2008, and Bandipora reported a killing on July 20, 2008, alongside one in Kulgam on November 4, 2007.[10][12] These areas align with Al-Badr's tactical focus on ambushes and support networks amid dense terrain and local sympathizers.[3] Activity has extended sporadically to the Jammu region, including Doda, Udhampur, and Poonch districts, where Al-Badr was linked to attacks like the May 2006 massacre of 35 Hindus in remote hamlets, attributed by police to joint operations with Lashkar-e-Taiba. An Afghan national tied to Al-Badr was arrested near the LoC in Poonch on August 11, 2023.[13][14] However, Jammu operations remain secondary to Valley strongholds, reflecting the group's emphasis on Kashmir-centric insurgency.[1]

Expansion and Tactical Focus

Al-Badr expanded its operations beyond the Kashmir Valley into the Jammu region, particularly districts such as Doda, Udhampur, and Rajouri, where it established presence through infiltration and local recruitment networks supported by training camps in Pakistan.[3] This growth occurred primarily in the late 1990s and early 2000s, leveraging cross-border logistics to increase militant strength from an initial cadre of defectors from Hizbul Mujahideen to several hundred operatives at its peak, focusing on sustaining insurgency amid counterterrorism pressures.[3] The group's tactical focus emphasized asymmetric guerrilla warfare to target Indian security forces and perceived collaborators, employing hit-and-run ambushes on patrols and convoys to maximize casualties while minimizing exposure.[3] Improvised explosive devices (IEDs) were frequently used for remote detonations against military vehicles and installations, alongside targeted assassinations of political workers, civilians, and informants to instill fear and disrupt governance.[3] Fidayeen-style suicide assaults, involving small teams storming fortified positions, represented a shift toward high-impact operations, as seen in attacks on paramilitary camps, though such tactics yielded limited strategic gains against fortified Indian defenses.[3]

Major Attacks and Militant Actions

Key Incidents and Chronology

Al-Badr was established in June 1998 by Pakistani Inter-Services Intelligence operatives as a splinter from the Markaz-ud-Dawa-wal-Irshad (later Lashkar-e-Taiba), with the aim of intensifying jihadist operations in Jammu and Kashmir to achieve merger with Pakistan.[1] In 1999, its cadres participated in the Kargil conflict intrusion, coordinated from Skardu under chief commander Bakht Zameen.[1] The group resumed overt activities in 2000, including the deployment of a suicide squad, five members of which were killed in a security forces raid near Ganderbal.[1] On September 5, 2001, Al-Badr issued a statement opposing India-Pakistan dialogue and calling for sustained jihad.[1] In October 2001, authorities uncovered a plot by Al-Badr to assassinate then-Chief Minister Farooq Abdullah and his son Omar Abdullah, leading to arrests.[10] On June 27, 2002, Al-Badr claimed responsibility for a grenade attack at Lal Chowk in Anantnag, killing one woman and injuring 33 others.[10] On December 20, 2002, militants from the group killed three girls, including two college students, in Hasiot village, Rajouri district.[1] On November 28, 2002, Al-Badr launched a rocket attack on Radio Kashmir in Srinagar, with no reported casualties.[10] In August 2003, the group issued diktats in Rajouri demanding women quit police jobs, wear veils, and restrict education.[1] On February 24, 2005, Al-Badr executed a fidayeen (suicide) attack in Srinagar, resulting in five deaths and four injuries.[10] Post-2005, Al-Badr's operational tempo declined amid intensified counterterrorism, with activities shifting to statements and sporadic recruitment; by 2013-2014, it issued calls for renewed jihad but claimed few direct attacks.[1] The Indian government proscribed Al-Badr on April 1, 2002, under the Prevention of Terrorism Act.[10]

Methods and Targets

Al-Badr militants have utilized guerrilla warfare tactics, including ambushes, hit-and-run assaults, and infiltration across the Line of Control to conduct operations in small, mobile units.[3] [2] These methods rely on small arms, grenades, improvised explosive devices (IEDs), and explosives for armed assaults, bombings, and sabotage against infrastructure.[2] The group is notable for employing fidayeen suicide squads, a tactic involving martyrdom-seeking attackers that distinguishes it among Kashmir-based outfits and aligns it operationally with groups like Jaish-e-Mohammed.[2] Such operations often feature coordinated strikes on security convoys or camps, as seen in a June 2002 ambush in Poonch district that killed seven Indian Army soldiers.[3] Targeted assassinations form a core method, focusing on political figures, government officials, human rights activists, and individuals labeled as pro-India or "traitors" to the insurgency.[3] [2] Examples include the 1992 killing of human rights activist H.N. Wanchoo and the 1991 assassination of official Mohammed Sayeed.[2] The group also enforces strict interpretations of Islamic law through selective violence, targeting moderate Muslims, women for non-compliance with dress codes, and sites associated with "un-Islamic" activities such as music shops or cinemas.[2] Primary targets encompass Indian security forces, military installations, and law enforcement personnel, with attacks aimed at disrupting counterinsurgency efforts.[3] [2] Civilian targets, particularly non-Muslims like Hindus and Kashmiri Pandits, have been prioritized to foster ethnic cleansing and communal tensions, as evidenced by the 2000 Chittisinghpora massacre of 36 Sikhs and the 2002 Kaluchak attack that killed 31 civilians, including families.[2] Government symbols, such as the 2001 Jammu and Kashmir legislative assembly assault, underscore efforts to undermine political processes.[2] These actions, often coordinated via the United Jihad Council with affiliates like Lashkar-e-Taiba, have resulted in over 200 deaths attributed to Al-Badr between 1998 and 2002.[2]

Designations and International Responses

Indian Government Proscription

The Government of India proscribed Al-Badr as a terrorist organization on 1 April 2002 under the Prevention of Terrorism Act, 2002 (POTA), classifying it among groups engaged in armed insurgency and attacks in Jammu and Kashmir.[1] This designation rendered membership, funding, and support for the group illegal, with penalties including imprisonment and asset forfeiture, aimed at disrupting its operational networks linked to cross-border militancy.[1] POTA's repeal in March 2004 led to the transfer of proscriptions to the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, 1967 (UAPA), maintaining Al-Badr's banned status and enabling continued enforcement against its activities, such as recruitment and arms procurement.[1] The Ministry of Home Affairs oversees periodic reviews of such designations, with Al-Badr remaining subject to counterterrorism measures due to documented involvement in over 100 incidents of violence between 2000 and 2002, including ambushes on Indian forces.[1] Enforcement has included arrests of alleged operatives and seizure of funds traced to Pakistani handlers, reflecting the proscription's role in broader efforts to sever external support for Kashmir-focused militancy.[1] Despite operational decline post-2005, the ban persists to prevent resurgence, with UAPA provisions allowing preemptive actions against rebranded factions.[1]

US and Other Designations

The United States Department of State identifies Al-Badr, also referred to as Al-Badr Mujahideen, as a terrorist organization operating in Jammu and Kashmir, attributing specific attacks to the group in its annual Country Reports on Terrorism and statistical annexes.[15][16] For instance, the 2023 annex records one incident and one fatality linked to Al-Badr Mujahideen in India, reflecting ongoing monitoring of its activities despite its diminished prominence.[15] U.S. designations of Kashmir-focused groups like Al-Badr emphasize their role in Islamist militancy, though it lacks the formal Foreign Terrorist Organization (FTO) status under U.S. law that imposes specific financial and material support prohibitions, unlike affiliates such as Lashkar-e-Taiba.[17][18] No specific proscriptions of Al-Badr appear on the United Nations Security Council's consolidated sanctions lists or the European Union's common foreign and security policy measures against terrorism.[19] Similarly, the United Kingdom's list of proscribed organizations under the Terrorism Act 2000 does not include Al-Badr, focusing instead on higher-profile global jihadist networks.[20] This limited international formal designation contrasts with broader recognition of its ties to Pakistan-based militancy but aligns with Al-Badr's operational decline and absorption into other groups since the early 2000s.[1]

Controversies and Alternative Perspectives

Claims of Legitimate Resistance

Al-Badr militants have framed their armed campaign as a legitimate jihad to counter Indian military presence in Jammu and Kashmir, portraying it as a defensive struggle to protect the Muslim population from alleged systematic oppression and human rights violations by Indian security forces. The group asserts that such resistance is sanctioned under Islamic principles, obligating believers to wage holy war against perceived non-Muslim occupation of Muslim lands. This narrative positions Al-Badr as mujahideen fighters seeking to enforce Sharia law and ultimately integrate the region into an Islamic polity, often aligned with Pakistani interests rather than independent Kashmiri self-determination.[3] In statements claiming responsibility for operations, Al-Badr has invoked religious duty to justify targeting Indian military installations and personnel, describing these as proportionate responses to India's revocation of Jammu and Kashmir's special status in August 2019 and subsequent security crackdowns. Supporters, including affiliated networks in Pakistan, argue that the insurgency upholds the right to resist foreign domination, drawing parallels to historical Islamic resistances while dismissing Indian counterterrorism measures as evidence of colonial subjugation. However, these claims are undermined by the group's involvement in attacks on civilians, which contradict conventional interpretations of jihad limiting violence to combatants.[21][3] The ideology emphasizes pan-Islamist solidarity, with Al-Badr recruiting from broader jihadist circles beyond local Kashmiris, including Afghan and Pakistani fighters, to sustain what they term a global ummah defense against Indian "Hindu nationalism." This broader framing elevates the Kashmir conflict to a religious war, legitimizing foreign involvement as fraternal aid rather than external aggression. Critics, including Indian and international analysts, contend that such rhetoric masks Pakistani state sponsorship, evidenced by training camps in Pakistan's border regions, rendering the resistance claim a proxy for geopolitical maneuvering rather than organic local grievance.[3][21]

Evidence of Pakistani Sponsorship and Criticisms

Al-Badr has been accused by Indian authorities of receiving direct sponsorship from Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI), including recruitment, training in camps located in Pakistan-occupied Kashmir, and logistical support to sustain operations across the Line of Control. In June 2020, Jammu and Kashmir Director General of Police Dilbag Singh asserted that Pakistan was actively financing the group, operating training facilities, and providing safe havens for militants while attempting to mask its involvement to evade international scrutiny. These claims align with broader patterns of Pakistani state elements backing Kashmir-focused militants, as documented in analyses of cross-border infiltration and arms supply routes.[2] The group's operational ties to Pakistan-based Lashkar-e-Taiba (LeT) further indicate shared resources and command structures facilitated by Pakistani territory, with Al-Badr militants reportedly undergoing joint training and coordinating attacks.[3] Captured operatives have confessed to receiving weapons, explosives, and ideological indoctrination from handlers in Pakistan, underscoring the ISI's role in directing fidayeen (suicide) squads and ambush tactics.[2] Such sponsorship is evidenced by the group's formation in 1998 from Hizbul Mujahideen dissidents, a split allegedly engineered to create a more pliable proxy for Pakistani strategic interests in escalating the insurgency.[3] Criticisms of Al-Badr center on its pattern of indiscriminate violence against civilians, including abductions, targeted killings, and bombings that violate international norms on armed conflict. On December 20, 2002, militants affiliated with the group executed three young girlsâ€”two college studentsâ€”in the Jammu region, an act decried as a deliberate terror campaign against non-combatants to instill fear.[1] The outfit has been implicated in numerous grenade attacks, such as those injuring four civilians in Jammu and Kashmir, highlighting its disregard for distinguishing between military and civilian targets.[3] These actions have drawn condemnation from human rights monitors for exacerbating communal tensions and prolonging civilian suffering in the region, with the group's pro-Pakistan orientation viewed as subordinating local grievances to foreign agendas.[3]

Current Status and Decline

Recent Developments Post-2010

Following the intensification of counterinsurgency operations in Jammu and Kashmir, Al-Badr's operational capacity diminished significantly after 2010, with security forces reporting multiple neutralizations and arrests of its cadres. In September 2017, Indian security personnel eliminated a self-styled Al-Badr "lone wolf" militant during Operation All Out, a sustained campaign targeting top insurgents.[22] By 2022, only four Al-Badr militants were reported killed amid a broader decline in active insurgents across the region, attributed to enhanced intelligence and cordon-and-search operations.[23] Al-Badr maintained limited involvement in logistical support for militancy, including arms infiltration. In August 2022, two jailed operatives linked to Al-Badr and Lashkar-e-Taiba were implicated in drone-dropped weapon consignments from Pakistan, highlighting hybrid tactics to sustain low-level threats despite cadre shortages.[24] The National Investigation Agency (NIA) arrested four terror associates in October 2021 for aiding Al-Badr and affiliated networks in recruitment and funding, underscoring persistent but fragmented support structures.[25] Post the August 2019 abrogation of Article 370, which revoked Jammu and Kashmir's special status, Al-Badr's activities further contracted amid reduced local recruitment and increased cross-border scrutiny. Pakistani handlers shifted toward deploying trained Al-Badr operatives for guerrilla-style attacks in the Jammu region, as noted by officials in July 2023, aiming to evade attribution while denying direct involvement.[26] In March 2024, a senior Al-Badr-linked commander, designated a terrorist by India's Ministry of Home Affairs, died under mysterious circumstances in Pakistan, reflecting internal pressures or rivalries within sponsored networks.[27] Recent investigations reveal Al-Badr's residual role in high-profile incidents. A key perpetrator of the April 2025 Pahalgam attack, Pakistani national Hashim Musa (alias Suleiman), had prior Al-Badr affiliations and involvement in multiple Jammu and Kashmir strikes, prompting NIA sketches and probes into cross-border linkages.[28] In June 2025, NIA raids at 32 locations targeted Pakistan-backed terror financing and overground worker networks, including those supporting Al-Badr, indicating ongoing but low-intensity efforts to revive capabilities amid sustained counterterrorism pressure.[29]

Counterterrorism Impacts and Remnants

![Indian security operations](![](. /assets/Flag_of_India.svg.png)) Indian security forces have conducted numerous cordon-and-search operations and encounters targeting Al-Badr militants, resulting in the elimination of key operatives and a marked decline in the group's capabilities. Between 2010 and 2022, operations led to arrests of divisional commanders, such as Munir Ahmad Bukhari in Sopore in June 2012, and militants like Sheikh Sajjad in May 2010, disrupting local networks.[30] Sustained efforts, including intelligence-driven strikes, neutralized top figures, with examples including the killing of two Al-Badr militants in a joint Army-police operation in Mitrigam, Pulwama district, on April 28, 2022.[12] These actions, part of broader counterterrorism strategies post-Article 370 abrogation in 2019, have dismantled recruitment and logistical support, reducing Al-Badr's operational footprint in the Kashmir Valley.[3] The impacts extend to a systemic weakening of Al-Badr's structure, with fewer high-profile attacks attributed to the group after 2010, reflecting effective containment by forces focusing on foreign-backed remnants.[30] In 2021, security forces killed two Al-Badr militants in an encounter in Srinagar's Harwan area on May 17, recovering arms and linking them to prior terror incidents.[31] Overall terror incidents in Jammu and Kashmir have declined significantly due to zero-tolerance policies and ecosystem dismantling, with Al-Badr suffering from leadership losses and cross-border support interruptions.[32] Remnants of Al-Badr persist, primarily as low-level cells sustained by Pakistan-based handlers, posing intermittent threats through small-scale ambushes or infiltrations.[30] Post-2019 developments, including enhanced surveillance and community engagement, have further marginalized these elements, though vigilance continues amid reports of occasional activations.[33] The group's proscription since 2002 and ongoing designations have isolated it internationally, limiting resurgence potential.[3]
