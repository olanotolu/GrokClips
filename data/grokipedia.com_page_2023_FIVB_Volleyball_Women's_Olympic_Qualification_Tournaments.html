2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments

The 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments were a series of three intercontinental tournaments organized by the Fédération Internationale de Volleyball (FIVB) from 16 to 24 September 2023 in Ningbo, China (Pool A), Tokyo, Japan (Pool B), and Łódź, Poland (Pool C), involving 24 national teams divided into pools of eight each to determine six spots in the women's volleyball tournament at the 2024 Summer Olympics.[1][2] Each pool followed a single round-robin format, with the top two teams advancing based on match wins, set ratios, and point differentials, yielding qualifiers including the Dominican Republic and Serbia from Pool A, Türkiye and Brazil from Pool B, and the United States and Poland from Pool C.[3][4] Notable outcomes included the host nations China and Japan failing to advance despite their status as recent Volleyball Nations League finalists and Asian powerhouses, underscoring the competitive depth where underdogs like the Dominican Republic (world No. 10) secured berths through decisive victories over higher-ranked opponents.[3][5] These events highlighted empirical performance metrics over reputation, with Serbia clinching qualification via a strong 5-2 record in Pool A and Türkiye dominating Pool B undefeated at 7-0.[3][4]

Tournament Background

Qualification Framework

The 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments constituted the primary intercontinental phase of the qualification process for the women's volleyball tournament at the 2024 Summer Olympics, allocating six of the twelve total spots. France secured automatic qualification as the host nation. The tournaments featured 24 teams, comprising the highest-ranked eligible national teams per the FIVB Volleyball Senior World Rankings excluding France, divided evenly into three pools of eight teams each. These events occurred concurrently from September 16 to 24, 2023, in host locations of Xi'an, China; Okayama, Japan; and Łódź, Poland, with the host nations pre-assigned to their respective pools for seeding purposes.[6][7][1] Each tournament operated as an independent competition where the top two finishing teams earned direct Olympic berths, determined through a competitive format emphasizing match outcomes, set ratios, and point differentials in accordance with FIVB pool standing procedures. This structure ensured that qualification reflected on-court performance among a field of elite contenders, with no prior continental championships serving as gateways—the selection relied solely on global rankings to prioritize competitive depth. The design balanced representation across confederations while focusing on merit-based advancement, as the participating teams represented a cross-section of AVC, CEV, CSV, NORCECA, and other regions.[6][7] The five remaining Olympic spots were assigned post-tournaments via the updated FIVB World Rankings after the Volleyball Nations League 2024 preliminary phase, prioritizing teams from confederations lacking qualified representatives before filling with the next highest-ranked eligible nations. This hybrid approach—combining tournament results with rankings—aimed to secure broad geographic diversity alongside performance excellence, though rankings-based allocation inherently favored consistently high-achieving programs. No universality places were explicitly designated beyond this mechanism, distinguishing volleyball's pathway from sports with dedicated wild cards for underrepresented nations.[6][8][7]

Event Format and Objectives

The 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments comprised three independent pool tournaments, each involving eight national teams, held from September 16 to 24, 2023, in China (Pool A), Japan (Pool B), and Poland (Pool C).[9][10] These events followed a single round-robin format within each pool, with every team facing all seven opponents once over seven matchdays, typically featuring four matches per day to complete the schedule efficiently.[10] The primary objective was to determine the final six qualification spots for women's volleyball at the 2024 Summer Olympics in Paris, allocated to the top two finishing teams from each pool based on points earned in the round-robin standings.[9] The 24 teams were drawn from the highest-ranked nations per the FIVB Senior World Rankings (excluding those already qualified via continental events or host status), ensuring competition among elite programs while adhering to Olympic universality principles.[9] Standings were calculated using match wins, set ratios, and point differentials as per FIVB protocols, prioritizing competitive merit over regional quotas beyond initial entry.[10]

Organization and Preparation

Host Selection Process

The Fédération Internationale de Volleyball (FIVB) organized the 2023 women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments across three host countries, selected to accommodate the top 24 non-qualified teams per FIVB Senior World Rankings as of the Volleyball Nations League preliminary phase.[11] Japan was the first host confirmed on October 16, 2022, chosen for its established volleyball infrastructure, history of hosting major events, and strong national teams, as highlighted by FIVB President Dr. Ary S. Graça F°.[12] On March 8, 2023, the FIVB announced China and Poland as the remaining hosts for the women's events, emphasizing their status as significant volleyball markets with dedicated fanbases and proven organizational capabilities.[9][11] No formal public bidding process was detailed in official announcements; selections appear to have been determined internally by the FIVB and Volleyball World based on logistical readiness and promotional value for the sport.[9] The host nations were assigned specific tournament pools according to their FIVB rankings at the time: the highest-ranked host to Pool A, the middle to Pool B, and the lowest to Pool C, ensuring competitive balance while leveraging home advantage.[1] Specific venues—Ningbo in China, Tokyo in Japan, and Łódź in Poland—were later unveiled to support the events from September 16 to 24, 2023.[13]

Venues and Scheduling

The 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments consisted of three separate events, each hosting one pool of eight teams in distinct locations: Pool A in Ningbo, China; Pool B in Tokyo, Japan; and Pool C in Łódź, Poland.[1][13] These host cities were selected by the FIVB to facilitate regional participation and logistical efficiency, with China, Japan, and Poland serving as the organizing nations.[9] All three tournaments ran concurrently from 16 to 24 September 2023, allowing for simultaneous competition across continents and alignment with international broadcast schedules.[14][15] Within each venue, the schedule followed a standardized structure: a preliminary round-robin phase from 16 to 21 September, where each team played seven matches, followed by semifinals on 23 September for the top four teams and placement matches, culminating in finals and bronze medal matches on 24 September to determine the two qualifiers per pool.[5] Matches were typically scheduled in multiple sessions daily, starting in the morning or afternoon local time, to accommodate team recovery and crowd attendance.[16] Specific arenas included the Yoyogi National Stadium in Tokyo for Pool B, with a capacity exceeding 13,000 spectators, and the Atlas Arena in Łódź for Pool C, accommodating around 13,800 fans.[17][18] The Ningbo venue for Pool A featured a mid-sized indoor facility suitable for international volleyball, though exact capacity details emphasized efficient hosting over maximum attendance. This parallel scheduling minimized travel burdens for participating teams while ensuring the FIVB's qualification timeline for the 2024 Paris Olympics.[9]

Pool Draw and Composition

The pool draw for the tournaments occurred on March 17, 2023, at FIVB headquarters in Lausanne, Switzerland.[14] The host countries—China, Japan, and Poland—were pre-assigned to Pools A, B, and C, respectively, with assignments determined by their positions in the FIVB Senior World Rankings: China (ranked highest among hosts) to Pool A, Japan (middle) to Pool B, and Poland (lowest) to Pool C.[1] This ensured the strongest host was in the first pool, promoting competitive balance across venues.[14] The remaining 21 participating teams were divided into four seeding pots based on their FIVB rankings as of the draw date, excluding the hosts.[14] Teams from higher pots were distributed to avoid concentrating top seeds in a single pool, with one team per pot drawn into each pool sequentially.[19] Each pool consisted of eight teams, competing in a round-robin format at the designated host venues: Ningbo for Pool A, Tokyo for Pool B, and Łódź for Pool C.[1] The resulting pool compositions were:

Participating Teams

Entry Criteria and Seeding

The entry criteria for the 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments required participation from 24 national teams, selected as the highest-ranked eligible entrants according to the FIVB Senior World Rankings, excluding teams already qualified for the 2024 Summer Olympics via continental qualification tournaments and suspended nations such as Russia due to international sanctions.[20][21] Prior to the tournaments, five teams had secured Olympic berths: host nation France, Italy (via the European qualification event), the United States (via the NORCECA qualification), and Kenya (via the African qualification).[7] The three host countries—China for Pool A in Ningbo, Japan for Pool B in Tokyo, and Poland for Pool C in Łódź—were automatically included among the 24 participants, with the remaining 21 slots filled by the top-ranked non-host eligible teams to ensure competitive balance and global representation.[9] Seeding for the pool composition draw relied on the FIVB Women's Senior World Rankings effective 1 January 2023, which positioned teams into seven pots of three after assigning hosts to their respective pools.[1] This ranking snapshot accounted for recent international performances, including the 2022 World Championships and Volleyball Nations League results, to distribute strength evenly across the three pools of eight teams each. The draw ceremony, held on 17 March 2023, allocated one team from each pot to every pool, preventing concentrations of top-ranked squads and promoting fair qualification opportunities for the six available Olympic spots (top two finishers per tournament).[14]

National Team Rosters

The 24 participating national teams submitted rosters of 12 to 14 players each to the FIVB, as required by the competition regulations, one day before the start of their respective pools.[22] These rosters typically included a balance of setters, outside hitters, opposites, middle blockers, and liberos, selected based on recent performances in continental qualifiers, the 2023 Volleyball Nations League, and domestic leagues, with emphasis on players' fitness and international experience to handle the round-robin format demanding up to seven matches per team.[14] In Pool A (Ningbo, China), China's roster featured core attackers Li Yingying (outside hitter), Gong Xiangyu (outside hitter), and Yuan Xinyue (middle blocker), who had led the team to the VNL final earlier in 2023, supplemented by setters like Diao Linyu and liberos such as Wang Mengjie for defensive stability.[13] Serbia relied on setter Maja Ognjenović, opposite Tijana Bošković, and libero Sladjana Mirković, drawing from their world championship pedigree, with middle blockers like Mina Popović providing blocking height. The Dominican Republic included veterans like Brayelin Martínez (outside hitter) and Prisilla Rivera, emphasizing offensive firepower.[23] Netherlands' squad highlighted setter Laura Dijkema and outside hitter Celeste Plak, while Canada fielded outside hitter Alexa Gray as a scoring leader alongside middle blocker Molly Kiablik. Czechia and Ukraine focused on emerging talents like middle blocker Helena Havelková (Czechia) and outside hitter Olena Dulieva (Ukraine), with Mexico rounding out the pool via players such as outside hitter Samantha Bricio-betancourt.[24] Pool B (Tokyo, Japan) saw Japan's roster with setter Nanami Seki, outside hitters like Tamaki Matsui and Nichika Yamada, and middle blocker Ayaka Araki, prioritizing speed and defense under coach Manabe Jun. Brazil's lineup included opposite Tainara Santos and setters like Macris Carneiro, leveraging Olympic champions such as middle blocker Carol for net dominance.[25] Turkey featured setter Cansu Özbay and outside hitter Eda Erdem Dündar (also captain and middle blocker), building on their VNL success with versatile attackers. South Korea, Bulgaria, Puerto Rico, Argentina, and Thailand incorporated regional standouts, including Bulgaria's outside hitter Elitsa Vasileva and Thailand's setter Pudkate Faarooq Nootsara, to challenge the favorites in the high-scoring environment.[13] For Pool C (Łódź, Poland), Poland's roster was anchored by outside hitter Magdalena Stysiak and setter Joanna Wołosz, with the host nation adding depth via middle blockers like Agnieszka Korneluk. The United States deployed veterans including outside hitter Jordan Larson, setter Lauren Carlini, and libero Justine Wong-Orantes, blending experience from multiple Olympics with younger blockers.[26] Italy excluded star opposite Paola Egonu but included setter Monica De Gennaro and outside hitter Miriam Sylla for tactical flexibility.[27] Germany's squad emphasized middle blocker Pia König and setter Lena Stigrot, while Thailand, Colombia, South Korea, and Slovenia featured key figures like Thailand's outside hitter Chatchu-on Moksri and Slovenia's setter Bojana Žilnik for upset potential.[10] These compositions reflected strategic choices amid injuries and form, with teams adapting lineups during the tournament based on match demands and player availability.

Competition Rules

Match Regulations

Matches in the 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments were conducted according to the FIVB Official Volleyball Rules 2021-2024 edition, which governed all aspects of play including court dimensions, player positions, and fault determinations.[28] Each match followed a best-of-five sets format, requiring a team to secure three sets to win.[29] Sets one through four were contested to 25 points under rally scoring, where a point is awarded on every rally regardless of which team serves, with a team required to win by at least two points and no upper limit on points per set.[28] The deciding fifth set, if necessary, was played to 15 points with the same two-point margin rule.[29] Matches utilized the FIVB Video Challenge Review (VCR) system for disputing certain decisions, such as foot faults or ball touches, with teams allocated two 30-second timeouts per set plus one technical timeout when a set reaches 8-8 in sets one through four or 5-5 in the fifth set.[28] Teams could make up to six substitutions per set, with a roster limit of 12 players per team, and libero players designated for defensive specialization without offensive serving or attacking rights above the net.[28] All equipment, including balls and nets, had to be FIVB-homologated to ensure uniformity and safety across the three pools held in Ningbo (China), Tokyo (Japan), and Łódź (Poland).[29] No deviations from these standard regulations were applied specifically for the qualification tournaments, maintaining consistency with FIVB international competition protocols.[29]

Tie-Breaking and Standings Procedures

In the 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments, each of the three pools consisted of eight teams competing in a single round-robin format, with teams playing seven matches apiece to determine final standings. Rankings within each pool were primarily based on the total number of match points accumulated, with the top two teams per pool advancing to the Paris 2024 Olympics. Match points were awarded as follows: three points for a victory by 3–0 or 3–1 sets; two points for a 3–2 set victory; one point for a 2–3 set loss; and zero points for a 0–3 or 1–3 set loss or forfeiture (the latter scored as 25–0 sets across three sets).[22] Tie-breaking procedures were applied sequentially when two or more teams ended with identical total points. The first criterion was the set quotient, calculated as the ratio of sets won to sets lost across all matches; the team with the higher ratio ranked higher. If sets remained tied, the point quotient was used, dividing total points scored by total points conceded; again, the higher ratio prevailed.[22] For unresolved ties, the FIVB evaluated head-to-head results among the tied teams. In cases of two tied teams, the outcome of their direct match determined the ranking, with points from that match applied first, followed by set and point quotients if necessary. For three or more tied teams, the same ranking criteria (points, set quotient, point quotient) were reapplied exclusively to the matches played among those teams; if still inconclusive, the overall set and point quotients from all pool matches were reconsidered. This process ensured rankings reflected competitive performance without relying on arbitrary factors like alphabetical order.[22]

Results and Analysis

Pool A (Ningbo, China)

Pool A of the 2023 FIVB Women's Volleyball Olympic Qualification Tournament took place from September 16 to 24, 2023, at Ningbo Beilun Gymnasium in Ningbo, China. Eight national teams participated in a single round-robin format, where each team played seven matches; victories in straight sets or 3-1 awarded three points, 3-2 wins gave two points, and losses were scored accordingly, with tie-breakers based on set ratio, points ratio, and head-to-head results. The top two teams qualified for the women's volleyball tournament at the 2024 Summer Olympics in Paris.[5][1] The competing teams were the host China, Serbia (2022 world champions), Dominican Republic, Netherlands, Canada, Ukraine, Czechia, and Mexico. Dominican Republic surprised by topping the standings with six wins and one loss, qualifying despite a competitive field; their key upset was a 3-1 victory over Serbia on September 22, led by strong offensive play. Serbia recovered from the defeat, securing second place and Olympic qualification with five wins, including a decisive 3-0 win over the Netherlands on September 23, where Tijana Bošković contributed significantly.[5][30][31] Canada finished third with five wins but missed qualification due to inferior tie-breakers against Serbia, despite victories over Ukraine (3-1), Czechia (3-0), and Mexico (3-0). Host China placed fourth, hampered by losses including to Canada and the Netherlands, failing to leverage home support. The Netherlands ended fifth after a final-day loss to Dominican Republic, while Ukraine, Czechia, and Mexico occupied the lower positions, with Mexico winless.[32][5]

Pool B (Tokyo, Japan)

Pool B was contested from September 16 to 24, 2023, at Yoyogi National Gymnasium in Tokyo, Japan. The eight participating teams—Argentina, Belgium, Brazil, Bulgaria, Japan, Peru, Puerto Rico, and Turkey—competed in a single round-robin format, with each team playing seven matches. The top two teams qualified directly for the women's volleyball tournament at the 2024 Summer Olympics in Paris.[5][14] Turkey topped the standings with an undefeated 7–0 record, demonstrating superior blocking and attacking efficiency across all matches, including a 3–0 straight-sets victory over Brazil on September 22 (25–21, 29–27, 25–19). This flawless performance secured their Olympic berth and highlighted their status as reigning European champions entering the tournament.[34][35] Brazil finished second with a 6–1 record, qualifying after a hard-fought 3–2 win against host Japan on the final day (25–21, 22–25, 27–25, 15–25, 15–10), which proved decisive in the tiebreaker for the second spot based on set and point ratios. Earlier, they suffered their sole loss to Turkey but rebounded with consistent wins over lower-ranked teams.[36][37] Japan placed third with a 5–2 record, including four straight-sets victories early in the tournament, but losses to Brazil and Turkey prevented qualification despite home crowd support and strong defensive play. The remaining teams—Belgium (likely 3–4), Bulgaria, Argentina, Puerto Rico, and Peru—finished lower, with Peru and Puerto Rico struggling against the top seeds, as evidenced by Turkey's 3–0 opening win over Puerto Rico (25–19, 25–16, 25–14).[5][38]

Pool C (Łódź, Poland)

Pool C of the 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments was contested in Łódź, Poland, from September 16 to 24, 2023, at the Atlas Arena.[5] The eight participating teams were the host nation Poland, the United States, Italy, Germany, Thailand, Colombia, the Republic of Korea, and Slovenia, representing four continents.[10] The competition followed a single round-robin format, with each team playing the others once; points were awarded as three for a 3-0 or 3-1 win, two for a 3-2 win, one for a 2-3 loss, and zero otherwise, with tiebreakers based on set ratio, points ratio, and head-to-head results.[5] The top two teams qualified for the women's volleyball tournament at the 2024 Summer Olympics in Paris.[10] The United States topped the pool with a 6–1 record, securing qualification despite a loss to Poland on September 23 (3–1).[5] Poland finished second with the same record, also qualifying after defeating Italy 3–1 in the final match on September 24.[5] Italy placed third, missing qualification on points despite strong performances, including wins over Germany and Thailand.[3] Thailand and Germany tied for fourth but were eliminated, with Thailand's upset 3–2 victory over Poland on September 20 highlighting the pool's competitiveness.[5] Lower-ranked teams like the Republic of Korea suffered winless campaigns, conceding heavy defeats such as 0–3 to Italy on September 16.[5]

Olympic Qualification Outcomes

Qualified Teams

The 2023 FIVB Volleyball Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments consisted of three separate events, each featuring eight national teams divided into two preliminary groups of four, followed by knockout stages to determine final standings; the top two teams from each pool secured qualification for the women's volleyball tournament at the 2024 Summer Olympics in Paris.[1] In Pool A, held in Ningbo, China from September 16 to 24, the Dominican Republic and the Netherlands earned the qualification spots after advancing through the preliminary rounds and performing strongly in the placement matches, with the host China failing to advance despite early wins.[39][40] Pool B, hosted in Tokyo, Japan over the same dates, saw Brazil and Turkey qualify, topping the standings with identical 7 points from preliminary matches (interpreted as match wins or equivalent scoring), ahead of host Japan which placed third despite a competitive showing.[5] For Pool C in Łódź, Poland, the United States and host Poland claimed the berths on September 24, with the U.S. overcoming an initial set loss to Germany in a decisive 3-1 victory to secure second place, while Poland dominated key encounters to finish first.[41] The following table summarizes the qualified teams by pool:

Path to Paris 2024 Olympics

The top two teams from each of the three 2023 FIVB Women's Olympic Qualification Tournaments—Serbia and the Dominican Republic from Pool A, Brazil and Turkey from Pool B, and Poland and the United States from Pool C—secured direct berths to the women's volleyball competition at the 2024 Summer Olympics in Paris.[42] These qualification spots were allocated via the tournament format, where teams competed in a preliminary round-robin phase followed by placement matches, with advancement determined by win-loss records, set ratios, and point ratios in case of ties.[8] The qualified teams entered a 12-nation field that also included the host nation France and winners from continental qualification events: Italy (CEV Europe), Kenya (CAVB Africa), Japan (AVC Asia), alongside the continental representatives for NORCECA and CSV integrated within the overall allocation. The Olympic tournament, held from 27 July to 10 August 2024 at venues including the South Paris Arena, followed a standard FIVB Olympic format: three preliminary pools of four teams each, with round-robin matches determining standings.[7] [43] Advancement from the preliminary phase required finishing first or second in a pool, or ranking among the top two third-placed teams across pools, based on match points, sets, and points ratios. The six advancing teams then competed in single-elimination quarterfinals, with winners proceeding to semifinals and classification matches for medal contention. This structure emphasized consistent performance across multiple matches, rewarding teams with strong depth and adaptability in high-stakes, short-set contests (best of five sets, to 25 points with a two-point margin).[43]
