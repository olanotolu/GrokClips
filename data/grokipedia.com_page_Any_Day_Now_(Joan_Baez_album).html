Any Day Now (Joan Baez album)

Any Day Now: Songs of Bob Dylan is the ninth studio album by American folk singer Joan Baez, released as a double LP in December 1968 by Vanguard Records and consisting entirely of cover versions of compositions by Bob Dylan.[1][2] Recorded over four days in Nashville in October 1968 with a team of session musicians, the album interprets Dylan's songs—drawing from his early folk period, rock explorations, and then-unreleased Basement Tapes material—in a country-folk style that highlights Baez's clear, emotive vocals and acoustic arrangements.[3][4] The release peaked at number 30 on the Billboard 200 chart, achieved gold certification for sales exceeding 500,000 copies in the United States, and featured the radio single "Love Is Just a Four-Letter Word," one of the few Dylan songs Baez recorded that had not yet appeared on one of his own albums.[5] As Baez's first full-length tribute to Dylan, with whom she shared a personal and musical relationship dating back to the early 1960s, the album underscored her role in popularizing his work during a period when his output was limited following his 1966 motorcycle accident, though some critics noted its variable success in matching the originals' intensity due to Baez's more restrained delivery.[6][5]

Background and Recording

Conception and Context

Any Day Now emerged from Joan Baez's deep engagement with Bob Dylan's songbook, which dated back to the early 1960s when she first incorporated his compositions into her live performances, helping to elevate his profile within the folk music scene. By 1968, following the end of their romantic involvement around 1965 and amid a period of personal and professional distance—during which the two were not on speaking terms—Baez nonetheless proceeded with a dedicated double album of his material, reflecting her enduring artistic respect for his lyrical depth and melodic innovations.[7][5] This project represented a deliberate curation of 20 Dylan songs, many lesser-known or from his mid-1960s output, selected for their thematic resonance with Baez's own interests in social justice, introspection, and human struggle.[5] The album's context was shaped by Baez's career trajectory in the late 1960s, as she transitioned from acoustic folk purity toward more produced arrangements influenced by the Nashville sound, where Dylan himself had recorded albums like Blonde on Blonde (1966). Baez has reflected that listening to Dylan's music dissolved any lingering resentments, underscoring the conception as an act of reconciliation through interpretation rather than collaboration.[7] The decision to focus exclusively on Dylan covers also aligned with Baez's history of interpretive covers, but this release amplified the scale, compiling material she had honed in concert over years into a studio statement amid her activism against the Vietnam War and civil rights advocacy.[8] Recording commenced in Nashville on September 11, 1968, during intensive sessions that yielded the bulk of the album's tracks, enlisting skilled local musicians for a richer, country-inflected backing that contrasted Baez's earlier minimalist style.[9] This move to Music City studios was Baez's first, driven by a desire for professional polish and sonic expansion, resulting in a marathon effort that produced not only Any Day Now but material for subsequent releases.[5] The album's genesis thus encapsulated Baez's commitment to Dylan's influence as a songwriter, prioritizing substantive homage over contemporary personal dynamics.[7]

Recording Sessions

The recording sessions for Any Day Now took place in September 1968 at Columbia Studios in Nashville, Tennessee, marking Baez's initial collaboration with the city's renowned session musicians known as the Nashville A-Team.[10][5] These sessions were structured as a intensive marathon effort spanning six days and comprising fourteen separate recording dates, during which Baez and the ensemble captured sufficient material to fill the double album's sixteen Bob Dylan covers, with additional tracks earmarked for her subsequent release.[10] Produced by Maynard Solomon of Vanguard Records, with Jack Lothrop serving as associate producer, the sessions emphasized country-inflected arrangements that diverged from Baez's prior folk-oriented work, incorporating pedal steel guitar, fiddle, and mandolin to enhance the Dylanesque material.[11][12] Baez handled lead vocals and acoustic guitar, backed by A-Team players including Pete Drake on pedal steel guitar, Fred Carter Jr. on mandolin and guitar, Kenny Buttrey on drums, Roy Huskey Jr. on bass, Johnny Gimble and Tommy Jackson on fiddles, and Hargus Robbins on piano, among others whose precise contributions varied across tracks.[5] Stephen Stills was present at the studio during this period, though his direct involvement in the recordings remains unconfirmed beyond photographic documentation.[13] The Nashville setting facilitated Baez's exploration of richer, more textured productions, leveraging the A-Team's expertise in crafting polished yet organic sounds, which contrasted with her earlier, sparser folk recordings and aligned with the era's growing folk-rock and country crossover trends.[14] Engineer Selby Coffeen handled the technical aspects, ensuring the sessions yielded high-fidelity results suitable for Vanguard's stereo LP format.[12]

Musical Content and Production

Song Selection and Arrangements

Baez selected 16 Bob Dylan compositions for the album, prioritizing lesser-known tracks and recent works over previously covered hits, drawing from over 100 demos Dylan provided her following his 1966 motorcycle accident.[5] This included unrecorded songs like "Love Is Just a Four-Letter Word," selections from the Basement Tapes era such as "Tears of Rage," and four from Dylan's John Wesley Harding (1967), alongside earlier pre-1967 material like "North Country Blues" and "Walls of Red Wing."[4] Her choices emphasized Dylan's narrative depth and emotional range, avoiding redundancy with her prior Dylan interpretations on albums like Joan Baez/5 (1964).[1] The arrangements, crafted during October 1968 sessions at CBS Studios in Nashville, integrated Baez's vocals with country and folk elements through top session musicians, marking her shift from East Coast folk production to Nashville's polished sound.[15] Produced by Maynard Solomon, tracks featured pedal steel guitar by Pete Drake, fiddle from Johnny Gimble, mandolin by Fred Carter Jr., drums by Kenny Buttrey, and guitars from Jerry Reed, Jerry Kennedy, and Grady Martin, with occasional contributions like Stephen Stills on guitar.[5] Experimental touches included Danelectro electric sitar on "You Ain't Goin' Nowhere" for a psychedelic edge, dobro and fiddle on ballads for rustic texture, and an a cappella rendition of "Tears of Rage" to highlight vocal purity.[5] This approach embedded Baez within the ensemble rather than overlaying her voice on orchestral strings, yielding empathetic reinterpretations that extended Dylan's originals.[1]

Vocal and Instrumental Style

Baez employs her signature clear, high soprano voice throughout Any Day Now, characterized by precise articulation and controlled vibrato that allows for empathetic interpretations of Dylan's lyrics, often softening the original's raw edge into a more plaintive folk delivery.[5] [1] On tracks like "Love Minus Zero/No Limit," her crystalline tone navigates complex phrasing with emotional restraint, while a cappella renditions such as "Tears of Rage" highlight her vocal purity and dynamic range, culminating in extended, wavering notes that evoke deep melancholy.[5] [4] This approach contrasts her earlier acoustic folk recordings by integrating fuller production, yet her voice remains the focal point, extending Dylan's meanings through subtle modulations and gospel-inflected exuberance on uptempo songs like "I Shall Be Released."[1] [4] Instrumentally, the album draws on Nashville's session musicians—totaling 18 across tracks—for varied, supportive arrangements that blend country-folk elements without overpowering Baez's vocals.[4] Recorded in October 1968 at Columbia's Nashville studio, production features light percussion, acoustic guitars, and specialized instruments like the Danelectro electric sitar on "Love Minus Zero/No Limit," dobro and fiddle on "You Ain’t Goin’ Nowhere," and pedal steel with piano evoking a Texas dancehall vibe on "Walls of Red Wing."[1] [5] These elements create diverse textures—brooding organs and violins for ballads like "North Country Blues," delicate string touches over the 11-minute "Sad Eyed Lady of the Lowlands"—transforming Dylan's raw compositions into polished, radio-friendly pop-folk hybrids while preserving their introspective core.[5] [4]

Release and Commercial Performance

Initial Release

Any Day Now, Joan Baez's ninth studio album and first full-length collection of Bob Dylan covers, was initially released in December 1968 by Vanguard Records as a gatefold double vinyl LP in stereo format.[2][16] The U.S. edition carried the catalog numbers VSD-79306/7, pressed in stereo with a focus on Baez's interpretations backed by Nashville session musicians.[17] This release followed intensive recording sessions earlier that year and marked Vanguard's continued partnership with Baez, emphasizing folk interpretations amid the label's shift toward broader production styles. International variants, such as those in Canada and Europe, appeared concurrently or shortly after, often mirroring the U.S. packaging and numbering.[2]

Chart Positions and Sales

Any Day Now peaked at number 30 on the US Billboard 200 chart, where it maintained a presence for 28 weeks.[5][18] The album achieved gold certification from the Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA), denoting shipments of 500,000 units in the United States.[19] In the United Kingdom, it reached number 27 on the album charts.[20]

Certifications and Reissues

The album Any Day Now was certified gold by the Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA) on January 31, 1972, denoting shipments of 500,000 units in the United States.[21] No higher certifications, such as platinum, have been awarded by the RIAA, and sales figures beyond the gold threshold remain unverified in official records.[22] Vanguard Records reissued the album on compact disc in 2005 as a digitally remastered edition, preserving the original 16-track sequence while adding two bonus tracks: live recordings of "Blowin' in the Wind" and "It Ain't Me Babe" from Joan Baez's 1967 Japan tour, originally issued by King Records.[23] This edition emphasizes the Nashville-recorded country influences in Baez's interpretations of Bob Dylan compositions.[24] Additional vinyl and digital reissues have appeared through various labels, but none introduce substantive new content beyond the 2005 bonuses.[2]

Critical Reception and Analysis

Contemporary Reviews

The New York Times review by Susan Lydon, published on January 26, 1969, described Any Day Now as a two-record set of Bob Dylan songs recorded in Nashville with local studio musicians, incorporating elements like "twanging dobro, wailing fiddle, and rocking piano." Lydon critiqued the album as ultimately unsuccessful, likening it to knowing only William Blake's Songs of Innocence while ignoring Songs of Experience, arguing that Baez invested little personal interpretation, resulting in "pale renditions" that lacked the vocal inflection, expression, and meaning of Dylan's originals.[25] She found the Nashville production enhanced slower tracks such as "North Country Blues" and "Boots of Spanish Leather," but deemed the overall effort "almost unbelievably tedious" at its worst, with Baez's voice insufficiently versatile for Dylan's range.[25] The standout track, in her view, was "You Ain’t Goin’ Nowhere," praised as "a simple, sweetly sexy number."[25]

Retrospective Evaluations

Retrospective evaluations have affirmed Any Day Now as a pinnacle of Joan Baez's interpretive work on Bob Dylan's catalog, emphasizing her vocal maturity and the album's polished Nashville production. AllMusic's Richie Unterberger praised Baez's renditions as "stellar," highlighting her "empathy for the material," "keen understanding of Dylan's sound world," and the effective contributions of session musicians like Jerry Kennedy on guitar, which elevated tracks beyond mere replication.[1] This view positions the album as a sophisticated tribute, distinct from Baez's earlier, more acoustic Dylan covers, with the double-LP format allowing deeper exploration of 23 songs spanning Dylan's early to mid-1960s output.[26] In a 2022 assessment, Best Classic Bands described the record as a "loving tribute" to Dylan, crediting its gold certification and peak at number 30 on the Billboard Pop Albums chart to Baez's emotive delivery, particularly on singles like "Love Is Just a Four-Letter Word," which gained radio play despite the folk-oriented material.[5] Critics have noted how the 1968 recording sessions in Nashville—Baez's first there—infused the arrangements with country influences, such as pedal steel and subtle orchestration, fostering a warmer, more introspective tone that retrospective listeners appreciate for revealing nuances in Dylan's lyrics often overlooked in his original versions.[5] The album's legacy includes recognition in curated lists of Dylan cover collections; for instance, it ranked 14th in Cover Me's 2021 compilation of the twenty best Bob Dylan tribute albums, acknowledging Baez's foundational role in amplifying Dylan's songs during his relative obscurity in the mid-1960s, though some placements reflect its exhaustive scope as both a strength and potential limitation compared to more selective efforts.[27] Aggregate user ratings, such as Sputnikmusic's 3.6 out of 5 from limited votes, suggest a mixed but generally positive reevaluation, with appreciation growing for Baez's ability to humanize Dylan's complex narratives through her clear, unwavering soprano.[28] Overall, later analyses credit the project with solidifying Baez's status as Dylan's most empathetic interpreter, influencing subsequent folk cover artists by demonstrating how production choices could bridge protest anthems and personal ballads.[29]

Achievements and Criticisms

Any Day Now achieved commercial success, peaking at number 30 on the Billboard 200 chart in January 1969 and maintaining presence for 20 weeks.[5][21] The Recording Industry Association of America certified the album gold on January 31, 1972, denoting shipments of 500,000 units in the United States.[30][31] It earned a nomination for Best Folk Recording at the 12th Annual Grammy Awards in 1969, recognizing Baez's interpretations of Bob Dylan's compositions.[32][30] The album solidified Baez's reputation as Dylan's premier interpreter, with critics highlighting her empathetic delivery and vocal depth across 16 tracks, including standout renditions of "Sad Eyed Lady of the Lowlands" and "Love Is Just a Four-Letter Word."[1] Its Nashville production marked Baez's first venture into that scene, blending folk purity with country elements to enhance Dylan's material.[1] Retrospectively, it has been hailed as one of the most consistently excellent Dylan cover collections.[33] Criticisms of Any Day Now are minimal in contemporary and later analyses, which predominantly emphasize its interpretive strengths over flaws.[1] Some observers have noted the album's heavy reliance on Dylan's songbook as potentially limiting Baez's original creative output during a transitional phase in her career, though this view lacks substantiation in major reviews and aligns more with broader biographical commentary than specific artistic critique.[30] The Grammy nomination loss to Joni Mitchell's Clouds underscores competitive folk landscape pressures but does not reflect inherent weaknesses in the recording itself.[29]

Personnel and Credits

Musicians

The album Any Day Now was recorded primarily in Nashville studios during September and October 1968, featuring Joan Baez's vocals supported by a core group of skilled session musicians drawn from the city's renowned "Nashville A-Team," many of whom had recently contributed to Bob Dylan's John Wesley Harding.[34][3] This ensemble provided instrumentation emphasizing country and folk elements, including guitars, pedal steel, fiddle, and rhythm section, to complement Baez's interpretations of Dylan's compositions.[4] Key performers included:

Production Team

The production of Any Day Now, Joan Baez's ninth studio album released by Vanguard Records in December 1968, was led by Maynard Solomon as primary producer.[36][16] Solomon, co-founder of Vanguard alongside his brother Seymour, had previously overseen Baez's early folk recordings and guided her transition to more arranged material.[12] Jack Lothrop served as associate producer (also credited as co-producer in some editions), contributing to the album's oversight during its Nashville sessions in October 1968.[12][36] Recording engineer Selby Coffeen handled the technical aspects, capturing Baez's vocals and the ensemble's performances at Columbia Studios in Nashville over 14 sessions spanning six days.[12][10] This setup marked Baez's first major venture into Nashville's studio environment, emphasizing a polished country-folk sound for her interpretations of Bob Dylan compositions, though production choices drew mixed retrospective views on their restraint compared to her rawer Vanguard origins.[37] No additional production personnel, such as remixers or mastering engineers, are distinctly credited beyond these core roles in original liner notes and verified discographies.[38]

Track Listing

Original LP Sides

The original 1968 vinyl edition of Any Day Now was issued as a gatefold double LP by Vanguard Records, featuring 16 cover versions of songs written by Bob Dylan across four sides.[17] The sequencing emphasized a mix of Dylan's early folk compositions, mid-1960s electric-era tracks, and selections from his 1966 Blonde on Blonde album, with Baez's arrangements adapting them to acoustic folk styles.[17] Side one opened with shorter, upbeat numbers transitioning to introspective pieces:
  • "Love Minus Zero/No Limit" – 2:41
  • "North Country Blues" – 5:00
  • "You Ain't Goin' Nowhere" – 2:55
  • "Drifter's Escape" – 2:50
  • "I Pity the Poor Immigrant" – 3:45[17]
Side two contrasted brevity with an extended closer:
  • "Tears of Rage" – 4:20
  • "Sad-Eyed Lady of the Lowlands" – 11:18[17]
Side three grouped reflective and narrative-driven songs:
  • "Love Is Just a Four-Letter Word" – 4:25
  • "I Dreamed I Saw St. Augustine" – 3:14
  • "The Walls of Redwing" – 3:45
  • "Dear Landlord" – 2:57
  • "One Too Many Mornings" – 3:10[17]
Side four concluded with themes of release, regret, and farewell:
  • "I Shall Be Released" – 3:55
  • "Boots of Spanish Leather" – 4:29
  • "Walkin' Down the Line" – 3:20
  • "Restless Farewell" – 5:43[17]

Bonus Tracks in Reissues

The 2005 remastered CD reissue of Any Day Now by Vanguard Records appends two bonus tracks to the original 16-track double album, both drawn from live performances during Joan Baez's 1967 tour of Japan and recorded by King Records.[23] These additions, positioned as tracks 17 and 18, consist of Baez's interpretations of Bob Dylan's "Blowin' in the Wind" and "It Ain't Me Babe," capturing her acoustic folk style in a concert setting.[24][39] The tracks enhance the album's focus on Dylan covers by providing contemporaneous live renditions, predating the studio recordings on the 1968 release.[40] Some variant reissues, such as those from Ace Records, advertise three bonus tracks, though specifics beyond the standard two live Japan performances remain unverified in primary listings.[41] These bonus inclusions reflect efforts to augment the album's archival value without altering the core studio content, which features Baez accompanying herself on guitar and drawing from Dylan's Basement Tapes era material.[6]
