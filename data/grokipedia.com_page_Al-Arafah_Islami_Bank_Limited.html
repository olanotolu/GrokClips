Al-Arafah Islami Bank


Al-Arafah Islami Bank PLC is a Shariah-compliant commercial bank headquartered in Dhaka, Bangladesh, incorporated as a private limited company on 18 June 1995 and commencing operations on 27 September 1995 to deliver financial services based on Islamic principles derived from the Quran and Sunnah.[1][2]
The bank, fully owned by indigenous Bangladeshi shareholders, operates 215 branches nationwide, employs 5,768 staff, and provides deposit mobilization, investment financing, SME and corporate lending, automated teller machines, mobile banking, and online platforms tailored to retail and business clients while adhering to profit-and-loss sharing models.[2][1]
Founded by Islamic scholars and businessmen including A.Z.M. Shamsul Alam, with a vision to pioneer ethical banking and contribute to national economic growth, AIBL has maintained paid-up capital of Tk. 10,649.02 million and reported a capital to risk-weighted assets ratio of 12.13% as of December 2024.[1][3][2]
While achieving consistent operational expansion, the institution has encountered regulatory scrutiny, including Bangladesh Bank's detection of irregularities and suspicious transactions linked to former leadership in early 2025, alongside allegations of board-level mismanagement and employee dismissals prompting corruption claims.[4][5]

Overview

Founding and Corporate Profile

Al-Arafah Islami Bank PLC was registered as a private limited company on 18 June 1995 and commenced operations following its inaugural ceremony on 27 September 1995.[1] The bank was founded by A.Z.M. Shamsul Alam, an Islamic scholar, economist, writer, and former bureaucrat, with sponsorship from renowned Islamic scholars and pious businessmen in Bangladesh.[2] It operates strictly in accordance with Shariah principles derived from the Quran and Sunnah, positioning itself as a fully Islamic banking institution without interest-based transactions.[2] As a public limited company, Al-Arafah Islami Bank PLC maintains 100% indigenous ownership, with 19,146 shareholders recorded as of 31 December 2021.[1] Its authorized capital stands at 15,000 million Bangladeshi Taka, while the paid-up capital is 10,649.02 million Taka as of 31 December 2023.[2] The bank employs 5,768 staff members and operates 215 branches across Bangladesh, including 25 authorized dealer branches, from its headquarters at Al-Arafah Tower in Dhaka.[2]

Mission, Vision, and Sharia Principles

Al-Arafah Islami Bank's stated mission emphasizes pioneering Islamic banking in Bangladesh while contributing to national economic growth.[6] This aligns with broader objectives of achieving success in both worldly and afterlife pursuits by following divine guidance, as articulated in the bank's foundational principles.[7] The institution commits to Sharia-based practices, equitable service delivery, and socio-economic development, including support for underprivileged communities through welfare-oriented initiatives.[8] The bank's vision focuses on becoming the top-performing financial entity in Bangladesh by balancing expansion with stability, thereby fostering innovation in interest-free banking models. This vision is operationalized through strict adherence to profit-and-loss sharing mechanisms, such as Mudaraba for depositors, where 70% of investment income was allocated to such accounts in 2007 as an example of equitable distribution.[8] Sharia principles form the core of Al-Arafah Islami Bank's operations, prohibiting riba (usury or interest) and ensuring all transactions conform to Fiqhul Muamalat, the Islamic jurisprudence of commercial dealings.[9] The Shariah Supervisory Committee, comprising seven scholars, economists, and bankers, oversees policy formulation, product certification, and annual compliance audits, voiding any non-compliant contracts and segregating impure income for charitable redistribution.[9] Key instruments include musharaka (partnership), bai-murabaha (cost-plus financing), bai-muazzal (deferred payment sales), and hire-purchase under ijarah, all monitored to eliminate gharar (uncertainty) and promote ethical wealth circulation.[9] This framework extends to training programs for staff and regular inspections, ensuring alignment with Bangladesh Bank's regulatory guidelines on Islamic finance.[9]

Al-Arafah Islami Bank PLC was registered as a private limited company on 18 June 1995 under the Companies Act of Bangladesh, with sponsorship from renowned Islamic scholars and pious businessmen dedicated to establishing Shariah-compliant financial institutions.[6] The bank's inaugural ceremony occurred on 27 September 1995, formally launching its operations as a fully Shariah-based commercial bank owned entirely by indigenous shareholders.[1] This founding reflected a deliberate effort to address the demand for interest-free banking in Bangladesh, drawing on Islamic economic principles to differentiate from conventional riba-based systems prevalent at the time.[2] Early operations emphasized deposit mobilization and investment activities aligned with Quran and Sunnah guidelines, including Mudaraba-based savings accounts for profit-sharing and current deposits without interest.[1] Financing was extended primarily through Murabaha cost-plus sales, Musharaka partnership modes, and Ijara leasing, all vetted by the bank's Shariah supervisory council to ensure compliance and ethical integrity.[6] The institution positioned itself as a pioneer in Islami banking, targeting socio-economic contributions by serving clients averse to conventional banking due to religious prohibitions on usury.[2] From 1995 to 2000, the bank consolidated its foundational structure by prioritizing operational stability, risk management under Shariah constraints, and gradual network development to build customer trust in an emerging Islamic finance sector.[1] This phase laid the groundwork for sustainable growth, with initial focus on urban centers for retail and small business financing, though detailed branch expansion metrics from this era remain limited in public records.[6] The approach underscored causal linkages between Shariah adherence and long-term viability, avoiding speculative practices to foster genuine economic value creation.[2]

Products and Services

Core Islamic Banking Instruments

Al-Arafah Islami Bank PLC (AIBL) utilizes core Islamic banking instruments that conform to Sharia principles, focusing on asset-backed transactions, profit-and-loss sharing, and avoidance of riba (usury). These instruments form the foundation of its investment portfolio, enabling financing for trade, industry, and services through modes such as murabahah, mudaraba, and musharakah. As of its 2023 annual operations, AIBL's investments heavily rely on these mechanisms to ensure ethical compliance and risk distribution between the bank and clients.[29][30][31] Murabahah (Cost-Plus Sale): This deferred payment sale is AIBL's predominant financing tool for working capital and trade needs. The bank procures commodities or goods requested by the client from third-party suppliers, then resells them to the client at a disclosed cost plus a fixed profit margin, payable in installments. This structure mitigates interest by tying returns to actual trade assets, with the bank assuming ownership risk during procurement. AIBL applies murabahah for imports, inventory financing, and consumer goods, ensuring all transactions involve tangible, Sharia-permissible items.[29][32] Mudaraba (Profit-Sharing Partnership): In mudaraba contracts, AIBL supplies full capital for client-managed ventures, sharing profits according to a predefined ratio while absorbing losses unless due to client negligence or breach. This equity-like mode supports entrepreneurship in sectors like agriculture and small enterprises, aligning incentives through performance-based returns rather than guaranteed yields. AIBL offers term-based mudaraba accounts for deposits and investments, emphasizing skill-based management by clients to generate halal income.[30] Musharakah (Joint Partnership): AIBL engages in musharakah for collaborative projects where the bank and client co-invest capital, distributing profits per agreement and losses proportional to contributions. This partnership mode suits long-term financing for infrastructure or business expansions, promoting shared risk and governance. Unlike debt instruments, musharakah requires active involvement or oversight to prevent moral hazard, with AIBL structuring deals to reflect true equity participation.[31] Additional core variants include bai muajjal for deferred commodity sales akin to murabahah and hire purchase under shirkatul melk (joint ownership leasing), where AIBL co-owns assets like vehicles or equipment, transferring full ownership upon final payments via installments. These instruments collectively comprised AIBL's investment mechanisms as outlined in its operational framework, prioritizing Sharia supervisory validation for each contract.[33][34]

Al-Arafah Islami Bank PLC offers digital banking services through its i-Banking platform, which enables customers to perform transactions such as balance inquiries, account statements, fund transfers, credit card bill payments, and utility bill payments in a Sharia-compliant manner.[35][36] The platform is accessible via a dedicated website and mobile applications available on Android and iOS, supporting user management features including account summaries and mini-statements.[37][38] A key innovation is the Islamic Wallet, launched as Bangladesh's first Sharia-based digital financial service, providing mobile financial services (MFS) through a dedicated app for secure online and offline payments, remittances, and merchant transactions while adhering to rigorous Sharia principles and security standards.[39][40] This service targets financial inclusion for the unbanked by offering value-added features like cash-in/cash-out at agent points and QR code payments, without reliance on interest-based mechanisms.[41] In September 2025, the bank partnered with Trust And Pay (TAP) to enhance its digital ecosystem, focusing on innovative Sharia-compliant solutions such as advanced payment gateways and expanded mobile services to broaden access across Bangladesh.[42][43] This collaboration aims to integrate seamless digital tools while maintaining compliance with Islamic finance norms, reflecting the bank's efforts to modernize operations amid Bangladesh's growing digital payment landscape.[44]

Asset Management and Risk Profile

Al-Arafah Islami Bank's asset management emphasizes Sharia-compliant instruments, with primary assets allocated to profit-sharing and financing modes such as murabaha, musharaka, and ijarah, alongside treasury investments in government securities like Bangladesh Government Islamic Investment Bonds (BGIIB) and Sukuk for liquidity and statutory requirements.[50] The Asset-Liability Management (ALM) desk oversees balance sheet positioning, monitoring funding gaps, re-pricing mismatches, and economic conditions to determine deposit and investment profit rates, reporting to the Asset-Liability Committee (ALCO) for strategic adjustments.[50] As of December 31, 2024, total funded exposures reached BDT 476,389 million, reflecting growth in investment portfolios while maintaining compliance with Bangladesh Bank's guidelines on cash reserve requirements (CRR), statutory liquidity ratios (SLR), and Sharia principles.[51] The bank's risk profile is governed by a comprehensive framework aligned with Basel III standards, incorporating credit, market, operational, and liquidity risks, with dedicated mitigation through provisioning, collateral, and committee oversight. Credit risk dominates, with non-performing investments at 15.14% of total outstanding investments (closing balance BDT 74,055.36 million), addressed via specific provisions (e.g., 20% for substandard assets, 100% for loss assets) and risk-weighted assets calculated under the standardized approach.[51] Market risk capital requirements stood at BDT 174.94 million (solo basis), primarily from foreign exchange and equity exposures, while operational risk employs the Basic Indicator Approach, yielding a capital charge of BDT 2,696.48 million based on 15% of average gross income.[51] Liquidity management remains robust, supported by time-bucket gap analysis and ratio monitoring, achieving a Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) of 104.51% and Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) of 105.26% as of year-end 2024, exceeding regulatory thresholds.[51] Overall capital adequacy reflects a Capital to Risk-Weighted Assets Ratio (CRAR) of 12.13% (solo) and 12.44% (consolidated), with Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) at 6.64% (solo), surpassing the minimum 10% total capital requirement and indicating resilience amid elevated non-performing exposures.[51] The Risk Management Committee provides board-level supervision, ensuring integration of foreign exchange, asset-liability, and compliance risks into daily operations.[52]

Controversies and Criticisms

Sharia Compliance Disputes

Empirical assessments of Sharia compliance in Bangladesh's Islamic banking sector, including Al-Arafah Islami Bank Limited (AIBL), reveal systemic challenges such as inconsistent application of Sharia standards across products like murabaha and musharaka financing, often due to the absence of mandatory central Sharia guidelines and reliance on internal supervisory boards.[64] A 2014 study of selected banks, encompassing AIBL, found moderate compliance levels but highlighted risks from operational shortcuts that could inadvertently incorporate riba-like elements, such as fixed-profit margins mimicking interest.[65] These issues stem from dual regulatory-audit structures under Bangladesh Bank, which prioritize financial stability over rigorous Sharia vetting, potentially allowing minor violations in investment contracts.[64] AIBL's Sharia Supervisory Committee, comprising seven scholars, conducts internal reviews and classifies violations as major (e.g., direct riba exposure) or minor (e.g., documentation lapses), imposing sanctions on non-compliant staff to mitigate risks.[9] Despite these mechanisms, sector-wide critiques note that self-regulation by bank-specific boards lacks external standardization, leading to criticisms that some practices, including deferred payment sales, border on conventional interest-based models without sufficient profit-sharing transparency.[66] No peer-reviewed analyses or regulatory reports as of October 2025 document major fatwa breaches or legal disputes uniquely targeting AIBL's compliance, distinguishing it from broader industry scrutiny where up to 30% of Islamic window operations in conventional banks report violation cases.[67] Critics, including academic evaluations, argue that without a centralized Sharia boardâ€”proposed but unimplemented since 2010â€”banks like AIBL face heightened non-compliance risks from human error or profit pressures, eroding depositor trust in riba-free claims.[68] AIBL has not faced publicized challenges from ulama or courts over specific products, but general empirical findings indicate room for enhanced disclosure on compliance audits to address perceptions of leniency in enforcing gharar (uncertainty) prohibitions.[69] Bangladesh Bank's quarterly Islamic banking reports, covering AIBL's operations through March 2023, affirm overall adherence without flagging disputes, though they underscore the need for stronger internal controls against latent Sharia risks.

Financial Irregularities and Regulatory Scrutiny

In February 2025, the Bangladesh Bank uncovered serious irregularities at Al-Arafah Islami Bank, including the misuse of corporate social responsibility (CSR) funds and other financial misconducts during special audits prompted by broader banking sector probes.[4] These findings contributed to heightened regulatory oversight, as the central bank intensified examinations of private banks amid systemic concerns over loan scams and capital shortfalls.[70] Senior officials at the bank were implicated in embezzling Tk 109 crore through fraudulent agent commissions, achieved by misclassifying corporate deposits as retail to inflate payouts, as revealed in investigations reported in October 2025.[71] Separately, allegations surfaced that the bank's CEO and CFO had embezzled Tk 5.4 million, with claims that they returned the funds amid internal scrutiny, highlighting persistent mismanagement under prior leadership.[72] In response to such issues, Bangladesh Bank dissolved the bank's board of directors in March 2025, citing corruption and mismanagement linked to the former vice chairman affiliated with the S. Alam Group, and appointed a new board to restore governance.[73] Regulatory actions extended to personnel changes, with managing director Syed Farman Ahmed and three other executives placed on leave in April 2025 pending completion of audits into financial irregularities.[74] Additionally, 547 former employees accused the reconstituted board of ongoing corruption, nepotism, and mismanagement in August 2025, prompting further internal verifications that led to the regularization of 864 officers after addressing recruitment irregularities identified since 2021.[5][75] The bank faced inclusion in Bangladesh Bank's asset quality review program announced in June 2025, targeting 11 private lenders including Al-Arafah to assess true non-performing loan exposures and capital adequacy amid a sector-wide crisis where the institution slipped into capital deficit.[76][77] Earlier, in 2023, liquidity strains traced to irregularities forced reliance on costlier interbank funding from state-owned lenders, exacerbating scrutiny over lending practices and risk provisioning.[78] While specific monetary penalties for these core irregularities remain unreported, isolated finesâ€”such as Tk 1 lakh on treasury heads for foreign exchange rate violationsâ€”underscore episodic compliance lapses.[79]

Efficiency and Structural Challenges in Islamic Banking

Islamic banks, including Al-Arafah Islami Bank Limited (AIBL) in Bangladesh, often exhibit lower operational efficiency compared to conventional counterparts due to Sharia-compliant restrictions that prohibit interest-based transactions and emphasize asset-backed, risk-sharing models like Mudarabah and Musharakah.[80] These structures necessitate higher monitoring and verification costs for profit-sharing arrangements, leading to elevated overheads; empirical analyses using Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) on Bangladeshi Islamic banks, including AIBL, reveal average technical efficiency scores below 90% in dual banking systems, attributed to limited scalability in financing complex projects without debt-like instruments.[61][81] AIBL's cost-to-income ratio improved from 70.04% to 57.96% between 2023 and 2024, signaling better resource utilization amid deposit growth, yet this masks persistent inefficiencies from manual Sharia audits and fragmented liquidity management.[82] In Bangladesh's dual system, Islamic banks like AIBL face competitive disadvantages, as conventional banks leverage interest-rate arbitrage and derivatives for hedging, while Islamic alternatives rely on underdeveloped Sukuk markets and commodity Murabahah, resulting in liquidity shortages during stress periods.[83][84] Structural challenges exacerbate these issues: the absence of robust Islamic interbank markets in Bangladesh limits short-term funding, exposing banks like AIBL to displaced commercial risk where investment account holders bear losses not fully shared by shareholders.[85] Regulatory harmonization remains incomplete, with Bangladesh Bank's guidelines requiring parallel conventional and Islamic windows, increasing compliance burdens and potential fatwa inconsistencies across Sharia boards.[86] Studies indicate that without systemic reforms, such as dedicated Islamic liquidity tools, efficiency gaps persist, with AIBL's investment-to-deposit ratio hovering around 88-90% reflecting constrained asset deployment.[87][46]

Key Milestones and Recognitions

Al-Arafah Islami Bank PLC was incorporated as a private limited company on June 18, 1995, and commenced banking operations on September 27, 1995, with the slogan "Always Shariah" emphasizing adherence to Islamic principles derived from the Quran and Sunnah.[6] The bank's founding was spearheaded by a group of Islamic scholars and professionals, including founder chairman A.Z.M. Shamsul Alam, aiming to introduce modern Sharia-compliant banking in Bangladesh.[2] By December 2023, the bank had expanded to 215 branches and employed 5,768 staff, reflecting steady growth from its initial setup as one of Bangladesh's early full-fledged Islamic banks.[2] In September 2025, Al-Arafah Islami Bank marked its 30th founding anniversary, highlighting its endurance in the competitive Islamic banking sector amid economic challenges.[88] The bank has received several recognitions for financial performance and operational excellence. In 2021, it was awarded as the highest taxpayer for the 2020-2021 fiscal year by relevant authorities, with the certificate presented by Bangladesh Bank Governor Fazle Kabir.[89] Its subsidiary, Millennium Information Solution Ltd., earned a prestigious award from Global Finance for contributions to banking technology, underscoring advancements in digital infrastructure.[6] In remittance services, Al-Arafah Islami Bank secured the Remittance Award 2023 from the Centre for Non-Resident Bangladeshis and the Remittance Award 2024 from the Ministry of Expatriates' Welfare and Overseas Employment, acknowledging strong inbound fund mobilization.[90][91] On November 16, 2024, it achieved Mastercard Excellence Awards for Debit Business (Domestic) and Prepaid Business (International) for 2023-24, received by Managing Director Farman R. Chowdhury from Bangladesh Bank Governor Dr. Ahsan H. Mansur.[92] These accolades affirm the bank's proficiency in card-based and cross-border transactions within Sharia-compliant frameworks.

Economic Contributions and Market Position

Al-Arafah Islami Bank Limited (AIBL) maintains a mid-tier position among Bangladesh's ten full-fledged Islamic commercial banks, contributing to a sector that commands roughly 25.81% of national banking deposits and 29.20% of investments as of early 2025. With total assets valued at BDT 656.3 billion and customer deposits totaling BDT 473.8 billion, AIBL facilitates Sharia-compliant financing that aligns with risk-sharing principles, distinguishing it from conventional interest-based models prevalent in the broader market. Its gross investments stood at BDT 456.8 billion as of December 31, 2023, directed toward sectors including trade, manufacturing, and small-to-medium enterprises via instruments such as Murabaha and Musharakah, which emphasize asset-backed transactions over debt creation.[93][45][94] The bank's operations support economic expansion by mobilizing domestic savings into productive ventures, with its deposit base enabling an investment-to-deposit ratio that sustains liquidity for real economic activities rather than speculative lending. In remittance inflowsâ€”a critical driver of Bangladesh's foreign exchangeâ€”AIBL handled approximately 9.52% of the Islamic banking segment's volume as of mid-2016, underscoring its role in channeling expatriate funds into local development. By adhering to profit-and-loss sharing models, AIBL theoretically mitigates moral hazard risks associated with fixed-interest loans, potentially fostering more resilient financing for infrastructure and agriculture, though empirical outcomes depend on effective implementation and regulatory oversight.[95][96]
