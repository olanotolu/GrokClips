Apert syndrome

Apert syndrome, also known as acrocephalosyndactyly type 1,[1] is a form of acrocephalosyndactyly, a congenital disorder characterized by malformations of the skull, face, hands and feet. It is caused by autosomal dominant mutations in the FGFR2 gene, most commonly a C to G transversion at nucleotide 755 (about two-thirds of cases) resulting in a p.Ser252Trp substitution, or at 758 (p.Pro253Arg).[2] In 1906, Eugène Apert, a French physician, described nine people sharing similar attributes and characteristics.[3] Linguistically, in the term "acrocephalosyndactyly", acro is Greek for "peak", referring to the "peaked" head that is common in the syndrome; cephalo , also from Greek, is a combining form meaning "head"; syndactyly refers to webbing of fingers and toes. In embryology, the hands and feet have selective cells that die in a process called selective cell death, or apoptosis, causing separation of the digits. In the case of acrocephalosyndactyly, selective cell death does not occur and skin, and rarely bone, between the fingers and toes fuses. The cranial bones are affected as well, similar to Crouzon syndrome and Pfeiffer syndrome. Craniosynostosis occurs when the fetal skull and facial bones fuse too soon in utero , disrupting normal bone growth. In Apert syndrome, this typically involves premature fusion of both coronal sutures, leading to brachycephaly (a short, wide head) or turricephaly (a tower-like skull). Fusion of additional sutures, such as the metopic or lambdoid, occurs in about 10-20% of cases.[2] Findings for the incidence of the syndrome in the population have varied, with estimates ranging from 1 in 65,000 to 1 in 200,000 live births.[4][5] Recent reviews as of 2025 confirm a prevalence of approximately 1 in 80,000 to 160,000 live births, with higher rates reported among individuals of Asian descent.[6][7][8]

Signs and symptoms

Craniosynostosis

The cranial malformations are the most apparent effects of acrocephalosyndactyly. Craniosynostosis occurs, in which the cranial sutures close too soon, though the child's brain is still growing and expanding.[9] Brachycephaly is the common pattern of growth, where the coronal sutures close prematurely, preventing the skull from expanding frontward or backward and causing the brain to expand the skull to the sides and upwards. This results in another common characteristic, a high, prominent forehead with a flat back of the skull. Increased intracranial pressure from craniosynostosis or associated hydrocephalus can contribute to cognitive issues, including intellectual disability, which is typically mild or absent in most cases.[9] A flat or concave face may develop as a result of deficient growth in the mid-facial bones, leading to a condition known as pseudomandibular prognathism. Other features of acrocephalosyndactyly may include shallow bony orbits and broadly spaced eyes.[9] Low-set ears are also a typical characteristic of branchial arch syndromes.[9]

Syndactyly

All acrocephalosyndactyly syndromes show some level of limb anomalies, so it can be hard to tell them apart. However, the typical hand deformities in patients with Apert syndrome distinguish it from the other syndromes.[10] The hands in patients with Apert syndrome always show four common features:[11]
  1. a short thumb with radial deviation
  2. complex syndactyly of the index, long and ring finger
  3. symbrachyphalangism
  4. simple syndactyly of the fourth webspace
The deformity of the space between the index finger and the thumb may be variable. Based on this first webspace, three different types of hand deformation can be diffentiated:
  • Type I: Also called a "spade hand". The most common and least severe type of deformation. The thumb shows radial deviation and clinodactyly but is separated from the index finger. The index, long and ring finger are fused together in the distal interphalangeal joints and form a flat palm. During the embryonic stage, the fusion has no effect on the longitudinal growth of these fingers, so they have a normal length. In the fourth webspace, we always see a simple syndactyly, either complete or incomplete.
  • Type II: Also called a "spoon" or "mitten" hand. This is a more serious anomaly since the thumb is fused to the index finger by simple complete or incomplete syndactyly. Only the distal phalanx of the thumb is not joined in the osseous union with the index finger and has a separate nail. Because the fusion of the digits is at the level of the distal interphalangeal joints, a concave palm is formed. Most of the time, we see complete syndactyly of the fourth webspace.
  • Type III: Also called the "hoof" or "rosebud" hand. This is the most uncommon but also most severe form of hand deformity in Apert syndrome. There is a solid osseous or cartilaginous fusion of all digits with one long, conjoined nail. The thumb is turned inwards and it is often impossible to tell the fingers apart. Usually proper imaging of the hand is very difficult, due to overlap of bones, but physical examination alone is not enough to measure the severity of deformation.

The feet are also affected, typically showing syndactyly involving the second through fourth toes, often with a broad forefoot and short, broad toes. The great toe may be involved in more severe cases, and osseous fusions can occur, leading to rigidity and gait issues.[9][6]

Dental significance

Common relevant features of acrocephalosyndactyly are a high-arched palate, pseudomandibular prognathism (appearing as mandibular prognathism), a narrow palate and crowding of the teeth.[9]

Other signs

Omphalocele has been described in two patients with Apert syndrome by Herman T.E. et al. (USA, 2010) and by Ercoli G. et al. (Argentina, 2014). An omphalocele is a birth defect in which an intestine or other abdominal organs are outside of the body of an infant because of a hole in the bellybutton area. However, the association between omphalocele and Apert syndrome is not confirmed yet, so additional studies are necessary.[12][13] Other common signs include hydrocephalus, which may require shunting; conductive or sensorineural hearing loss due to recurrent ear infections or structural anomalies; vision problems such as strabismus, amblyopia, or exposure keratitis from proptosis; and obstructive sleep apnea from midface hypoplasia.[9][14]

Causes

Apert syndrome is an autosomal dominant disorder caused by mutations in the fibroblast growth factor receptor 2 (FGFR2) gene on chromosome 10.[15][2] Males and females are affected equally. Almost all cases are sporadic, resulting from de novo mutations. The offspring of a parent with Apert syndrome has a 50% chance of inheriting the condition. In 1995, A.O.M. Wilkie published a paper showing that Apert syndrome is caused by specific defects in the FGFR2 gene.[15][2] Approximately two-thirds of cases are due to a C to G mutation at position 755 (c.755C>G; p.Ser252Trp) in the FGFR2 gene, and about one-third are due to a C to G mutation at position 758 (c.758C>G; p.Pro253Arg).[2][16] These are male-specific mutation hotspots: in a study of 57 cases, the mutation always occurred on the paternally derived allele.[17] On the basis of the observed birth prevalence of the disease (1 in 70,000), the apparent rate of C to G mutations at this site is about .00005, which is 200- to 800-fold higher than the usual rate for mutations at CG dinucleotides. Moreover, the incidence rises sharply with the age of the father. Goriely et al. (2003) analyzed the allelic distribution of mutations in sperm samples from men of different ages and concluded that the simplest explanation for the data is that the C to G mutation gives the cell an advantage in the male germline.[16] The mechanisms by which FGFR2 mutations cause both craniosynostosis and syndactyly involve altered expression and function of FGFR2 isoforms and related receptors during development.[18] The main isoforms of FGFR2 are KGFR (keratinocyte growth factor receptor, or IIIb variant), which is active in the metaphysis and interphalangeal joints, and FGFR2-Bek (IIIc variant), which is active in the metaphysis and diaphysis as well as the interdigital mesenchyme. FGFR1, a related receptor, is expressed in the diaphysis. These point mutations increase the ligand-dependent activation of FGFR2 and its isoforms, leading to loss of specificity and binding of additional fibroblast growth factors (FGFs).[19] Since FGF signaling suppresses apoptosis, the interdigital mesenchyme persists, resulting in syndactyly. FGF also promotes replication and differentiation of osteoblasts, leading to early fusion of cranial sutures and craniosynostosis. This shared pathway explains the co-occurrence of both symptoms in Apert syndrome.

Diagnosis

Prenatal diagnosis may be suspected via 2D or 3D ultrasound or fetal MRI, identifying features like abnormal skull shape, midface hypoplasia, and syndactyly, potentially confirmed by genetic testing on amniotic fluid or chorionic villi.[9] Diagnosis is typically by the apparent physical characteristics and can be aided by skull X-ray or head CT examination. Molecular genetic testing can confirm the diagnosis.[20]

Treatments

Craniosynostosis

Surgery is needed to prevent the premature fusion of the coronal sutures from damaging brain development. The primary intervention is typically fronto-orbital advancement (FOA) or cranial vault remodeling, performed in infancy (around 6-12 months of age) to expand the skull, relieve intracranial pressure, and allow normal brain growth.[2] Later, to address midface hypoplasia, surgeries such as LeFort III or monobloc midface distraction osteogenesis, which detach the midface or the entire upper face, respectively, from the rest of the skull, are performed to reposition them forward into the correct plane. These procedures are usually done around school age or adolescence, depending on severity, and are carried out by plastic surgeons, neurosurgeons, and oral and maxillofacial surgeons, often in collaboration.[2]

Syndactyly

Although there is no universal standard treatment for syndactyly in Apert syndrome due to variability in severity, consensus guidelines exist for managing hand and foot malformations, emphasizing an individualized approach that balances functionality and aesthetics. Treatment is guided by the Upton classification of hand types and typically begins around 6 months of age for hands.[22][23] For hands, initial surgery often releases the first and fourth interdigital spaces to enable grasping, a key developmental milestone. Subsequent releases address the second and third spaces. Approaches vary by type:[24]
  • Type I hands (spade-like, with simple syndactyly of digits 2-4) usually require only interdigital web space releases; first web deepening and thumb clinodactyly correction may be needed.
  • Type II hands (mitten-like, with complex syndactyly) involve initial release of the first and fifth rays, followed by second and third web spaces; thumb clinodactyly correction and possible phalangeal lengthening are recommended. Recurrent syndactyly in the second web often requires revision due to pseudoepiphysis.
  • Type III hands (rosebud-like, with severe bony fusions) are most complex; initial release of the first and fourth web spaces aims to convert to a type I configuration. Macerations and infections are addressed early. Thumb lengthening increases the first web; in severe cases, index finger amputation may be considered after weighing functional benefits against psychological impacts. Later releases target remaining webs.
Secondary revisions address contractures and improve aesthetics as the child grows.[23] Foot syndactyly in Apert syndrome is generally milder, often involving toes 2-4, and does not always require intervention. When needed, surgical separation is performed, typically after hand procedures (around 1-3 years), to prevent pain, deformity, or functional issues; simple web releases suffice in most cases.[8]
