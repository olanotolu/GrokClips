Quarterly Publication of Individuals Who Have Chosen to Expatriate

The Quarterly Publication of Individuals Who Have Chosen to Expatriate is a mandated notice issued by the United States Internal Revenue Service (IRS) in the Federal Register, compiling the names and addresses of individuals who relinquished U.S. citizenship or terminated long-term lawful permanent residency in a manner subjecting them to the expatriation tax provisions under sections 877(a) or 877A of the Internal Revenue Code.[1][2] Enacted through section 6039G of the Internal Revenue Code as part of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996, the publication requires the Secretary of the Treasury to disclose such details quarterly for the preceding calendar quarter, focusing exclusively on "covered expatriates" rather than all who expatriate.[2][3] Inclusion criteria apply to those with an average annual net income tax liability exceeding an inflation-adjusted threshold (such as $190,000 for expatriations in 2024), a net worth of $2 million or more on the expatriation date, or failure to certify compliance with U.S. tax obligations for the prior five years via Form 8854.[4][5] These individuals face an exit tax under section 877A, treating unrealized gains on worldwide assets as realized and taxable, akin to a deemed sale, which underscores the publication's role in enforcing citizenship-based taxation amid global mobility.[4] Since its inception, the lists have recorded varying volumes, with annual totals of covered expatriates rising sharply after the 2010 Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA) imposed stringent foreign financial reporting requirements, contributing to compliance costs that prompted increased renunciations among overseas Americans; for instance, quarterly lists in the mid-2010s often exceeded 1,000 names, though recent figures have moderated to hundreds per quarter.[6][7] The mechanism has drawn scrutiny for potential privacy implications, as it publicly identifies high-net-worth or non-compliant individuals without exemptions for non-covered expatriates, whose numbersâ€”tracked separately by the State Departmentâ€”far outnumber published cases and often reflect burdens from worldwide income taxation rather than evasion.[8][9] Despite calls for repeal or anonymization, the statutory requirement persists, serving as a transparency tool tied to anti-deferral rules aimed at preventing tax avoidance through expatriation.[2]

Legal Basis and History

Enactment and Original Purpose

Section 6039G of the Internal Revenue Code, which mandates the quarterly publication of individuals who have relinquished United States citizenship or terminated long-term residency, was enacted on August 21, 1996, as part of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), Public Law 104-191.[10] This provision initially applied to expatriations occurring on or after February 6, 1995, requiring the Secretary of the Treasury to compile and release the list from information provided by the Secretary of State.[11] Originally designated as section 6039F upon inclusion in HIPAA, it was redesignated as 6039G through subsequent technical corrections to align with existing code structure.[12] The requirement built upon earlier expatriation tax measures introduced in 1966 via the Foreign Investors Tax Act, which added Section 877 to target individuals renouncing citizenship primarily to evade U.S. taxes, imposing a 10-year regime of taxation on certain U.S.-source income for "tax-motivated" expatriates defined by income and net worth thresholds.[13] HIPAA's 1996 reforms expanded these rules by replacing the prior alternative minimum tax approach with a mark-to-market deemed sale of worldwide assets for "covered expatriates"â€”those with average annual net income tax liability exceeding a specified amount or net worth over $2 millionâ€”who failed to certify U.S. tax compliance for the prior five years.[14] The publication mandate under Section 6039G complemented these anti-avoidance mechanisms by promoting transparency regarding expatriations, enabling public scrutiny of high-net-worth individuals perceived to be fleeing tax obligations.[15] The original intent focused narrowly on deterring tax avoidance among affluent expatriates rather than addressing broader immigration or citizenship policy issues, with the quarterly disclosures serving to raise awareness and potentially discourage renunciation as a tax strategy amid growing concerns over revenue losses from wealthy individuals shifting residences abroad.[16] In the late 1990s, following enactment, the lists reflected minimal expatriation activity, consistent with limited pre-globalization incentives for such actions beyond isolated tax-driven cases.[17]

Key Amendments and Evolution

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA), enacted on August 21, 1996, introduced Section 6039G to the Internal Revenue Code, requiring the Secretary of the Treasury to publish quarterly in the Federal Register the names of individuals relinquishing U.S. citizenship who were subject to taxation under Section 877(a) due to presumed tax avoidance motives. This amendment marked the initial formalization of the quarterly publication process, aimed at enhancing transparency and enforcement in expatriation-related tax compliance. The American Jobs Creation Act of 2004 repealed Sections 877 and 6039G effective for expatriations after June 3, 2004, eliminating the expatriation tax regime and thereby suspending the statutory basis for the quarterly publications during the subsequent period.[18] This repeal reflected a temporary shift away from punitive exit taxation, though it did not alter the underlying Immigration and Nationality Act provisions governing expatriation itself. The Heroes Earnings Assistance and Relief Tax Act of 2008 (HEART Act), signed into law on June 17, 2008, reinstated and expanded the framework by enacting Section 877A, which imposes a mark-to-market exit tax on "covered expatriates"â€”individuals with a net worth exceeding $2 million or an average annual net income tax liability surpassing $139,000 (as indexed for inflation in subsequent years) over the prior five taxable years, or those failing to certify tax compliance.[4] The HEART Act also restored Section 6039G, linking publication requirements to notifications under the new Section 877A and resuming quarterly Federal Register listings for affected expatriates. The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA), incorporated into the Hiring Incentives to Restore Employment Act of 2010 and effective for expatriations after March 18, 2010, integrated enhanced due diligence by mandating that all expatriates file Form 8854 to certify five years of prior U.S. tax compliance, with non-certification triggering covered expatriate status and the exit tax; this bolstered enforcement interconnections but left the core publication protocol under Section 6039G unchanged.[4] Post-2008 procedural practices have emphasized consistent quarterly releases in the Federal Register by the Internal Revenue Service, with listings compiled from State Department notifications and without recorded procedural pauses amid enforcement-focused refinements.[19]

Publication Process and Content

Criteria for Inclusion on the List

The Quarterly Publication of Individuals Who Have Chosen to Expatriate, mandated by Internal Revenue Code (IRC) Section 6039G, includes the names of U.S. citizens who formally relinquish their citizenship and long-term lawful permanent residents who terminate their residency status.[11] U.S. citizens qualify for inclusion upon approved renunciation of nationality, typically executed via an oath before a U.S. diplomatic or consular officer and confirmed by the Department of State, followed by filing Form 8854, Initial and Annual Expatriation Statement, with the IRS alongside their final U.S. tax return.[20] Long-term residents, defined under IRC Section 877A(g)(5) as lawful permanent residents (green card holders) who held that status for at least eight of the 15 taxable years ending with the year prior to termination, become eligible upon formal abandonment of residency, such as submitting Form I-407, Record of Abandonment of Lawful Permanent Resident Status, to U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) or Customs and Border Protection (CBP), and similarly filing Form 8854.[5][21] Inclusion requires certification of U.S. tax compliance for the five taxable years preceding expatriation via Part III of Form 8854, unless the individual qualifies as a covered expatriate subject to alternative rules under IRC Section 877A.[5] The IRS compiles names from these certifications and publishes only the individuals' namesâ€”without addresses, dates, or other personal detailsâ€”in the Federal Register within 30 days after each calendar quarter's close.[22] This applies to all such expatriates, regardless of whether they meet the criteria for "covered expatriate" status, which triggers the mark-to-market exit tax on worldwide assets under IRC Section 877A but does not limit list publication.[4] Covered expatriates, a subset of those on the list, are determined by specific thresholds: net worth of $2 million or more on the expatriation date; average annual net income tax liability exceeding $201,000 for the five years ending before expatriation (as adjusted for inflation under IRC Section 877A(g)(2)(B)); or failure to certify full compliance with U.S. tax obligations for those five years.[23] These thresholds ensure that higher-wealth or non-compliant expatriates face deemed sale taxation on unrealized gains, with an exclusion amount of $866,000 for 2024 (inflation-adjusted annually), but the publication itself derives solely from expatriation events confirmed via Form 8854 filings, not exit tax applicability.[24]

Exemptions, Exclusions, and Limitations

The quarterly publication under Internal Revenue Code Section 6039G is restricted to individuals who voluntarily relinquish U.S. citizenship or terminate status as long-term lawful permanent residents (defined as holding a green card for at least eight of the prior 15 tax years) in a manner triggering expatriation tax rules under Section 877A, and who comply with filing requirements via Form 8854 to certify U.S. tax obligations for the preceding five years.[4][11] This scope excludes losses of U.S. nationality lacking the requisite intent to expatriate, as stipulated in Immigration and Nationality Act Section 349(a), such as incidental effects from foreign nationality acquisition laws without an affirmative renunciation act before a U.S. consular officer.[25] Green card surrenders by non-long-term residents or those not electing expatriate treatment under Section 877A are not included, as they fall outside the definition of tax expatriation; formal abandonment via Form I-407 updates immigration records but triggers listing only if the individual qualifies as a long-term resident and files Form 8854 accordingly.[4][21] Dual nationals who first renounce their non-U.S. nationalityâ€”rendering them exclusive U.S. citizensâ€”prior to U.S. relinquishment are captured if the subsequent act meets voluntary expatriation criteria and tax reporting, whereas purely passive or unintentional nationality losses via foreign legal mechanisms remain exempt from both expatriation tax and publication.[4][25] A key limitation is the publication's dependence on Form 8854 filings, which undercounts total expatriations compared to U.S. Department of State records of formal citizenship renunciations (typically 5,000â€“6,000 annually in recent years), as non-filers or those with processing delays evade inclusion despite completing State Department procedures via Form DS-4080.[26][5] Section 6039G authorizes regulatory exemptions for classes of individuals where application would impose undue burden, though no broad exemptions for covered expatriates (those with average annual net income tax liability exceeding approximately $190,000, net worth of $2 million or more, or failed compliance certification) have been routinely invoked, preserving the list's focus on high-stakes tax events.[11][27]

Statistics and Trends

Historical Expatriation Numbers

Prior to the implementation of the quarterly publication requirement under Internal Revenue Code section 6039G, effective for expatriations after February 6, 1995, data on U.S. citizenship renunciations were limited and primarily tracked by the Department of State, showing negligible annual volumes often below 100 individuals in the early 1990s.[28] Following the start of Federal Register listings in 1997, expatriation numbers remained consistently low through the 1990s, typically under 200 per year, reflecting limited incentives for formal renunciation amid relatively stable global economic conditions and lower international mobility for U.S. citizens.[29] Into the early 2000s, annual figures continued at subdued levels, averaging 200 to 400 renunciations, as documented in State Department records, with the Federal Register publications capturing subsets of these cases focused on tax-relevant expatriates.[28] This period established a baseline of modest expatriation activity, gradually rising in tandem with broader trends in globalization and increased cross-border opportunities, though still far below later surges. By 2008, the total reached 743 individuals listed across quarterly Federal Register notices, marking a notable uptick from prior decades without corresponding major policy shifts in taxation.[30] These historical volumes, derived from aggregated government publications, underscore expatriation as a rare occurrence pre-2010, with annual counts rarely exceeding 500 and often aligning with demographic shifts toward greater expatriate communities rather than acute fiscal pressures.[31] Discrepancies between State Department totals and Federal Register listings arose due to the latter's focus on individuals subject to expatriation tax provisions under sections 877 or 877A, potentially underrepresenting non-covered cases.[6]

Post-FATCA Surge and Recent Data

The implementation of FATCA in 2010 correlated with a marked increase in expatriations, culminating in a peak of 5,815 individuals listed in the quarterly publications for 2016.[32] This represented the highest annual total recorded, with quarterly figures showing fluctuations, including peaks exceeding 1,200 in the fourth quarter of high-volume years.[32] Numbers subsequently moderated but stayed substantially above pre-2010 baselines, reflecting sustained elevated rates through the decade. Recent years indicate an upward trajectory, with 3,256 expatriations published in 2023 rising to 4,819 in 2024â€”a 48% increaseâ€”according to aggregations of Federal Register data.[33] Quarterly variations persisted, such as 635 in the fourth quarter of 2024, often driven by year-end processing.[33] In 2025, the first quarter alone recorded 1,285 listings, a 102% jump from the prior quarter, signaling ongoing momentum into the year.[34]

Drivers of Expatriation

U.S. Citizenship-Based Taxation and Compliance Costs

The United States employs a citizenship-based taxation (CBT) system, under which its citizens are subject to federal income tax on their worldwide income regardless of residence, a policy shared only with Eritrea among nations worldwide.[38][39] This extraterritorial approach contrasts with the residency-based taxation predominant in other countries, where tax liability typically ends upon relocation abroad, and imposes ongoing obligations even on individuals who have never resided in the U.S.[40][41] CBT creates risks of double taxation for U.S. citizens abroad, as foreign-earned income may be taxed by both the host country and the U.S., notwithstanding mitigation tools like the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE), which for tax year 2025 allows exclusion of up to $130,000 in qualifying foreign-earned income, or the Foreign Tax Credit.[42][43] The FEIE applies solely to earned income and requires meeting physical presence or bona fide residence tests, leaving unexcluded categories such as investment income, pensions, or capital gains fully exposed to U.S. taxation, potentially without full offset from foreign credits if rates differ.[44][45] Compliance with CBT entails substantial administrative burdens, including annual U.S. tax filings (Form 1040) and reporting of foreign financial assets via FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR) for aggregate account values exceeding $10,000 at any point in the year, irrespective of income level or tax liability.[46] Form 8938, requiring disclosure of specified foreign financial assets above thresholds starting at $200,000 for single filers living abroad, adds further complexity, with non-compliance penalties up to $10,000 per form plus additional fines for continued failure.[47] These requirements disproportionately affect lower-income expatriates, who face reporting obligations and potential penalties without corresponding tax due, often necessitating professional assistance that elevates effective costs.[48] Estimates for professional tax preparation services range from $530 to $1,600 annually for basic compliance, though more intricate cases involving multiple forms can exceed this, compounded by the time value of self-preparation efforts.[49][50] For those contemplating expatriation, Section 877A imposes an exit tax on "covered expatriates"â€”defined as individuals with an average annual net income tax liability exceeding $206,000 over the prior five years (2025 threshold), net worth over $2 million, or failure to certify five years of tax complianceâ€”treating worldwide assets as sold at fair market value the day before renunciation, thereby taxing unrealized gains as ordinary income.[51][52] This mark-to-market regime, enacted in 2008, functions as a deferred capital gains realization, with liabilities potentially reaching millions for high-net-worth individuals, though deferral elections are available with security.[53] Expatriation under CBT reflects a rational economic calculation where the cumulative burdens of perpetual worldwide taxation and compliance outweigh the perceived benefits of retaining citizenship, driven primarily by administrative costs rather than evaded tax liabilities, as evidenced by analyses of renunciation trends.[31] Individuals weigh factors such as restricted access to foreign banking, inheritance complications, and opportunity costs against U.S. passport privileges, often concluding that relinquishment minimizes long-term fiscal drag without implying disloyalty.[54][28]

Impact of FATCA and Global Banking Restrictions

The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA), enacted in 2010 and with reporting requirements taking effect in 2014, obligates foreign financial institutions to identify and report U.S. account holders to the Internal Revenue Service or face a 30% withholding tax on certain U.S.-source payments, imposing substantial compliance burdens on these institutions.[55] This has led foreign banks, particularly in the European Union, to classify U.S. citizens as high-risk clients due to elevated reporting costs, potential penalties, and regulatory scrutiny, resulting in widespread denial of new accounts, closures of existing ones, and restrictions on services for Americans abroad.[56][57] These banking barriers have created practical hardships for U.S. expatriates, who often rely on local financial systems for daily transactions, mortgages, and investments, with many reporting inability to open routine checking or savings accountsâ€”frequently those with balances under $10,000â€”prompting a reevaluation of maintaining U.S. citizenship.[58] Post-2014 implementation, expatriation renunciations surged, reaching record levels in 2015 with over 4,200 cases, a more than 25% increase from 2014's 3,415, as non-residents found ongoing compliance untenable amid severed access to global banking.[59] Recent surveys underscore the persistence of these issues, with 49% of U.S. expatriates in 2025 planning to or seriously considering renunciation, up from 30% in 2024â€”a 63% relative increaseâ€”explicitly linking the decision to banking exclusion and FATCA-driven compliance obstacles that isolate Americans from integrated foreign financial markets.[60][61] This exclusion not only disrupts personal finances but accelerates the trend of expatriates opting out of U.S. citizenship to regain normal banking access abroad.[62]

Broader Political and Economic Motivations

A significant portion of U.S. citizens opting for expatriation cite political disillusionment, including frustration with perceived overreach in government policies and a growing sense of alienation from the U.S. political landscape. Among those considering renunciation, 55% identified dissatisfaction with the direction of the U.S. government as a primary driver, reflecting broader concerns over regulatory burdens and policy trajectories that conflict with their lived experiences abroad.[63] This sentiment is compounded by 74% of expats reporting feelings of unfair representation by U.S. authorities, often tied to a perception that domestic policies fail to account for the realities of overseas life.[63] Survey data indicate a sharp uptick in such considerations, rising from 30% of expats in 2024 to 49% in 2025, with over half of this group attributing their stance to political factors amid heightened national polarization.[60] [64] Experts note that while tax compliance remains a trigger, these decisions increasingly symbolize a rejection of cultural and ideological shifts in the U.S., such as deepening divisions that erode national cohesion.[61] Economically, expatriates often favor host countries with residency-based taxation frameworks that align better with territorial economic integration, enabling seamless participation in local markets without extraterritorial obligations.[63] Among renunciation contemplators, 58% highlighted stronger economic and social ties to their adopted nations, prioritizing stability, lower living costs, and career opportunities unencumbered by U.S.-centric requirements.[63] This preference underscores a pragmatic shift toward jurisdictions perceived as more fiscally sustainable and less prone to the inflationary pressures or debt accumulation seen in U.S. fiscal policy debates. A notable demographic trend involves "accidental Americans"â€”those born abroad to U.S. parents who discover their citizenship status later in life, often through encounters with global banking restrictions or inheritance matters. Upon learning of attendant reporting duties, many view retention as incompatible with their de facto foreign identities, prompting renunciation to affirm long-standing expatriate realities.[29] [65] Surveys reveal limited awareness of mitigative options like the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion among this group, with 45% unfamiliar even with basic streamlined filing processes, exacerbating perceptions of an overly intrusive system.[63]

Controversies and Criticisms

Privacy Concerns and Public Disclosure Effects

The IRS's quarterly publication, mandated by Internal Revenue Code Section 6039G, discloses the full names of covered expatriatesâ€”individuals whose expatriation triggers tax reporting due to meeting net worth, income, or compliance thresholdsâ€”without providing an opt-out mechanism for privacy protection.[11] This public listing in the Federal Register exposes personal identities to indefinite online accessibility, potentially inviting stigma, professional repercussions, or targeted harassment from critics who view expatriation as unpatriotic or financially motivated.[6] Critics, including Swiss lawyer Filippo Noseda, have condemned the practice as a "name and shame" tactic that functions as government-sanctioned doxxing, arguing it unfairly penalizes individuals rationally severing ties to a citizenship-based taxation system imposing global compliance burdens.[66] Unlike the U.S. State Department's annual anonymized tallies of nationality losses, which aggregate expatriations without identifying details to safeguard privacy, the IRS list offers no such anonymity, amplifying risks of media sensationalism that frames renunciations as "tax flight" by the wealthy rather than responses to regulatory costs. Historical coverage has spotlighted high-profile cases, such as celebrities or executives, leading to public scrutiny and assumptions of evasion motives, even when expatriations stem from dual citizenship conflicts or banking access issues under laws like FATCA.[67] Proponents of reform advocate modernizing Section 6039G to anonymize listings or limit disclosures, aligning with data privacy norms that balance governmental transparency with individual rights against unwarranted exposure.[68] The publication's stated intent is to promote deterrence against perceived tax avoidance via expatriation and foster public awareness of such trends, yet detractors contend this rationale inadequately justifies the privacy intrusion, as the measure correlates with neither reduced expatriations nor proven causal impact on behavior.[68] Empirical persistence of expatriations despite the list underscores its limited efficacy as a disincentive, while prioritizing collective policy signaling over personal autonomy in a context where U.S. citizenship uniquely entails worldwide tax obligations absent in peer nations.[69] This tension highlights broader debates on whether mandated public shaming serves truth-seeking governance or merely enforces compliance through social pressure.

Inaccuracies in Data Representation

The U.S. Treasury's Quarterly Publication lists only "covered expatriates" as defined under Internal Revenue Code section 877A, encompassing individuals whose net worth exceeds $2 million, whose average annual net income tax liability surpasses $201,000 for 2024, or who fail to certify five years of tax compliance on Form 8854.[4] This criterion excludes the vast majority of expatriations, understating the overall scale; for example, an examination of 35,790 expatriations from 2005 to 2018 revealed that just 5.4% involved net worth above $2 million, with over 17,000 (47.6%) reporting under $50,000.[70] Consequently, the published figures capture neither non-covered citizen renunciations nor green card relinquishments below thresholds, leading to incomplete representation of expatriation volumes compared to total consular-processed losses of nationality. The list further compounds inaccuracies by including both U.S. citizens and long-term lawful permanent residents (those holding green cards for at least eight of the prior 15 years) who meet covered status, without distinguishing between them in aggregate counts.[6] This blending inflates perceptions of citizen-specific renunciations when data are misinterpreted as such, diverging from U.S. State Department records of Certificates of Loss of Nationality, which tally only formal citizenship relinquishments regardless of wealthâ€”resulting in historical mismatches across agencies due to differing scopes and processing timelines.[67] For 2024, Treasury quarterly tallies showed volatility (e.g., 345 in Q1 rising to 2,123 in Q3), but underreporting persists for dual nationals or pre-Form 8854 compliant expatriates not triggering covered status, as well as delays in IRS-State synchronization.[71] Media narratives often depict listed expatriates as affluent tax evaders, yet empirical data indicate the median expatriate's wealth and tax profile fall below covered benchmarks, with many driven by compliance costs rather than high-stakes avoidanceâ€” a portrayal attributable to selective focus on visible high-profile cases amid institutional tendencies toward alarmist framing of fiscal mobility.[70] This overlooks non-listed cases, including lower-asset dual citizens expatriating via treaty positions or abandonment without formal renunciation, distorting public understanding of expatriation's demographic breadth.[5]

Debates Over Underlying Tax Policies

Critics of citizenship-based taxation (CBT) argue that it represents an outdated policy ill-suited to modern global mobility, as only the United States and Eritrea impose income taxes on citizens based solely on nationality rather than residence.[72][41] This rarity underscores potential inefficiencies, with opponents citing competitive disadvantages such as restricted banking access abroad and incentives for high-skilled individuals to relinquish U.S. citizenship, contributing to a form of brain drain.[43] Empirical evidence links these expatriations more to burdensome compliance requirements than to deliberate tax evasion, as annual filing obligations under CBTâ€”enforced regardless of tax liabilityâ€”impose costs often exceeding several thousand dollars per return, even for those owing no net tax after credits like the foreign earned income exclusion.[73][74] Proponents defend CBT as a mechanism to capture revenue from citizens' global assets, aligning tax obligations with the enduring benefits of U.S. citizenship, such as passport privileges and potential consular protections. However, data reveals its fiscal yield from non-residents is negligible relative to overall IRS collectionsâ€”estimated at under 0.01% when accounting for administrative overheadâ€”suggesting that enforcement costs, including complex reporting forms like FBAR and Form 8938, outweigh direct revenue gains.[75] This disparity challenges left-leaning characterizations of expatriators as "unpatriotic" for prioritizing fiscal allegiance elsewhere, as causal analysis prioritizes verifiable drivers like compliance friction over normative judgments of loyalty.[76] Reform advocates, often from right-leaning perspectives emphasizing limited government, highlight CBT's overreach in extraterritorial enforcement, which validates expatriation as a rational response to perceived intrusions on personal sovereignty and economic liberty.[77] Legislative efforts reflect this, including 2025 proposals like the Tax Simplification for Americans Abroad Act, which seeks streamlined filing for overseas residents, and bills enabling an election to residency-based taxation (RBT) for those living abroad, potentially exempting non-U.S.-sourced income from CBT while preserving territorial principles for domestic earners.[78][77] Such measures aim to mitigate distortions without abandoning revenue bases tied to physical presence, though defenders caution that shifting to pure RBT could exacerbate double non-taxation risks for mobile assets.[75]

Comparisons and Broader Context

Differences from Other Expatriation Data Sources

The U.S. Department of State's Certificates of Loss of Nationality (CLN) data record all formal instances of U.S. nationality relinquishment processed through diplomatic channels, resulting in consistently higher annual totals than the Treasury's quarterly publication. For instance, during peak expatriation periods in the mid-2010s, State Department CLN issuances exceeded 6,500 cases annually, compared to approximately 4,000 entries on the Treasury lists.[79] This methodological divergence stems from the CLN's comprehensive capture of voluntary expatriations irrespective of tax reporting obligations, whereas the quarterly list draws exclusively from IRS-identified cases under sections 877(a) and 877A of the Internal Revenue Code.[6] IRS aggregates derived from Form 8854 filings encompass a wider array of expatriation certifications, as the form mandates reporting from all individuals expatriating after June 4, 2004, to affirm prior tax compliance and calculate any exit tax liability.[20] Unlike the public quarterly publication, which discloses only a subset of names based on Treasury data availability, Form 8854 aggregates remain internal and non-public, introducing potential variances due to asynchronous filing timelines relative to citizenship loss documentation.[5] Private trackers, such as selective compilations in Forbes reporting, prioritize high-profile renunciationsâ€”often celebrities or executivesâ€”from the official Federal Register notices, systematically undercounting non-notable cases that constitute the majority of expatriations.[80] These informal lists employ ad hoc methodologies focused on media relevance rather than exhaustive enumeration, lacking the procedural rigor and legal mandates of government datasets.[80]

International Perspectives on Taxation Models

The overwhelming majority of the world's approximately 195 sovereign states, exceeding 190 countries, employ residency-based taxation (RBT), under which individuals are taxed on worldwide income only if they reside in the country or meet specific residency criteria, rather than citizenship-based taxation (CBT) that imposes obligations irrespective of location.[41][40] This global norm aligns tax liability with physical presence and economic activity within the jurisdiction, facilitating international mobility and reducing administrative burdens on non-residents. In contrast, CBT, as practiced by the United States and Eritrea, extends taxing rights to citizens abroad on global income, a model scholars describe as anomalous in modern international tax frameworks due to its potential to hinder cross-border labor flows and investment by decoupling tax duties from territorial ties.[81][82] Eritrea maintains the only other active CBT system, levying a 2% diaspora tax on the foreign-earned income of its non-resident citizens to fund national reconstruction, a policy initiated in the early 1990s amid post-independence fiscal pressures.[41][83] However, this regime has drawn international condemnation for coercive collection methods, including threats of denied consular services or family harassment, prompting a 2016 United Nations report to recommend its suspension as a violation of human rights conventions, though enforcement persists amid broader U.S. and EU sanctions on Eritrea for unrelated geopolitical issues.[84][85] Unlike the U.S. model, Eritrea's tax is flat and ad hoc, applying selectively rather than comprehensively, yet it exemplifies the practical challenges of extraterritorial enforcement, including evasion and diplomatic backlash.[82] Historically, several nations have experimented with CBT but abandoned it in favor of RBT due to enforcement difficulties, low compliance yields, and domestic opposition. For instance, Mexico imposed citizenship-based levies in the mid-20th century but reverted after finding non-resident tracking infeasible; similarly, Romania, Bulgaria, Vietnam, the Philippines, and Myanmar tested variants during periods of fiscal strain or emigration controls, only to discontinue them as global integration favored territorial systems.[72][86] These short-lived efforts, often lasting less than a decade, underscore CBT's rarity and unsustainability outside unique contexts like wartime revenue needs, as seen in the U.S. adoption during the Civil War in 1861â€“1864, which endured due to entrenched legal precedents rather than comparative efficacy. Empirical observations from tax policy analyses indicate that RBT jurisdictions experience lower incentives for formal expatriation tied to tax avoidance, as residency shifts suffice to alter liabilities without severing national ties, correlating with reduced citizenship renunciations in peer economies.[75][87]

Economic and Policy Implications

Effects on U.S. Revenue and Competitiveness

The exit tax imposed under Internal Revenue Code Section 877A primarily affects covered expatriatesâ€”those with average annual net income tax liability exceeding $206,000 over the prior five years, Aggregate annual revenue from these taxes remains limited, as only a fraction of the roughly 5,000â€“6,000 annual renunciations involve covered expatriates with substantial taxable gains, yielding treatment that hampers the nation's appeal as a hub for international talent and innovation.[91]

Potential Reforms and Alternatives

One proposed reform involves transitioning from citizenship-based taxation to an elective residency-based system, under which U.S. citizens living abroad could opt to be taxed solely on U.S.-sourced income, mirroring practices in most other developed nations. The Residence-Based Taxation for Americans Abroad Act (H.R. 10468), introduced by Representative Darin LaHood on December 18, 2024, would enable qualifying citizens abroad to elect non-resident status for federal income tax purposes, thereby excluding foreign-earned income from U.S. taxation while preserving reporting on domestic sources.[39][93] This approach has garnered support from former IRS commissioners, who argue it aligns U.S. policy with international norms and reduces compliance disincentives for overseas Americans without forgoing revenue from domestic activities.[94] Alternatives to full residency-based shifts include enhancing compliance mechanisms, such as expanding the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) and providing targeted exemptions under the Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA). The FEIE amount increased to $130,000 for tax year 2025, allowing more expats to exclude foreign-earned wages from U.S. taxation, though it applies only to earned income and requires meeting residency or physical presence tests.[95] Proposals in 2025 tax legislation, including elements of the "One Big Beautiful Bill," sought to streamline FATCA reporting thresholds and reduce burdens for low-asset expats by exempting smaller foreign accounts from mandatory disclosures, aiming to mitigate the estimated $20,000+ annual compliance costs cited by affected individuals.[96][97] Broader tax system overhauls, like variants of the FairTax Act (H.R. 25, reintroduced in 2025), propose replacing income taxation with a national sales tax, effectively territorializing individual taxation by focusing on consumption rather than worldwide income, which could eliminate expatriation incentives tied to global reporting.[98] These reforms draw empirical support from rising U.S. expatriation filings, which reached 1,285 in the first quarter of 2025â€”a 102% increase from the prior quarterâ€”and surveys indicating 49% of expats considered renunciation in 2025 due to tax complexities, compared to 30% in 2024.[99][60] In contrast, countries employing residency-based systems, such as Canada and Australia, report negligible tax-driven citizenship renunciations, suggesting U.S. policy stasis sustains outflows amid static reforms since 2010.[77]
