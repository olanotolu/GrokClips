Elizabethtown, Pennsylvania

Elizabethtown is a borough in Lancaster County, Pennsylvania, United States, situated in the southeastern part of the state within the Pennsylvania Dutch region.[1] As of the 2020 United States Census, its population was 11,639 residents.[1] The community functions primarily as a suburban residential area with a mix of light industry, education, and agriculture, benefiting from proximity to larger urban centers like Harrisburg and Lancaster.[2] Founded in the mid-18th century by settler Barnabas Hughes, who named the town after his wife Elizabeth and began selling lots around 1763, Elizabethtown was officially incorporated as a borough on April 13, 1827.[3][4] Early development was driven by Scots-Irish and Pennsylvania German settlers, with economic roots in milling, iron production, and farming.[4] A defining feature is Elizabethtown College, a private liberal arts college chartered in 1899 by members of the Church of the Brethren to provide higher education in the region, which has shaped the borough's cultural and educational landscape.[5] The town also features an Amtrak station serving Keystone Corridor trains, facilitating commuter access to Philadelphia and Harrisburg.

History

Founding and Early Settlement

The area that became Elizabethtown was first settled by European immigrants in the early 18th century, following Native American habitation and initial European exploration in Lancaster County. Captain Thomas Harris, a Scots-Irish settler, established the earliest known permanent European presence in 1730 by acquiring land and setting up a trading post along Conoy Creek, which served as a key stop between Lancaster and Harrisburg.[6] [7] By around 1745, Harris had constructed the Sign of the Bear Tavern on the site, including associated distillery, barn, and tannery buildings, which functioned as the first major commercial hub in the region and facilitated trade along emerging routes.[8] In 1753, Barnabas Hughes, an immigrant from County Donegal, Ireland, who had arrived in Pennsylvania around 1750, purchased the Bear Tavern and surrounding land from previous owners, marking the formal founding of the town.[6] [9] Hughes laid out the settlement with a central square in a "diamond" pattern, naming streets such as Market Street, High Street, Rose Alley, Cherry Alley, and Peach Alleyâ€”many of which retain their original designationsâ€”and christened the community Elizabethtown in honor of his wife, Elizabeth.[10] [11] This grid design reflected practical considerations for trade and agriculture, positioning the town as a waypoint on the Lancaster-Harrisburg Turnpike.[6] Early inhabitants comprised a mix of Scots-Irish like Harris and Hughes, alongside Pennsylvania Dutch (German-speaking) families such as the Brennemans, Nissleys, and Grubbs, who acquired nearby lands previously held by French proprietors including Chartier, Letort, and Bezaillion.[7] [12] The settlement remained primarily agricultural, with the tavern evolving into a vital center for commerce, lodging, and social exchange, though its growth was initially modest due to the frontier's sparse population and reliance on overland trade paths.[6] [3]

19th-Century Expansion

Elizabethtown's expansion accelerated in the early 19th century, driven by its strategic position along key trade routes connecting Philadelphia, Baltimore, Harrisburg, and Carlisle. The town's incorporation as a borough on April 13, 1827, formalized its growth from a trading post centered around the Bear Tavern, established along the Lancaster-Harrisburg Turnpike, into a structured municipality with a central square and diamond-patterned street layout including Market, High, Rose, Cherry, and Peach alleys.[6] This development supported burgeoning commerce, as turnpikes like the Lancaster-Middletown (opened 1805) and Philadelphia-Pittsburgh (1811) funneled traffic through the area, fostering economic activity beyond its agricultural roots.[12] Infrastructure improvements further propelled expansion, particularly in transportation and water-based trade. Elizabethtown emerged as a leader in canal boat construction during the mid-19th century, leveraging proximity to the Susquehanna River, Conoy Creek, and Conewago Creek to build vessels for regional canal systems, including the Susquehanna and Tidewater Canal.[13] Local mills, such as the Grubb Mill established in 1871, processed grain for markets as far as Baltimore, while early iron production at Mount Vernon Furnace (founded 1801) and blacksmithing operations diversified the economy. Wagon manufacturing, exemplified by the Anchor Spring Wagon Works in 1896, capitalized on the demand for durable transport vehicles suited to Pennsylvania's roads.[12] The arrival of railroads in the 1830s integrated Elizabethtown into broader networks, with the town becoming a stop on the Pennsylvania Railroad main line by the late 19th century, enhancing freight and passenger movement.[12] Agricultural processing expanded alongside tobacco cultivation, with warehouses and cigar factories proliferating; by 1859, Lancaster Countyâ€”dominated by Elizabethtown's environsâ€”accounted for two-thirds of Pennsylvania's tobacco yield. Financial institutions like the Farmer's Bank (1869) and Elizabethtown National Bank (1885) supported this diversification, though some faced instability. Population grew steadily, reflecting these opportunities, with the borough's residents increasing from approximately 700 in 1860 to 1,473 by 1900, indicative of modest but consistent urbanizing pressures.[12] Subdivisions created hamlets such as Wilsonburgh (1811) and Irishtown (1813), extending settlement patterns outward from the core.

20th-Century Industrialization and Growth

The establishment of small-scale manufacturing in Elizabethtown at the turn of the 20th century marked the onset of industrialization, building on the borough's agrarian base in Lancaster County. In 1904, U.S. Congressman Aaron S. Kreider constructed the Kreider Shoe Manufacturing Company facility, introducing shoe production as a key local industry that employed residents and spurred related economic activity. Several additional shoe factories followed, operating until the last closed in 1979, reflecting a period of sustained but specialized manufacturing tied to consumer goods.[8][14] Food processing emerged as another pillar, exemplified by the Klein Chocolate Company, founded in 1913 by brothers William and Frederick Kleinâ€”immigrants from West Prussia who had previously worked at the Hershey Chocolate Company. The firm produced milk chocolate in a facility on South Market Street, achieving notable success and using promotional strategies such as sponsoring a semi-professional baseball team in 1919 that won approximately 80% of its games, including exhibitions against Major League Baseball clubs. This venture contributed to local employment and visibility until its eventual integration into larger operations.[15][16][17] Printing and publishing gained footing mid-century with the founding of Continental Press in 1937 by Horace E. Raffensperger, which focused on supplemental educational materials for PreK-12 markets and expanded to support curriculum and testing needs. These industries, alongside established institutions like Elizabethtown College (founded 1899) and the Masonic Village (established around 1900), facilitated population growth from 1,861 residents in 1900 to 11,887 by 2000, with accelerated expansion after World War II as housing and commercial development extended into surrounding areas.[18][19]

Post-2000 Developments

Elizabethtown's population grew modestly in the post-2000 era, increasing by 160 residents from 2000 to 2023 to reach 11,857, with an annual growth rate of approximately 0.91% in recent years.[20] [21] Employment expanded by 2.97% between 2022 and 2023 to 6,490 workers, while median household income rose to $67,000, reflecting economic stability amid broader regional trends in manufacturing, education, and services.[22] Infrastructure upgrades included the Keystone Corridor Improvement Project, announced in 2004, which renovated the Elizabethtown Amtrak station with restored flooring, woodwork, lighting, a new slate roof, paved parking, landscaping, and enhanced platform access to support electrified high-speed rail service.[23] Passenger ridership on the corridor doubled since 2000, bolstering connectivity to Philadelphia and Harrisburg.[24] Elizabethtown College initiated a multi-year campus transformation in 2000 under a plan announced the prior year, adding facilities, academic programs, and expanding to 204 acres, with further projects like the Young Center renovation and addition completed by 2016.[25] [26] The 2005 Downtown Master Plan drove over $14.5 million in public investments for business district revitalization, complemented by the 2010 Regional Strategic Plan for coordinated growth with adjacent townships.[27] [28] Recent initiatives include proposals for 200 residential units above commercial spaces in the downtown area as of 2025.[29]

Demographics

Population Trends

The population of Elizabethtown borough has shown modest growth over recent decades, characteristic of a stable small urban center within the broader expanding Lancaster County. The 2010 United States Census recorded 11,545 residents, reflecting a slight decline from the 2000 count of 11,887 amid regional economic shifts and suburbanization patterns. By the 2020 Census, the population had rebounded to 11,639, an increase of 94 individuals or approximately 0.8% over the decade, with annual growth averaging less than 0.1%.[40] Post-2020 estimates indicate continued slow expansion, driven primarily by net in-migration to Lancaster County rather than natural increase alone. The population reached an estimated 11,857 by 2023, up 0.44% from 2022, aligning with county-wide trends where migration accounts for most gains amid aging demographics and low birth rates in non-Amish areas. Government and Politics

Local Governance Structure

Elizabethtown operates under the standard borough form of government established by the Pennsylvania Borough Code, featuring a council-manager structure where the elected Borough Council serves as the legislative body and appoints a professional manager to handle executive functions.[44] The Borough Council consists of six members, with two representatives elected from each of the three wards, serving staggered four-year terms to ensure continuity.[44] [45] Council members are responsible for enacting ordinances, approving budgets, setting tax rates, and appointing the Borough Manager, while meetings are held monthly to address policy matters.[44] The Mayor is elected at-large for a four-year term and holds limited executive powers, primarily acting as the chief conservator of the peace and supervising the police department to enforce borough laws and ordinances.[45] [46] The Mayor may veto council ordinances, though this can be overridden by a two-thirds vote, but does not preside over council meetings, a role filled by an elected Council President.[45] The Borough Manager, appointed by the Council, serves as the chief executive officer, overseeing daily operations, implementing council policies, managing departments such as public works and finance, preparing the annual budget, and acting as Borough Secretary and Treasurer.[47] [44] This arrangement delegates administrative duties to a professional manager, allowing the elected officials to focus on legislative and policy decisions.[45] Elections for council seats and the mayoral position occur in odd-numbered years during municipal primaries and general elections, with ward-based voting for council to reflect local representation.[48]

Political Debates and Local Issues

In July 2024, Elizabethtown Borough Council approved a non-sanctuary resolution by a 4-2 vote, directing local police to fully cooperate with federal and state law enforcement agencies on immigration enforcement, including detaining individuals for ICE.[53] The measure faced significant opposition, with 14 bipartisan public comments against it compared to three in favor, reflecting divides over local involvement in federal immigration policy.[54] During discussions, Council member Jay Hynicker stated, "We've had Blacks in Elizabethtown for a long time. We've never had a problem with them," in reference to contrasting local minority communities with potential issues from undocumented immigrants; the remark drew accusations of racism from critics, prompting Hynicker to apologize at subsequent meetings in July and August 2024 amid calls for his resignation and demands for greater diversity initiatives.[55] [56] [57] Local debates have increasingly centered on school policies, particularly those addressing gender identity and educational content. In late 2024 and early 2025, the Elizabethtown Area School Board, in consultation with the conservative Independence Law Center, proposed and approved two policies: one requiring parental consent for students under 18 to update gender identity or preferred pronouns in school records, and another limiting athletic participation to biological sex as indicated on birth certificates.[58] [59] These measures sparked protests, including student walkouts in November 2024 and opposition labeling them as restrictive toward transgender students, though proponents argued they safeguard parental rights and competitive fairness in sports.[60] [61] Similarly, a March 2025 proposal to remove books with sexually explicit content from school libraries drew resident backlash, with 13 speakers opposing the changes at a board meeting, highlighting tensions between content curation for minors and access to materials.[62] Drug-related concerns have also fueled discourse, with a school board member claiming in 2024 that Mexican drug cartels operate within the borough; investigations confirmed fentanyl distribution linked to cartel-supplied networks but found no evidence of structured local cartel operations, deeming the assertions partially accurate yet overstated.[63] Amid these culture-war flashpoints, 2025 borough elections saw candidates for mayor and council emphasizing practical matters like infrastructure, property taxes, and zoning over divisive social issues, signaling a potential pivot despite ongoing community polarization.[46]

Economy

Major Industries and Employers

The economy of Elizabethtown employs approximately 6,490 residents as of 2023, with health care and social assistance constituting the largest sector at 1,096 jobs, followed by educational services at 806 jobs and retail trade.[22] Manufacturing also plays a notable role, supported by facilities such as the Mars Chocolate plant, which produces confectionery products and contributes to local employment in food processing.[64] Agriculture remains relevant due to the surrounding Lancaster County farmlands, involving crop production and related processing, though it employs fewer residents directly within borough limits.[65] Key employers include Elizabethtown College, a private liberal arts institution that sustains around 806 educational services positions through faculty, staff, and administrative roles.[22] [66] Masonic Villages of Pennsylvania operates a major continuing care retirement community in the borough, bolstering the health care sector with nursing, administrative, and support staff.[66] In manufacturing and distribution, Kyocera Document Solutions established a new facility in May 2025 to handle order fulfillment for office equipment, potentially expanding local logistics jobs.[67] Smaller firms like Hollinger Services contribute in printing and services, though precise employment figures vary by economic reports.[66]

Education

Public K-12 System

The Elizabethtown Area School District provides public K-12 education to students in Elizabethtown borough and portions of surrounding townships in northwest Lancaster County, Pennsylvania.[71] The district enrolls approximately 3,640 students across five schools, with a minority enrollment of 20% and 27.1% of students classified as economically disadvantaged.[72] It employs 234 full-time equivalent teachers, yielding a student-teacher ratio of about 15.6:1.[73] The district's schools include three elementary schoolsâ€”Bainbridge Elementary School, East High Street Elementary School, and Bear Creek Schoolâ€”serving grades K-5 or K-6 configurations; Elizabethtown Area Middle School for grades 6-8; and Elizabethtown Area Senior High School for grades 9-12.[74] [75] The high school serves 1,293 students.[76] Assessments align with Pennsylvania state standards, utilizing the Pennsylvania System of School Assessment (PSSA) for grades 3-8 in English language arts, mathematics, and science, and Keystone Exams in algebra I, literature, and biology for high school graduation eligibility.[77] Academic performance is rated above average relative to other Pennsylvania districts, with the high school ranked 209th out of 686 statewide and achieving a four-star rating.[78] The four-year graduation rate stands at 95%, placing it in the top 30% of Pennsylvania high schools.[75] On Keystone-aligned measures, advanced proficiency rates at the high school include 13.5% in English language arts/literature (below the statewide average of 15%), 21% in mathematics/algebra, and 26% in science/biology.[79] The district's overall academic growth score is 70, compared to the statewide average of 75.4.[79]

Recent Educational Controversies

In January 2025, the Elizabethtown Area School District board voted 6-3 to adopt two policies addressing gender identity: one establishing sex-based distinctions in athletics, barring males identifying as female from competing in female-designated contact sports, and another requiring staff to use biologically accurate pronouns unless directed otherwise by a parent or guardian for students under 12.[61][80] The policies, drafted with input from the Independence Law Centerâ€”a conservative legal group advocating for parental rights and biological definitions of sexâ€”aimed to align district practices with state law emphasizing sex over gender identity in school activities.[81] Opponents, including some students and parents, argued the measures discriminated against transgender individuals and could foster a hostile environment, leading to protests outside board meetings where demonstrators chanted against the board's decisions.[82][60] Supporters countered that the policies safeguard fairness in female sports, citing inherent physiological differences between males and females that persist post-puberty, regardless of hormone treatments, as evidenced by performance data in athletic competitions.[83] The vote exacerbated preexisting divisions from the 2023 school board elections, where candidates backed by conservative groups emphasizing parental oversight gained seats, prompting a French documentary that chronicled the heated race and its aftermath.[84] In April 2025, a lawsuit filed by district residents alleged that board members violated Pennsylvania's Sunshine Act by conducting private discussions to advance these and related policies, purportedly influenced by religious motivations, though the complaint did not specify evidence of proselytizing in classrooms.[85] The suit remains pending as of October 2025, with no judicial rulings issued. Separately, in September 2024, parents criticized board member Amy Clickner for a Facebook post labeling certain educational materials as "grooming" content, viewing it as inflammatory rhetoric that undermined civil discourse, though Clickner defended her statements as parental advocacy against age-inappropriate curricula.[86] Budget-related decisions in 2025 also sparked contention, including a June vote to eliminate dedicated library staffing at middle and high schools as part of an $82.1 million plan that raised property taxes by 2.5% while drawing from reserves to cover a $1.48 million shortfall.[87][88] An August proposal to cut a marching band busâ€”saving $4,000 but risking overcrowding for 150 studentsâ€”drew parental backlash over diminished extracurricular support, prompting a reversal after public outcry at a board meeting.[89][90] These fiscal pressures stemmed partly from Pennsylvania's state budget impasse, delaying over $3 billion in education aid and forcing districts like Elizabethtown to navigate rising costs without full funding.[91]

Nearby Higher Education

Elizabethtown College, a private liberal arts institution founded in 1899 by the Church of the Brethren, is situated within the borough and enrolls about 1,519 undergraduates as of fall 2023, offering 53 majors and emphasizing experiential learning through programs like off-campus study and internships.[92][93] Prominent four-year institutions within commuting distance include Millersville University of Pennsylvania, a public university 17 miles southeast in Millersville that is part of the Pennsylvania State System of Higher Education and serves roughly 5,853 students with bachelor's and master's degrees across fields like education, business, and sciences.[94][95] Franklin & Marshall College, a private liberal arts college established in 1787, lies approximately 15 miles east in Lancaster and has an enrollment of 1,990 undergraduates focused on interdisciplinary studies and research opportunities.[96] Further options encompass Penn State Harrisburg, a branch campus of Pennsylvania State University located 21 miles northwest in Middletown, which provides associate through doctoral programs to over 5,000 students in areas such as engineering, business, and law.[97][98] For two-year education, the Harrisburg Area Community College operates a Lancaster campus about 20 miles east, delivering associate degrees, certificates, and transfer pathways in over 100 programs to support workforce entry and articulation to four-year schools.[99][100]

Infrastructure and Transportation

Roadways and Highways

Pennsylvania Route 283 (PA 283), a 29-mile freeway, serves as the primary east-west corridor through Elizabethtown, linking the borough to Harrisburg approximately 20 miles west and Lancaster 15 miles east.[101] The route parallels the historic alignment of U.S. Route 230 and includes an interchange at mile marker 14 with PA 743, providing direct access to Elizabethtown's central business district and Hershey to the north.[102] Recent maintenance on PA 283 in Lancaster County has included bridge inspections over Elizabethtown Road and Snyder Road, with lane restrictions implemented for safety. Ongoing resurfacing extends from Esbenshade Road in Rapho Township to State Road in East Hempfield Township, addressing wear on this high-volume artery.[103] Pennsylvania Route 230 (PA 230), designated as Market Street within Elizabethtown, functions as the main surface arterial through the borough, connecting to PA 283 north of town and extending eastward to Mount Joy.[104] This 28.4-mile state highway originates at U.S. Route 22 in Harrisburg and supports local commerce along its two-lane alignment in the downtown area.[105] Infrastructure upgrades, such as the 2023 bridge replacement on Market Street spanning Donegal Creek, have aimed to enhance structural integrity amid increasing traffic demands.[104] Pennsylvania Route 743 (PA 743) offers north-south access, intersecting PA 283 at the Elizabethtown/Hershey exit and proceeding through the borough's core toward Dauphin County.[106] In Elizabethtown, PA 743 briefly concurs with PA 230 and PA 241, facilitating connections to regional routes like PA 241 from the Susquehanna River area.[107] Rehabilitation efforts on structures along PA 743, including those designated as Elizabethtown Road, have maintained open traffic flow while addressing deterioration.[106] The borough encompasses about 40 miles of public roadways across its 2.6 square miles, with state highways integrated into the local network under PennDOT oversight for higher-order maintenance.[44]

Public Transit and Utilities

Elizabethtown is served by Amtrak's Keystone Service trains at the Elizabethtown station, located at 50 S. Wilson Avenue, offering intercity rail connections to major cities including Philadelphia, New York City, and Harrisburg.[108] The station provides a waiting room open from 6:00 a.m. to 3:00 p.m. on weekdays, with free short-term and long-term parking available.[108] [109] Trains on this route operate with frequent service, typically requiring about 1 hour and 28 minutes to reach Philadelphia.[110] Local bus transportation is provided by the Red Rose Transit Authority (RRTA), which has offered affordable public bus services throughout Lancaster County since 1976.[111] Route 18/Elizabethtown connects the borough's Amtrak station and central areas to Downtown Lancaster, passing through Mount Joy, Landisville, and Rohrerstown, with service on weekdays and Saturdays; schedules are subject to traffic variations.[112] [113] Water services are managed by the Elizabethtown Area Water Authority (EAWA), which operates from 211 W. Hummelstown Street and maintains the local water system, including billing and emergency response available weekdays from 7:30 a.m. to 4:00 p.m.[114] Sanitary sewer and wastewater treatment fall under the Elizabethtown Borough and the Elizabethtown Regional Sewer Authority (ERSA), with quarterly billing combined with trash and recycling services; residents can contact ERSA for issues like slow drainage at (717) 367-5947.[115] [116] Natural gas is supplied by UGI Utilities, Inc., serving customers in the region with options for payments and service starts.[117] Electricity distribution is provided by PPL Electric Utilities, the primary regulated utility for the area.[118]

Community and Culture

Annual Events and Traditions

The Elizabethtown Fair, an annual agricultural exposition recognized as one of Pennsylvania's 108 state fairs, takes place each August at 900 East High Street, typically spanning six days from Monday to Saturday. It features livestock auctions, crop and craft competitions, commercial vendor booths, amusement rides, a free petting zoo, and evening fireworks, drawing families for its emphasis on rural heritage and community exhibits with free general admission.[119][120][121] Second Fridays form a recurring monthly tradition on the second Friday of each month, from approximately 4:00 p.m. to 8:00 p.m. in downtown Elizabethtown, where local shops extend hours, street vendors set up, food trucks operate, and live music or themed activitiesâ€”such as vehicle showcases or costume eventsâ€”encourage pedestrian exploration and bolster small businesses.[122][123][124] The Holiday Parade, held annually in early December along South Market Street, assembles community groups, floats, and marching bands for a one-hour procession starting at 4:00 p.m., themed around elements like holiday traditions to promote seasonal goodwill and local involvement, with organization led by the nonprofit GEARS and sponsorship from MARS Wrigley.[125][126][127]

Media, Libraries, and Civic Life

Local media in Elizabethtown primarily consists of regional outlets serving Lancaster County, as the borough lacks dedicated commercial radio or television stations.[128][129][130] The Elizabethtown Advocate serves as a key local print publication, focusing on community news, while The Etownian, published by Elizabethtown College, covers borough events alongside campus affairs.[131] Broader coverage comes from LNP | LancasterOnline, which includes Elizabethtown in its Lancaster County reporting.[132] The borough's official website provides updates on local government and events.[133] The Elizabethtown Public Library, located at 10 South Market Street, operates as the primary public library serving the borough and is affiliated with the Library System of Lancaster County.[134][135] It offers standard services including book loans, digital resources, and community programs, with hours of 9 a.m. to 4 p.m. on Mondays, Fridays, and Saturdays, and 9 a.m. to 7 p.m. Tuesdays through Thursdays as of 2025.[134] The library also processes passport applications, hosts events like coffee and tea brewing workshops, and supports educational initiatives for the greater Elizabethtown area.[134][136] Civic life in Elizabethtown centers on service-oriented organizations that promote community welfare and youth development. The Rotary Club of Elizabethtown emphasizes local service projects to enhance quality of life, drawing on a tradition of Rotarian involvement.[137] The Elizabethtown Lions Club, established in 1941, prioritizes vision screening, youth programs, and community aid, meeting biweekly to coordinate efforts.[138] The Elizabethtown Area Communities That Care, a nonprofit, works to mitigate youth risks such as substance use through prevention strategies and partnerships.[139] Additional groups like the Community Cupboard provide food assistance, reflecting grassroots support networks.[140] These entities foster engagement without reliance on larger institutional frameworks, aligning with the borough's small-town structure.

Notable Residents

Gene Garber (born November 13, 1947), a former Major League Baseball relief pitcher who appeared in 951 games over 19 seasons from 1969 to 1988 with teams including the Pittsburgh Pirates, Kansas City Royals, Philadelphia Phillies, and Atlanta Braves, grew up in Elizabethtown and attended Elizabethtown High School, where he earned four varsity letters in baseball.[141][142] Steve Spence (born May 9, 1962), a long-distance runner who represented the United States at the 1992 Barcelona Olympics in the marathon and won a bronze medal in the marathon at the 1991 World Championships in Athletics, was born and raised in Elizabethtown, attending Lower Dauphin High School before competing collegiately at Shippensburg University.[143][144] Paul Gottfried (born 1941), a political philosopher and paleoconservative intellectual known for his critiques of neoconservatism and works such as After Liberalism (1999) and The Strange Death of Marxism (2005), served as the Horace Raffensperger Professor of Humanities at Elizabethtown College from 1980 until his retirement.[145] Chet Williamson (born June 19, 1948), an author of horror, fantasy, and mystery fiction with over 20 novels including Lowland Rider (1988) and Ash Wednesday (1990), as well as the centennial history of Elizabethtown College, was born and raised in Elizabethtown.[146]
