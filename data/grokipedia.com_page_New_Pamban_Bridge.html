New Pamban Bridge

The New Pamban Bridge (pronounced: /pɑːmbən/ romanised: pāmban) is a vertical lift railway sea bridge that connects the town of Mandapam in mainland India with Rameswaram on Pamban Island. The new bridge is constructed parallel to the old Pamban Bridge opened in 1914 and was planned to replace the same. The bridge stretches across a length of 2.07 km (1.29 mi) and is the first vertical lift sea bridge in India. It consists of 100 spans including a 72.5 m (238 ft) vertical lifting central section. The construction of the bridge was completed in 2024 and the bridge was opened for traffic in April 2025.[1][2]

Pamban Bridge

Background

The old Pamban Bridge was opened in February 1914 and carried rail traffic between Rameswaram in Pamban Island and mainland India.[3] The bridge underwent several overhauls over the years to extend its lifespan.[4] The bridge was damaged in an accident in 2013.[5][6] In 2015, the Ministry of Railways sanctioned ₹25 crore (US$3.7 million) to upgrade the bridge, including replacing a key span.[7] In December 2018, fissures developed on the bridge, which halted railway traffic; the Government of India then officially confirmed plans to build a replacement bridge.[8][9][10] In December 2022, rail transportation on the old bridge was suspended permanently as the bascule section had weakened significantly due to corrosion.[11]

Planning and construction

In December 2018, the Government of India announced that a new railway bridge would be constructed near the old Pamban Bridge at a cost of ₹535 crore (US$64 million).[10][12] The construction contract was awarded to Rail Vikas Nigam Limited. The foundation stone for the new bridge was laid by prime minister Narendra Modi in November 2019 and the construction commenced in February 2020.[13][14] The construction of the pillars and spans for a single track was completed in September 2024 with trial runs conducted in October 2024.[15] The bridge was expected to be opened for traffic by late 2024 post the approval of the Commission of Railway Safety (CRS).[12] In late November, the CRS permitted operation of trains at a maximum speed of 75 km/h (47 mph) with the speed further limited to 50 km/h (31 mph) on the lifting bascule section. However, the CRS highlighted several concerns and discrepancies in the construction of the bridge such as the usage of non standard designs that are not approved by the Research Design and Standards Organisation, deviation from standard welding procedures that reduced the stress carrying capacity by 36% and insufficient addressing of corrosion.[16][17] In response, the Southern Railway clarified that the bridge was designed by the Spanish consultancy Técnica y Proyectos S.A. according to European and Indian design codes and was proof checked by experts from IIT Madras and IIT Bombay.[18] In response to the CRS findings, the Railway Ministry appointed a five-member high-level committee, which certified the bridge's structural safety in early April 2025.[19] In January-February 2025, further trials were conducted with simultaneous lifting of the bascule sections of the old and the new bridges, and the running of train rakes.[20] In March 2025, final trials and rehearsals were conducted prior to the opening of the bridge for traffic.[21] The bridge was opened for traffic on 6 April 2025 by the Indian prime minister.[22]

Design

The new bridge is constructed parallel to the old bridge and spans a length of 2.07 km (1.29 mi). It features a 72.5 m (238 ft) central vertical lift span supported by two 35.6 m (117 ft) steel towers, using cables, sheaves, 315 MT counterweights, and hydraulic jacks to raise it up to 17 m (56 ft). It is the first vertical lift sea bridge in India.[16][23][24] It has 99 spans of 18.3 m (60 ft) length each apart from the vertical lifting span. It provides a navigational air clearance of 22 m (72 ft) above sea level when closed, about 3 m higher than the old bridge. The vertical lifting central span of the bridge allows ships of up to 22 m (72 ft) height to pass through when closed, with the lift enabling taller vessels up to 39 m (128 ft).[24][15] Designed by TYPSA (Spain) and proof-checked by IIT Madras, the bridge is designed to carry a double track railway line, though currently operational with a single track.[7][7][26][18]
