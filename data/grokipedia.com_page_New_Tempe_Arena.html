New Tempe Arena

The New Tempe Arena was a proposed 16,000-seat multi-purpose venue in Tempe, Arizona, intended to serve as the permanent home for the National Hockey League's Arizona Coyotes hockey team.[1][2] Envisioned as the centerpiece of a $2.1 billion privately funded entertainment district on city-owned land near Tempe Town Lake, the project encompassed hotels, retail outlets, residential apartments, office spaces, and a theater, with construction slated to begin in 2024 if approved.[3][4] The Tempe City Council unanimously approved the development agreement in November 2022, but required voter ratification via referendum due to the public land sale.[5] The proposal faced significant opposition, including a lawsuit from the City of Phoenix alleging interference with airport operations and traffic patterns at nearby Sky Harbor International Airport, as well as local concerns over increased congestion, environmental impacts, and skepticism toward sports franchise owners' financial commitments.[6][7] On May 16, 2023, Tempe voters rejected the three referendum measuresâ€”authorizing the land sale, development agreement, and leaseâ€”by a margin of approximately 53% to 47%, effectively halting the project.[3][8][9] The defeat contributed to the Arizona Coyotes' subsequent temporary relocation to shared facilities and eventual franchise sale and transfer to Utah in 2024, leaving the New Tempe Arena unrealized amid broader debates on public subsidies for professional sports venues despite its private funding structure.[10][7]

Overview

Project Summary

The New Tempe Arena project was a proposed $2.1 billion entertainment district centered on a new 17,000-seat multi-purpose arena intended to become the permanent home of the National Hockey League's Arizona Coyotes. Developed by team owner Alex Meruelo through his entity Black Acre Development, the initiative aimed to construct the facility on a 46-acre parcel of city-owned land in south Tempe, Arizona, approximately two miles from Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport. The development, formally submitted to the Tempe City Council in November 2022, promised private financing without direct taxpayer subsidies, in exchange for a long-term ground lease on the public land; it included not only the arena but also a 6,000-seat live music theater, over 350,000 square feet of retail and dining space, a hotel, office buildings, and public plazas designed to host watch parties and fan experiences.[3][11][12] Proponents, including Meruelo, projected the district would generate significant economic benefits, such as up to 4,000 construction jobs and annual revenues exceeding $100 million from events, while addressing the Coyotes' long-standing arena instability after playing in a college rink since 2021. The arena design emphasized modern architecture with features like premium seating, luxury suites, and versatile event capabilities for hockey, concerts, and other spectacles, drawing on input from firms like Gensler for an innovative layout integrated with surrounding mixed-use elements. However, the proposal faced scrutiny over potential strains on local infrastructure, including traffic congestion, water consumption estimated at over one million gallons daily from the Central Arizona Project, and indirect public costs embedded in the lease terms, despite assertions of no upfront city funding.[13][12][14] In February 2023, the Tempe Town Council approved the development agreement by a 6-1 vote, advancing it to a public referendum required due to the land's public status. On May 16, 2023, Tempe voters rejected the measure 53% to 47%, effectively halting the project amid concerns from residents and analysts about unproven economic projections and environmental impacts. The defeat prompted the Coyotes to explore alternative sites, including a later-proposed development in northeast Phoenix, while the Tempe site remained undeveloped as of 2025; the franchise's NHL operations were subsequently suspended, leading to its relocation and rebranding as the Utah Hockey Club for the 2024-25 season.[3][10][15]

Location and Context

The proposed New Tempe Arena was planned for a 46-acre site owned by the City of Tempe, located at the southeast corner of South Priest Drive and Rio Salado Parkway, adjacent to Tempe Town Lake in the Phoenix metropolitan area.[16][17] This position placed the development along the Salt River, approximately 2 miles east of Arizona State University's Tempe campus and within 10 miles of Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport.[17][18] Tempe, a city of about 180,000 residents, functions as a suburban hub in the East Valley region of Maricopa County, characterized by its university-driven economy and recreational amenities like the man-made Town Lake, which hosts events and supports urban revitalization efforts.[17] The site's selection stemmed from its centrality to the Phoenix area's 4.5 million population, accessibility via Loop 202 freeway and light rail, and potential to integrate with existing waterfront developments, aiming to create a year-round entertainment district without relying on public subsidies.[4][17] However, the location raised logistical challenges, including limited access to the city's primary Central Arizona Project water supply, necessitating alternative sourcing for the project's estimated 1 million gallons daily usage, and height restrictions due to proximity to Sky Harbor Airport, which prompted a 2023 lawsuit from Phoenix citing aviation safety concerns.[14][18] These factors contributed to debates over environmental impacts and infrastructure strain in a desert region prone to water scarcity and traffic congestion.[14][3]

Historical Background

Arizona Coyotes' Arena Challenges

The Arizona Coyotes franchise, originally relocated from Winnipeg to Phoenix in 1996, faced persistent arena-related difficulties that stemmed from financial strains, ownership turnover, and contentious municipal relationships. After initially sharing America West Arena (now Footprint Center) with the NBA's Phoenix Suns, the team moved to the newly constructed Gila River Arena in Glendale in 2003, a $220 million facility where the city covered up to $180 million in costs while team principals funded the remainder. This relocation aimed to secure a dedicated venue but quickly entangled the franchise in disputes over operating subsidies and lease terms, as Glendale taxpayers shouldered annual shortfalls exceeding $15 million in some years due to low attendance and revenue.[19] Ownership instability exacerbated these issues, with the team cycling through multiple groups amid mounting debts. Original Phoenix-era owner Richard Burke sold to Steve Ellman in 2001, who spearheaded the Glendale move but encountered financial distress, leading to Jerry Moyes's acquisition in 2005. By 2009, Moyes's leveraged buyout contributed to bankruptcy filing, with the NHL assuming operational control to avert relocation and recover $61 million in losses and costs. Subsequent salesâ€”to Ice Edge Holdings in 2010 (which defaulted), then Renaissance Sports & Entertainment (RSE) in 2013â€”failed to stabilize finances, as the franchise reported consistent operating losses tied to arena revenue sharing and subdued ticket sales averaging under 14,000 per game in peak seasons.[20][21][22] Lease negotiations with Glendale devolved into litigation, highlighting misaligned incentives between the cash-strapped municipality and the revenue-dependent team. In 2013, Glendale approved a 15-year, $225 million management deal with RSE, but the arrangement unraveled in 2015 when the city council voted 5-2 to terminate it, alleging violations of state gift-of-public-funds laws after the city manager hired a firm linked to RSE principals. The Coyotes countersued for $200 million, securing a temporary restraining order and eventually a settlement allowing continued play through 2021-22, though at reduced terms that strained team operations. By August 2021, Glendale opted against renewal, citing over $200 million in cumulative subsidies and arena underutilization, forcing the Coyotes to a temporary home at Arizona State University's 5,000-seat Mullett Arena starting in 2022â€”a college facility ill-suited for NHL attendance and revenue needs.[23][24][25] These cumulative challengesâ€”marked by adversarial landlord relations, fiscal hemorrhaging, and venue precarityâ€”undermined the franchise's viability in Arizona, prompting owner Alex Meruelo's 2019 purchase for $300 million and subsequent pursuit of independent arena control to escape dependency on public funding and hostile leases.[26][22]

Initial Negotiations and Land Pursuit (2021â€“2022)

In the summer of 2021, the City of Tempe issued a Request for Proposals (RFP) seeking development of approximately 46 acres of city-owned land at the northeast corner of Rio Salado Parkway and Priest Drive, adjacent to Tempe Town Lake.[27][28] The site, a former landfill requiring environmental remediation, was targeted for a mixed-use project including a sports arena, up to 1,000 multifamily housing units, and 200,000 square feet of retail space.[29][30] This RFP followed the Arizona Coyotes' ongoing search for a permanent arena after their lease at Gila River Arena in Glendale was set to expire after the 2021â€“22 season.[31] By July 2021, the Coyotes, owned by Alex Meruelo, entered preliminary discussions with Tempe officials about relocating to the East Valley site, envisioning an arena alongside an entertainment complex.[31] On September 2, 2021, the team submitted the sole response to the RFP, bidding on two parcels within the 46-acre tract as the initial step toward securing the land for a proposed $1.7 billion development.[32][33] Tempe city staff began reviewing the proposal shortly thereafter, focusing on its alignment with zoning, environmental, and aviation constraints near Phoenix Sky Harbor Airport.[30] Negotiations progressed into 2022 amid the team's interim arrangements, including a February agreement to play at Arizona State University's new 5,000-seat multipurpose arena starting in the 2022â€“23 season while pursuing the Tempe site.[34] On June 6, 2022, the Tempe City Council voted to authorize staff to negotiate a formal development agreement with the Coyotes, advancing the land pursuit despite concerns over traffic, water usage, and FAA height restrictions raised by stakeholders like Sky Harbor Airport authorities.[27][35] This phase emphasized the team's commitment to remediating the contaminated land and integrating public benefits, though no binding land acquisition had yet occurred.[36]

Proposal Development

Formal Submission and Town Council Process (2022â€“2023)

On June 2, 2022, the Tempe City Council voted 5-2 to authorize formal negotiations with the Arizona Coyotes and their development partner, Bluebird District Development, regarding a proposed arena and entertainment district on 45 acres of city-owned land near the Loop 202 freeway and Rio Salado Parkway.[37][38] The decision followed an initial concept submission by the team in September 2021 and addressed concerns over traffic, environmental impacts, and public subsidies, with council members citing potential economic benefits as outweighing risks during deliberations.[27] Negotiations spanned several months, culminating in the release of detailed development agreements on November 22, 2022, which outlined a privately financed $2.1 billion project including a 16,500-seat arena, retail spaces, a hotel, and public parks, without requiring direct city funding beyond land lease terms.[39] On November 29, 2022, the council held a four-hour public hearing featuring 90 minutes of predominantly supportive comments from residents and stakeholders, after which it unanimously approved (7-0) the necessary ordinances and agreements to advance the plan to a binding voter referendum.[40][41] This approval set the referendum for May 16, 2023, requiring a simple majority for authorization, as stipulated under Tempe's charter for major land use decisions on public property.[42] The process drew scrutiny for limited transparency in some negotiation phases, though public sessions adhered to Arizona's open meeting laws at the time; subsequent investigations in 2024 alleged violations in related closed-door discussions, but these postdated the 2022-2023 approvals.[43] Council support emphasized the project's projected 6,000 construction jobs and $1.5 billion in annual economic output, based on team-submitted analyses, while opponents highlighted unverified fiscal projections and potential strain on local infrastructure.[44]

Financing and Economic Projections

The proposed Tempe Entertainment District, encompassing the arena, was projected to cost $2.1 billion in total, with developers committing to fund approximately $1.9 billion privately, covering arena construction, hotels, retail, and other private developments without direct use of general taxpayer revenues for the core facilities.[3][45] Public involvement was confined to approximately $200â€“208 million in bonds for infrastructure enhancements, including site remediation ($73 million) and utilities like roads and sewers, to be issued through a Community Facilities District (CFD) and repaid solely from district-generated taxes over 30 years.[46][47] The CFD repayment mechanism diverted half of the city's transaction privilege (sales) tax rate (0.9% of the 1.8% total), three-quarters of lodging taxes (3.75% of 5%), 19.6% of primary property taxes after an eight-year Government Property Lease Excise Tax abatement, and a developer-imposed 2.3% surcharge on retail sales, with additional developer contributions if shortfalls occurred.[47] Proponents argued this structure ensured no net subsidy, as bonds would be self-liquidating via incremental on-site activity, with developers also handling land acquisition costs after winning a state auction for the site.[40] Critics, however, contended the diversions effectively subsidized infrastructure by redirecting $2.70 in city revenues for every $1 in projected new district revenue, as much activity would substitute existing spending elsewhere in Tempe rather than represent genuine economic expansion.[47] Economic projections from supporters, including a study by Arizona State University's W.P. Carey School of Business, estimated net new sales tax revenues $34 million higher than consultant baselines, with overall district benefits reaching hundreds of millions in tax gains and thousands of jobs from visitor spending on events, lodging, and retail.[48] These forecasts assumed high attendance, including 45 non-hockey events annually at the arena, driving $244 million in broader impacts without significant leakage or cannibalization from nearby venues.[49] Opposing analyses, such as the Grand Canyon Institute's fiscal review, forecasted a net present value loss of $65.7 million to Tempe's general fund over 30 years, with CFD costs totaling $104.4 million NPV against $38.7 million NPV in visitor spending gains, due to overoptimistic assumptions ignoring substitution effects, event feasibility, and the opportunity cost of tax diversions.[47] The Institute emphasized that arena-focused projections often inflate multipliers by treating relocated spending as "new," a methodological flaw evident in similar sports facility evaluations.[50][47]

Design and Planned Features

Arena Architecture and Capacity

The New Tempe Arena was designed by MANICA Architecture as a multi-purpose venue primarily for National Hockey League games, with a planned seating capacity of 18,000.[51] Earlier proposals described the arena accommodating approximately 16,000 spectators, intentionally smaller than the Arizona Coyotes' prior home at Gila River Arena to prioritize fan proximity over maximum attendance.[52] [17] Architectural features emphasized a larger lower bowl configuration to position more fans closer to the ice surface, aligning with contemporary trends in sports venue design that enhance sightlines and immersion.[52] The arena was intended as the centerpiece of Phase 1 in the broader entertainment district, integrating with adjacent facilities like a team practice area while maintaining a focus on hockey-specific optimizations such as improved acoustics and event versatility for concerts and other gatherings.[53] No detailed specifications on structural materials or exterior aesthetics were publicly released beyond conceptual renderings, reflecting the project's emphasis on functional efficiency within the $1.7 billion development framework.[51]

Entertainment District Components

The proposed Tempe Entertainment District (TED) encompassed a mixed-use development surrounding the central arena, featuring residential, commercial, hospitality, and office elements designed to create a year-round destination. Key components included approximately 1,675 residential units, intended to provide housing options integrated with the district's urban layout.[54] Commercial spaces comprised 313,000 square feet of retail and restaurant facilities, aimed at supporting dining, shopping, and entertainment activities to draw visitors beyond hockey events.[54] [55] Hospitality features involved two hotels totaling 500 roomsâ€”one with 300 rooms and another with 200â€”positioned to accommodate event attendees and tourists.[54] [55] Office development included 320,000 square feet of Class A space, targeted at professional and business tenants to foster economic activity during non-event periods.[54] Additional amenities encompassed a 50,000-square-foot theater venue for concerts and performances, a sports lounge for fan engagement, and three parking garages providing nearly 6,900 spaces to manage vehicular traffic.[55] [56] The district's overall footprint spanned about 3.4 million square feet, with phased construction projected to begin in late 2022 and complete by 2027, emphasizing private financing for non-arena elements to minimize public subsidy reliance.[54] These components were envisioned to generate ancillary revenue through sales taxes and property values, though critics questioned the projections' realism given regional market saturation for similar venues.[47]

Public Debate and Referendum

Arguments in Support

Supporters of the Tempe Entertainment District, including Arizona Coyotes owner Alex Meruelo and project developer Bluebird Development, emphasized that the $2.1 billion proposal would be entirely privately financed, with no direct use of taxpayer dollars for construction or operations.[57] The financing structure relied on a community facilities district (CFD) mechanism, where bonds would be repaid through future sales taxes, property taxes, and other revenues generated by the district itself, shielding the city from financial risk if projections fell short.[58] Economic analyses commissioned by proponents, such as a study by the Seidman Research Institute at Arizona State University, projected significant benefits including the creation of approximately 6,000 jobs during construction and ongoing operations, alongside $690 million in net economic activity for Tempe over time.[59] These figures accounted for the district's mixed-use componentsâ€”an NHL arena seating 16,000, a 6,000-seat music venue, retail space, offices, hotels, and residential unitsâ€”which were expected to draw visitors, boost local spending, and increase tax revenues without requiring city subsidies.[54] Supporters highlighted the transformation of a long-dormant landfill site into a revenue-generating hub as an optimal use of underutilized public land, arguing it would enhance Tempe's competitiveness as an entertainment destination in the Phoenix metropolitan area.[57] The proposal garnered endorsements from Tempe Mayor Corey Woods and four former mayors, who viewed it as the strongest sports franchise agreement in Arizona history due to its low fiscal exposure for the city and potential to retain the NHL franchise locally.[3] NHL Commissioner Gary Bettman publicly backed the plan, underscoring its role in stabilizing the Coyotes' operations amid prior arena instability in Glendale and preventing relocation that could deprive Arizona of professional hockey.[60] Advocates contended that the district's diversified revenue streams, including non-hockey events, would ensure long-term viability and community benefits like improved walkability and urban revitalization around Tempe Town Lake.[61]

Arguments in Opposition

Opponents of the proposed Tempe Entertainment District, which included a 16,500-seat arena for the Arizona Coyotes, argued that the project would impose significant fiscal burdens on the city without commensurate benefits. A fiscal analysis by the Grand Canyon Institute concluded that the net present value of projected visitor revenues over 30 years was $39 million, insufficient to cover the $104 million in costs associated with the Community Facilities District bonds, resulting in a $65 million net loss to Tempe.[47] Critics highlighted tax diversions, including half of sales taxes (0.9% of 1.8%), three-quarters of lodging taxes (3.75% of 5%), and 19.6% of property taxes for 30 years, which would reduce general fund growth by diverting $2.70 in revenue for every $1 gained.[47] Additionally, the arena was set for a 30-year property tax exemption, while other district facilities received eight-year exemptions, with visitors bearing excise taxes but developers benefiting from over $500 million in Government Property Lease Excise Tax breaks.[62] Grassroots groups like Tempe 1st contended that these incentives favored owner Alex Meruelo, who had a history of unpaid obligations including $1.3 million in Glendale taxes, raising doubts about fiscal responsibility.[63][62] Environmental and resource concerns centered on unsustainable water demands in Arizona's arid climate. The district was projected to consume up to 1.09 million gallons of water daily, including 12,000 to 15,000 gallons per ice sheet for arena operations, exacerbating regional shortages amid ongoing conservation efforts.[14] Opponents, including councilmember Lauren Kuby, noted the absence of a formal environmental assessment, potential greenhouse gas emissions from refrigerants, and disruptions such as dam relocation near Tempe Town Lake, which could conflict with remediation needs for the site's soil.[62][63] Traffic impacts were also criticized, with fears of congestion from up to 45 annual events plus 41 hockey games overwhelming local roads, despite a city-commissioned study claiming manageable flows.[3][64] Social and developmental arguments emphasized risks of gentrification and inadequate community benefits. The $2.1 billion project offered only $2 million toward affordable housing from the developer, likely driving up local prices in a market already strained, as stadium developments historically do.[62] Critics like Dawn Penich-Thacker argued for alternative land uses on the 46-acre public site that could yield better tax revenues without such distortions.[7] The negotiation process, conducted with limited public input and announced just before Thanksgiving 2022, fueled perceptions of opacity, with opponents viewing the May 16, 2023, referendumâ€”where all three propositions failed by margins up to 58%â€”as a rejection of subsidizing private sports interests over resident priorities.[62][3]

Voter Referendum and Results (May 2023)

In a special election held on May 16, 2023, Tempe voters considered three propositions to authorize the New Tempe Arena and a surrounding entertainment district on approximately 46 acres of city-owned land west of Tempe Town Lake.[65] The ballot measures arose from a citizen referendum after opponents collected over 3,000 valid signatures to challenge the Tempe City Council's unanimous approval of the development agreement in November 2022.[66][65]

Controversies and Legal Challenges

Environmental and Site-Specific Disputes

The proposed 46-acre site for the arena and entertainment district, situated at the northeast corner of Priest Drive and Rio Salado Parkway on city-owned land, comprised a former municipal landfill closed in the 1960s and subsequently used for compost and debris disposal, imposing an estimated $70 million environmental remediation liability on Tempe that developers committed to assuming as part of the lease terms.[68][69] This remediation, involving soil and groundwater cleanup for contaminants from historical waste, was positioned by supporters as converting a non-revenue-generating eyesore into a taxable asset, but opponents contested the site's suitability, noting that subsurface conditions remained unverified without excavation.[70] The site's brownfield designation amplified toxicity disputes, with critics arguing that decades of unregulated dumping likely harbored hazardous materials beneath visible rocks and debris, potentially complicating development and posing long-term health risks absent comprehensive pre-construction testing.[71] Proponents countered that remediation protocols, aligned with state environmental standards, would address identified pollutants like heavy metals or leachates, drawing from prior assessments that found no acute dangers but acknowledged legacy waste challenges typical of mid-20th-century landfills.[68] Water resource strains emerged as a focal environmental contention, with projections indicating the district's residential, commercial, and arena components could demand 1.09 million gallons daily from Central Arizona Project supplies, exacerbating concerns in a region facing chronic shortages and Gilbert's recent allocations cuts.[14] Opponents, including local advocacy groups, asserted this volume lacked sufficient impact modeling for aquifer drawdown or drought resilience, particularly given Arizona's reliance on Colorado River allocations.[72] Flood vulnerability constituted a core site-specific dispute, stemming from the parcel's adjacency to the Salt River channel (Rio Salado Parkway), a historically flood-prone dry wash subject to episodic overflows from upstream reservoirs or monsoons, as evidenced by prior inundations in the 1990s and recurrent risks mapped in Tempe's floodplain zones.[73] While developers pledged engineered barriers and drainage enhancements, skeptics highlighted the area's 100-year flood designation and potential for event-driven disruptions, arguing the elevated structures and impervious surfaces could worsen downstream flows without broader riparian mitigation.[74][62] These issues fueled referendum opposition, though no formal environmental litigation ensued prior to the May 2023 rejection.[63]

Intergovernmental Conflicts and Lawsuits

In March 2023, the City of Phoenix filed a lawsuit against the City of Tempe, alleging that the proposed Tempe Entertainment Districtâ€”intended to house the New Tempe Arena, retail spaces, and approximately 1,995 multi-family residential unitsâ€”violated a 1994 intergovernmental agreement restricting residential development within certain flight paths east of Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport to mitigate aircraft noise and potential litigation.[75] The agreement, established to protect airport operations and limit housing exposure to noise levels exceeding 65 decibels, prohibited multi-family residences in the affected zone, a condition Phoenix argued Tempe breached by approving the district's land use framework in February 2023.[76] The Arizona Coyotes' development entity, Bluebird District Development, responded in April 2023 by filing a $2.3 billion notice of claim against Phoenix in Maricopa County Superior Court, asserting that the lawsuit interfered with the project's entitlement value and seeking damages equivalent to the district's projected economic worth, including lost opportunities from the arena and ancillary developments.[77] Phoenix officials countered that their action solely enforced existing contractual obligations rather than targeting the arena itself, emphasizing the need to prevent noise-related lawsuits that could burden airport users.[78] This escalation highlighted tensions over land use authority between the neighboring municipalities, with Phoenix citing federal aviation regulations and historical agreements to justify its intervention.[79] The disputes persisted beyond the May 2023 referendum rejecting the project, but Phoenix and Tempe reached a settlement in December 2024, agreeing to dismiss all related lawsuits and establishing new guidelines for future development on the 46-acre site, including restrictions on residential density near flight corridors while allowing non-residential uses like commercial or recreational facilities.[80] [81] The agreement, signed by mayors Kate Gallego and Corey Woods, resolved decades of friction over Sky Harbor's surrounding properties without financial concessions, focusing instead on mutual commitments to Federal Aviation Administration compatibility standards.[82] Separately, the Arizona Attorney General's Office investigated Tempe in 2024 for potential violations of state open meeting laws during city council deliberations on the arena proposal, ruling in October that three private meetings in 2022 contravened public notice requirements but finding no improper use of resources to influence the referendum.[83] This state-level probe underscored oversight of local governance but did not result in further litigation or penalties beyond the advisory determination.[84]

Aftermath and Legacy

Impact on Arizona Coyotes Franchise

The rejection of the New Tempe Arena proposal by Tempe voters on May 16, 2023, exacerbated the Arizona Coyotes' long-standing arena instability, forcing the franchise to continue playing home games at the 5,000-seat Mullett Arena on Arizona State University's campus for the 2023â€“24 NHL season.[10][85] This temporary venue, shared with the college team, drew average attendance below 4,500 per game, contributing to financial strain and operational challenges for owner Alex Meruelo, who had invested in the franchise amid prior bankruptcies and relocations from Winnipeg in 1996.[85][86] Subsequent attempts by Meruelo to secure an alternative site in the Phoenix area, including a bid for state trust land in north Phoenix announced in June 2023, collapsed due to regulatory hurdles and lack of local government support, further diminishing the franchise's viability in Arizona.[85][86] The Tempe failure catalyzed negotiations that culminated in the NHL Board of Governors approving the $1.2 billion sale of the team's hockey operations to Utah Jazz owner Ryan Smith on April 18, 2024, relocating the active franchise to Salt Lake City for the 2024â€“25 season under the temporary name Utah Hockey Club, later rebranded as the Utah Mammoth in May 2025.[87][88][89] Meruelo retained the dormant Arizona Coyotes name, branding, and conditional rights to reactivate the franchise within five years if a suitable arena is built in Arizona, but he dissolved the remaining business operations in June 2024, effectively ending active NHL presence in the state.[88][90] This outcome marked the conclusion of nearly three decades of NHL hockey in Arizona, attributed by league officials and analysts to chronic mismanagement, inadequate facilities, and failure to secure public-private partnerships for a modern venue.[86][87] As of early 2025, Maricopa County officials expressed interest in exploring NHL expansion or reactivation to return professional hockey, though no concrete plans have materialized.[91]

Site Redevelopment and Current Status (2023â€“2025)

Following the rejection of the entertainment district proposal in the May 16, 2023, referendum, where Tempe voters opposed the development by a margin of approximately 53% to 47%, the city regained full control over the 46-acre site at the northeast corner of Priest Drive and Rio Salado Parkway.[67] [92] The Arizona Coyotes, who had pursued a long-term lease and development rights, abandoned their plans for the location, shifting focus to alternative sites elsewhere in the Phoenix metropolitan area.[82] This outcome left the publicly owned land, previously designated for an arena, hotels, retail, and apartments, available for alternative municipal priorities, with initial discussions centering on residential and mixed-use options to address housing needs.[93] Legal disputes immediately emerged, primarily between Tempe and neighboring Phoenix, which operates Sky Harbor International Airport adjacent to the site. Phoenix filed a lawsuit in March 2023, alleging that the proposed development violated a longstanding intergovernmental agreement restricting building heights and densities due to aircraft noise contours and safety concerns.[18] [80] Tempe countered that post-referendum planning would comply with aviation regulations, but the litigation stalled progress on any redevelopment. No construction occurred on the site throughout 2023 and most of 2024, as the city conducted environmental assessments and solicited developer interest while the courts deliberated.[94] On December 4 and 5, 2024, Phoenix and Tempe city councils unanimously approved a settlement resolving the lawsuits, explicitly permitting multifamily residential development on the parcel while imposing height limits averaging 65 feet and noise mitigation measures.[92] [82] This agreement cleared regulatory hurdles, enabling Tempe to advance requests for proposals from developers focused on apartments to alleviate regional housing shortages. As of early 2025, the site remains undeveloped, with city officials identifying it as a prime opportunity for housing amid broader revitalization efforts around Tempe Town Lake, though no specific groundbreaking dates or selected developers have been announced.[93] Nearby projects, such as the Modera Rio Salado apartments, have progressed toward occupancy in summer 2025, signaling momentum for residential growth in the vicinity but not directly on the former arena parcel.[95]
