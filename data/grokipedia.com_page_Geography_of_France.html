Territorial Extent

Metropolitan France


Metropolitan France refers to the European portion of France, comprising the continental mainland and the island of Corsica, along with adjacent islands in the Atlantic Ocean, English Channel, and Mediterranean Sea.[9] This territory excludes overseas departments and collectivities.[9] It accounts for about 82% of France's total land area.[10] The land area of Metropolitan France measures 543,940 square kilometers, establishing it as the largest country in the European Union by land area.[1] This surpasses Spain's 498,980 square kilometers and makes up the bulk of the EU's continental territory.[11] Corsica, with an area of 8,680 square kilometers, forms an integral region within this domain.[9] Positioned in Western Europe, Metropolitan France shares land borders with eight countries: Belgium and Luxembourg to the northeast, Germany, Switzerland, and Italy to the east, Monaco to the southeast, and Spain and Andorra to the south.[12] Its western and northwestern coasts front the Atlantic Ocean, while the southeastern coastline borders the Mediterranean Sea.[12] These boundaries total approximately 2,889 kilometers in length.[13] Metropolitan France spans latitudes from approximately 41Â° N in Corsica to 51Â° N in the north, and longitudes from about 5Â° W in Brittany to 10Â° E along the eastern frontier.[14] The geographic center lies near 46Â° N, 2Â° E.[14] This positioning places it within the temperate zone of the continent, influencing its role as a central hub in European geography.[14]

Geology and Relief

The geological foundation of Metropolitan France derives from the Variscan orogeny, a Late Paleozoic mountain-building event (approximately 370â€“300 million years ago) resulting from the collision of Biodiversity and Environment

Metropolitan France hosts diverse ecological systems spanning temperate broadleaf and mixed forests, Mediterranean scrublands, and alpine meadows, supporting over 85,000 known living species, including more than 11,900 plants, 43,700 animals, and 14,200 fungi.[35][36] These biomes feature dominant flora such as beech (Fagus sylvatica) and oak (Quercus spp.) in temperate zones covering approximately 32% of the land area, evergreen shrubs like maquis in the Mediterranean south, and herbaceous plants in high-altitude pastures above the treeline.[37] Fauna includes a high diversity of amphibians, birds, and mammals, with metropolitan France accounting for 40% of Europe's vascular plant species.[38] Endemism is concentrated in isolated regions like Corsica, which harbors over 130 plant species unique to the island and vertebrates such as the Corsican nuthatch (Sitta whiteheadi) and mouflon (Ovis gmelini musimon), alongside shared endemics in the Pyrenees including the Pyrenean desman (Galemys pyrenaicus).[39] Approximately 2,000 species are endemic to France overall, though around 2,500 introduced species pose competition and habitat alteration risks, with management efforts targeting invasives like the Asian hornet (Vespa velutina).[35][40] Protected areas encompass 21% of terrestrial territory, including 11 national parks such as the Vanoise and Ã‰crins, which enforce strict conservation, and the EU Natura 2000 network designating sites for 415 species and 133 habitats across about 20% of the land.[41] Historical deforestation peaked in the 19th century due to agricultural expansion, but reforestation initiatives and rural depopulation have reversed this trend, increasing forest cover by roughly 7% in the last three decades through natural regeneration and planting.[42] Agricultural intensification has reduced biodiversity in lowlands via habitat fragmentation and pesticide use, contrasting with resilient upland ecosystems.[43]

Natural Hazards

France experiences a range of natural hazards, primarily floods, storms, wildfires, avalanches, and seismic activity, with risks concentrated in specific regions due to its topography and climate variability.[54] Flooding is the most frequent and widespread threat, often triggered by intense Mediterranean episodes or Atlantic storms, affecting river basins across the country.[55] In October 2024, heavy rainfall led to flash floods impacting 380 municipalities in departments including ArdÃ¨che, RhÃ´ne, and Loire, resulting in declarations of natural disaster zones and widespread evacuations.[56] Historical records indicate cyclical patterns in flood severity, with events like the 1999 Storm Lothar causing 88 fatalities and damaging infrastructure across 79 departments through winds exceeding 150 km/h.[57] Wildfires pose significant risks in the Mediterranean south, exacerbated by dry summers and vegetation accumulation, with summer 2025 seeing an unprecedented blaze in the CorbiÃ¨res region burn 16,000 hectares in 24 hours, threatening vineyards and requiring 2,500 firefighters.[58] Avalanches occur annually in the Alps, where steep slopes and heavy snowfall lead to fatalities primarily among off-piste skiers and hikers; Geography of Overseas Territories

Physical Diversity

The overseas territories of France encompass a spectrum of landforms far more varied than the predominantly temperate plains, plateaus, and alpine ranges of metropolitan France. French Guiana, situated on the South American mainland, features low-lying coastal plains with mangroves and swamps transitioning to central savannas and southern forested highlands of the Tumuc-Humac range, part of the Precambrian Guiana Shield, with inselbergs reaching a maximum elevation of 851 meters at Bellevue de l'Inini.[65] This ancient, stable cratonic terrain supports dense tropical vegetation over 98% of its 83,534 kmÂ² area. Political Geography

Administrative Divisions

France's administrative structure divides the country into hierarchical units that align governance with geographic and cultural diversity. Metropolitan France consists of 13 regions, established through a 2016 reform that merged former regions to streamline administration while preserving local identities.[79] These regions oversee broader territorial planning, economic development, and transport, reflecting variations such as Brittany's Atlantic coastal features and Provence's Mediterranean landscapes.
