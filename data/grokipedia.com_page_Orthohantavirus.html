Orthohantavirus

Orthohantavirus is a genus of enveloped, single-stranded, negative-sense RNA viruses in the family Hantaviridae and order Bunyavirales, primarily hosted in rodents of the families Muridae and Cricetidae, with occasional spillover to humans causing severe zoonotic illnesses.[1][2][3] These viruses are transmitted to humans mainly through inhalation of aerosols containing urine, feces, or saliva from infected rodents, without evidence of sustained person-to-person spread.[4][5] In humans, orthohantaviruses induce two distinct syndromes: hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS), predominant in Eurasia and characterized by vascular leakage, renal failure, and hemorrhage, and hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS), prevalent in the Americas and marked by acute respiratory distress due to pulmonary edema.[4][6] Global incidence exceeds 200,000 cases annually, predominantly HFRS in Asia, with case-fatality rates ranging from 1-15% for HFRS and up to 38% for HPS, underscoring the viruses' public health impact despite the absence of specific antiviral therapies or widely available vaccines.[7][8] Defining characteristics include host-specific adaptation, with each virus maintaining persistent asymptomatic infections in its rodent reservoir, and pathogenesis driven by endothelial cell dysfunction rather than direct cytopathology.[2][6]

Taxonomy and Phylogeny

Classification and Nomenclature

Orthohantavirus is a genus within the family Hantaviridae, subfamily Mammantavirinae, order Bunyavirales, and realm Riboviria, as classified by the International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses (ICTV).[9][1] The genus encompasses single-stranded, negative-sense RNA viruses that primarily infect muroid rodents, soricid shrews, and talpid moles, with over 60 species recognized as of the 2024 ICTV taxonomy update.[1][10] The nomenclature "Orthohantavirus" was adopted by the ICTV to delineate rodent-borne hantaviruses from those infecting non-rodent hosts, such as bat-associated viruses now placed in separate genera like Merinoevirus.[11] This prefix, derived from the Greek orthÃ³s meaning "straight" or "correct," signifies the prototypical lineage historically grouped under the genus Hantavirus, which was established in 1980 following the isolation of Hantaan virus in 1978 from the striped field mouse (Apodemus agrarius) near the Hantan River in South Korea.[12][13] Prior to this reclassification, all hantaviruses fell under a single genus Hantavirus within the family Bunyaviridae, but phylogenetic analyses revealing distinct host-specific clades prompted the 2017 creation of the order Bunyavirales and subfamily restructuring, elevating Hantaviridae to family status and renaming the core genus.[12][11] Species names follow the ICTV's binomial format, combining the genus name with a species epithet, such as Hantaan orthohantavirus for the etiological agent of hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome in Asia or Sin Nombre orthohantavirus for the cause of hantavirus pulmonary syndrome in the Americas.[1][14] This standardization, formalized in ICTV guidelines around 2021 and refined through 2025, emphasizes host association and geographic origin in epithet selection while avoiding overlap with non-orthohantavirus lineages.[15] Family-level nomenclature retains "hantavirid" for members broadly, distinguishing it from the genus-specific "orthohantavirus" to reflect evolutionary divergence.[12]

Evolutionary Origins and Relationships

Orthohantaviruses have co-evolved with their reservoir hosts, primarily rodents from the orders Rodentia and Eulipotyphla, for over 100 million years, as indicated by molecular clock analyses aligning viral phylogenies with host divergence timelines around 90â€“100 million years ago during early placental mammal radiation.[16][17] This long-term association typically follows a "one virusâ€“one host" pattern, with 23 established and 30 provisional species hosted by 51 rodent, shrew, and mole species, supporting co-speciation driven by vertical transmission within host lineages.[16] However, strict co-divergence is challenged by documented host-switching eventsâ€”both ancient (e.g., cross-order jumps to shrews or moles) and recentâ€”and segmental reassortment, which introduce genetic diversity beyond host-specific evolution.[18][17] Phylogenetic analyses divide rodent-borne orthohantaviruses into major clades mirroring host subfamilies: an Old World lineage encompassing Murinae (e.g., Hantaan virus) and Arvicolinae (e.g., Puumala virus), and a New World lineage dominated by Sigmodontinae (e.g., Sin Nombre and Andes viruses) within Cricetidae.[17][18] Basal lineages, such as Thottapalayam virus from Asian shrews, suggest an Asian origin with posterior probability of 0.89, followed by independent dispersals to the Americas via shrews and rodents, likely through Beringian connections, fostering clade-specific radiations in novel host niches.[19] Genetic divergence rates (0.7 Ã— 10â»â· to 8.7 Ã— 10â»â· substitutions/site/year) and segment-specific phylogenies (e.g., 15â€“19% nucleotide differences in Puumala virus S segments between lineages) underscore this host-driven structuring, though reassortment affects up to 19% of genomes in some populations.[18] Beyond rodents, orthohantaviruses in shrews, moles, and bats reveal a polyphyletic distribution, implying ancestral breadth and potential for inter-order transmission, with non-rodent hosts exhibiting higher genetic diversity that predates rodent associations.[19] Recent diversification, such as New World clades emerging 500â€“600 years ago, highlights adaptive host jumps tied to ecological shifts, like post-glacial host isolation 23â€“28 thousand years ago for Eurasian lineages.[18] These dynamics refute a singular recent origin (e.g., ~2000 years ago in Asia) for all diversity, favoring a multifaceted model integrating ancient co-speciation with episodic horizontal transfers.[18][16]

Virology

Genome Organization and Structure

The genome of orthohantaviruses is composed of three distinct segments of single-stranded, negative-sense RNA, designated as large (L), medium (M), and small (S), with a total length of approximately 11â€“12 kb.[18][20] These segments are independently encapsidated by the nucleocapsid protein and packaged into enveloped virions.[21] The L segment measures 6.5â€“6.6 kb and encodes the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp), a ~250 kDa protein essential for viral transcription and replication.[20][22] The M segment, spanning 3.7â€“3.8 kb, contains a single open reading frame that translates into a polyprotein precursor, which undergoes cleavage to yield the envelope glycoproteins Gn (~50 kDa) and Gc (~50â€“65 kDa); these mediate viral attachment, entry, and assembly.[20][21] The S segment, the shortest at 1.8â€“2.1 kb, encodes the nucleocapsid protein N (~50 kDa), which binds genomic RNA to form helical ribonucleoprotein complexes and plays roles in replication, transcription, and immune evasion.[20][18] Each segment features untranslated regions (UTRs) at both termini, with partial sequence complementarity between the 5' and 3' ends enabling panhandle formation critical for polymerase recognition and genome packaging.[21] Unlike some other bunyaviruses, orthohantavirus genomes lack dedicated non-structural proteins such as NSs, relying on ambisense coding strategies minimally or not at all across species.[18] Segment lengths and coding capacities exhibit minor variations among orthohantavirus species, reflecting host adaptation and evolutionary divergence.[20]

Replication Cycle

Orthohantaviruses attach to host cells via their envelope glycoproteins Gn and Gc, which interact with receptors such as Î²3-integrins on endothelial or epithelial cells.[23] Entry proceeds through clathrin-dependent endocytosis or macropinocytosis, with low pH in endosomes triggering conformational changes in Gc to mediate fusion of viral and endosomal membranes, thereby releasing viral ribonucleoproteins (vRNPs)â€”consisting of the tripartite negative-sense RNA genome encapsidated by nucleocapsid (N) protein and associated with RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp, L protein)â€”into the cytoplasm.[23][24] Transcription occurs in the cytoplasm, where RdRp initiates mRNA synthesis by cap-snatching: the endonuclease activity of L cleaves 5' caps from host mRNAs (typically 8â€“17 nucleotides long), using them to prime complementary synthesis from the viral genomic RNA (vRNA) templates.[23][25] This process employs a prime-and-realign mechanism, in which initial non-templated nucleotides are added before the nascent chain realigns to the template for processive elongation, terminating at segment-specific signals (e.g., CCCACCC for the S segment).[25] The resulting capped, polyadenylated viral mRNAs are exported to host ribosomes for translation, yielding early N protein, glycoprotein precursor (from M segment, cleaved into Gn and Gc), and later RdRp.[24] N protein enhances translation efficiency and encapsidates nascent RNAs.[23] Replication follows transcription, with RdRp switching to synthesize full-length positive-sense antigenomic RNA (cRNA) de novo from vRNA templates, again via prime-and-realign initiation (starting with GTP alignment to the vRNA 3' end).[25] cRNA, encapsidated by N, then templates new vRNA.[24] Assembly occurs as vRNPs interact with Gn/Gc in the Golgi apparatus for Old World orthohantaviruses or at the plasma membrane for New World species; virions bud, acquiring a lipid envelope with embedded glycoproteins, and are released via exocytosis without significant cytopathic effects in reservoir hosts.[23] The entire cycle, from entry to release, typically spans 24â€“48 hours in permissive cells, with N protein regulating the balance between transcription and replication by modulating RdRp access.[25][24]

Ecology and Reservoirs

Natural Rodent Hosts

Orthohantaviruses are maintained as persistent, asymptomatic infections in specific rodent reservoir hosts, primarily within the families Muridae and Cricetidae, with each viral species demonstrating strict host specificity to one or a few closely related rodent species.[26][2] This co-evolutionary association results in rodents shedding infectious virus in urine, feces, and saliva without overt disease, facilitating zoonotic transmission to humans through aerosolized excreta.[6] In the Americas, orthohantaviruses linked to hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) are predominantly hosted by sigmodontine rodents of the subfamily Sigmodontinae. The Sin Nombre orthohantavirus, responsible for most U.S. HPS cases, is carried by the deer mouse (Peromyscus maniculatus), with prevalence in rodent populations correlating to human outbreaks in the southwestern United States as documented since 1993.[27][28] Other notable reservoirs include the rice rat (Oryzomys palustris) for Bayou virus in Louisiana and the southeastern U.S., and the cotton rat (Sigmodon hispidus) for Black Creek Canal virus in Florida.[29][28] Eurasian orthohantaviruses, primarily causing hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS), are associated with murid rodents. Hantaan virus circulates in the striped field mouse (Apodemus agrarius) across East Asia, while Seoul virus infects urban brown rats (Rattus norvegicus) globally, including in port cities.[30] Puumala virus, causing milder nephropathia epidemica in Europe, is hosted by the bank vole (Myodes glareolus), with infection rates in rodents fluctuating seasonally and tied to population densities.[31] Spillover infections to non-reservoir rodents occur rarely but do not sustain viral circulation.[26]

Environmental and Geographic Factors

The distribution of orthohantaviruses is closely tied to the geographic ranges of their specific rodent reservoir hosts, with viruses in the Old World (primarily Eurasia) circulating among murid rodents like the striped field mouse (Apodemus agrarius) and bank vole (Myodes glareolus), while New World viruses in the Americas are associated with sigmodontine rodents such as the deer mouse (Peromyscus maniculatus).[33][5] This host-specificity results in regional disease patterns, including higher incidence of hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) in temperate forests and agricultural areas of Asia and Europe, and hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) in arid and semi-arid grasslands of North and South America.[34][35] Climatic variables, including temperature, precipitation, and humidity, drive orthohantavirus ecology by influencing rodent population cycles and virus shedding rates, with warmer and wetter conditions often correlating with increased host densities and environmental persistence of viral particles in excreta.[33][36] For example, episodic heavy rainfall events promote vegetation proliferation, boosting seed production and thus rodent irruptions, which amplify orthohantavirus prevalence in local ecosystems.[37][38] Notable causal links exist between large-scale climate phenomena like El NiÃ±o-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) and outbreak dynamics; the 1993 HPS epidemic in the Four Corners region of the United States followed exceptional precipitation from the 1992â€“1993 El NiÃ±o, which increased deer mouse populations by up to 10-fold in affected habitats, leading to peak human cases in spring 1993.[37][39] Similar patterns have been observed in subsequent ENSO events, where anomalous warming and rainfall enhance reservoir abundance, though drought phases can concentrate rodents near human settlements, indirectly raising zoonotic risk.[40][41] Habitat alterations, including deforestation, agriculture intensification, and urbanization, further modulate transmission by fragmenting rodent habitats and increasing human-rodent interfaces, with studies showing prolonged epidemics in urbanized areas due to sustained high rodent densities in peridomestic environments.[42][43] Abiotic factors such as soil moisture and vegetation cover also play roles, as evidenced by ecological niche models identifying arid grasslands and shrublands as high-risk zones for HPS in South America based on reservoir distributions.[44] Overall, these factors underscore a causal chain from climatic forcing to host demography to heightened spillover potential, independent of human behavioral changes.[36][38]

Transmission

Zoonotic Transmission to Humans

Orthohantaviruses are transmitted to humans primarily through the inhalation of aerosolized viral particles from the urine, feces, or saliva of infected rodent reservoir hosts.[45] This aerosolization occurs when dried excreta or contaminated nesting materials are disturbed, such as during cleaning of infested areas or activities that raise dust in rodent habitats.[46] The viruses remain infectious in rodent excreta for days to weeks under favorable environmental conditions, facilitating indirect exposure without direct contact with live animals.[47] Secondary routes of zoonotic transmission include rodent bites, which can introduce the virus directly into tissues, though this is rare compared to aerosol inhalation.[48] Contamination of mucous membranes or abraded skin with fresh excreta or saliva may also lead to infection, particularly in occupational settings like farming or rodent control where handling occurs.[49] Transmission via contaminated food or water is possible but uncommon and typically requires heavy contamination in enclosed environments.[48] Risk factors for human infection are heightened in endemic regions through activities involving rodent exposure, such as cleaning barns, cabins, or storage areas with infestations; outdoor pursuits like hiking or trapping; and occupations involving peridomestic rodents.[50] Globally, an estimated 150,000â€“200,000 human cases occur annually, predominantly in Asia and Europe for Old World orthohantaviruses causing hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome, and in the Americas for New World species causing hantavirus pulmonary syndrome.[51] No evidence supports arthropod vectors or fomites as significant transmission modes; rodent-to-human contact remains the causal pathway.[18]

Rare Human-to-Human Cases

Human-to-human transmission of orthohantaviruses is exceedingly rare and has been documented exclusively for Andes virus (ANDV), a South American species primarily associated with hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) in Argentina and Chile.[52][53] Epidemiological investigations, supported by genetic sequencing of viral strains from linked cases, indicate that transmission occurs through close interpersonal contact, likely via respiratory droplets or fomites during the prodromal phase of illness, when patients exhibit fever and mild symptoms before severe respiratory distress.[54][55] The first confirmed instances of person-to-person spread were reported in 1996 in El Bolson, Argentina, where genetic analysis of ANDV isolates from a primary case and secondary household contacts revealed identical sequences, ruling out independent rodent exposures. Subsequent clusters in Patagonia, including a 2002-2003 outbreak in Chile with 11 secondary cases traced to index patients via household and community contacts, further substantiated this mode, with attack rates among close contacts reaching up to 20-30% in some familial settings.[54] A prominent superspreader event occurred in EpuyÃ©n, Argentina, starting November 2, 2018, involving 34 confirmed HPS cases in a community of approximately 4,000, where genomic epidemiology identified chains of transmission up to four generations deep, with 75% of cases linked person-to-person rather than via rodents.[55][56] In contrast, no credible evidence supports human-to-human transmission for other orthohantaviruses, such as Sin Nombre orthohantavirus in North America. Serological surveys of over 250 healthcare workers exposed to HPS patients in the 1993 Four Corners outbreak found zero secondary infections, despite direct contact with bodily fluids.[57] Similarly, global surveillance data from the CDC and WHO report no verified interpersonal spread outside ANDV-endemic regions, emphasizing that orthohantavirus infections remain predominantly zoonotic.[58] While some systematic reviews have questioned the strength of ANDV transmission evidence due to potential confounding by shared rodent exposures, the convergence of molecular, contact-tracing, and temporal data from multiple independent outbreaks affirms its occurrence, albeit limited to intimate, prolonged contacts.[59][60]

Diseases and Pathogenesis

Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome (HFRS)

Hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) is an acute zoonotic illness primarily caused by Old World orthohantaviruses, including Hantaan virus (HTNV), Seoul virus (SEOV), Dobrava-Belgrade virus (DOBV), and Puumala virus (PUUV), which are maintained in rodent reservoirs such as the striped field mouse (Apodemus agrarius) for HTNV and bank vole (Myodes glareolus) for PUUV.[61] [62] Transmission to humans occurs mainly via inhalation of aerosolized virus from rodent urine, feces, or saliva, with rare cases from bites or contaminated objects; incubation typically lasts 1 to 2 weeks, ranging up to 8 weeks.[63] [61] The disease progresses through five distinct phases: a febrile phase lasting 3 to 7 days, characterized by sudden high fever (often >39Â°C), intense headache, myalgia, back and abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and flushed face with conjunctival injection; a hypotensive phase of hours to 2 days involving vascular leakage, hypotension, tachycardia, and hemorrhagic signs such as petechiae, ecchymoses, and gastrointestinal bleeding; an oliguric phase of 3 to 7 days with acute kidney injury (AKI), marked by oliguria (<400 mL/day urine output), azotemia (elevated serum creatinine >2 mg/dL), proteinuria, and hematuria; a diuretic phase lasting several days to weeks with polyuria (>2 L/day) and gradual resolution of renal dysfunction; and a convalescent phase of 3 to 6 months for full recovery, though fatigue and proteinuria may persist.[64] [61] Laboratory findings include initial leukocytosis (>10,000/Î¼L), thrombocytopenia (<100,000/Î¼L), and hemoconcentration, followed by renal markers like elevated blood urea nitrogen and creatinine.[63] Severity varies by causative virus: HTNV and DOBV induce severe forms with case fatality rates (CFR) of 5% to 15%, often requiring renal replacement therapy (RRT) in up to 30% of cases, while PUUV causes milder nephropathia epidemica (NE) with CFR <1% and RRT in ~5%; overall CFR ranges from 0.4% to 15%.[62] [61] Pathogenesis involves immune-mediated endothelial dysfunction rather than direct viral cytopathology, with hantaviruses infecting vascular endothelial cells via Î²3 integrins, particularly in renal glomeruli and tubules, triggering a cytokine storm (e.g., TNF-Î±, IL-6, type I interferons) that upregulates vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and bradykinin, disrupting adherens junctions (e.g., VE-cadherin) and causing plasma leakage without necrosis.[64] [61] This leads to thrombocytopenia from platelet consumption and activation on infected endothelium, consumptive coagulopathy with fibrinogen depletion, and interstitial nephritis from CD8+ T-cell infiltration, resulting in AKI through reduced glomerular filtration and tubular injury; renal medullary endothelial cells show highest viral loads, correlating with proteinuria severity.[64] Hypotension arises from hypovolemia due to third-space fluid shifts, while hemorrhagic diathesis stems from endothelial damage and platelet dysfunction rather than disseminated intravascular coagulation in most cases.[61] Long-term sequelae, such as chronic kidney disease, are rare (<1%), primarily in severe HTNV infections.[64]

Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS)


Hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) is a severe, acute respiratory illness caused by orthohantaviruses endemic to the Americas, most notably Sin Nombre orthohantavirus in North America and Andes orthohantavirus in South America.[65] These viruses primarily infect via inhalation of aerosolized rodent excreta, leading to a disease characterized by rapid progression from flu-like symptoms to life-threatening pulmonary edema and shock.[66] The case-fatality rate stands at approximately 38%, with deaths occurring mainly within 24-48 hours of the onset of cardiopulmonary symptoms.[4] The clinical course unfolds in distinct phases. The prodromal phase, lasting 3-6 days after an incubation period of 7-39 days, features fever, intense myalgias, headache, nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain, often mimicking influenza or other viral infections.[65] This is followed by the cardiopulmonary phase, where patients develop cough, shortness of breath, tachycardia, and hypotension, culminating in non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema, hypoxemia, and respiratory failure; approximately 40% of cases require mechanical ventilation.[66] A minority progress to a convalescent phase with gradual recovery, though some survivors report persistent fatigue and dyspnea.[65] Pathogenesis centers on infection and replication within pulmonary capillary endothelial cells, triggering an aberrant immune response rather than direct viral cytopathology.[65] This induces a cytokine storm, including elevated levels of interleukin-6 and other mediators, which disrupts endothelial barrier function and increases vascular permeability, resulting in plasma leakage into the alveolar spaces without substantial inflammation or tissue necrosis.[67] Hantaviruses sensitize endothelial cells to vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), amplifying permeability via enhanced VEGFR2 signaling, while the endothelium remains intact morphologically.[68] Autopsy findings confirm minimal lung damage beyond edema, underscoring the role of immune-mediated vascular leak in driving mortality.[66]

Underlying Mechanisms


Orthohantaviruses infect primarily microvascular endothelial cells, utilizing receptors such as Î²3-integrins and protocadherin-1 for entry, without causing direct cytopathic effects or apoptosis in these cells.[69][70] Viral replication occurs productively in endothelial cells, leading to persistent infection, but pathogenesis arises from dysregulated host immune responses rather than viral burden alone.[69] In humans, this manifests as capillary leak syndrome, characterized by increased vascular permeability and organ-specific edema, contrasting with asymptomatic persistent infections in rodent reservoirs where regulatory immune mechanisms, including Tregs and anti-inflammatory cytokines like TGF-Î², prevent pathology.[69][71] The immune-mediated damage involves a robust CD8+ T cell response that correlates with disease onset and severity, peaking during the critical phase of hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) or hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS).[71] These cytotoxic T cells, alongside CD4+ T cells exhibiting Th1/Th2 profiles, release proinflammatory cytokines such as TNF-Î±, IL-6, IFN-Î³, and IL-1Î², contributing to a cytokine storm that impairs endothelial barrier function.[71][70] Elevated IL-6 levels, in particular, strongly associate with fatal outcomes in HPS (case fatality rate 20-40%) and severe HFRS (0.1-15%).[71] Endothelial dysfunction results from multiple pathways, including internalization of vascular endothelial cadherin (VE-cadherin) and downregulation of tight junction proteins like ZO-1 and occludin, which disrupt adherens junctions and increase paracellular permeability.[70] Hantavirus infection sensitizes endothelial cells to vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), promoting actin reorganization and nitric oxide production, while activation of the kallikrein-kinin system generates bradykinin, further exacerbating leakage.[69][70] Complement activation, evidenced by elevated C3a, C5a, and membrane attack complex, alongside immunothrombosis involving platelet and neutrophil adhesion via integrins, amplifies vascular injury and thrombocytopenia.[70] In HFRS-causing Old World hantaviruses, renal glomerular and tubular endothelial tropism predominates, leading to proteinuria and acute kidney injury, whereas New World viruses like Sin Nombre preferentially target pulmonary endothelium, causing non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema in HPS.[69] Despite these differences, the core mechanism remains immune-driven endothelial hyperpermeability without widespread necrosis.[69][71]

Epidemiology

Global Distribution and Incidence

Orthohantaviruses exhibit a global distribution tied to the geographic ranges of their rodent reservoir hosts, with over 40 recognized species or strains identified across continents. In Eurasia and Africa, Old World hantaviruses such as Hantaan virus (HTNV), Seoul virus (SEOV), and Puumala virus (PUUV) predominate, primarily causing hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS). These are associated with murid rodents like the striped field mouse (Apodemus agrarius) for HTNV in East Asia, the brown rat (Rattus norvegicus) for SEOV with cosmopolitan urban spread, and the bank vole (Myodes glareolus) for PUUV in Europe. In the Americas, New World hantaviruses including Sin Nombre virus (SNV) in North America and Andes virus (ANDV) in South America cause hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS), hosted by sigmodontine rodents such as the deer mouse (Peromyscus maniculatus) and long-tailed rice rat (Oligoryzomys longicaudatus), respectively.[72][73][74] Human incidence exceeds 200,000 cases annually worldwide, predominantly HFRS in Asia and Europe, though underreporting is common due to diagnostic limitations and mild cases. In Asia, China reports 9,000â€“12,000 HFRS cases yearly, mainly from HTNV and SEOV, with historical peaks exceeding 100,000 cases in the 1980s but sustained endemic transmission; case-fatality rates (CFR) range from 1â€“15%. Europe sees 10,000â€“20,000 HFRS cases annually, largely nephropathia epidemica from PUUV in Scandinavia, Finland, and Central Europe, with lower CFR (<1%) and seasonal peaks in summer. The Americas report fewer HPS cases, totaling around 1,000 confirmed annually across North and South America, with higher CFR (30â€“50%); the United States has documented 864 cases from 1993 through 2022, concentrated in the Southwest and linked to SNV.[75][76][77] Sporadic cases occur elsewhere, including Africa where SEOV circulates in rodents but human infections remain poorly documented, and emerging detections in regions like Panama with 17 cases in 2022. Incidence fluctuates with rodent population dynamics influenced by climate and land use, leading to outbreaks; for instance, U.S. cases averaged 20â€“30 yearly post-1993 Four Corners outbreak, while South American ANDV transmission shows human-to-human potential absent in most strains. Global surveillance gaps persist, particularly in non-endemic areas, potentially underestimating true burden.[77][78][79]

Major Outbreaks and Recent Trends

One of the earliest documented large-scale outbreaks of orthohantavirus-associated hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) occurred during the Korean War from 1951 to 1954, affecting approximately 3,200 United Nations soldiers, primarily due to Hantaan virus exposure in rodent-infested areas.[80] This event marked a significant early recognition of hantavirus pathogenicity in humans outside endemic regions. In the Americas, the 1993 Four Corners outbreak in the southwestern United States, caused by Sin Nombre orthohantavirus, represented the first identified cluster of hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS), with initial cases linked to increased deer mouse populations following environmental changes; it resulted in 48 confirmed cases and heightened global awareness of New World hantaviruses.[81] [82] Endemic outbreaks persist in Asia, particularly China, where HFRS cases have been reported since 1931 and now account for over half of the estimated 15,000â€“200,000 annual global HFRS incidents, driven by rodent reservoirs like the striped field mouse.[83] [75] In South America, outbreaks of Andes orthohantavirus, notable for rare documented human-to-human transmission, have occurred periodically, such as in El BolsÃ³n, Argentina, in 1995â€“1996, with clusters exceeding 20 cases in localized areas.[72] A smaller but significant cluster happened in Yosemite National Park, USA, in 2012, where 10 HPS cases, three fatal, were traced to rodent-infested tent cabins, representing the largest U.S. outbreak since 1993.[84] Recent trends indicate stable but persistent low-level incidence in the Americas and Europe, with the United States reporting 864 cumulative HPS/HFRS cases from 1993 through 2022, averaging 20â€“30 annually, often sporadic and tied to rodent exposure in rural or peridomestic settings.[79] In Europe, notification rates for hantavirus infections reached historic lows in 2023, similar to 2020, with fluctuations linked to rodent population dynamics rather than broad increases.[85] Globally, annual cases remain estimated at 200,000, predominantly HFRS in Asia, though emerging concerns include potential amplification from climate-driven rodent surges and urban spread of Seoul orthohantavirus; isolated U.S. cases, such as Bayou virus in Louisiana (2020â€“2023) and a 2021 Michigan incident, underscore ongoing phylogeographic risks without evidence of widespread resurgence.[75] [29] [46] Orthohantaviruses are classified as emerging with pandemic potential due to high case-fatality rates (up to 40% for HPS) and similarities to severe respiratory illnesses, though no major post-2012 outbreaks have exceeded historical scales.[86]

Diagnosis, Treatment, and Prevention

Diagnostic Approaches

Diagnosis of orthohantavirus infections relies primarily on laboratory confirmation, as clinical symptoms overlap with other febrile illnesses such as influenza, leptospirosis, or dengue. Early diagnosis within the first 72 hours post-symptom onset is challenging due to low viremia levels before antibody responses develop.[4] Exposure history to rodent-infested environments is crucial for suspicion, but definitive identification requires serological or molecular testing.[87] Serological assays detect virus-specific immunoglobulin M (IgM) and immunoglobulin G (IgG) antibodies, which typically appear 4â€“10 days after infection. Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) is the standard method for acute diagnosis, with IgM capture ELISA used by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) to confirm hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) or hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS).[66] IgG ELISA distinguishes past from active infections and identifies the specific orthohantavirus species via neutralization assays or immunofluorescence.[88] Cross-reactivity among orthohantaviruses necessitates confirmatory tests like plaque reduction neutralization for species differentiation.[89] Reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) targets viral RNA in blood, plasma, or tissues, offering high sensitivity during the acute viremic phase before seroconversion.[90] Quantitative RT-PCR on whole blood or autopsy specimens (e.g., lung, kidney) detects New World orthohantaviruses, aiding rapid diagnosis in fatal cases.[46] Semi-nested RT-PCR enhances specificity for confirmation, particularly in regions like Brazil where serological tests may lag.[91] Virus isolation in cell culture is rarely performed due to biosafety level 3 requirements and low yield.[65] In postmortem examinations, immunohistochemistry on formalin-fixed tissues visualizes viral antigens, supporting epidemiological investigations.[87] The same diagnostic approaches apply to both HPS and HFRS, though regional variations in testing availability can lead to underdiagnosis; for instance, inconsistent U.S. state protocols have been noted as a barrier.[87][92]

Clinical Management and Therapeutics

No specific antiviral treatment for orthohantavirus infections has been approved by regulatory authorities such as the FDA or EMA, with clinical management relying primarily on supportive care to address organ dysfunction and complications.[87] Patients require prompt hospitalization, often in an intensive care unit (ICU), with monitoring for multi-organ failure, including cardiovascular collapse and coagulopathy.[66] Early intervention improves outcomes, as progression to severe phasesâ€”such as capillary leak syndrome in hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) or oliguric renal failure in hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS)â€”can occur rapidly, within days of symptom onset.[93] For HPS, predominant in the Americas and caused by viruses like Sin Nombre orthohantavirus, treatment focuses on respiratory and hemodynamic support. Mechanical ventilation is essential for acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), which affects up to 75% of cases and drives the 38% case-fatality rate.[66] Fluid resuscitation must be cautious to avoid exacerbating pulmonary edema, with vasopressors used for hypotension; extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) has been employed in refractory cases, correlating with survival rates exceeding 50% in small cohorts when initiated early.[93] Antivirals like ribavirin show limited efficacy against New World hantaviruses, with randomized trials demonstrating no significant mortality benefit and potential toxicity.[94] In HFRS, prevalent in Eurasia from Old World orthohantaviruses such as Hantaan and Puumala, management emphasizes renal support alongside general measures. Intravenous fluids correct hypovolemia during the hypotensive phase, while hemodialysis addresses acute kidney injury in severe cases, which occurs in up to 50% of Hantaan infections.[95] Ribavirin, administered intravenously (e.g., 33 mg/kg loading dose followed by 16 mg/kg every 6 hours for 4 days, then tapered), reduces mortality from 10-15% to under 5% when started within 5 days of fever onset, based on meta-analyses of clinical data from endemic regions like China and Korea; however, its use is not universally recommended due to teratogenicity and anemia risks, and evidence is derived from non-randomized studies.[96] [95] Experimental therapeutics remain investigational, with monoclonal antibodies showing promise in neutralizing multiple orthohantaviruses in animal models but lacking human trial data as of 2025.[97] Combinations of ribavirin with agents like favipiravir exhibit synergistic antiviral effects in vitro against Hantaan virus, yet clinical translation is pending.[98] Overall, outcomes depend on rapid recognition and ICU capabilities, underscoring the absence of curative options.[99]

Control Measures and Public Health Strategies

Rodent control remains the primary strategy for preventing orthohantavirus transmission, as these viruses are maintained in rodent reservoirs and spread to humans via inhalation of aerosolized excreta, without sustained human-to-human spread. Effective measures include sealing building entry points larger than 1/4 inch with steel wool or metal flashing, removing food sources and nesting materials, and using snap traps to capture rodents, which avoids the secondary hazards of poisons or glue traps that may increase contamination risks. [58] [100] Habitat modifications, such as clearing brush and storing firewood away from dwellings, further reduce rodent proximity to human environments. [27] Cleanup of infested areas requires personal protective equipment including rubber gloves and N95 respirators, followed by ventilation for at least 30 minutes, and application of a 10% bleach solution or EPA-registered disinfectant to saturate urine, droppings, nests, and carcasses for 5-10 minutes prior to removal, thereby inactivating the virus and preventing aerosol generation. [101] These protocols, emphasized by public health agencies, have been associated with reduced incidence in occupational and residential settings where implemented. [58] Public health surveillance integrates case reporting through systems like the U.S. National Notifiable Diseases Surveillance System, which documented 864 hantavirus cases from 1993 to 2022, enabling early detection and response. [79] [102] In regions with hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS), inactivated vaccines targeting Hantaan and Seoul viruses, deployed in China since 1995 and South Korea, provide protective efficacy of approximately 86% against severe disease in vaccinated high-risk groups, though boosters are needed due to waning immunity. [103] [104] No licensed vaccine exists for hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) in the Americas, underscoring reliance on non-pharmaceutical interventions. [20] Outbreak responses incorporate ecological monitoring of rodent populations, influenced by climate factors like El NiÃ±o events that drive population booms, to inform targeted education campaigns and resource allocation in endemic areas. [75] [31] Community-level strategies prioritize awareness in rural and occupational groups, such as farmers and cleaners, where exposure risks are elevated. [58]

History and Research Developments

Discovery and Early Investigations

The recognition of diseases now known to be caused by orthohantaviruses dates to outbreaks of hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) during the Korean War (1950â€“1953), which affected approximately 3,200 United Nations troops with symptoms including fever, hemorrhage, hypotension, and renal failure, yielding a case-fatality rate of 5â€“15%.[12] These cases were concentrated among soldiers near the Hantan River in South Korea, prompting epidemiological investigations that implicated rodent exposure but failed to identify a viral agent despite extensive efforts, including attempts to propagate the pathogen in laboratory animals and cell cultures.[105] In 1978, South Korean virologist Ho Wang Lee and collaborators isolated the etiologic agent of Korean HFRS from lung tissues of the striped field mouse (Apodemus agrarius), the primary reservoir, as well as from blood and urine of acutely ill patients.[106] Named Hantaan virus after the riverine focus of the war-era outbreak, this enveloped RNA virus was propagated in Vero E6 cells and confirmed via immunofluorescence using convalescent patient sera, establishing its antigenic distinctiveness from other known pathogens.[107] This breakthrough, published in the Journal of Infectious Diseases, resolved a 25-year mystery and facilitated serological surveys revealing HFRS's endemicity in Asia, with retrospective links to earlier European cases of nephropathia epidemica.[108] Early post-isolation studies emphasized rodent-to-human transmission via aerosolized excreta, ruling out direct interpersonal spread, and classified the virus within the Bunyaviridae family (later elevated to Hantaviridae).[12] Investigations expanded rapidly in the 1980s, identifying additional Old World orthohantaviruses such as Puumala virus (isolated in 1979 from bank voles in Finland, causing milder nephropathia epidemica) and Seoul virus (from urban rats in Korea and later globally).[12] These efforts involved trapping rodents for virus isolation, genetic sequencing precursors, and mapping reservoir distributions, underscoring host specificityâ€”each virus tied to a single rodent genusâ€”and ecological drivers like population booms post-rice harvesting.[105] The discovery of New World orthohantaviruses occurred in May 1993 amid an outbreak of acute respiratory distress in the Four Corners region of the southwestern United States, initially dubbed the "Navajo flu" but later termed hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS).[82] CDC investigations linked casesâ€”starting with a fatal young Navajo couple in March 1993â€”to exposure to deer mice (Peromyscus maniculatus), from which Sin Nombre virus was isolated within months via lung tissue culturing and confirmed by PCR and sequencing, revealing genetic divergence from Eurasian strains yet shared bunyavirus traits.[46] Early probes highlighted environmental factors, including El NiÃ±o-driven rodent population surges from increased precipitation, and established HPS's high fatality (around 38% initially) due to pulmonary capillary leak rather than renal dominance.[82] This spurred global surveillance for rodent-borne hantaviruses, confirming their presence in the Americas from Canada to Argentina.[109]

Advances in Vaccines and Pathogenicity Studies

Vaccine development against orthohantaviruses has progressed through inactivated, subunit, and nucleic acid platforms, though no universally approved vaccine exists due to challenges in achieving durable, cross-protective immunity across diverse serotypes. Inactivated whole-virus vaccines, such as Hantavax for Hantaan orthohantavirus (HTNV), were developed in the 1980s and deployed in endemic regions like Korea and China, providing initial protection rates of 80-90% that wane after 6-12 months, necessitating annual boosters.[20] Nucleic acid vaccines, including DNA and mRNA candidates, have shown promise in preclinical models by inducing robust T-cell and neutralizing antibody responses; for instance, DNA vaccines encoding HTNV and Puumala orthohantavirus (PUUV) glycoproteins advanced to phase 1 clinical trials in 2024, demonstrating safety and immunogenicity in humans with detectable neutralizing antibodies post-vaccination.[110] [20] Recombinant viral vector vaccines, like modified vaccinia Ankara expressing nucleoprotein (rMVAHantaNP), protected against Seoul (SEOV) and HTNV challenge in animal models, highlighting potential for multivalent formulations.[111] Pathogenicity research has advanced through improved cellular and animal models, revealing that orthohantaviruses primarily infect vascular endothelial cells without causing cytopathic effects, with disease driven by immune-mediated vascular leakage rather than direct lysis. Old World orthohantaviruses (e.g., HTNV, PUUV) exhibit tropism for renal endothelium, correlating with hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) via upregulated VEGF and disrupted adherens junctions, while New World species (e.g., Sin Nombre orthohantavirus, SNV) preferentially target pulmonary vasculature, leading to hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) through endothelial dysfunction and cytokine storms.[112] [21] Recent human lung xenograft mouse models have confirmed permissiveness to multiple orthohantavirus species, enabling evaluation of viral dissemination and immune responses in vivo, which traditional rodent models inadequately replicate due to asymptomatic infections in reservoirs.[113] Studies on entry mechanisms emphasize Î²3-integrin and protocadherin-1 as key receptors, with variations influencing tissue tropism and severity; disruptions in these pathways underscore causal roles in capillary permeability without widespread inflammation.[114] Innate immunity investigations highlight type I interferon deficiencies in severe cases, linking impaired antiviral signaling to unchecked replication and exacerbated immunopathology.[115] These findings inform targeted interventions, though gaps persist in reverse genetics systems for full viral lifecycle manipulation.[21]
