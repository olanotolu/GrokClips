Anwar Shah Kashmiri

Anwar Shah Kashmiri (November 1875 – May 1933) was an Islamic scholar of Kashmiri descent, recognized as one of the foremost muhaddiths (experts in hadith) of the early 20th-century Indian subcontinent, particularly within the Deobandi school of Sunni thought.[1][2][3] Born in the village of Dudwan in Kashmir's Lolab Valley to a pious scholarly family tracing descent from early Muslim migrants, he began Quranic recitation and basic Islamic sciences under his father, Muazam Ali Shah, from age five.[1][3] By his early teens, Kashmiri pursued studies in Hazara (now in Pakistan), Gangoh, and Gujarat, before enrolling at Darul Uloom Deoband in 1894, where he completed the canonical hadith corpus—Sahih al-Bukhari, Sunan al-Tirmidhi, and others—under mentors like Mahmud al-Hasan.[1][2] Appointed Shaykh al-Hadith at Deoband in 1908, he taught major hadith compilations such as Sahih Muslim, Sunan al-Nasa'i, and Sunan Ibn Majah for over two decades, innovating pedagogical methods that emphasized analytical depth and cross-referencing with earlier authorities like Shah Waliullah Dehlawi.[1][2] His prodigious memory allowed verbatim recitation of texts with precise page references, earning him acclaim as the "modern muhaddith."[3] Kashmiri also founded madrasas, including Faiz-e-Aam in Kashmir (1903) and Jamia Islamia in Dhabel, Gujarat, while authoring works like Anwar al-Mahmud fi Sharh Sunan Abi Dawud (a two-volume commentary on Sunan Abu Dawud), Mushkilat al-Qur'an (on Quranic difficulties), and Ikfar al-Mulhidin (refuting Ahmadiyya claims).[2][3] Among his defining influences were training luminaries such as Mufti Muhammad Shafi Usmani, Idris Kandhlawi, and Mufti Mahmud, who credited his methods for advancing hadith preservation amid colonial-era challenges to Islamic learning.[1][2] Kashmiri's efforts fortified orthodox Hanafi-Maturidi scholarship against heterodoxies, leaving a legacy of rigorous textual fidelity that shaped subsequent generations of South Asian ulama until his death in Deoband, where he was buried.[1][3]

Biography

Early Life and Education

Anwar Shah Kashmiri was born in November 1875 in the village of Dudwan in the Lolab Valley of Kashmir to Mawlana Muazam Ali Shah, a local religious scholar.[3][1] His family traced its scholarly lineage to the seventh generation through Baba Amjad Sheikh Masood Narwari, a Mughal-era migrant from Srinagar known for Islamic learning and service at shrines.[3] By age six, he had completed Quranic recitation under his father's guidance, followed by foundational studies in Arabic syntax, morphology, Persian, and basic Islamic jurisprudence from local teachers including Molvi Abdul Jabbar and Molvi Ghulam Mohammad.[3][1] Around age thirteen, Kashmiri relocated to the Hazara Division for three to four years of advanced instruction in metaphysics and theology at local madrasas.[3][1] In 1893 (1311 AH), at approximately age eighteen, he enrolled at Darul Uloom Deoband, where he pursued formal Islamic higher education, focusing on key Hadith collections such as Sahih al-Bukhari and Sunan al-Tirmidhi.[4][1] His primary teachers at Deoband included Shaykh al-Hind Mahmud al-Hasan, Muhaddith Khalil Ahmad Saharanpuri, and Mawlana Ishaq Amritsari, among others who delivered lectures on advanced texts.[1] Kashmiri completed his studies and received a sanad (certification) in fiqh, Hadith, and tafsir in 1896 (1314 AH) from Rashid Ahmad Gangohi.[3] He then proceeded to Gangoh for specialized training in Hadith sciences and spiritual bay'ah under Gangohi, marking the culmination of his formative education.[3][1]

Family and Personal Background

Anwar Shah Kashmiri was born on 27 Shawwāl 1292 AH (corresponding to 16 October 1875) in the village of Dhoodwan (also recorded as Dudwan or Wudwan) in the Lolab Valley of Kashmir, into a pious Sayyid family tracing its lineage to Baghdad, with ancestors fleeing Mongol invasions in 1258 CE and settling in Kashmir via Multan and Lahore.[5][3] He was the second son among six sons of his parents, Muʿazzam Shah (also known as Pīr Muḥammad Muʿazzam Shāh), a devout scholar and adherent of the Suhrawardīyyah Sufi order, and his mother, Maal Deedi (or Maalded), noted for her piety; the couple also had two daughters.[5][3] Kashmiri was the seventh-generation descendant of Bābā Amjad Sheikh Masʿūd Narwarī, a figure from Srinagar's Narwara locality who migrated to the Lolab Valley during the Mughal era.[3] Kashmiri maintained celibacy until the age of 44, marrying in 1919 (some accounts note 1918) a woman from a Sayyid family in Gangoh, with the arrangement facilitated by his colleague Mawlānā Ḥabīb al-Raḥmān Ludhyānawī.[5][3] The couple had five children between 1919 and 1933, including three sons who became scholars: the eldest, Azhar Shāh Qaysar; a middle son; and the youngest, Muḥammad Anzar Shāh Kashmīrī, who later served as Shaykh al-Ḥadīth at Dār al-ʿUlūm Deoband Waqf.[5]

Scholarly Career

Teaching Positions and Institutions

Anwar Shah Kashmiri commenced his formal teaching career at Darul Uloom Deoband in 1908, following his completion of advanced studies there under mentors including Mahmud Hasan Deobandi.[1] Appointed by Shaykh al-Hind Mahmud Hasan as Sadr Mudarris (head lecturer) and Shaykh ul-Hadith, he delivered lectures on core Hadith compilations such as Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, Sunan al-Tirmidhi, Sunan al-Nasa'i, and Sunan Ibn Majah.[1] [6] His tenure as Shaykh al-Hadith lasted nearly thirteen years, during which he trained approximately 2,000 scholars in Hadith sciences amid the seminary's rigorous curriculum.[6] [1] Internal disputes at Deoband, including opposition to certain administrative or ideological shifts, prompted his resignation and relocation in 1927 to Jamia Islamia Talimuddin in Dabhel, Gujarat, alongside figures like Azizur Rahman Usmani.[1] [2] There, he assumed a prominent teaching role focused on Hadith, contributing to the institution's development as a center for traditional scholarship and serving for five years until health concerns necessitated a return to Deoband.[1] During this Gujarat phase, he also helped establish Faiz-e Aam madrasa, dedicating efforts to advancing Hadith pedagogy over a three-year sub-period.[2] Prior to his Deoband appointment, Kashmiri had engaged in Hadith instruction in the Hijaz region for several years after receiving authorization from Mahmud Hasan, gaining exposure to diverse scholarly circles during travels post-Hajj in 1906.[2] [6] In 1932, he briefly resumed teaching at Dabhel before returning to Deoband owing to illness, where he continued until his death on May 28, 1933.[1] His instructional approach emphasized analytical depth and memorization, influencing generations across these Deobandi-affiliated institutions despite periodic institutional tensions.[2]

Contributions to Hadith Studies

Anwar Shah Kashmiri served as a principal instructor in Hadith at Dar al-Ulum Deoband from 1908 to 1927, where he delivered lectures on the Sihah Sittah, the six canonical collections of Hadith, including extensive coverage of Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim over eighteen years.[7] He subsequently taught at Dar al-Ulum Dhabel until his death in 1933, training approximately 2,000 students in Hadith sciences, many of whom became prominent scholars such as Mufti Muhammad Shafi‘ and Maulana Idris Khandelwi.[7] His teaching emphasized practical application, drawing students from across India due to his reputation for depth and innovation in Hadith exposition.[8] Kashmiri's methodology integrated Arabic grammar, rhetoric, and narrator criticism (ilm al-rijal and jarh wa ta'dil) to extract the intended meaning of Hadith, often resolving Quranic ambiguities through prophetic traditions.[8] He analyzed chapter headings (tarjumat al-abwab), legal rulings (ahkam), and the broader implications of narrations, classifying authentic Hadith into categories based on reporter reliability and scholarly consensus while prioritizing Hanafi jurisprudence without rigidly excluding other schools.[7] This approach allowed for interpretive flexibility, such as employing tafsir bi-l-ra'y (opinion-based exegesis) when contextual evidence supported it, diverging from stricter traditionalism to yield novel insights into narrator chains and textual authenticity.[8] His lectures formed the basis for several posthumous publications that advanced Hadith commentary. Fayd al-Bari, a four-volume Arabic work on Sahih al-Bukhari compiled by Badre Alam Merathi from Kashmiri's Deoband sessions and published in Cairo in 1938, critiques earlier commentaries like Ibn Hajar's Fath al-Bari and highlights juristic applications.[7] Other key texts include Anwar al-Mahmud fi Sharh Sunan Abi Da'ud (two volumes), Al-'Arf al-Shadhi (two-volume Arabic commentary on Jami' al-Tirmidhi, edited in 1338 AH), and Ma'arif al-Sunan (six volumes on Sunan al-Tirmidhi, published 1963), alongside marginal notes on Sunan Ibn Majah.[8][7] These works, often compiled by students like Mohammad Siddique Nazib Abidi, underscore his role as Shaykh al-Hadith, with enduring influence in South Asian Islamic scholarship.[8]

Literary Works and Publications

Major Texts on Hadith and Fiqh

Anwar Shah Kashmiri's contributions to Hadith literature primarily consist of commentaries derived from his extensive teaching sessions at institutions such as Dar al-Ulum Deoband, where he specialized in the six canonical collections. His most prominent work, Fayḍ al-Bārī ʿalā Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, a four-volume Arabic commentary on Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, was compiled by his student Mawlana Badr al-ʿĀlam Mīrāthī from lecture notes delivered between 1925 and 1927. Published in Cairo by Maṭbaʿah al-Ḥijāzī with funding from Jamʿiyyat ʿUlamāʾ Transvaal in South Africa, it emphasizes juristic derivations (fiqh al-ḥadīth), chain-of-transmission analysis (ʿilm al-rijāl), and critiques of chapter headings, distinguishing itself through Kashmiri's integration of Hanafi jurisprudence with broader scholarly traditions.[7] Complementing this, Anwār al-Maḥmūd fī Sharḥ Sunan Abī Dāwūd, an Urdu commentary in two volumes on Sunan Abī Dāwūd, was assembled by Mawlana Ṣiddīq Najīb Ābadī from Deoband lectures, with annotations by Mawlana Shabbīr Aḥmad ʿUthmānī; it focuses on the legal implications of prophetic traditions, though later editions noted its partial obsolescence due to evolving scholarship. Kashmiri also produced works on Sunan al-Tirmidhī, including the Arabic Al-ʿArf al-Shadhī (two volumes, compiled by Mawlana Chirāgh Ṣāḥib) and the revised Maʿārif al-Sunan (six volumes, edited by ʿAllāma Sayyid Muḥammad Yūsuf Banūrī in 1963), which rectify compilation errors and elaborate on gradings of narrations alongside Hanafi applications. Manuscripts for commentaries on Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim and Sunan Ibn Mājah exist but remain unpublished or lost, underscoring the oral-to-written nature of his pedagogical output.[7][2] In Fiqh, Kashmiri authored treatises defending Hanafi positions against competing madhabs, often rooted in Hadith evidence. Nayl al-Firqadayn fī Mas’alah Rafʿ al-Yadayn (145 pages, published 1931 in Delhi, second edition 1996 in Karachi), composed intermittently from 1909 to 1927, argues against raising hands beyond the initial takbīr in prayer, drawing on prophetic practices and early juristic consensus. Similarly, Kashf al-Saṭr ʿan Ṣalāh al-Witr (100 pages, first published posthumously in 1934 in Delhi) delineates the Hanafi ruling of treating Witr as three continuous rakʿahs, refuting Shafiʿi and Hanbali views with textual and analogical proofs. Other Fiqh-oriented texts include Faṣl al-Khiṭāb fī Umm al-Kitāb (106 pages, 1918 Deoband edition) on obligatory recitation of Sūrah al-Fātiḥah in prayer, and Al-Nūr al-Fāʾid ʿalā Naẓm al-Farāʾiḍ (192 poetic stanzas, 1936 Muradabad), a mnemonic simplification of inheritance laws. These works, typically concise and polemical, prioritize evidentiary rigor over expansive theorizing, reflecting Kashmiri's emphasis on Hadith-grounded jurisprudence.[7]

Poetic and Other Writings

Anwar Shah Kashmiri demonstrated proficiency in Arabic and Persian poetry, composing an estimated 1,500 poems throughout his life.[7] His poetic inclination emerged early, shaped by his three brothers—Yasin, Abd Allah, and Sulayman—who actively engaged in Persian poetry composition, and was further nurtured through participation in literary gatherings such as the Nadiyat al-Adab poetry sessions at Dar al-Ulum Deoband, organized by Mawlana I’isa Ali Amrohi.[7] Among his poetic compositions, the Qasida Hubbur Rasul stands out as an Arabic ode devoted to expressing devotion to the Prophet Muhammad.[9] [7] Additional poems praising the Prophet and his teachers appear in collections like Nuzhat al-Khawatir (pp. 251–270) and Nafohat al-Anbar (pp. 176–216).[7] Select verses were published in the Muhajir newspaper on December 21, 1927, while a private collection of his poetry remains held by Qatil Zayn al-Abidin Sajjad Mirathi.[7] Kashmiri also produced Al-Harb al-Khatim ‘ala Huduth al-‘Alam, a 400-stanza Arabic poem composed in 1926 that critiques modern scientific theories on the origins of the universe through metaphysical argumentation.[7] Beyond verse, he authored an unpublished treatise outlining principles of prose and poetry composition, which was lost in a fire at his residence in Wirnu, Kashmir.[7] These works reflect his integration of literary form with intellectual discourse, though many remain scattered or unpublished due to his primary focus on scholarly exegesis.[7]

Theological and Polemical Engagements

Debates Against Qadiani-Ahmadis

Anwar Shah Kashmiri engaged in polemical debates and legal testimonies against Qadiani-Ahmadis, primarily to defend the Islamic doctrine of khatm al-nubuwwah (finality of prophethood) against claims that Mirza Ghulam Ahmad represented a subordinate prophethood.[10] His efforts focused on refuting Ahmadi interpretations of eschatological prophecies, such as the descent of Jesus (nuzul al-Masih), which Ahmadis argued supported Mirza Ghulam Ahmad's messianic role rather than a literal return of Isa ibn Maryam.[11] A key involvement was his testimony as an expert witness in the Muqaddama Bahawalpur court case spanning 1926 to 1935, where Qadiani-Ahmadis faced challenges to their doctrinal positions under the jurisdiction of the Nawab of Bahawalpur.[12] Due to severe illness, Kashmiri was transported to the court on a bed, yet he delivered arguments drawing on hadith sciences to affirm orthodox Sunni positions against Ahmadi claims of continued revelation.[12] This case, initiated by Muslim scholars including those from Deoband, sought judicial clarification on whether Ahmadis constituted a separate religious community, with Kashmiri's contributions emphasizing textual evidence from prophetic traditions.[13] Kashmiri authored Al-Tasrih bima Tawatir fi Nuzul al-Masih, a treatise systematically critiquing the Ahmadi assertion that Jesus died in Kashmir and was not ascended alive, as per mainstream Islamic creed.[11] Relying on chains of narration (tawatur) in hadith literature, he argued for the physical ascension and future descent of Jesus, countering Ahmadi reliance on non-prophetic reports and geographical claims.[1] These writings, circulated among scholars, aimed to limit Ahmadi propagation by clarifying discrepancies with established prophetic reports.[14] His broader polemics included public refutations of Mirza Ghulam Ahmad's self-proclaimed status, viewing it as incompatible with the finality of Muhammad's prophethood, and he preemptively corresponded with regional authorities amid rising Ahmadi influence in areas like Kashmir.[15] These engagements, rooted in Kashmiri's expertise in hadith authentication, positioned him as a defender of Sunni orthodoxy, though Ahmadi responses often framed such oppositions as reactionary without addressing underlying textual disputes.[16]

Responses to Accusations of Tahrif and Other Disputes

Kashmiri faced accusations, primarily from Shia polemicists, of endorsing textual tahrif (alteration) in the Quran, stemming from a misinterpreted passage in his commentary Fa'id al-Bari 'ala Sahih al-Bukhari.[17] [18] In volume 4, page 98, while analyzing Sahih al-Bukhari's hadith 850 on tampering with prior scriptures (Torah and Gospel), Kashmiri critiques the view limiting tahrif to interpretive distortion (tahrif al-ma'na), arguing it logically extends to the Quran—which is divinely protected from any alteration per Quran 15:9—thus necessitating acknowledgment of textual tahrif (tahrif lafzi) in earlier books only.[17] He states: "What is established in my opinion is that there was textual tampering as well... And Allah knows the best!"[17] Defenders, citing Kashmiri's broader oeuvre, refute the claim as a decontextualized misrepresentation, noting his explicit affirmations of Quranic inerrancy elsewhere, such as in Ihata-e-Darul 'Uloom (pp. 93-94), where he upholds the text's preservation against innovation-driven distortions.[17] Shia sources, which themselves advocate Quranic tahrif in certain traditions, selectively quote to portray Sunni scholars like Kashmiri as inconsistent, though such accusations overlook his consistent Sunni orthodoxy rejecting any Quranic alteration.[18] [19] In Hadith scholarship, Kashmiri addressed potential scribal errors without implying systemic tahrif, as in Fa'id al-Bari (vol. 2, p. 267), where he examined manuscripts of Ibn Abi Shaybah's Musannaf and found no trace of the phrase "under the navel" in a prayer-related narration, deeming it a copyist interpolation alongside scholars like Muhammad Hayat al-Sindhi— a methodological caution, not doctrinal endorsement of textual unreliability.[20] This stance drew criticism from rival Hanafi traditionalists favoring the addition, but Kashmiri prioritized manuscript evidence over unsubstantiated transmissions.[20] Broader theological disputes included his refutations of Qadiani (Ahmadi) interpretations, where he charged Mirza Ghulam Ahmad with altering Quranic verses and prophetic hadiths to support false prophethood claims, such as reinterpreting finality of prophethood (khatm al-nubuwwah).[12] Ahmadis countered by alleging Deobandi inconsistencies, but Kashmiri's responses in public debates and texts like those against British-backed Qadianism emphasized unaltered Islamic sources, rejecting any prophetic abrogation post-Muhammad.[12]

Students, Influence, and Legacy

Notable Disciples

Among the most prominent disciples of Anwar Shah Kashmiri was Mufti Muhammad Shafi Usmani (1897–1976), who completed his daurah (advanced course) in Hadith under Kashmiri's supervision at Darul Uloom Deoband in 1335 AH (1916–1917 CE), studying key texts such as Sahih al-Bukhari, Sunan al-Tirmidhi, and Al-Shama'il.[21][1] Usmani later founded Darul Uloom Karachi in 1951 CE and served as Pakistan's first Grand Mufti, authoring influential works like Ma'ariful Qur'an, a multi-volume Tafsir that integrated Hadith scholarship.[22] Another key student was Muhammad Yusuf Banuri (1908–1977), who studied under Kashmiri and succeeded him as a leading Hadith expert, founding Jamia Uloom-ul-Islamia Allama Yusuf Banuri Town in Karachi, which continues to train scholars in traditional Islamic sciences.[1][23] Banuri's efforts preserved Kashmiri's emphasis on rigorous Hadith authentication, as evidenced by his defenses of classical texts and opposition to unverified attributions.[24] Idris Kandhlawi (d. 1974 CE) also graduated under Kashmiri's tutelage at Deoband, becoming a noted scholar of Hadith and fiqh, contributing to the propagation of Deobandi methodology through teaching and authorship.[1][25] Qari Muhammad Tayyib (d. 1976 CE), another disciple, served as rector of Darul Uloom Deoband from 1943 to 1973 CE, overseeing the institution's expansion and upholding Kashmiri's standards in Qur'anic recitation and Hadith studies.[1] Kashmiri's teaching at Deoband produced over 2,000 graduates between 1913 and 1933 CE, many of whom advanced Islamic scholarship in the Indian subcontinent by establishing madrasas and authoring texts that echoed his mastery of Hadith chains and critical analysis.[26] These disciples collectively extended his legacy, emphasizing empirical verification of narrations over unsubstantiated claims, though some faced critiques for rigid adherence to certain interpretive traditions.[1]

Enduring Impact on Islamic Scholarship

Anwar Shah Kashmiri's teaching tenure at Darul Uloom Deoband from 1913 to 1931 produced over 2,000 Hadith scholars, forming a cadre that disseminated his rigorous approach to prophetic traditions across the Indian subcontinent and beyond.[27] These students, including figures like Mufti Muhammad Shafi and Maulana Shabbir Ahmad Uthmani, carried forward his emphasis on authenticating narrations through ilm al-rijal (narrator biography) and reconciling apparent contradictions among the four Sunni madhhabs, with priority given to Hanafi positions supported by primary texts.[27] This pedagogical legacy reinforced Deobandi institutions' focus on textual fidelity amid colonial-era challenges to Islamic orthodoxy. His compiled lectures, notably Fayd al-Bari—a four-volume Arabic commentary on Sahih al-Bukhari transcribed by students like Badr-i-Alam Mirathi—endure as reference works for advanced Hadith analysis, integrating Arabic grammar, rhetorical scrutiny, and contextual exegesis to derive jurisprudential rulings.[27][8] Similarly, commentaries such as Al-Arf al-Shadhi on Jami al-Tirmidhi and Anwar al-Mahmud on Sunan Abi Dawud exemplify his method of prioritizing sahih (authentic) hadiths over weaker ones for fiqh derivations, influencing subsequent South Asian scholarship in countering interpretive laxity.[8] Arab contemporaries, including Rashid Rida al-Misri, acclaimed him as a preeminent muhaddith, underscoring his cross-regional validation.[8] Kashmiri's polemical defenses against groups like the Qadianis (Ahmadis) and accusations of Quranic tahrif (alteration) fortified Sunni doctrinal boundaries, with his arguments—grounded in historical chains of transmission and empirical scrutiny of sources—continuing to inform responses to modernist deviations.[27] By advocating tafsir bi-l-ra'y (opinion-based exegesis) only when anchored in robust evidences rather than isolated weak reports, he modeled a balanced rationalism that persists in contemporary Hanafi-Maturidi circles, promoting causal analysis of texts without compromising revelation's primacy.[8] This framework has shaped global Islamic education, particularly in seminaries tracing lineages to Deoband, ensuring Hadith's role in addressing evolving jurisprudential needs.[27]

Criticisms and Counterarguments

Critics from Shiite and certain sectarian perspectives have accused Kashmiri of endorsing tahrif (textual alteration) of the Quran, citing a passage in his Faid al-Bari commentary on Sahih al-Bukhari (Book 48, Hadith 850), which discusses tampering with Jewish and Christian scriptures.[17] In the text, Kashmiri remarks that adopting a view limiting tampering to meaning alone "entails there is tampering with the Qur’an as well," adding, "What is established in my opinion is that there was textual tampering as well" (Faid al-Bari, vol. 4, p. 98).[17] Defenders counter that this statement forms a hypothetical argument to expose the flaws in the interpretational-tampering theory by extending it absurdly to the Quran, whose preservation Kashmiri upheld unequivocally elsewhere; contemporaries noted his extreme caution in Quranic matters, avoiding unsubstantiated claims.[17] The context pertains solely to prior scriptures, not the Quran, rendering the accusation a misrepresentation exploited by theological opponents.[17] Kashmiri's refutations of Ahmadi doctrines—labeling Mirza Ghulam Ahmad a disbeliever for altering Quranic verses and Hadith meanings—and Barelvi beliefs like hazir nazir (the Prophet's omnipresent awareness) elicited rebuttals from those communities, who charged him with undervaluing prophetic omniscience or innovation in creed.[12][28] Such positions, grounded in selective Hadith authentication, were seen by critics as prioritizing madhhab loyalty over unqualified prophetic exaltation.[29] Proponents respond that Kashmiri's critiques adhered strictly to authenticated narrations, rejecting unsubstantiated attributions of supernatural knowledge to the Prophet post-demise, as in his analysis of salutations implying non-hearing; this approach aligned with Hanafi-Maturidi caution against anthropomorphism or excess, validated by endorsements from scholars like Mustafa Sabri.[28][23] His courtroom testimony against Ahmadis in the 1926–1935 Bahawalpur proceedings further demonstrated evidentiary rigor over polemical bias.[12] Salafi-leaning voices have occasionally faulted his Maturidi preferences in creed for diverging from early Salaf views, yet his Hadith methodology—emphasizing chains and content—remains broadly respected beyond sectarian lines.[30]

Death

Final Years and Passing

Following internal disputes at Darul Uloom Deoband, Anwar Shah Kashmiri resigned his position in 1346 AH (1927 CE) and relocated to Jamia Islamiyyah Dabhel in Gujarat, where he taught advanced Hadith texts until 1351 AH (1932 CE).[4][1] There, he maintained his rigorous scholarly routine despite emerging health challenges, focusing on instruction in Sahih al-Bukhari and other core works.[1] By 1932, deteriorating health compelled his return to Deoband for medical attention, where severe illnesses impaired his organs and limited physical mobility, including hand function for handling books.[26][1] He adapted by positioning texts on a chair and relying on assistance to turn pages, yet continued addressing students and engaging in study until his condition worsened critically.[1] Kashmiri passed away in Deoband on 28 May 1933 (3 Safar 1352 AH), during the last third of the night preceding Monday.[3][26] He was buried in an orchard on the seminary's outskirts.[1]
