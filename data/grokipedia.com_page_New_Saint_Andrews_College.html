New Saint Andrews College


New Saint Andrews College is a private, coeducational classical Christian liberal arts college founded in 1994 in Moscow, Idaho.[1] It provides bachelor's and master's degrees through a curriculum centered on the great books of Western civilization, rhetoric, and a Reformed Christian worldview aimed at equipping students to influence culture faithfully under Christ's lordship.[2][3] The college, with approximately 240 students, emphasizes the classical trivium—grammar, logic, and rhetoric—as the foundation for intellectual and moral formation.[4] In 2023, it achieved full accreditation from the Northwest Commission on Colleges and Universities after previously holding candidacy status with the Transnational Association of Christian Colleges and Schools.[5][1] Closely affiliated with Christ Church and its pastor Douglas Wilson, a key figure in its establishment, the institution promotes a postmillennial eschatology and robust cultural engagement that prioritizes biblical authority over secular norms, engendering opposition from progressive academics and media outlets wary of its patriarchal and theologically conservative stances.[6][7]

History

Founding and Early Years (1994–2000)

New Saint Andrews College was established in 1994 in Moscow, Idaho, by the Christ Church congregation as a private classical Christian liberal arts institution.[8] Douglas Wilson, senior pastor of Christ Church and a founding board member, initiated the college to provide an education modeled on the seventeenth-century Harvard curriculum, adapted to emphasize Christian principles and cultural reformation.[9][10] Classes began in the fall of 1994 with an enrollment of four students, operating from facilities in downtown Moscow, including the historic Skattaboe Block at Fourth and Main Streets.[4] The initial program focused on rigorous study of classical texts, logic, rhetoric, and theology, integrated with Reformed evangelical commitments, without formal accreditation at the outset.[4][8] The college's early development relied on its organic ties to Christ Church, which served as its founding body and provided administrative and communal support.[8] Enrollment remained small through the late 1990s, reflecting a deliberate emphasis on intensive, seminar-style instruction over rapid expansion. In 1998, New Saint Andrews conferred its first associate degrees to two students, validating the foundational model amid limited resources.[4][8] By 2000, the institution had established a core curriculum but continued to operate modestly, prioritizing depth in classical Christian education over institutional scale.[11]

Expansion and Institutional Development (2001–Present)

In 2001, New Saint Andrews College acquired the historic Skatteboe Block, a two-story brick building constructed in 1892 at the corner of 4th and Main Streets in downtown Moscow, Idaho, which became its primary campus facility.[12] [13] This acquisition enabled the institution to consolidate operations in a dedicated space, supporting operational growth beyond its initial rented quarters.[14] Enrollment expanded significantly in the ensuing decades, reflecting increased interest in its classical Christian liberal arts model. Total student numbers grew from 181 in 2017 to 351 by 2023, with full-time undergraduates comprising the majority at 281.[15] [16] Undergraduate applications reached 126 in 2023, marking a 6.67% year-over-year increase.[16] Academic offerings developed to include graduate-level programs, such as the Master of Arts in Theology and Letters, alongside the core Bachelor of Arts in Liberal Arts.[17] The college now provides two graduate programs, one of which is available online or through distance education.[18] In June 2024, the City of Moscow approved the college's expansion of administrative offices into an adjacent downtown building, addressing space needs amid ongoing institutional maturation.[19]

Mission and Educational Philosophy

Core Principles of Classical Christian Education

Classical Christian education at New Saint Andrews College centers on the lordship of Jesus Christ over all domains of knowledge and culture, integrating rigorous liberal arts study with a Reformed theological framework. This approach posits that true wisdom derives from Scripture as the infallible rule of faith and practice, supplemented by historic creeds such as the Apostles' Creed, Nicene Creed, and Definition of Chalcedon.[2] The curriculum emphasizes primary texts from the Western tradition—spanning ancient Greece, Rome, medieval Europe, and early modern periods—to foster analytical reading, critical assessment, and articulate expression, all evaluated through a biblical lens that contrasts pagan philosophies with Christian truth.[20] Unlike fragmented modern specialties, this method promotes an integrative view of disciplines, mirroring the relational unity of the Trinity to reveal God's created order.[20] A foundational principle is cultural leadership grounded in biblical wisdom, equipping graduates to engage and reform society across vocations such as family, church, and civil life, rather than withdrawing into isolation.[2] This entails cultivating humility, knowledge, and rhetorical skill to advance Christ's kingdom, rejecting secular relativism in favor of objective truth derived from divine revelation and empirical observation of creation. The college's single, non-major curriculum reinforces this by requiring students to master classical languages like Latin, Greek, and Hebrew for direct access to foundational texts, including Scripture, thereby building grammatical precision, logical rigor, and persuasive eloquence akin to the trivium's progression.[21] [20] Theological fidelity shapes every aspect, with faculty and trustees adhering to Protestant, Reformed confessions like the Westminster Confession of Faith, ensuring that education serves God's glory rather than utilitarian ends.[2] Students commit to a code of conduct affirming Christian ethics, fostering a communal environment where learning occurs amid worship and mutual accountability. This contrasts with secular academia's often ideologically driven narratives, prioritizing causal reasoning from first causes—ultimately God—over ideologically biased interpretations prevalent in mainstream institutions.[2] The result is graduates positioned not merely to consume culture but to steward it faithfully, applying Trinitarian relationality to interdisciplinary synthesis and ethical discernment.[20]

Theological Foundations and Cultural Goals

New Saint Andrews College grounds its educational enterprise in a confessional Reformed theological framework, affirming the absolute authority of the Scriptures of the Old and New Testaments as the sole infallible rule of faith and practice.[2] This foundation incorporates historic ecumenical creeds, including the Apostles’ Creed, the Nicene Creed, and the Definition of Chalcedon, alongside key Reformational documents such as the Westminster Confession of Faith (1646), the Heidelberg Catechism, the Belgic Confession, and the Canons of Dort.[2] The college's theology is explicitly Trinitarian, Protestant, Reformed, and evangelical, positioning theology as the "queen of the sciences" that orients all academic disciplines toward the lordship of Jesus Christ over every aspect of existence.[2][22] Required theology courses for freshmen and seniors integrate these doctrines, emphasizing biblical inerrancy and the cultural mandate derived from Genesis to exercise dominion over creation in accordance with God's revealed will.[22] The institution's doctrinal commitments reject neutrality in knowledge or culture, insisting that all truth is ultimately logocentric and grounded in God's nature, with objective standards of the true, good, and beautiful serving as guides for human endeavor.[23] This perspective draws from common-sense realism and opposes relativistic or subjectivist epistemologies, viewing worship—particularly corporate Lord's Day observance—as the highest liberal art and the wellspring of cultural vitality.[23] Eschatologically optimistic, the college anticipates extensive and intensive Christian influence over the world through divine blessing and faithful obedience, rather than retreat or pessimism, with success attributed ultimately to God's sovereignty rather than mere human strategy.[23] Culturally, New Saint Andrews aims to produce graduates equipped to reform and shape society under Christ's comprehensive lordship, fulfilling the mission to "graduate leaders who shape culture living faithfully under the Lordship of Jesus Christ."[2] This involves cultivating biblically informed leadership in family, church, civil government, and vocation, emphasizing fruitful covenantal marriage, distinct gender roles with a focus on masculine responsibility, and rejection of the "culture of death" exemplified by the sexual revolution.[23] The curriculum fosters generalist thinkers via classical liberal arts, prioritizing character formation, joy in obedience, and dominion-oriented vocations over narrow specialization or trend-chasing, with an eye toward rebuilding Christendom through regenerate, self-governing individuals who advance economic liberty, conserve the good, and steward wealth as blessings.[23][22] Such goals position the college as a countercultural force, training students to make truth, beauty, and goodness tangible in opposition to envy-driven egalitarianism and autonomy.[23]

Academics

Curriculum Structure and Requirements

New Saint Andrews College structures its undergraduate curriculum around a classical Christian liberal arts model, emphasizing integrated study of theology, humanities, sciences, and languages within a Reformed theological framework. The academic year consists of four 8-week terms—Jerusalem, Nicea, Chalcedon, and Westminster—allowing for accelerated, intensive coursework.[24] Instruction occurs primarily through colloquium-style classes, which combine lectures, discussions, and original source readings, supplemented by weekly disputations (formal debates) to foster rhetorical skills and critical engagement.[24] [3] The Bachelor of Arts degree in Liberal Arts and Culture requires 124 semester credits, earned via a prescribed sequence of colloquium terms rather than traditional departmental majors. Students must complete four terms each in 11 core colloquium areas: Lordship (theology), Classical Rhetoric, Music, History and Historiography, Mathematics, Science, Philosophy, Literature, Principia Theologiae (advanced theology), Mathematics and Western Culture, and additional theology or philosophy sequences. Additionally, 12 terms of language colloquium are mandated, including four in Latin and proficiency in reading one classical language (Latin, Greek, Hebrew, or Middle English), focusing on primary texts from Scripture and Western canon authors. Nine terms of electives, each worth 2 credits, allow customization in areas like advanced languages, writing, or sciences.[24] The program culminates in a two-term Senior Thesis, requiring 7,500–10,000 words on an approved topic, defended privately and optionally publicly, to demonstrate mastery of research and argumentation.[24] [3] Graduation demands fulfillment of all credit and colloquium requirements, with at least eight of the final 12 credits completed in residence, and submission of a graduation application by the Nicea Term deadline. Oral examinations punctuate certain colloquia, such as in church music or languages, to assess comprehension and application. An Associate of Arts degree, mirroring the BA's structure but at 64 credits with reduced requirements, accepts no new students after the 2024–2025 academic year due to a moratorium. A 66-credit Music Certificate option integrates four years of instrumental or choral performance alongside core studies for BA candidates.[24] This non-modular, cohort-based approach prioritizes depth in foundational disciplines over specialization, aiming to equip graduates for cultural leadership grounded in biblical lordship.[3]

Degree Programs and Enrollment

New Saint Andrews College's undergraduate program confers a Bachelor of Arts in Liberal Arts and Culture, featuring a unified major without departmental specializations. The four-year curriculum mandates foundational courses in classical rhetoric, Latin, theology, history, philosophy, literature, mathematics, and sciences, progressing from introductory studies in music and freshman theology to advanced electives, senior theology, and a thesis requirement. This structure prioritizes proficiency in original languages such as Latin, Greek, Hebrew, or Middle English, alongside integrated humanities and quantitative disciplines to cultivate analytical and cultural engagement skills.[22] At the graduate level, the college offers Master of Arts, Master of Fine Arts, Master of Studies, and Master of Divinity degrees, primarily concentrated in theology, the arts, and education. Programs like the Master of Arts in Theology and Letters emphasize historical, philosophical, and literary studies grounded in theological frameworks, while options such as Classical Christian Studies provide low-residency formats for professional educators and leaders. The Master of Divinity operates through the affiliated Greyfriars program, targeting ministerial training. These degrees require tuition commitments and align with the institution's commitment to classical methodologies over modern specialization.[25][17] Enrollment remains intentionally limited to promote seminar-style instruction and personal mentorship, eschewing large-scale operations in favor of a model akin to seventeenth-century Harvard or Oxford, with faculty-led recitations and no reliance on teaching assistants. In fall 2024, undergraduate enrollment totaled 309 students, with 43.4% male and 56.6% female representation; graduate cohorts are smaller, contributing to an overall student body under 400. The student-faculty ratio supports this intimate scale, enabling rigorous dialogue in core disciplines.[26][1]

Student Life and Campus Environment

Daily Life and Community Dynamics

Students at New Saint Andrews College engage in a structured daily routine centered on rigorous classical Christian liberal arts education, with classes, recitations, and disputations held in the college's downtown Moscow facilities, including the historic Skattaboe Block at 405 South Main Street.[27] [28] The academic year divides into four eight-week terms—Jerusalem, Nicea, Chalcedon, and Westminster—with examinations in the final week of each and limited breaks, excluding Easter.[28] A modest dress code applies, requiring men to wear button-up shirts and dress pants and women dresses or skirts with dressy tops, while black academic robes are mandatory for formal events like convocations after completing 32 credits.[28] Living arrangements emphasize independence and community integration, as the college provides no dormitories or on-campus housing; students rent local apartments or houses, often sharing with peers or boarding with host families affiliated with area churches, at costs ranging from $600 to $1,500 per month.[29] [28] This setup, supported by housing leads from admissions, mirrors post-collegiate responsibilities and fosters immersion in Moscow's community, where students patronize local businesses, use coffee shops as informal study spaces, and access city parks for recreation.[27] [29] Off-campus facilities like the Tyndale Library, with over 45,000 volumes, and the Commons area enforce practical norms such as no napping, covered drinks only, and cleanup after use to maintain order.[28] Community dynamics revolve around a tight-knit, Reformed Christian ethos, with students organized into three halls—Jericho, Ashdown, and Malta—to promote leadership and interaction, guided by "Whips" (student leaders) who coordinate events like finals-week communal meals.[29] Mandatory church attendance at local congregations, such as Christ Church or Trinity Reformed Church, integrates spiritual formation with social life, including ministries like the Collegiate Reformed Fellowship and diaconal service through groups like Students for the Relief of the Oppressed, which organizes charity events.[29] [28] Student-initiated clubs, registered via the Populi platform by October 1 annually, include Civics Club (hosting candidate forums), Men's Rugby, Women's Volleyball, Rowing, Fencing, Fine Arts, Athletics, Soccer, and Catalyst journalism, alongside traditions like the annual Fall Carnival featuring games, food, and costume contests.[29] [28] Governance emphasizes personal and communal holiness under Christ's lordship, enforced through an annual student pledge committing to sexual purity, doctrinal soundness, academic integrity, and cultural engagement, with violations—such as drunkenness or marijuana use—subject to disciplinary review by a hearing board, potentially leading to probation, suspension, or expulsion.[28] This framework, supported by a 14:1 student-faculty ratio for mentorship, cultivates a collaborative atmosphere of debate, fellowship among like-minded believers, and preparation for broader societal influence, distinct from typical dormitory-based college experiences.[29] [27]

Extracurricular Activities and Traditions

Student organizations at New Saint Andrews College include the Civics Club, which explores local government and hosts an annual candidate forum in Moscow, Idaho.[29] The Students for the Relief of the Oppressed raises funds and awareness for persecuted churches through charity races, balls, and dinners in partnership with Trinity Reformed Church.[29] The Catalyst club produces a semesterly student magazine focused on journalism.[29] Clubs require a student leader, treasurer, and faculty advisor, with annual registration via the college's Populi system by October 1 and funds managed through institutional accounting.[30] Athletic activities emphasize intramural and club sports integrated into the hall system, comprising three residential halls—Ashdown, Jericho, and Malta—that foster leadership and competition.[29] [30] Men's rugby is a longstanding tradition, featuring an annual pumpkin rugby match in the fall to celebrate the season.[29] Other sports include women's volleyball (student-coached with a winter tournament), NSA Rowing (in its seventh year, training for national championships), fencing (twice-weekly sessions in foil for coordination and discipline), soccer (open to all skill levels for teamwork), and an athletics club offering training regardless of experience.[29] Intramural sports such as basketball and track and field contribute points to the annual NSA Cup competition, where halls vie for a trophy across academics, arts, and athletics categories, with winners celebrated at a college-sponsored event.[30] The Fine Arts Club promotes art study and engagement with Moscow's cultural scene.[29] Class whips—student representatives (four per upper class, balanced by gender)—organize events like freshman parties, finals-week soup meals, and Christmas decorating to encourage spiritual growth and community.[30] Halls host two major annual events, including a change of leadership ceremony with summaries and gratitude expressions.[30] Students are encouraged to participate in local church ministries, such as the Collegiate Reformed Fellowship or nursing home visits, alongside required attendance at confessional Protestant churches.[30] Traditions include the annual Fall Carnival, hosted by students for two decades to serve Moscow families with food, booth games, face-painting, and costume competitions.[29] [31] Students sign "The Book" at registration to mark matriculation and at commencement for graduation.[30] Faculty mentors host soup nights for freshmen to build fellowship.[30] These activities reinforce the college's emphasis on community, service, and cultural engagement within a classical Christian framework.[29]

Accreditation and Regulatory Challenges

Pursuit of Accreditation and Legal Disputes

In May 2002, the board of New Saint Andrews College voted to withdraw its application for candidacy status with the Northwest Commission on Colleges and Universities (NWCCU), citing anticipated denial due to the institution's classical Christian model, small size, and lack of traditional campus facilities, which evaluators deemed insufficient for regional standards.[32] The college instead pursued accreditation through the Transnational Association of Christian Colleges and Schools (TRACS), a faith-based accreditor recognized by the U.S. Department of Education, achieving candidate status in 2003 and full accreditation in 2005. TRACS accreditation supported operations but limited access to certain federal student aid programs available only to regionally accredited institutions, prompting renewed pursuit of NWCCU candidacy in the 2010s.[1] After a multi-year evaluation process involving self-studies, peer reviews, and compliance demonstrations, NWCCU granted New Saint Andrews full regional accreditation on July 21, 2023, affirming the college's academic standards, governance, and student outcomes despite its non-residential, seminar-based structure.[5][33] Parallel to accreditation efforts, the college has faced legal disputes with the city of Moscow, Idaho, over zoning and land use regulations. In August 2024, New Saint Andrews challenged a zoning administrator's appeal of a Board of Adjustment decision that permitted expansion of college facilities, alleging discriminatory application of municipal codes that hindered equitable treatment compared to secular institutions.[34] These proceedings, ongoing as of late 2024, stem from broader regulatory scrutiny of the college's integration with local Christian organizations, including Christ Church, amid claims of selective enforcement against religious entities.[35] The disputes have not directly impacted accreditation but highlight operational tensions in a community divided over the college's cultural and theological influence.[36]

Current Status and Operational Implications

New Saint Andrews College maintains full accreditation from the Northwest Commission on Colleges and Universities (NWCCU), granted on July 21, 2023, following a multi-year pursuit that addressed prior institutional challenges.[5] It also holds institutional accreditation from the Transnational Association of Christian Colleges and Schools (TRACS) since 2005, reflecting its emphasis on faith-integrated education. As of the 2024-2025 academic year, the college operates with approximately 305 undergraduate students, including 261 full-time and 44 part-time enrollees, underscoring its small-scale model focused on intensive classical liberal arts training.[37] The attainment of NWCCU accreditation has enabled eligibility for Title IV federal student aid programs, potentially broadening access for students beyond private funding or church-affiliated support from entities like Christ Church in Moscow, Idaho.[33] This shift mitigates earlier limitations on financial aid and credit transferability, which had constrained growth and recognition in secular academic networks. However, the college's niche commitment to postmillennial Reformed theology and cultural reconstructionism continues to orient operations toward a select demographic, resulting in sustained low enrollment relative to regional peers and reliance on tuition of $18,000 annually supplemented by endowments and donations.[11] Operationally, NSA functions from leased facilities in downtown Moscow, with no owned campus, which supports a compact, urban-integrated environment but exposes it to municipal oversight. In August 2024, the college initiated legal action against the City of Moscow's Zoning Administrator over a disputed zoning interpretation that allegedly hinders equitable facility use, raising questions of selective enforcement amid local tensions with progressive community elements.[38] While not halting core activities, such disputes could delay expansion or adaptations for growing graduate programs, including a recently launched Master of Arts in Theology. The institution's financial stability appears tied to its ecclesiastical ecosystem, insulating it from market volatility but amplifying risks from reputational controversies linked to founder Doug Wilson's public stances.[21]

Controversies and Criticisms

Theological and Social Positions

New Saint Andrews College maintains a confessional Reformed theological framework, endorsing the Westminster Confession of Faith (1646), Heidelberg Catechism, Belgic Confession, and Canons of Dort as the most accurate summaries of Scripture's teachings on doctrine, sacraments, and church order.[2] Its statement of faith declares the Old and New Testaments as the sole infallible rule for faith and practice, while affirming Trinitarian orthodoxy through creeds like the Apostles' Creed, Nicene Creed, and Chalcedonian Definition.[2] Faculty and board members, required to affirm this statement, integrate biblical authority across liberal arts disciplines to foster cultural engagement under Christ's lordship.[2] The college's affiliation with the Communion of Reformed Evangelical Churches (CREC) incorporates controversial elements such as paedocommunion, permitting baptized infants and young children to receive the Lord's Supper without personal profession of faith or self-examination, a practice defended as covenantal continuity but rejected by confessional Presbyterian bodies like the PCA for lacking explicit New Testament precedent and risking unworthy participation.[39] Some faculty align with Federal Vision theology, which stresses the covenant's objective efficacy in incorporating members into Christ but has been condemned by Reformed assemblies—including the 2007 PCA report—for blurring distinctions between justification by faith alone and sacramental union, potentially undermining sola fide.[40] Doug Wilson, a founding board member and senior fellow, promotes postmillennial eschatology, anticipating widespread gospel triumph and cultural transformation prior to Christ's return, as evidenced in the college's emphasis on equipping students for societal influence.[23] Socially, the institution reflects Wilson's advocacy for biblical patriarchy, positing male headship as divinely ordained in household, church, and broader authority structures, with women called to supportive roles rather than egalitarian interchangeability.[41] This stance, articulated in Wilson's writings as an inescapable reality redeemable through Scripture, opposes modern feminism and same-sex relationships, aligning with CREC declarations affirming biblical sexuality confined to heterosexual marriage.[42] [41] Theonomic convictions, held by Wilson, extend Old Testament civil laws as a moral blueprint for just governance, fueling criticisms of the college's vision as theocratic despite its rejection of state funding to preserve doctrinal independence.[1] [43] These positions, aimed at rebuilding Christendom through education and piety, have elicited accusations of authoritarianism from secular media and intra-Reformed opponents, though proponents substantiate them via exegetical fidelity to Mosaic and apostolic norms.[44]

Conflicts with External Institutions and Local Community

![Scatteboe Block, site of New Saint Andrews College's main campus][float-right] In August 2024, New Saint Andrews College (NSA) became embroiled in a zoning dispute with the city of Moscow, Idaho, over the use of property at 414 South Main Street for its Pierian art gallery and administrative offices.[38] The conflict arose after the city's Zoning Administrator denied a permit, citing restrictions on educational uses within the Central Business (CB) zoning district, which prioritizes commercial activities.[45] NSA appealed to the Board of Adjustment (BOA), which initially ruled in its favor on June 11, 2024, but the city appealed to the Moscow City Council.[45] On September 3, 2024, the City Council unanimously reversed the BOA's decision, upholding the administrator's denial and prohibiting NSA from operating administrative functions at the site due to the educational nature of the use.[45] College President Benjamin Merkle argued that the ruling reflected inconsistent enforcement of zoning laws, noting that NSA's main campus in the historic Scatteboe Block—acquired in 2001—had operated under previously permissive codes without issue, and claimed the college faced unequal treatment compared to other downtown entities.[38] [12] The decision was final, with no further appeals possible, potentially limiting NSA's ability to expand operations in downtown Moscow.[45] Beyond regulatory clashes, NSA has faced opposition from segments of the local community, particularly residents affiliated with the more liberal University of Idaho, which dominates Moscow's cultural landscape. Tensions stem from NSA's affiliation with the Communion of Reformed Evangelical Churches (CREC) and its promotion of classical Christian education, which some view as promoting conservative social positions at odds with progressive norms. For instance, a 2021 promotional advertisement for NSA was described by local critics as transphobic, reportedly unifying opposition against the institution.[46] Earlier instances include 2004 community backlash over a conference on Southern slavery hosted by CREC affiliates, which escalated cultural divides in the college town.[47] These frictions highlight broader resistance to NSA's growth amid perceptions of an encroaching conservative influence, though the college maintains its operations align with longstanding zoning allowances and community revitalization efforts.[48]

Achievements and Broader Impact

Educational Outcomes and Alumni Success

New Saint Andrews College exhibits graduation rates above the national average for similar institutions, with the U.S. Department of Education's College Scorecard reporting a 79% completion rate for students who begin their studies.[49] Independent analyses vary, including a six-year graduation rate of 52% from College Raptor and 82% from Scholarships360, reflecting the small cohort size and rigorous classical curriculum that demands sustained engagement.[50][51] In 2023, the college conferred 39 undergraduate and graduate degrees, underscoring its scale as a niche liberal arts provider focused on depth over volume.[16] Alumni outcomes demonstrate versatility across professional domains, with graduates crediting the program's emphasis on rhetoric, logic, and ethical formation for adaptability in real-world applications.[52] For instance, Daniel Foucachon (class of 2010) leveraged his education to found Kepler Education, an online classical teaching consortium, and serve as CEO of Roman Roads Press, a curriculum publisher, after roles in finance, videography, and entrepreneurship.[52] Nick Heid (class of 2007) applied rhetorical training to sales, audio production, and eventually owning a law practice, noting its value in tailoring communication to diverse audiences.[52] Danny Bradley (class of 2020) transitioned into real estate and talent recruitment sales, attributing professional diligence to the character development fostered at the college.[52] Broader alumni trajectories include civil engineering, firefighting, flight instruction, financial investment, real estate, retail management, and homemaking, alongside pursuits of advanced degrees at institutions such as Harvard University, the University of Notre Dame, and the University of St. Andrews.[52] These paths align with the college's stated aim to produce culture-shaping leaders through a curriculum that prioritizes transferable intellectual and moral competencies over vocational specialization.[3] While empirical longitudinal data on earnings or employment rates remains limited due to the institution's size and non-accredited status, self-reported accounts highlight sustained career progression in fields valuing critical thinking and principled decision-making.[52]

Contributions to the Classical Christian Movement

New Saint Andrews College, established in 1994 in Moscow, Idaho, by Douglas Wilson and associates from Christ Church, emerged as an early exemplar of classical Christian higher education amid the movement's resurgence in the 1990s. Starting with just four students, the institution grew to enroll nearly 200 by emphasizing a rigorous liberal arts curriculum grounded in the trivium and quadrivium, integrated with Reformed theology and primary sources from Western Christian tradition. This model built on the foundational work of the nearby Logos School, founded by Wilson in 1981, which pioneered K-12 classical Christian schooling and influenced over 100 subsequent schools by 2000.[53][54][55] The college contributes through specialized programs designed to equip educators and cultural leaders within the movement. Its low-residency Master of Studies in Classical Christian Studies degree focuses on liberal arts and humanities, enabling practitioners to deepen their command of classical texts while applying a biblical worldview to teaching. Complementing this, an online graduate-level course on the History of Classical Christian Education traces Western paideia from antiquity, endorsed by movement leaders to foster historical awareness among educators. These offerings align with the college's certification in the Association of Classical Christian Schools (ACCS) Certified College Program, positioning it as a preferred pathway for ACCS high school graduates seeking advanced classical training.[56][57][3][58] Faculty and leadership at New Saint Andrews exert broader influence via intellectual output and institutional affiliations. Douglas Wilson, a founding board member and senior fellow, has authored works promoting classical methods as essential for cultural reformation, establishing him as a leading proponent of the movement. Provost Christopher Schlect advances pedagogical discussions, arguing for classical education's role in forming resilient minds amid cultural chaos, as seen in his contributions to forums like the Society for Classical Learning. As a charter affiliate of ACCS and participant in its events, such as honor choir performances, the college reinforces networks that have expanded classical Christian schools nationwide since the 1990s.[59][9][60]

Notable Individuals

Key Faculty Members

Dr. Douglas Wilson serves as Trustee and Senior Fellow of Theology, teaching courses in Greek, Latin, theology, rhetoric, and electives, including involvement in the MFA program. A founding board member established in 1994, he earned two B.A. degrees after U.S. Navy service and pastors Christ Church in Moscow, Idaho, shaping the college's classical Christian ethos through curriculum emphasizing Reformed theology and cultural engagement.[9] Dr. Benjamin Merkle, president since at least 2010 and Senior Fellow of Theology, holds a DPhil in Oriental Studies and MSt from the University of Oxford, focusing on biblical languages and theology in instruction. His leadership oversees academic rigor aligned with the college's mission to equip students for cultural reformation via liberal arts.[61] N.D. Wilson, Writer in Residence and Fellow of Literature, joined as adjunct faculty in 2001 and founded the Camperdown Writer's Kiln MFA program. Holding a B.A. from New Saint Andrews (1999) and M.A. in liberal arts from St. John's College (2001), he teaches narrative craft and worldview integration, authoring fiction and non-fiction that advance Christian storytelling.[62][61] Dr. Gordon Wilson, Senior Fellow of Natural History since 2003, instructs on biology from a young-earth creationist framework, rejecting Darwinian evolution as culturally corrosive. With a Ph.D. from George Mason University, he promotes empirical observation of nature's design through works like The Riot and the Dance and lectures emphasizing biblical causality over materialist narratives.[63][64] Dr. Joe Rigney, Fellow of Theology since April 2023, earned a Ph.D. from the University of Chester and brings pastoral experience, teaching systematic theology and cultural critique to foster dominion-oriented scholarship. His prior roles include academic leadership at Bethlehem College & Seminary, aligning with the college's postmillennial optimism.[65] Dr. Jayson Grieser, Fellow of Literature since 2007, pursued M.A. and doctoral studies in literature, specializing in narrative theory and ethics to train students in discerning truth amid secular fictions.[66] Dr. Timothy Harmon, Vice President of Academics and Chief Academic Officer, holds a Ph.D. in Divinity from the University of Aberdeen, M.A. and Th.M. from Western Seminary, overseeing curriculum delivery in theology and related fields.[67]

Prominent Alumni

N.D. Wilson, who graduated in 1999, is a recognized author of fantasy literature, including the 100 Cupboards trilogy and the Ashtown Burials series, which have been praised for their imaginative storytelling rooted in Christian themes.[68] He has also directed films such as The Riot and Dance (2018), documenting natural history from a creationist perspective, and serves as a fellow of literature and adjunct faculty at New Saint Andrews College.[69] Daniel Alders, who received a B.A. in 2009, was elected as a Republican to the Texas House of Representatives for District 6 in November 2024, representing parts of Smith County.[70] Prior to his political career, Alders worked as a realtor and commercial property manager in Texas.[71] Alumni like these exemplify the college's focus on producing graduates who engage in cultural and public leadership roles, often within conservative Christian frameworks, though broader empirical data on alumni outcomes remains limited to self-reported successes in ministry, education, and business.[72]
