Boies Schiller Flexner LLP


Boies Schiller Flexner LLP is an American law firm founded in 1997 by prominent litigators David Boies and Jonathan Schiller, with Donald Flexner joining as a named partner in 1999.[1][2] The firm specializes in high-stakes litigation, antitrust matters, complex commercial disputes, crisis management, and international arbitration, emphasizing an aggressive, trial-ready approach across diverse industries.[3] Headquartered in New York City with additional offices throughout the United States, London, and Milan, it operates as a unified entity focused on delivering outcomes through over 450 trials in U.S. and foreign courts.[3][4] Renowned for representing both plaintiffs and defendants in bet-the-company cases, Boies Schiller Flexner has achieved significant victories, including a record $4.1 billion antitrust recovery for American Express and landmark decisions enhancing antitrust enforcement under the Basic v. Levinson framework.[5][6] The firm's attorneys, many recognized in rankings such as Best Lawyers in America, have handled high-profile matters ranging from public corruption and commodities fraud to consumer class actions and pro bono efforts improving public services.[7][1] However, it has encountered challenges, including substantial partner departures and internal criticisms stemming from representations in controversial cases such as those involving Harvey Weinstein and Theranos, which prompted associate dissatisfaction and firm shrinkage.[8][9]

History

Founding and early development

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP was established in 1997 by trial lawyers David Boies and Jonathan D. Schiller. Boies, who had been a partner at Cravath, Swaine & Moore, set up the firm's initial operations in Armonk, New York. Simultaneously, Schiller, leaving Kaye Scholer, launched a Washington, D.C. office to support the new venture.[1] The founders aimed to create a firm centered on aggressive, high-stakes litigation, drawing on their established trial experience and mutual trust developed through prior professional interactions. Early emphasis was placed on complex commercial disputes, consumer class actions, and plaintiff-side representations, with an entrepreneurial model that prioritized trial readiness over traditional Big Law structures.[10][11] In 1999, Donald L. Flexner, a former partner at Crowell & Moring, joined the partnership, leading to the firm's renaming as Boies Schiller Flexner LLP. This period marked initial growth through recruitment of skilled litigators and attraction of bet-the-company cases, solidifying the firm's niche in elite, outcome-driven advocacy while maintaining a commitment to pro bono efforts.[1][2]

Expansion and operational growth

Following its founding in 1997 with simultaneous offices in New York City and Washington, D.C., Boies Schiller Flexner expanded domestically by establishing additional U.S. locations, including in Miami, Florida; Boca Raton, Florida; Los Angeles, California; Oakland, California; and Albany, New York, to broaden its national litigation footprint and access regional client bases.[1][12] In 2015, the firm relocated its New York headquarters to 55 Hudson Yards, signaling investment in infrastructure amid growing operations.[13] The firm's first international expansion occurred in 2013 with the opening of a London office, led by partner Natasha Harrison, marking its initial foray outside the United States to handle cross-border disputes.[14] By 2017, the London office had grown to six partners and 14 other lawyers, focusing on dispute resolution.[15] Although the office experienced significant partner departures in 2022, it was subsequently rebuilt through lateral hires, contributing to overall head count gains in 2023.[16] Further European growth followed in November 2021 with the Milan office, launched with founding partners Luigi M. Macioce, Alessia Allegretti, and Stefano Zonca, alongside an initial team of 15 lawyers to serve as a continental base for international investments and transactions.[17][18] In December 2023, the firm opened a Rome office with a five-lawyer team led by Natalia Bagnato, enhancing proximity to EU clients and complementing the Milan presence for matters involving Italian and broader European operations.[19][20] These moves aligned with a strategy of modest lateral partner additions to bolster practice areas like antitrust and commercial litigation, supporting revenue recovery post-internal transitions.[21]

Leadership transitions and recent challenges

In November 2023, Boies Schiller Flexner LLP announced that co-founder David Boies would step aside as firm chair in 2025 following a series of transition missteps, including failed succession attempts that led to partner departures and internal instability.[22][23] The firm had previously installed new managing partners in 2019, including Nicholas Gravante Jr. in New York and Natasha Harrison in London, as part of efforts to guide it into a second generation of leadership, alongside office head changes in multiple U.S. locations.[24][12] Partners elected Matthew Schwartz, a veteran trial lawyer and former federal prosecutor, as the new chair on December 8, 2023, with him assuming the role on January 2, 2025, after working alongside Boies through 2024.[25][26] This concluded a multi-year process marked by two prior unraveled succession plans and what Schwartz later described as a "chaotic" period that ultimately strengthened the firm by clarifying priorities.[26][27] The transition exposed challenges including factional internal politics among name partners' fiefdoms and significant lateral exits, contributing to revenue declines in prior years before a rebound.[9][28] In 2023, gross revenue rose 11.8% to reflect contingency successes and modest headcount growth of 2%, reversing earlier downturns, while 2024 saw a 57.5% revenue surge driven by high-stakes payouts and expansion.[28][29] Despite these financial recoveries, the firm's boutique litigation modelâ€”reliant on star partners and contingency feesâ€”amplified vulnerabilities during leadership vacuums, as noted in analyses of elite firms' succession dilemmas.[30]

Practice Areas

Core litigation focus

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP's core litigation practice emphasizes high-stakes trial and appellate work in complex commercial disputes, representing both plaintiffs and defendants across federal, state, and international jurisdictions. The firm handles matters in diverse forums, including litigation, arbitration, mediation, and negotiation, with a foundational principle of preparing every case as if destined for trial to build a robust factual record and adapt strategies accordingly.[31][3] This trial-centric approach has resulted in over 450 trials in U.S. federal and state courts, as well as proceedings in England and Italy, alongside participation in more than 200 international arbitrations.[3] The practice prioritizes pragmatic resolution, assessing litigation costs, risks, and benefits to advise settlement when aligned with client objectives or advancing to verdict when strategic advantages warrant it. Expertise spans industries without limitation, but centers on demanding commercial conflicts requiring deep investigative work, compelling advocacy, and cross-border coordination.[31][32] Chambers USA recognizes the firm's proficiency in such disputes, noting its in-house talent for dissecting intricate cases and delivering precise legal writing.[32] Within this core focus, antitrust litigation forms a pillar, encompassing criminal and civil enforcement, merger challenges, and private actions for clients in technology, healthcare, and other sectors.[33][34] Securities disputes, product liability, and class actions also feature prominently, with the firm securing outcomes in high-profile cases through aggressive trial tactics and appellate preservation of issues.[35][36] White-collar defense and government investigations integrate into the practice, leveraging the same emphasis on trial readiness to navigate regulatory scrutiny and criminal proceedings.[37] This breadth underscores a commitment to outcome-driven representation over rote procedural handling.

Specialized practices and client strategies

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP maintains specialized litigation practices in areas such as antitrust, technology disputes, crisis management, sports and gaming, and art and cultural assets, tailoring expertise to high-stakes, industry-specific challenges. In antitrust matters, the firm advises on complex enforcement actions and mergers, earning recognition for its capabilities in Florida, where it handles investigations and litigation for corporate clients navigating regulatory scrutiny.[33] The technology practice focuses on intellectual property, data privacy, and consumer class actions, representing innovators in disputes involving software, cybersecurity, and digital platforms.[38] Crisis management involves rapid response to public scandals and congressional inquiries, integrating legal defense with media and legislative strategies for executives and companies in sectors like media, finance, and consumer goods.[39] In sports and gaming, the firm counsels premier organizations on commercial contracts, regulatory compliance, and disputes, leveraging experience in league governance and athlete representation.[40] The art and cultural assets group addresses ownership claims, valuations, fraud, and repatriation issues for collectors and institutions, often in cross-border contexts.[41] Additional niches include aerospace and satellite operations, covering investment protection and space-related contracts, as well as professional malpractice defense for law firms facing high-exposure claims.[42][43] Client strategies emphasize lean, entrepreneurial team structures that foster direct partner involvement and cost efficiency, with associates encouraged to build relationships and seek assignments proactively.[44] The firm develops cases methodically for trial while assessing litigation risks and alternatives, representing clients as plaintiffs or defendants across U.S. courts, international arbitrations, and forums in England and Italy.[3] For global matters, multi-jurisdictional plans coordinate resources from offices in New York, Washington, D.C., London, and Milan, prioritizing aggressive, creative tactics like bespoke e-discovery to secure early advantages.[45][46] This approach extends to private clients, devising integrated strategies for asset protection and disputes involving high-net-worth individuals.[46]

Notable Representations

Political and electoral cases

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP gained prominence in political litigation through David Boies's representation of Vice President Al Gore in the 2000 Florida presidential election recount. Following machine tallies showing a narrow lead for George W. Bush, Gore's team, led by Boies, sought manual recounts in select counties, arguing that undervotesâ€”ballots not registering a presidential choice in initial scansâ€”warranted further scrutiny under Florida law. Boies argued before the Florida Supreme Court on December 7, 2000, advocating for expanded recounts, which the court partially approved by ordering a statewide manual recount of undervotes.[47] The U.S. Supreme Court, in Bush v. Gore (531 U.S. 98), halted the recount on December 12, 2000, in a 5-4 decision, citing equal protection concerns over varying recount standards, effectively securing Florida's 25 electoral votes for Bush and determining the presidential outcome.[48] This case, unsuccessful for Gore, highlighted the firm's early expertise in high-stakes election disputes.[49] In 2018, the firm, led by Boies, coordinated with the League of United Latin American Citizens (LULAC) to file four federal lawsuits in California, Texas, Pennsylvania, and Michigan challenging states' winner-take-all allocation of Electoral College votes. The suits, initiated on February 21, 2018, contended that this method violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment by disproportionately diluting the voting power of minority groups, such as Latino voters, whose preferences often diverged from statewide majorities.[50] The complaints sought proportional allocation of electors based on congressional district results or popular vote shares to enhance representational equity. The cases were dismissed on grounds including lack of standing and failure to overcome the constitutional tradition of winner-take-all systems, established since the 19th century in most states.[49] The firm has continued involvement in ballot access and referendum disputes. On August 2, 2024, Boies Schiller Flexner filed suit in New York state court against the New York Board of Elections, alleging that the ballot language for a proposed Equal Rights Amendment (ERA) referendum violated state readability standards under Election Law Â§ 16-106 by using overly complex phrasing that obscured the measure's implications for rights like abortion and discrimination protections.[51] [52] The litigation aimed to compel revised, clearer wording ahead of the November 2024 ballot, reflecting ongoing efforts to influence electoral presentation of constitutional amendments. Earlier, firm attorneys submitted an amicus brief in the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit supporting ratification efforts by Nevada, Illinois, and Virginia for the ERA, arguing against time limits on congressional approval.[53] These cases underscore the firm's focus on constitutional challenges intersecting with electoral processes, though outcomes have varied amid judicial deference to established voting mechanisms.

Corporate disputes and antitrust matters

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP has represented clients in high-stakes antitrust litigation, securing significant settlements and defending against challenges in both plaintiff and defendant roles. The firm acted as lead counsel in In re Auction Houses Antitrust Litigation (S.D.N.Y.), achieving a $512 million settlement against Sotheby's and Christie's for price-fixing conspiracies in the sale of fine art, wine, and other collectibles from 1993 to 2000.[54] In 2024, the U.S. Supreme Court rejected appeals challenging a $2.67 billion settlement the firm helped negotiate in antitrust claims against the Blue Cross Blue Shield Association, where subscribers alleged plans conspired to suppress competition and inflate premiums through restrictive membership rules.[55] The firm has pursued antitrust claims in emerging markets, including representing Panini America in a lawsuit against Fanatics, Inc., filed in 2022, alleging monopolistic practices and unfair competition in the sports trading cards industry, such as exclusive NFL and NBA licensing deals that excluded competitors.[33] In May 2025, Boies Schiller Flexner filed class actions under RICO and antitrust laws against major claims administrators (including Crawford & Company and Sedgwick) and banks like JPMorgan Chase, accusing them of a scheme to steer mass-tort settlements toward affiliated entities, creating an anticompetitive "self-dealing" network that inflated fees and limited client options.[56] In corporate disputes, the firm has handled complex commercial litigation, including a 2010 settlement for salesforce.com in a patent infringement suit brought by Microsoft, resolving claims over software features without admitting liability.[57] Boies Schiller Flexner also secured a $7 million settlement in 2023 for plaintiffs in an antitrust suit involving media outlets like The Salt Lake Tribune, targeting defendants for anticompetitive practices in local advertising markets, though full details of the underlying claims remain tied to ongoing journalistic coverage.[58] These representations underscore the firm's role in prosecuting and defending against alleged market manipulations, often leveraging government antitrust expertise from partners' prior DOJ and FTC tenures.[59]

Entertainment and high-profile individual clients

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP has represented prominent figures and entities in the entertainment sector, including film producer Harvey Weinstein, whom the firm advised for over a decade prior to his 2017 sexual misconduct allegations and subsequent conviction.[8] The firm's involvement with Weinstein drew internal criticism and contributed to partner departures, as some attorneys cited ethical concerns over defending a client accused of serial abuse amid shifting public and legal scrutiny.[8] In media and First Amendment litigation, the firm has defended major outlets in high-stakes defamation and content disputes, leveraging its expertise to protect journalistic interests against libel claims and regulatory challenges.[60] This practice extends to advising entertainment companies on free speech issues arising from controversial publications or broadcasts. The firm has also handled cases for high-profile individuals in sports entertainment, representing New York Yankees owner George Steinbrenner in disputes with Major League Baseball and the team itself in broadcast rights conflicts involving the YES Network.[40] Additional sports clients include NASCAR, the National Football League, and the Dallas Cowboys, where Boies Schiller provided litigation support in labor, antitrust, and ownership matters central to the industry's commercial operations.[40] David Boies, a founding partner, represented Virginia Giuffre, a victim in the Jeffrey Epstein sex-trafficking scandal, pursuing claims against Epstein and associates, including a high-profile defamation suit against Alan Dershowitz that settled in 2022.[61] This representation underscored the firm's role in victim advocacy within entertainment-adjacent scandals involving elite networks.[61]

Key Personnel

Founding and senior partners

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP was established in 1997 by prominent litigators David Boies and Jonathan D. Schiller in New York City.[11][44] The founders, who had collaborated previously and built mutual trust through joint professional experiences, aimed to create a boutique firm specializing in high-stakes litigation without the constraints of larger institutional practices.[11] In December 1999, antitrust specialist Donald Flexner joined as a named partner, expanding the firm's name to Boies, Schiller & Flexner LLP and bolstering its expertise in complex commercial disputes.[62] Boies, known for representing Al Gore in the 2000 U.S. presidential election recount and challenging Microsoft in antitrust proceedings, serves as founding partner and chairman emeritus, retaining a permanent seat on the executive committee.[63] Schiller, who has overseen international expansions including offices in London and Milan, holds the role of founding partner and managing partner, also maintaining executive committee membership.[64] Current senior leadership includes managing partners Sigrid McCawley, Stuart Singer, and Matthew L. Schwartz, who guide the firm's operations across its U.S. and international offices.[35] These partners, alongside the founders, direct strategy in litigation-heavy practices, with the firm maintaining approximately 77 partners and 92 other lawyers as of recent rankings.[35]

Notable alumni and departures

Nicholas Gravante Jr., who served as co-managing partner of Boies Schiller Flexner from December 2019 until December 2020, departed alongside partners Philip Iovieno, Karen Dyer, and Lawrence Brandman to join Cadwalader, Wickersham & Taft, where Gravante became head of the commercial litigation practice.[65][66] Natasha Harrison, deputy chair and managing partner of the London office, relinquished her leadership role in September 2021 and left the firm in January 2022 to launch Harrison Law, LLP, recruiting nearly all personnel from Boies Schiller's London branch.[67][68] Karen Dunn, a management committee member and appellate specialist who argued cases before the U.S. Supreme Court, exited in June 2020 for Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison, accompanied by vice chair William Isaacson.[69][70] Susan Estrich, a media commentator and former political strategist who represented Roger Ailes at the firm, departed in 2020 to co-found Estrich Goldin, LLP.[71] In 2019, partners Kyle Roche, Jason Cyrulnik, and Vel Freedman left to form Roche Freedman, LLP, which pursued class actions against cryptocurrency entities including Ripple Labs and obtained a $1.4 billion judgment against BitMEX in 2022.[72] Fifteen partners, led by figures such as Damien Marshall and Quyen Ta, transitioned en masse to King & Spalding in April 2020, contributing to the firm's recruitment of 33 lawyers overall from Boies Schiller.[73] Other alumni include Ann O'Leary, who joined Jenner & Block in 2018 before serving as chief of staff to Vice President Kamala Harris, and Lee Wolosky, who moved to Jenner & Block and later engaged in national security advisory roles.[72]

Controversies

Ethical conflicts and dual representations

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP and its founding partner David Boies have encountered ethical scrutiny over dual representations that potentially compromised client loyalties. In 2017, Boies personally assisted client Harvey Weinstein in efforts to suppress investigative reporting on allegations of sexual misconduct, including contracting with the private intelligence firm Black Cube to identify and discredit potential sources, while Boies Schiller simultaneously served as outside counsel to The New York Times, whose reporters were among those targeted.[74] This arrangement prompted the Times to terminate its relationship with the firm, describing the conduct as a "grave betrayal of trust" that violated ethical duties of candor and loyalty under rules like New York Rule of Professional Conduct 1.7, which prohibits representations with concurrent conflicts absent informed consent.[75] Boies acknowledged the investigators' tactics exceeded instructions but maintained no direct conflict existed, as the work focused on non-Times journalists; ethics experts, however, argued the dual roles created an unacceptable risk of divided allegiance, particularly given the firm's ongoing advisory role to the newspaper.[74][76] In the Theranos matter, Boies held concurrent roles as a company director, shareholder, and outside counsel from around 2011, representing Theranos in patent disputes and other litigation while owing fiduciary duties to shareholders as a board member, which legal analysts identified as an inherent conflict under corporate governance standards and ethics rules like ABA Model Rule 1.7, as his personal financial stake could impair objective advice to the company.[77] This duality came under examination during Elizabeth Holmes's 2021 criminal trial, where prosecutors questioned Boies's joint representation claims over communications with Holmes, arguing it blurred lines between corporate and individual counsel and potentially shielded privileged discussions amid fraud allegations.[78] Boies defended the arrangement as aligned incentives rather than a conflict, but critics, including in post-scandal analyses, highlighted how it may have delayed accountability for Theranos's misleading practices, contributing to the firm's reputational strain without formal disciplinary action.[79][80] The firm faced another apparent conflict in 2022 during FirstEnergy Corp. shareholder derivative litigation stemming from a bribery scandal, when Boies Schiller applied to represent shareholder plaintiffs suing directors for breach of fiduciary duties in U.S. District Court before Judge John Adams, even though the firm had defended former FirstEnergy vice president Eileen Mikkelsen in parallel derivative proceedings before Judge Algenon Marbley and signed her $180 million settlement agreement in March of that year.[81] This overlap risked adverse interests, as pursuing claims against directors could implicate Mikkelsen's prior conduct, violating conflict rules under ABA Model Rule 1.9 governing former client duties; the firm withdrew its application upon identification of the issue, allowing new counsel to proceed with discovery.[81] No court sanctions resulted, but the episode underscored recurring challenges in the firm's high-stakes, multi-party engagements.[82]

Criticisms of case selections and outcomes

The firm's representation of Harvey Weinstein amid allegations of sexual misconduct has been a focal point of criticism, with detractors arguing that selecting such a client prioritized financial gain over ethical considerations and firm reputation. In October 2017, following The New York Times and The New Yorker exposÃ©s detailing dozens of women's accusations against Weinstein, David Boies hired the Kroll agency to investigate accusers and journalists, including efforts to monitor actress Ambra Battilana Gutierrez.[76] The New York Times described this as a "grave betrayal of trust" and "intolerable" conduct, while ethics experts contended it violated professional norms by undermining sources rather than mounting a substantive defense.[76] [83] Weinstein's criminal convictions in New York (May 2020, rape and assault) and Los Angeles (December 2022, rape) underscored the case's unsuccessful outcome for the firm, with Weinstein's 23-year sentence affirmed on appeal in June 2024.[84] Critics have also targeted the firm's involvement with Theranos, where Boies served on the board from 2015 and provided legal representation during the company's unraveling amid federal fraud investigations. The selection drew scrutiny for aligning with a client later exposed for misleading investors about blood-testing technology, culminating in Elizabeth Holmes's conviction on four wire fraud counts in January 2022 and a sentence of over 11 years.[85] Boies's dual roles were faulted for potential conflicts, as the firm advised on disclosures while the board concealed issues, contributing to a reputational hit evidenced by partner departures citing unease with such engagements.[8] Additional rebukes have centered on associations with Jeffrey Epstein-related litigation, where Boies represented accuser Virginia Giuffre against Alan Dershowitz but faced questions over prior Epstein referrals and exploratory schemes to solicit funds from Epstein-linked individuals, raising ethics flags about opportunistic case pursuit.[86] In a 2020 malpractice suit, former clients accused Boies and partner Michael Underhill of overly aggressive tactics in a trade-secrets dispute, including unfounded threats that allegedly prolonged litigation without merit, leading to claims of fiduciary breaches.[87] These episodes have fueled broader arguments that the firm's appetite for headline-grabbing, high-risk mattersâ€”often defending powerful figures accused of grave wrongdoingâ€”has yielded suboptimal results, internal attrition, and suits questioning strategic choices, though Boies has defended such representations as essential to adversarial justice.[79]

Internal firm issues and partner exits

In 2020, Boies Schiller Flexner LLP experienced a significant exodus of partners, with 15 departing to join King & Spalding in April, contributing to a broader wave where over 50 partners left in the first eight months of the year and more than 80 partners and counsel exited since January 2019.[73][9] Insiders attributed these departures to internal management tensions, including dissatisfaction with firm leadership's handling of high-profile representations such as Theranos and Harvey Weinstein, where decisions by founders David Boies and Jonathan Schiller reportedly overrode the management committee's efforts on succession planning and ethical guidelines.[8] The firm responded to the partner losses by terminating administrative staff and associates, reflecting financial strain from reduced partnership revenue.[88] A notable internal conflict arose in early 2021 involving partner Joshua Schiller, son of co-founder Jonathan Schiller, who was arrested on charges of assault and criminal mischief related to an alleged domestic violence incident; the firm's internal investigation into the matter divided senior partners, exacerbating leadership struggles and contributing to further exits.[89] Additional departures followed, including two partners in January 2021 amid a leadership shake-up and three New York-based partners in May 2022, one of whom was co-general counsel.[90][91] Reports also surfaced of interpersonal issues, such as allegations of bullying by Jonathan Schiller toward associates and senior partners, which sources described as demeaning behavior that undermined firm morale.[92] Leadership transitions compounded these challenges, with deputy chair Natasha Harrison stepping down in September 2021 despite being viewed as a potential successor to David Boies, and Boies announcing plans to relinquish his chairman role by the end of 2024.[93][94] These events highlighted systemic issues in succession planning at elite litigation boutiques reliant on founding partners, with ongoing partner attrition through 2022, including in the London office, though the firm continued selective hiring to stabilize operations.[95][30]

Legacy and Influence

Contributions to legal precedents

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP has contributed to legal precedents primarily through its involvement in high-stakes appellate litigation, particularly before the U.S. Supreme Court, in areas such as securities regulation, constitutional rights, antitrust enforcement, and foreign sovereign immunity. In the securities domain, the firm represented investors in Halliburton Co. v. Erica P. John Fund, Inc. (2014), where partner David Boies argued that the Court should preserve the fraud-on-the-market presumption of reliance established in Basic Inc. v. Levinson (1988). The unanimous decision upheld the presumptionâ€”allowing securities class actions to proceed without individualized proof of relianceâ€”but required defendants to rebut it with direct evidence of no price impact from misstatements, refining class certification standards under Rule 10b-5 while rejecting calls to overturn Basic.[96][97] In constitutional law, the firm played a pivotal role in advancing marriage equality precedents. As co-counsel with Theodore Olson in Hollingsworth v. Perry (2013), Boies Schiller Flexner challenged California's Proposition 8 ban on same-sex marriage, securing a district court ruling that it violated equal protection and due process. The Supreme Court's dismissal for lack of proponent standing effectively reinstated same-sex marriage in California, creating a circuit split that influenced United States v. Windsor (2013) and culminated in Obergefell v. Hodges (2015), which mandated nationwide recognition of such unions. This litigation established key frameworks for scrutinizing state ballot initiatives under the Fourteenth Amendment and standing limitations in defender-of-marriage-act challenges.[98] The firm's antitrust practice has shaped class action and monopolization precedents. In private follow-on litigation to United States v. Microsoft Corp. (2001), partners advanced arguments reinforcing liability under Section 2 of the Sherman Act for predatory conduct in bundling and market exclusion, influencing subsequent circuit court rulings on tying arrangements and innovation harm assessments. Additionally, in In re Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation, the firm secured a $4.1 billion recovery for merchants against Visa and Mastercard in 2013â€”the largest private antitrust settlement at the timeâ€”establishing benchmarks for certifying nationwide classes in horizontal price-fixing claims involving network effects.[5] In international law, Boies Schiller Flexner represented heirs in Cassirer v. Thyssen-Bornemisza Collection Foundation (2022), where the Supreme Court unanimously held that U.S. courts must apply federal common law choice-of-law analysis under the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act's (FSIA) expropriation exception, rejecting forum law's default application and favoring the law of the situs (Germany) for Nazi-looted art claims. This clarified FSIA procedures, prioritizing prescriptive comity while vacating and remanding for reevaluation, and marked a second Supreme Court victory for the clients in 2025 via a grant-vacate-remand order.[99][100]

Broader impact on litigation trends

Boies Schiller Flexner LLP has contributed to the resurgence of aggressive antitrust litigation against technology companies, exemplified by its role in high-profile cases that echo the firm's foundational involvement in the U.S. government's 1998 Microsoft antitrust suit, where partner David Boies served as lead counsel and helped establish precedents for challenging monopolistic practices in software markets.[101][102] More recently, the firm represented Rumble in a 2025 Google antitrust trial preparation and secured a settlement requiring Google to delete billions of user tracking records from incognito mode activities, reinforcing trends toward heightened scrutiny of data privacy and market dominance in digital platforms.[101][103] The firm's emphasis on contingency fee arrangements in complex disputes has mirrored and amplified a broader shift in litigation economics, where over 40% of its 2024 revenue derived from such payouts, enabling pursuit of high-risk, high-reward cases without upfront client funding and incentivizing firms to target deep-pocketed defendants in antitrust and intellectual property matters.[29] This model, yielding a 57.5% gross revenue increase to undisclosed figures in 2024 amid growing demand for trial-ready expertise, has encouraged the proliferation of specialized boutiques over traditional full-service firms for bet-the-company litigation, as seen in alumni spin-offs adopting similar lean, plaintiff-oriented structures.[29][104] In class actions, Boies Schiller has positioned itself at the forefront of consumer and privacy-driven suits, earning recognition as a leading group for securing multimillion-dollar resolutions, such as a $65.5 million au pair wage settlement in 2023 and ongoing antitrust class actions against claims administrators alleging anticompetitive schemes.[103][105] These outcomes reflect and propel trends toward expansive class certifications in tech and labor disputes, where firms leverage e-discovery and cross-jurisdictional strategies to aggregate claims against corporate giants.[45][56] Amid a litigation landscape where fewer than 2% of civil cases reach trialâ€”driven by escalating costs and alternative dispute resolutionâ€”the firm's maintenance of a robust trial practice, including multiple back-to-back trials for partners, counters the pervasive settlement culture by demonstrating the value of jury-ready advocacy in high-stakes matters.[106][107] David Boies has observed that practitioners now "litigate toward a settlement," with many large-firm litigators lacking jury trial experience, yet Boies Schiller's boutique approach sustains pressure for accountability through credible trial threats, influencing a hybrid model blending negotiation with preparedness.[106][107]
