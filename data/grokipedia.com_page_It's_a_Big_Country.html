It's a Big Country

It's a Big Country: An American Anthology is a 1951 American portmanteau film produced by Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer, comprising eight independent vignettes directed by seven filmmakers that portray diverse slices of life across the United States.[1][2] The segments, linked thematically by a framing device questioning "which America," highlight themes of opportunity, community, and national unity through stories ranging from an elderly immigrant's pride in her adopted homeland to a veteran's return and a courtroom drama on equality.[1][3] Directed by Clarence Brown, Don Weis, John Sturges, Richard Thorpe, Charles Schnee, William A. Wellman, and with an uncredited contribution from Robert Rossen, the film features a star-studded ensemble cast including Ethel Barrymore as a devoted naturalized citizen, Gary Cooper in a brief appearance, Gene Kelly as a performer, William Powell as a reflective traveler, Van Johnson as a soldier, and Nancy Davis (later Nancy Reagan) in a supporting role.[2][1] Released in the early Cold War era, it reflects post-World War II patriotic sentiments, emphasizing America's melting-pot ethos and individual freedoms, though its episodic structure drew mixed reviews for uneven quality among the shorts.[4][3] Despite lacking major awards, the production stands out for assembling top Hollywood talent in a collaborative anthology format intended to affirm national identity amid global ideological conflicts.[2]

Production

Development and Intent

It's a Big Country originated as a project at Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer under the oversight of Dore Schary, who assumed the role of production head in 1951 after Louis B. Mayer's departure from the studio.[5] The concept drew from an earlier sketch by Lucille Schlossberg, originally acquired for RKO's Till the End of Time in 1946 and subsequently adapted by screenwriter Allen Rivkin, reflecting MGM's interest in anthology formats to highlight varied American narratives.[5] Producer Robert Sisk managed the production, aiming to craft a film that would appeal to audiences by demonstrating the breadth of U.S. citizenship through distinct episodes.[5] Schary explicitly framed the film as a "propaganda picture" and "message picture," designed to underscore national unity amid Cold War tensions with the Soviet Union, where collectivist ideologies posed ideological challenges to American individualism.[5] In the post-World War II era, as U.S. cultural exports faced scrutiny abroad, the production sought to promote assimilation and personal achievement by depicting geographic, ethnic, and ideological diversity across vignettes, countering foreign narratives that portrayed America as divided or materialistic.[5] This approach aligned with broader studio efforts to foster patriotic self-promotion, emphasizing pluralism without overt confrontation to suit international markets.[5] Creative decisions included employing seven directors for the eight segmentsâ€”Clarence Brown, Don Hartman, John Sturges, Richard Thorpe, Charles Schnee, William Wellman, and Joseph Loseyâ€”to mirror the nation's multifaceted identities and leverage star power for commercial viability, while streamlining production efficiency in an era of rising costs.[5] One planned episode featuring African-American baseball players was ultimately excised to sidestep potential domestic backlash, illustrating the balance between inclusive intent and pragmatic constraints.[5] These choices prioritized a cohesive portrayal of American exceptionalism rooted in individual agency over state-directed uniformity.[5]

Plot Segments

Episode Summaries and Themes

The anthology film It's a Big Country consists of eight segments, each portraying vignettes of American life that underscore motifs of individual initiative, diverse contributions to national identity, and the pursuit of opportunity across social, ethnic, and regional lines.[6] In the opening segment directed by Richard Thorpe, a loquacious traveler (James Whitmore) engages a reserved professor (William Powell) on a transcontinental train, extolling the virtues of America until the professor counters that the nation encompasses multifaceted "Americas"â€”political, historical, and culturalâ€”culminating in an elderly woman's proud declaration that prompts the query, "Which America?" This frames the film's exploration of pluralistic experiences.[6] The second segment, helmed by John Sturges, follows elderly Irish-American widow Mrs. Brian O'Riordan in Boston, who discovers she was omitted from the 1950 U.S. Census and seeks redress through a newspaper editor; a reporter's feigned official visit evolves into genuine advocacy, securing congressional attention and her proper enumeration, highlighting bureaucratic resolution through persistent personal effort.[6] Segment three adopts a documentary format, presenting a montage of African American accomplishments, including military cadets, uniformed women, and notable figures in various fields, to illustrate collective progress and integration into broader societal roles without a scripted narrative.[6] Directed by Charles Vidor, the fourth segment, "Rosika, the Rose," depicts a Hungarian immigrant family's initial resistance to their daughter's inter-ethnic marriage, which resolves in familial reconciliation, emphasizing adaptation and harmony amid cultural blending.[8] In Don Weis's "Letter from Korea," a Jewish tailor named Maxie Klein receives correspondence from his son serving in the Korean War, evoking pride in the young man's service and the intergenerational embrace of American duties.[2] Clarence Brown's sixth segment features Gary Cooper as a Texas rancher who directly addresses the audience to dispel exaggerated myths about the state's size, bravado, and customs, portraying a grounded regional pride rooted in everyday realities.[2] William A. Wellman's "Minister in Washington" portrays a rural minister (Fredric March) in the capital recounting a wartime anecdote from World War II, where community solidarity during a crisis exemplifies moral leadership and national resilience.[8] The concluding "Four Eyes," directed by Don Hartman, centers on a teacher (Nancy Reagan) intervening to support an immigrant child bullied for wearing eyeglasses, facilitating educational access and challenging "old world" inhibitions against self-improvement in favor of American ideals of empowerment.[2] Across these narratives, recurring motifs include personal agency in navigating obstacles, the expansive opportunities for immigrants, minorities, and ordinary citizens, and a unifying national fabric woven from disparate individual triumphs.[6]

Historical Context

Cold War Motivations

It's a Big Country was released on November 20, 1951, at a time when the Korean War had intensified following the Chinese People's Volunteer Army's intervention in October 1950, which reversed United Nations advances and prolonged fierce combat through 1951, heightening American anxieties over communist expansion.[2][6] The film's anthology structure, comprising eight vignettes directed by prominent MGM talent, aligned with Hollywood's broader surge in anti-communist filmmaking during this period, exemplified by releases like I Was a Communist for the FBI earlier in 1951, which dramatized infiltration of domestic subversive networks.[9] These productions emphasized themes of individual agency and free enterprise as bulwarks against totalitarian ideologies, reflecting studio executives' efforts to navigate House Un-American Activities Committee scrutiny while bolstering national morale.[10] MGM production head Dore Schary spearheaded It's a Big Country as a deliberate "message picture" to underscore American cultural pluralism and unity, positioning the nation's vast geographic and social diversity as evidence of inherent superiority over Soviet-style collectivism.[10] Schary, who contributed to the screenplay, framed the film as propaganda celebrating immigrant assimilation and self-determination, drawing on post-World War II refugee experiences where over 400,000 displaced persons from Europe entered the U.S. under the 1948 Displaced Persons Act, many integrating into the workforce amid economic expansion.[10] This approach mirrored State Department initiatives in cultural diplomacy, which from the late 1940s promoted U.S. films abroad to showcase democratic vitality and counter Soviet narratives, though MGM's project remained primarily domestic in intent.[11] The vignettes' focus on personal initiative and opportunity resonated with 1950 Census Bureau data revealing a U.S. population of 150,697,361â€”up 15 percent from 1940â€”fueled by immigration, high birth rates, and industrial growth that enabled rapid upward mobility for newcomers, contrasting sharply with reports of stagnation in Eastern Bloc economies.[10] By highlighting stories of resilience across ethnic and regional lines, the film implicitly argued for the causal efficacy of American institutions in fostering prosperity, a narrative grounded in empirical postwar recovery metrics like a gross national product rise from $228 billion in 1945 to $300 billion by 1950, rather than imposed equality. Such motivations positioned the anthology as a cinematic affirmation of exceptionalism, prioritizing verifiable socioeconomic successes over ideological conformity.[10]

Portrayal of American Values

The anthology film It's a Big Country portrays American values by emphasizing the melting pot's dynamics, where ethnic and religious integration occurs through personal effort and voluntary adaptation rather than coercive state policies. In one segment directed by William Wellman, Ethel Barrymore plays Mrs. Riordan, an elderly Irish-American widow who initially rejects her son's engagement to a Protestant woman due to longstanding sectarian divides, but ultimately reconciles through self-reflection and family bonds, highlighting individual resilience in bridging cultural divides.[6] Similarly, Fredric March's vignette as an Italian immigrant father underscores pride in assimilation, as he celebrates his son's American-born achievements, reflecting how personal ambition within a free society fosters unity across heritages.[2] Meritocracy emerges in depictions of intellectual and entrepreneurial pursuits rewarded by individual merit, countering claims of inherent systemic barriers. A segment featuring Edward G. Robinson as a professor challenged by a student (Keefe Brasselle) on factual errors illustrates the value of free inquiry and accountability, where the younger man's evidence-based rebuttal prevails, affirming that competence, not pedigree, determines outcomes in open discourse. Gary Cooper's rancher character aids a lost Mexican youth, portraying self-reliant aid rooted in shared human endeavor rather than entitlement, which aligns with the film's celebration of verifiable successes like frontier ingenuity and military service by immigrants' descendants. These narratives causally link personal agency to societal strength, as unrestricted opportunity allows talent to rise independently of origin.[8] Resilience and upward mobility are tied to freedoms enabling recovery from adversity, with individual stories mirroring empirical patterns of immigrant success. The returning GI played by Van Johnson forgives his wife's infidelity, embodying forgiveness and recommitment amid postwar disruptions, while a reporter's encounter with a Native American (Chief Yowlachie) reveals liberty's essence in self-determination over imposed narratives. Such portrayals resonate with historical data: post-World War II America saw high intergenerational mobility, with children of immigrants exhibiting upward income rates exceeding those of U.S.-born families, driven by economic liberty and low barriers to entry rather than redistributive interventions. From 1947 to 1965, working-class and immigrant offspring experienced marked advancement, with absolute mobility rates peaking as freedoms post-Depression and war facilitated effort-based gains. Reception and Analysis

Contemporary Critical Response

Bosley Crowther, reviewing for The New York Times on January 9, 1952, characterized It's a Big Country as "a cheery, sentimental estimation of life in these United States, warmly wrapped up in an all-star package," praising its "generally pleasant little items" that offered amusing reflections of human foibles through vignettes featuring stars like Ethel Barrymore as a wise grandmother and Gary Cooper in a comic Texas episode.[4] He highlighted the episodic structure's diverting quality, including segments on immigration, racial tolerance, and everyday heroism, but deemed the film "uneven and generally disjointed" in execution.[4] Crowther's primary reservation centered on the narration by Louis Calhern, which he faulted for "shallow chauvinism" that evoked a "rocks-and-rills 'commercial'" with "blushless enthusiasm" and intrusive patriotic flourishes set against tunes like "God Bless America," rendering the tone overly promotional rather than substantive.[4] Absent this element, he concluded, the picture would qualify as "an easy film to watch and generally enjoy," though limited in depth and avoiding "points of critical controversy."[4] Trade and popular reception echoed this ambivalence, with the film often labeled a "message picture" emphasizing national unity and diversity amid Cold War imperatives, yet critiqued for propagandistic overreach that idealized America without probing its segregative realities.[14] Some outlets commended the star cameosâ€”spanning Spencer Tracy, Judy Garland, and othersâ€”for injecting vitality into the patriotic sketches, viewing them as wholesome affirmations of core values like opportunity and tolerance.[6] Overall, 1950s critics found merit in its episodic patriotism but consensus on flaws like sentimentality and superficial narration, reflecting broader unease with Hollywood's didactic anthology efforts.

Financial Performance

The production budget for It's a Big Country totaled $1,013,000.[2] According to Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer's internal records, the film generated $526,000 in distributor rentals from the US and Canada and $129,000 from international markets, for a worldwide total of $655,000. This outcome yielded a net loss of $677,000 after accounting for production costs and studio overhead. The anthology structure, featuring eight loosely connected vignettes rather than a unified storyline, likely constrained its commercial reach, appealing primarily to audiences interested in patriotic themes amid limited marketing emphasis on star power. Released on November 20, 1951, it competed against MGM's own high-profile spectacles, including Quo Vadis, which earned roughly $30 million domestically and averted deeper studio financial distress.[15] Other 1951 MGM releases like An American in Paris also posted strong returns, exceeding $4 million in domestic rentals and underscoring the uneven performance across the studio's slate. It's a Big Country's modest earnings reflected broader challenges in monetizing experimental formats during a year when epic narratives dominated box office leaders.[16]

Modern Interpretations

In scholarly analyses, It's a Big Country has been interpreted as a deliberate Cold War-era "message picture" designed to counter totalitarian ideologies by affirming American pluralism and individual opportunity, with producer Dore Schary explicitly framing it as propaganda to foster national cohesion amid global tensions.[5] Post-1950s reexaminations, such as those in film historiography, highlight its vignettesâ€”depicting immigrant integration and ethnic contributionsâ€”as prescient endorsements of assimilation processes empirically validated by longitudinal data showing successive immigrant generations achieving socioeconomic parity with natives through language acquisition, spatial dispersion, and occupational mobility.[17][18] These portrayals align with causal patterns of upward mobility observed in U.S. history, where barriers to entry diminish over generations without reliance on state-enforced equality. Progressive critiques, often rooted in deconstructive frameworks, fault the film for perpetuating a "myth of national unity" that glosses over contemporaneous racial and class fractures, portraying its optimism as naively assimilationist and insufficiently attuned to structural inequities.[5] Such deconstructions, prevalent in academic discourse, emphasize superficial treatments of diversity, attributing them to Hollywood's industrial imperatives rather than authentic pluralism. Evidence-based rebuttals counter that the film's emphasis on voluntary integration reflects real-world outcomes, including second-generation immigrants attaining earnings trajectories comparable to native-born populations by adulthood, underscoring the efficacy of opportunity-driven narratives over imposed collectivism.[19] Contemporary accessibility has facilitated renewed viewer engagement, with the film airing on Turner Classic Movies (TCM) and previously streaming on platforms like fuboTV, enabling platforms such as Letterboxd to host discussions reframing its "propaganda elements" as grounded depictions of melting-pot dynamics fostering freedom and equality.[20][21] User annotations on these sites often recast the anthology's pro-freedom ethosâ€”evident in stories of personal agency across ethnic linesâ€”as a realistic counter to totalitarian uniformity, supported by the film's alignment with documented assimilation successes rather than idealized fiction.[22]
