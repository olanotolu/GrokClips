Howrah–Nagpur–Mumbai line

The Howrah–Nagpur–Mumbai line (also known as Mumbai–Kolkata line) is a railway line in India connecting Kolkata and Mumbai via Nagpur. The 1,968-kilometre-long (1,223 mi) railway line was opened to traffic in 1900.

Geography

The Howrah–Nagpur–Mumbai line cuts across the central parts of India in an east–west direction and traverses the plains of lower West Bengal, the southern part of Chota Nagpur Plateau, the Deccan Plateau, the Western Ghats and finally the Western Coastal Plains. In the Chota Nagpur section, it passes through difficult and picturesque terrains, including the verdant Saranda forests with tunnels between Mahadevsal and Posoita stations.

History

The first train in India travelled from Bori Bunder in Bombay to Tannah (current Thane) on 16 April 1853. By May 1854, Great Indian Peninsula Railway's Bombay–Tannah line was extended to Callian (current Kalyan). Bhusawal station was set up in 1860 and in 1867 the GIPR branch line was extended to Nagpur. While the entire Mumbai–Nagpur line was 5 ft 6 in (1,676 mm) broad gauge, the next part from Nagpur to Rajnandgaon was metre gauge. The Nagpur Chhattisgarh Railway started construction of the 240 km (149 mi) Nagpur–Rajnandgaon section in 1878, after surveys were started in 1871. The Nagpur–Tumsar Road section was opened in April 1880 and the Tumsar Road–Rajnandgaon section in December 1880.[1] The GIPR and EIR, working jointly, completed the Howrah–Allahabad–Mumbai line thereby establishing a connection between Kolkata and Mumbai in 1870. The great famine of 1878 was an impetus for the fast completion of the Nagpur Chhattisgarh Railway track, but by then the idea of a route from Mumbai to Kolkata, shorter than the one via Allahabad, had set in. The Bengal Nagpur Railway was formed in 1887. Amongst its major objectives were taking over of the Nagpur Chhattisgarh Railway and its conversion to broad gauge—the metre gauge Nagpur–Rajnandgaon section was converted starting in 1888—and extension of its system by a 1,014 km (630 mi) line to Asansol on EIR's main line. The entire task was completed by 1891 and Nagpur was connected to Asansol. However, the line via Asansol was never seriously used as a link to Howrah for passenger traffic. The Sini–Kharagpur–Kolaghat line was opened in 1898–99. The Kolaghat–Howrah section was completed in 1899–1900. The entire line was opened with the completion of the bridge across the Rupnarayan River, near Kolaghat, on 19 April 1900.

Electrification

The entire line is electrified, with all sections operational on 25 kV 50 Hz AC overhead lines. Electrification was completed in phases: the South Eastern Railway portion by the early 2010s, South East Central Railway by 2020, and Central Railway fully by 2023, achieving 100% coverage across zones as of 2025.[2][3]

Speed limits

The Howrah–Nagpur–Mumbai line is classified as a Group A route, intended for speeds up to 160 km/h per a 2008 Indian Railways correction slip. However, as of 2025, the maximum permissible speed on the line remains 130 km/h, following enhancements under Mission Raftaar initiatives.[4]

Passenger movement

Several stations on the Howrah–Nagpur–Mumbai line are among India's busiest. As of fiscal year 2023–2024, the following stations on this route ranked in the top 100 by passenger revenue (a key indicator of booking and movement volume): Howrah (rank 2), Nagpur (rank 19), Kalyan (rank 21), Raipur (rank 54), Kharagpur (rank 73), Tatanagar (rank 77), Nashik Road (rank 78), Bilaspur (rank 85), and Durg (rank 98). [5] Stations such as Rourkela, Badnera, Akola, Bhusawal, and Manmad, previously noted as top booking stations, no longer appear in the top 100 by this metric.

Golden Quadrilateral

The Howrah–Nagpur–Mumbai line is a part of the Golden Quadrilateral. The routes connecting the four major metropolises (New Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai and Kolkata), along with their diagonals, known as the Golden Quadrilateral, carry 58 percent of the freight and 52 percent of the passenger traffic, although they form only 16 percent of the length.[6]
