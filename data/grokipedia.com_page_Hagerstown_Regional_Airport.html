Hagerstown Regional Airport


Hagerstown Regional Airport (IATA: HGR, ICAO: KHGR), officially designated Richard A. Henson Field, is a county-owned public-use airport located in Washington County, Maryland, United States, five miles north of Hagerstown and immediately adjacent to the Pennsylvania state line.[1][2][3]
Established in 1928 on farmland acquired for early aircraft manufacturing, it ranks among the oldest continuously operating airports in the country and historically hosted production facilities for companies such as Kreider-Reisner and Fairchild Aircraft.[4][5][6]
The facility spans 693 acres with a primary runway measuring 7,000 by 150 feetâ€”Maryland's second longestâ€”capable of accommodating jet aircraft, complemented by a 3,165-by-100-foot crosswind runway, and it functions as a reliever airport for the congested Washington-Baltimore airspace while supporting general aviation, cargo, and limited scheduled passenger service.[7][8][9]
Allegiant Air provides the airport's sole commercial passenger operations, offering nonstop flights to leisure destinations in Florida and South Carolina, with total passenger traffic reaching approximately 67,000 enplanements and deplanements in 2024 amid broader aviation recovery trends.[10][11][3]

Historical Development

Origins and Early Operations (1920sâ€“1940s)

The Hagerstown Regional Airport originated as a private airfield established on July 4, 1928, when the Kreider-Reisner Aircraft Company purchased 60 acres of farmland north of Hagerstown, Maryland, to support aircraft testing and production activities.[5][12] Initially known as Kreider-Reisner Field, the site facilitated the company's development of biplanes like the Challenger model, reflecting the era's burgeoning private-sector investment in aviation infrastructure driven by manufacturing needs rather than public subsidy.[6] In 1929, Sherman Fairchild acquired a majority interest in Kreider-Reisner, renaming it Fairchild Aircraft and initiating expansions that enhanced the airfield's capabilities for assembly, test flights, and limited early commercial operations.[12][13] Fairchild's integration shifted focus toward scalable production, including civilian and emerging military prototypes, with the facility becoming a hub for engineering innovations amid the Great Depression's economic constraints.[14] This private enterprise model underscored local industrial initiative, positioning the airport as integral to Hagerstown's economy without initial reliance on governmental expansion.[15] During World War II, the airfield supported national defense through Fairchild's manufacture of over 5,000 primary trainer aircraft, such as the PT-19 Cornell and PT-26 Cornell, which were tested on-site and supplied to U.S. military flight schools for pilot instruction.[6][16] Production also included cargo planes like the C-82 Packet, leveraging the airport's runways for operational evaluations and contributing to wartime readiness via efficient private contracting rather than centralized federal control.[6] These activities highlighted the site's practical role in aviation logistics, with local facilities enabling rapid prototyping and delivery amid global demands.[13] By 1933, amid shifting economic priorities, the City of Hagerstown acquired the airfield from Fairchild Aviation Corporation, marking its transition to public municipal ownership while preserving continuous operations as one of the nation's early enduring airports founded on industrial foundations.[12] This local governmental step ensured sustained viability through the late 1940s, prioritizing practical maintenance over expansive federal involvement.[4]

Postwar Commercialization and Growth (1950sâ€“1980s)

Following World War II, Hagerstown Regional Airport transitioned toward greater civilian use, with commercial air transport gradually expanding to meet regional demands from manufacturing and industrial activity in western Maryland. Scheduled passenger service commenced on October 1, 1962, when Henson Aviation launched operations as Hagerstown Commuter, providing daily flights between Hagerstown and Washington National Airport using small propeller aircraft.[4][17] This initiative, founded by local aviator Richard A. Henson, capitalized on the airport's proximity to industrial hubs like Fairchild Aircraft's facility, which produced regional airliners such as the F-27 Friendship from 1958 onward, indirectly bolstering aviation infrastructure and demand. Facility upgrades supported this nascent commercialization, including a major expansion in 1959 that constructed a new passenger terminal to accommodate growing traffic.[18] Local funding and operations emphasized self-reliance, with county-led improvements such as paving enhancements predating the period but enabling postwar viability without substantial federal subsidies. By the mid-1960s, Henson upgraded to Allegheny Commuter affiliations, operating 9-passenger Beech Queen-Air aircraft from November 1967, followed by 15-passenger Beech King Airs in 1969.[5] These developments reflected causal ties to Hagerstown's economic base in manufacturing, where general aviation and emerging cargo operations sustained activity amid limited initial enplanements. The 1970s and 1980s saw further growth through regional carriers, with Henson introducing 30-passenger Short 330 turboprops in October 1977 to handle increased demand.[5] Airfield enhancements, including a commissioned Instrument Landing System (ILS) for Runway 27 on September 16, 1976, high-intensity runway lights on May 22, 1978, and a Visual Approach Slope Indicator (VASI) for Runway 9 in November 1981, improved safety and capacity for short-haul flights.[5] The Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 facilitated efficient service by feeders like Henson, which benefited small airports by reducing reliance on trunk carriers and competing with larger hubs such as Baltimore-Washington International. Despite challenges from proximity to major facilities drawing higher-volume traffic, operations persisted via robust general aviationâ€”tied to local industryâ€”and cargo support for Hagerstown's factories, culminating in the airport's renaming to Washington County Regional Airport in 1981 to signify expanded regional role.[4]

Modernization and Challenges (1990sâ€“Present)

In the 1990s, Hagerstown Regional Airport encountered declining commercial passenger service amid post-deregulation competition from larger hubs like Baltimore-Washington International Airport, prompting a strategic shift toward general aviation and cargo operations to sustain viability. US Airways, the primary carrier, transported around 37,000 passengers annually in the mid-1990s via flights to its Baltimore and Pittsburgh hubs, but service contracted as the airline reoriented its network away from smaller fields lacking sufficient feeder traffic.[19][20] Washington County's 1981 acquisition of the airport from municipal control facilitated modernization efforts, including the 1998 renaming to Hagerstown Regional Airport â€“ Richard A. Henson Field in tribute to the longtime Fairchild test pilot and local aviation figure, which coincided with operational enhancements to bolster efficiency and attract non-commercial users. This pivot capitalized on the site's legacy in aircraft production, maintaining activity through general aviation amid commercial retrenchment.[12] Challenges intensified post-9/11 with reliance on federal Essential Air Service (EAS) subsidies for commuter connectivity, which proved unstable; the program ended in October 2019, terminating Southern Airways' subsidized daily flights to Baltimore and Hagerstown's eligibility for such support. Traffic dips persisted into the COVID-19 era, with 2020 volumes severely curtailed by pandemic restrictions.[21][22] Resilience emerged via unsubsidized low-cost carrier entry, as Allegiant Air launched seasonal and year-round service in June 2021 to leisure destinations like Orlando/Sanford and St. Pete-Clearwater, emphasizing point-to-point routes to sun spots for underserved regional demand. This market-oriented approach drove recovery, yielding a 46% passenger increase in 2021 over 2020 lows without dependency on bailouts or mandates.[23][22]

Physical Facilities and Capabilities

Runways, Taxiways, and Airfield Infrastructure

Hagerstown Regional Airport operates two asphalt runways designed for diverse weather conditions and aircraft types. The primary runway, 09/27, measures 7,000 feet in length by 150 feet in width and supports precision instrument approaches via instrument landing systems (ILS) at both ends, enabling operations in low-visibility scenarios.[24][25] The secondary crosswind runway, 02/20, is 3,165 feet by 100 feet, providing directional flexibility for wind variations and accommodating smaller general aviation aircraft with weight-bearing capacities up to 170,000 pounds for double-tandem gear.[25][26] The airfield encompasses 693 acres, facilitating safe runway-taxiway separations and compliance with Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) standards for non-hub primary airports.[7] Taxiway infrastructure includes multiple connectors, with recent rehabilitations enhancing pavement integrity; in October 2025, state and federal funding supported projects on three taxiways, including Taxiway A pavement rehabilitation and hangar taxi lane improvements, as part of ongoing maintenance to sustain operational efficiency.[27][28] Supporting airfield operations, the airport provides Jet A and Avgas fueling, over 40,000 square feet of heated hangar space suitable for aircraft up to Gulfstream class, and maintenance services through fixed-base operators, ensuring readiness for general aviation, regional jets, and occasional larger transient aircraft.[29][30] These elements collectively enable reliable handling of the airport's annual operations exceeding 50,000, focused on regional connectivity without disproportionate infrastructure scale.[9]

Passenger Terminal and Ground Services

The passenger terminal at Hagerstown Regional Airport (HGR) features a compact layout designed for efficient processing of low-volume commercial traffic, primarily serving Allegiant Air's leisure routes. Recent expansions have added seating areas and weather-protected hold rooms, along with an enlarged TSA security checkpoint spanning approximately 1,775 square feet to accommodate screening needs.[31][1] Amenities within the terminal include free Wi-Fi access, a snack bar called "The Flight Deck" offering prepackaged snacks, candies, chips, and drinks, as well as vending machines for additional refreshments. Rental car services are available on-site, facilitating ground transportation for arriving passengers. Baggage handling is streamlined for Allegiant's operations, with check-in primarily handled online or via self-service kiosks due to limited staffed ticket counter hours that vary monthly.[3][32][33] Security screening at HGR benefits from minimal congestion, with average wait times under one minute, enabling passengers to arrive two hours prior to departure as recommended by TSA and airline guidelines. Parking options emphasize affordability and convenience, with short- and long-term lots directly in front of the terminal charging a maximum of $5 per day after the first free hour, a structure implemented in early 2025 to support maintenance without relying on local taxes.[34][35][32] As an enterprise fund managed by Washington County, HGR operates on a self-sustaining basis, funding terminal and ground services through user fees and revenues rather than taxpayer subsidies common at larger airports. This model aligns with the facility's focus on cost-effective services, including short overall processing times that contrast with delays at high-traffic hubs.[36][37][38]

Air Service and Connectivity

Current Airlines and Destinations

As of October 2025, Hagerstown Regional Airport (HGR) is served exclusively by Allegiant Air, a low-cost carrier specializing in point-to-point routes to leisure destinations, with no service from major network airlines or hubs.[39][3] This model targets underserved regional demand in western Maryland and surrounding areas, offering affordable nonstop flights without connections, prioritizing operational efficiency over extensive route networks.[1] Allegiant operates year-round nonstop service from HGR to three primary destinations: Orlando/Sanford International Airport (SFB) in Florida, St. Petersburg/Clearwater International Airport (PIE) in Florida, and Myrtle Beach International Airport (MYR) in South Carolina.[40][41] These routes cater to vacation travel, with frequencies adjusted seasonally to match demand; for example, flights to MYR and PIE typically increase during peak summer and fall periods.[42] Service expansions have driven growth, reflecting market responsiveness. In 2024, Allegiant added 81 flights, increasing total round trips to 293â€”a 38% rise from 212 in 2023â€”primarily boosting capacity to PIE and MYR.[43] Passenger enplanements grew by approximately 30% in 2024 compared to 2023, reaching over 43,000 departures.[11] For spring through fall 2025, Allegiant announced further schedule enhancements, including increased frequencies to popular Sunshine State and coastal routes to sustain momentum.[44][45]

Historical Airline Operations

Scheduled commercial passenger service at Hagerstown Regional Airport commenced on October 1, 1962, when Henson Aviation launched Hagerstown Commuter operations using small piston-engine aircraft to connect the airport with Washington National Airport in the nation's capital.[46][17] This low-cost, high-frequency model targeted local demand in the absence of larger carrier interest, reflecting the era's reliance on independent commuter feeders for small-market access.[47] By November 15, 1967, Henson affiliated with Allegheny Airlines as the inaugural Allegheny Commuter partner, inaugurating service with a 9-passenger Beechcraft Queen Air on the Hagerstown-Washington route; this marked a pivotal shift to codeshare integration, boosting connectivity and passenger volumes through Allegheny's expanding network.[5][48] Fleet expansions followed, with 15-passenger Beechcraft King Airs introduced in 1969 and 30-passenger Short Brothers 330 turboprops added in October 1977, enabling service to additional Allegheny hubs amid growing regional demand.[5] As Allegheny rebranded to USAir in 1979, Henson continued as USAir Express, maintaining short-haul routes primarily to Baltimore-Washington International and Pittsburgh International Airports using turboprop aircraft suited to the airport's infrastructure.[5] The 1990s and early 2000s saw progressive route curtailments under USAir (later US Airways), driven by deregulation-induced competition from low-cost carriers, escalating fuel prices, and passenger leakage to proximate major airports like BWI and Dulles, which offered more destinations at lower effective costs.[49] By the mid-2000s, subsidized Essential Air Service (EAS) propped up minimal commuter flights via US Airways Express subsidiaries, but low ridershipâ€”often below 10 passengers per departureâ€”prompted discontinuations, including the final Pittsburgh route around 2008, shifting emphasis toward general aviation and occasional cargo operations.[50] Pre-Allegiant era operations thus underscored small-market vulnerabilities, with brief charters (e.g., international relief flights) supplementing but not sustaining scheduled service amid dominant non-commercial activity tied to the region's manufacturing legacy.[5]

Traffic Statistics and Trends

Annual Passenger and Cargo Volumes

The Federal Aviation Administration records annual commercial passenger enplanements at Hagerstown Regional Airport as follows: 29,105 in calendar year 2019, dropping to 14,373 in 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic (-50.62% change), recovering to 23,227 in 2021 (+61.60%), 32,197 in 2022 (+38.62%), 32,331 in 2023 (+0.42%), and preliminarily 41,508 in 2024 (+28.38%).[51][52][53][54][55] These figures reflect the airport's status as a primary non-hub commercial service facility, with post-pandemic rebound exceeding 2019 levels by 2022 amid stabilized regional service primarily to Florida and other leisure destinations.[54]

Peak Destinations and Seasonal Patterns

Hagerstown Regional Airport's peak destinations consist primarily of leisure-focused routes to Florida and South Carolina, served exclusively by Allegiant Air nonstop flights to St. Pete-Clearwater International Airport (PIE), Orlando/Sanford International Airport (SFB), and North Myrtle Beach Grand Strand Airport (CRE).[39] [3] These three routes handle the airport's entire scheduled commercial passenger traffic, with PIE recording the highest departure frequency at an average of one flight daily.[59] Seasonal patterns feature elevated flight frequencies during summer periods to accommodate tourism demand to beach destinations. In 2025, from May 13 to September 2, the airport adds 18 extra flights to Myrtle Beach and St. Pete-Clearwater, increasing options beyond standard year-round service to Orlando/Sanford.[60] Recent summer schedules have peaked at ten weekly flights across these routes, compared to reduced winter frequencies of three weekly to select Florida cities in off-peak months like February and March.[61] [62] This variability reflects demand for affordable warm-weather escapes, with summer upticks tied to vacation travel from the airport's serving region of western Maryland and adjacent Pennsylvania counties, where direct low-cost access to subtropical climates drives enplanement surges on these specific corridors.[45]

Economic and Community Impact

Direct Employment and Revenue Generation

Hagerstown Regional Airport's direct employment encompasses on-airport roles with the airport authority, airlines, fixed-base operators, and ground service providers, totaling 905 jobs as of 2023.[63] These positions generate $45.5 million in annual payroll, supporting core functions including air traffic control, passenger handling, maintenance, and administrative operations.[63] Revenue generation relies on user fees rather than external subsidies, with aeronautical sources such as landing feesâ€”e.g., $300 for mid-sized jets under proposed FY2026 ratesâ€”forming a primary stream alongside non-aeronautical income from parking and concessions.[64] A new $5 daily parking fee, introduced in early 2025 with the first hour free, aims to fund facility expansions and maintenance amid rising demand.[35] These revenues have sustained operations without deficits in most years, as the airport's budget remains self-sufficient, drawing no county operational funding beyond debt service contributions.[12][65] From 2023 to 2025, employment and revenue growth align empirically with a 38% year-over-year increase in flights during 2024, projecting up to 90,000 passenger enplanements and bolstering fee-based income tied to higher aircraft movements and parking usage.[24] This expansion in service volume directly correlates with sustained payroll for on-site roles, as greater operational throughput necessitates additional ground handling and support staff without proportional subsidy reliance.[63] Unlike many underutilized regional airports dependent on taxpayer subsidies to cover shortfalls, Hagerstown's user-pays structure prioritizes financial independence, where revenue scales causally with activity levels to fund upkeep and avoid chronic deficits.[66][12]

Broader Regional Economic Contributions

The operations at Hagerstown Regional Airport generate nearly $98 million annually in salary and wages across Washington County and surrounding areas, contributing to household income and consumer spending that circulates through local economies. Local businesses benefit from approximately $136 million in sales revenue tied to airport-related activities, including procurement of goods and services by tenants and visitors, which supports supply chains in retail, hospitality, and construction sectors. Additionally, the airport's activities yield over $10 million in state and local tax revenues each year, funding public services such as education and infrastructure without relying on subsidies for core general aviation and cargo functions.[37] Beyond direct transactions, the airport enhances regional competitiveness in manufacturing and logistics by facilitating cargo operations and general aviation access, particularly along the I-70 and I-81 corridors that position Hagerstown as a logistics hub. Companies in aerospace defense, such as Sierra Nevada Corporation and MAG Aerospace, have expanded at the airport since 2008, leveraging its runway capabilities and Foreign Trade Zone status to import/export components efficiently, thereby sustaining high-value jobs in advanced manufacturing and reducing reliance on distant ports. These activities create multiplier effects, where each dollar of airport output generates additional economic activity through induced spending and inter-industry linkages, as modeled in state aviation impact assessments.[24][67] Maryland Department of Transportation studies quantify these broader stimuli at over $20 million in annual indirect and induced impacts for regional airports like Hagerstown, driven by visitor expenditures and business expansions rather than subsidized passenger service alone. This diversification into non-commercial aviation has provided resilience during economic downturns, maintaining steady cargo and corporate flight volumes that buffered Washington County against passenger traffic declines, such as those post-2008 recession, by prioritizing market-driven operations over volatile commercial routes.[63][68]

Accessibility and Ground Transportation

Road and Highway Access

Hagerstown Regional Airport is positioned approximately 3 miles north of downtown Hagerstown, Maryland, enabling efficient vehicular access for passengers from the local area and surrounding regions.[69] The facility's entrance is reachable via Showalter Road, which connects directly to major roadways and avoids the dense urban traffic patterns found in proximity to larger metropolitan airports.[69] Primary highway connectivity is provided by Interstate 81 (I-81), with the airport located less than 0.25 miles from the interstate at Exit 10; this short distance allows for quick on- and off-ramps, typically adding only 2-3 minutes to interstate travel for approaching vehicles.[69][9] U.S. Route 11 runs parallel to I-81 and adjoins the airport property, serving as a complementary arterial route for north-south travel without requiring interstate entry.[3] Interstate 70 (I-70) lies about 6 miles west, linking the airport to east-west corridors toward Frederick and beyond.[9] This configuration supports access from the tri-state region of Maryland, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia, with the airport situated just 0.5 miles south of the Maryland-Pennsylvania border, reducing drive times for cross-border commuters compared to distant hubs like Baltimore-Washington International Airport (BWI), which requires 75-80 miles and 1.5 hours of driving from Hagerstown under normal conditions.[70] Local drives to HGR from central Hagerstown cover 5.4 miles in roughly 10-15 minutes, bypassing congestion and facilitating integration with I-81's freight pathways that bolster regional logistics.[71][72]

Parking and Local Transit Options

Hagerstown Regional Airport provides ample on-site parking directly in front of the terminal, encompassing both short-term and long-term options designed for convenience in a car-dependent region of western Maryland.[69] As of early 2025, parking incurs a fee of $5 per day, with the first hour free, marking a shift from prior free access to fund airport improvements while remaining significantly more affordable than rates at larger metropolitan airports, where daily fees often exceed $20â€“$30.[35] [73] This structure applies uniformly across terminal lots, eliminating the need for off-site alternatives and minimizing hassles for travelers.[74] Local transit options emphasize practicality over extensive networks, reflecting the airport's role in a rural-suburban area with limited public infrastructure. On-site rental car services from providers such as Enterprise and Hertz facilitate immediate vehicle access for passengers, supporting the prevalence of personal or rented automobiles in the region.[69] Taxi services and ride-sharing platforms like Uber operate from designated curbside areas outside the terminal, offering direct pickups without pre-arrangement requirements common at busier hubs.[75] [76] Washington County provides bus service via its transit system, connecting the airport to downtown Hagerstown and nearby points, though schedules are infrequent and geared toward local commuters rather than high-volume air travel.[69] Pedestrian and bicycle access remains minimal, with no dedicated paths or racks emphasized in airport facilities, underscoring the site's integration into an automobile-reliant landscape where over 90% of regional trips involve personal vehicles.[1] User reports highlight the advantages of low-cost parking and straightforward ground access compared to congestion and surcharges at major airports like BWI or Dulles, though options for non-drivers are constrained, often necessitating pre-planned taxi or shuttle arrangements from local providers.[3] [77] Private shuttles to regional destinations exist but are not airport-operated, with services like Airport Xpress available for longer transfers to Baltimore or beyond.[75]

Safety and Operational Incidents

Notable Accidents and Incidents

On September 23, 2020, an experimental amateur-built Glasair 1RG, registration N324DJ, was substantially damaged when it collided with terrain approximately 500 feet short of runway 33 during an attempted return to the airport following a reported loss of engine power shortly after takeoff. The pilot and one passenger were uninjured, and the National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB) investigation determined the probable cause as the pilot's failure to maintain adequate airspeed during the aborted landing maneuver, resulting in an aerodynamic stall.[78][79] On May 30, 2006, a Classic Aircraft Corporation Waco YMF-5 biplane, registration N369AS, was substantially damaged during a forced landing at the airport after the engine lost power in flight. The NTSB found the cause to be a partial loss of engine power during approach to runway 27, attributed to carburetor icing exacerbated by the pilot's management of engine controls; the sole occupant sustained minor injuries.[80][81] A historical incident occurred on March 3, 1955, involving a U.S. Air Force Fairchild C-119G Flying Boxcar that crashed on final approach to the airport, landing too low and causing the undercarriage to shear off, leading to a runway excursion and post-impact fire. All 13 crew members evacuated safely, but the aircraft was destroyed by fire; investigations cited pilot error in altitude management during approach as the primary factor.[82] Hagerstown Regional Airport has maintained a low incidence rate of accidents, with NTSB records showing primarily general aviation events involving small aircraft and no fatal crashes in commercial operations, reflective of its limited scale and focus on regional and private flights rather than high-volume passenger service.[78][80] Post-incident NTSB analyses have consistently identified causal factors such as mechanical malfunctions, environmental conditions like wind shear, or pilot decision-making, with recommendations centered on individual procedural enhancements rather than airport infrastructure deficiencies.[81]

Safety Protocols and Improvements

Hagerstown Regional Airport's primary runway (09-27), measuring 7,000 feet by 150 feet, supports FAA-certified precision instrument approaches, facilitating safer operations during adverse weather.[83] This capability aligns with federal standards for enhanced guidance and reduced approach minima.[7] The airport maintains runway safety areas (RSAs) in compliance with FAA requirements, providing clear zones beyond runway ends and sides to absorb potential overruns or veer-offs, as evidenced in ongoing rehabilitation efforts that include RSA closures for safety upgrades.[84][85] In October 2025, Washington County approved designs and funding for rehabilitating Taxiways A, H, and associated T-hangar lanes, utilizing state and federal grants to repair pavements, improve markings, and ensure compliance with FAA separation standards from the primary runway.[27][28] These projects target prevention of surface incursions by addressing deteriorated conditions in Airport Design Group III facilities, with allocated costs including $342,000 for Taxiway A and $203,009 for T-hangar taxi lanes.[86] Personnel training emphasizes regulatory compliance through triennial full-scale emergency simulations, testing coordination among airport staff, local responders, and airlines to uphold operational readiness.[87] On-site weather observation systems deliver automated surface observing system (ASOS) data, integrated with regional forecasts for Appalachian terrain influences like fog and icing, enabling proactive adjustments to flight schedules and ground movements.[2][8]

Recent and Planned Developments

Infrastructure Upgrades (2020s)

In summer and fall 2024, Hagerstown Regional Airport undertook several projects to improve operational efficiency and passenger amenities, including enhancements to terminal signage with new painting, entrance signage, awnings, and upgrades to gutters and downspouts.[88] These initiatives aimed to modernize the facility's exterior while addressing maintenance needs, with completion targeted to coincide with increased commercial service demands.[88] On October 24, 2024, the airport received a $5.25 million grant from the Federal Aviation Administration under the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law to expand the terminal building, focusing on the east side to add ticketing counters, passenger queuing areas, and baggage claim space.[89] This project, executed by Washington County with federal funding, addresses space constraints from prior renovations and supports capacity for growing low-cost carrier operations without additional county expenditures beyond oversight.[36] The expansion is projected to increase terminal area by at least 20%, enhancing throughput for peak travel periods.[90] For 2025, the Washington County Board of Commissioners approved design and bidding for taxiway rehabilitations on Taxiways A, H, and T-hangar lanes, funded partly by state and federal grants with county-led implementation.[27] Allocations included $342,000 for Taxiway A and $203,009.87 for the T-hangar taxi lane, targeting pavement resurfacing to improve safety and reduce delays on these Airport Design Group III facilities, some of which were last rehabilitated in 2015.[86] These upgrades prioritize structural integrity amid rising general aviation and commercial traffic, with bidding to precede grant reimbursements.[28]

Flight Expansion Initiatives

In the 2020s, Hagerstown Regional Airport (HGR) has pursued flight expansion primarily through enhanced service frequencies offered by Allegiant Air, its sole commercial passenger carrier, focusing on leisure destinations in Florida and South Carolina rather than introducing new routes or additional airlines.[1] These initiatives emphasize seasonal increases to popular vacation spots, including Orlando/Sanford (SFB), St. Pete-Clearwater (PIE), and Myrtle Beach (MYR), with year-round nonstop flights to SFB and seasonal service to the others.[39] A key expansion announced in August 2024 projected a 31.5% capacity increase for spring 2025, adding 67 flights overall compared to the prior year, with specific enhancements such as three weekly flights to PIE from October 2024 through March 2025.[45] Further growth followed in November 2024, with 18 additional flights scheduled between May 13 and September 2, 2025: service to PIE ramped up to three flights per week from May 25 to August 14, then two weekly through early September, while MYR saw added Sunday and Thursday departures from June 5 to September 1.[60] These changes represented a 5.9% increase in seats and 3.9% more flights to MYR, and 2.4% more seats with 5.9% additional flights to SFB, compared to summer 2024 levels.[91][92] Supporting these operational expansions, airport officials secured $6.8 million in Federal Aviation Administration grants in October 2024 for terminal improvements, including eastward expansion to reclaim office space lost to security enhancements and to accommodate potential future growth in passenger volumes or new services.[36] Such infrastructure upgrades are positioned to enable further route development, though no new destinations beyond Allegiant's current network have been confirmed as of late 2025.[36] Prior to these frequency boosts, Allegiant's service at HGR, which began in the mid-2010s, had remained stable with limited seasonal variations, underscoring the recent initiatives as a response to rising regional demand for direct leisure travel options.[93]
