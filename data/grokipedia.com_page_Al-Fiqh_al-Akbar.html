Al-Fiqh al-Akbar

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar (Arabic: الفقه الأكبر, "The Greater Understanding") is a short early treatise on Islamic theology attributed to Abū Ḥanīfa (d. 150 AH/767 CE), the founder of the Ḥanafī school of jurisprudence, delineating essential Sunni doctrines including God's absolute oneness (tawḥīd), the eternity of His attributes distinct from His essence, the supremacy of faith over works in defining belief, and refutations of anthropomorphic literalism and sectarian deviations.[1][2] The text's authorship by Abū Ḥanīfa himself remains contested in modern scholarship, with evidence suggesting it more likely represents a compilation of his oral teachings by disciples like Abū Muṭīʿ al-Balḫī, though traditional Ḥanafī sources uphold its direct attribution.[3][4] As one of the earliest surviving works on ʿaqīda (creed), it has profoundly influenced Ḥanafī-Māturīdī theology, emphasizing rational affirmation of divine transcendence while countering Muʿtazilite rationalism and early literalist tendencies, and continues to be studied for its concise exposition of orthodoxy amid theological disputes of the formative Abbasid era.[1][5]

Authorship and Historical Context

Attribution to Abu Hanifa

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar is traditionally attributed to Abu Hanifa al-Nu'man ibn Thabit (80–150 AH/699–767 CE), the foundational figure of the Hanafi madhhab, who formulated his teachings in Kufa during the nascent Abbasid era following the 132 AH/750 CE overthrow of the Umayyads. Operating in a milieu of theological contention, Abu Hanifa's creed addressed foundational beliefs amid challenges from sects like the Jahmiyya and Qadariyya, with the text serving as a distilled summary of his oral aqidah instructions rather than a verbatim written composition.[6][7] The work's transmission originates from Abu Hanifa's immediate disciples, notably Hammad ibn Abi Hanifa (d. circa 180 AH/796 CE), his son and primary student, and Abu Mu'ti Muhammad ibn Muqatil al-Balkhi (d. 183 AH/799 CE), who relayed distinct recensions later known as al-Fiqh al-Akbar and al-Fiqh al-Absat, respectively. These chains anchor the text's provenance to 2nd-century AH/8th-century CE Kufa, predating widespread written kalam treatises and reflecting Abu Hanifa's preference for oral pedagogy in jurisprudence and theology.[6][4] Initial attestations linking the creed explicitly to Abu Hanifa emerge in 3rd–4th AH/9th–10th century sources, such as biographical and theological compilations that cite it as his authoritative refutation of deviant doctrines, with scholars like al-Tahawi (d. 321 AH/933 CE) integrating its positions into early Sunni creedal formulations. This early reception underscores the text's role in preserving Abu Hanifa's positions before the proliferation of Maturidi and Ash'ari systematizations.[3][6]

Debates on Authenticity and Provenance

The attribution of Al-Fiqh al-Akbar to Abu Hanifa (d. 767 CE) has been a subject of longstanding debate among Islamic scholars, with traditional Sunni authorities largely affirming its authenticity through chains of transmission (isnad) tracing back to his disciples, such as Hammad ibn Abi Hanifa, while no autograph manuscript survives from the 8th century.[7] Early transmissions, including those referenced in Hanafi circles by the 10th century, position the text as a direct reflection of Abu Hanifa's dialectical responses to contemporaries, often compiled orally rather than in written form during his lifetime.[8] Critics, including some muhaddithun and modern academics, argue that the text represents a later compilation by students like Abu Muti' al-Balkhi (d. 814 CE), pointing to the absence of explicit mentions in pre-9th-century biographical works on Abu Hanifa and the presence of two distinct recensions (Fiqh al-Akbar I and II), with the latter showing signs of editorial layering.[4] In a 2022 study published in the Journal of the American Oriental Society, Ramon Harvey contends that al-Fiqh al-akbar II—the more widely circulated version—likely emerged as a misidentified extension of Abu Hanifa's teachings on fiqh, with historical references to "al-Fiqh al-akbar" prior to the 14th century actually pertaining to the longer al-Fiqh al-absat, a compendium of his legal opinions rather than a creedal treatise.[3] Harvey's analysis draws on manuscript evidence and citation patterns in medieval catalogs, suggesting a conflation that solidified only after the Mongol era, when Hanafi-Maturidi orthodoxy sought to canonize Abu Hanifa's creed amid theological rivalries. Potential anachronisms further fuel skepticism, as the text employs terminology and formulations resonant with post-Abu Hanifa kalam debates, such as nuanced discussions on the createdness of divine speech (kalam Allah) and the lafdh (verbal expression) controversy, which intensified in the 9th century under Mu'tazili influence after Abu Hanifa's death.[9] For instance, phrases addressing whether the Qur'an is "created in wording but uncreated in meaning" echo mihna-era (833–848 CE) disputes absent from Abu Hanifa's documented fatwas, which focused more on immediate jurisprudential queries than systematic theology.[10] Defenders counter that such elements fit the oral, dialogic style of Kufan scholarship in Abu Hanifa's era, where creedal positions were elicited in debates with proto-Mu'tazila figures like Wasil ibn Ata (d. 748 CE), and that uninterrupted isnad from reliable narrators like al-Tahawi's Hanafi predecessors validate the content's fidelity despite textual evolution.[6] This view holds that compilation by disciples preserved Abu Hanifa's intent amid an era of predominantly oral transmission, with later attributions serving to authenticate Hanafi orthodoxy against emerging Ash'ari and Mu'tazili syntheses.[11] The debate underscores broader tensions in Islamic intellectual history between isnad-based traditionalism and philological-historical criticism, with traditionalists prioritizing doctrinal continuity—evidenced by endorsements from scholars like al-Taftazani (d. 1390 CE)—over chronological precision, while academic approaches highlight how 14th-century Hanafi revivals may have retroactively shaped the text's provenance to counter Salafi critiques.[8] Empirical manuscript studies, such as those cataloging pre-12th-century fragments, reveal no definitive resolution, as surviving copies date from the 11th century onward and vary in phrasing, suggesting editorial interventions without disproving core attribution.[3]

Content and Doctrinal Core

Structure and Key Statements

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar takes the form of a brief, aphoristic creed composed of approximately 80 assertive propositions that enumerate essential doctrines of Sunni orthodoxy.[12] [13] Presented without rigid formal divisions, the text employs a declarative style, often beginning sentences with affirmations such as "We say" or direct negations of heterodox views, to assert beliefs succinctly and without supporting argumentation.[12] The treatise commences with a foundational declaration of the pillars of faith: "It is obligatory [for a person] to state: I believe in Allah, His angels, His scriptures, His messengers, resurrection after death, that destiny, good and evil, is from Allah Most High, the Reckoning, the Scale, Paradise, and Hellfire; and that they are all true."[12] [13] This sets the tone for subsequent affirmations centered on tawhid, including the explicit rejection of the Mu'tazilite position on the Qur'an's createdness: "We do not say that the Qur'an is created."[14] [13] Central statements on divine transcendence emphasize: "Allah Most High is One, not in terms of the number, but in that he has no partner," while affirming eternal attributes: "He possesses a hand, a countenance, and a self, as He has mentioned in the Qur’an."[12] These are paired with negations of anthropomorphism: "He is not like unto anything from among His creation and nothing from among His creation is like unto Him."[12] [13] The creed thus prioritizes unadorned orthodoxy through parallel positive and negative formulations.[12]

Positions on Tawhid and Divine Attributes

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar affirms tawhid as the absolute oneness of God, distinct from any numerical connotation, emphasizing that He has no partners, neither begets nor is begotten, and possesses no equal or likeness in His creation.[15][12] God is described as eternal and everlasting, existing with His names and attributes without change, acquisition, or resemblance to anything created.[13] This oneness precludes any composition, multiplicity, or dependency, positioning God as the singular, self-subsistent originator of all existence.[15] The text delineates divine attributes into two categories: those of the essence (sifat al-dhat), which are eternal and inseparable from God's being, and those of action (sifat al-fi'l), which pertain to His willed effects on creation but remain pre-eternal in their essence.[12] Essential attributes include life, power, knowledge, will, hearing, sight, and speech, all affirmed as real, uncreated, and distinct from God's essence without implying superaddition or temporal origination; doubting their eternity constitutes disbelief.[15][13] Speech, in particular, is God's uncreated attribute, not consisting of sounds, letters, or bodily organs, as exemplified by the Qur'an being His verbatim word.[15] Specific attributes such as hand (yad), face (wajh), and self (nafs)—mentioned in the Qur'an—are upheld as genuine divine realities, neither metaphorical substitutes for power or favor nor created entities, but affirmed in their textual sense without inquiring into modality (bi-la kayf).[12][13] This approach rejects both tashbih (anthropomorphic likening to creation, implying spatial limits or form) and ta'til (negation or stripping of attributes, as in interpreting them solely as actions without eternal reality).[15] Attributes are thus eternal concomitants of God's essence, unknowable in their "how" yet obligatory to affirm as stated in revelation, preserving transcendence while countering rationalist dilutions.[12]

Views on Faith, Destiny, and the Unseen

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar defines faith (iman) as assent and affirmation of the heart, accompanied by confession of the tongue and performance of actions by the limbs.[1] While the essential content of faith remains constant across believers, its degree varies in terms of certainty and affirmation, increasing with obedience and decreasing with heedlessness, though grave sins do not expel a believer from the fold of faith unless accompanied by denial of their prohibition.[16] This position maintains that a Muslim who commits major sins remains designated as a believer, subject to divine forgiveness or punishment, without equating such a person to disbelievers.[17] On destiny (qadar), the text asserts that God is the sole Creator of all human actions, both good and evil, which occur through His will, knowledge, decree, and predestination inscribed on the Preserved Tablet.[13] Humans acquire (kasb) these actions by their own volition and power, which God creates concurrently, thereby upholding human responsibility without implying absolute compulsion or independent free will detached from divine causation.[1] Guidance arises from God's generosity, while misguidance stems from His justice toward those who deserve it, ensuring all events align with divine predetermination.[12] The unseen (ghayb) encompasses core realities affirmed without delving into speculative details: belief in God's angels as obedient servants; His revealed books, including the uncreated Qur'an as divine speech; His messengers, exemplified by Muhammad as the final prophet free of sin; resurrection after death; the accounting of deeds on balanced scales; and the eternal realities of paradise and hell, where God will be visible to believers through corporeal sight.[17] These elements form integral pillars of faith, with intercession permitted by God's leave on the Day of Judgment, emphasizing acceptance of divine decree over anthropomorphic interpretations or denial.[1]

Theological Significance in Early Sunni Thought

Relation to Early Heresies and Opponents

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar counters the Jahmiyya, followers of Jahm ibn Safwān (d. circa 745 CE), who denied God's eternal attributes and asserted the Qur'an's created nature, by affirming divine attributes such as the hand as real without modality or resemblance and declaring the Qur'an as Allah's uncreated speech.[12][18] This stance aligns with Abu Hanifa's explicit takfir of Jahm for such negations, demarcating orthodox boundaries against ta'ṭīl (stripping attributes).[18] Against the Qadariyya, early proponents of human autonomy in actions who rejected divine predestination (qaḍāʾ and qadar), the text upholds eternal divine decree and will as attributes, stating that Allah creates all acts of obedience and disobedience while humans acquire them without independent origination.[12][13] This refutes their overemphasis on free will as undermining primary divine agency, insisting instead on Allah's comprehensive knowledge and creation of future events without compulsion to belief or unbelief.[12] The creed's reliance on scriptural texts over speculative rationalism echoes the Ahl al-Ḥadīth approach, prioritizing transmitted meanings from Qur'an and Sunnah against the proto-Mu'tazili tendencies toward kalām (dialectical theology) that emerged in the mid-8th century.[12] Predating the full systematization of Mu'tazila under Wāṣil ibn ʿAṭāʾ (d. 748 CE), it establishes proto-Sunni demarcation by rejecting extremes like equating major sins with unbelief (as in Khawārij views) while affirming faith's verbal and act-based dimensions without exiting Islam through transgression.[12] This preserves causal primacy of divine will in knowledge and creation, avoiding secondary causalities that dilute God's direct governance.[13]

Alignment with Salaf and Early Creeds

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar affirms the divine attributes as described in the Quran and prophetic traditions, such as Allah's hand (yad), face (wajh), and soul (nafs), while emphasizing that these are real and befitting His majesty, without resemblance to created beings or inquiry into modality (bila kayf).[12][13] This approach parallels the Salaf's methodology, as exemplified in reports from early authorities like Imam Malik ibn Anas, who stated regarding Allah's ascent over the Throne (istawa 'ala al-'arsh): "The way it is, without asking how" (al-istawa' 'alayhi ka-ma waqa'a bila kayf), privileging textual transmission over speculative interpretation.[7] The text's insistence on uncreated essential attributes (sifat al-dhat), eternal alongside Allah's essence, counters Mu'tazili negation (ta'til) while upholding transcendence (tanzih), reflecting the balanced affirmation (ithbat) seen in traditions such as the Prophet's descriptions of Allah's descent (nuzul) in the last third of the night, accepted by the Salaf without likening to spatial movement.[12] Regarding faith (iman), Al-Fiqh al-Akbar defines it as inner conviction (tasdiq bi-l-qalb) coupled with verbal confession (iqrar bi-l-lisan), with actions as a separate obligatory category whose performance strengthens belief but whose omission constitutes sin rather than outright disbelief unless denying an obligatory act.[19] This aligns with prophetic traditions delineating faith's spectrum, such as the hadith: "Faith is in the heart, confession on the tongue, and actions of the limbs; none of you truly believes until his desires align with what I have brought," indicating interplay between verbal affirmation, internal soundness (as in the hadith of the heart's lump: "If it is sound, the whole body is sound"), and deeds without equating the latter's fluctuation to core belief's nullification. Early creeds among the Salaf, preserved in athar from Companions like Ibn Mas'ud, similarly emphasize faith's foundation in heart and tongue while integrating obedience, rejecting both Kharijite expulsion for major sins and Murji'ite detachment of deeds from belief.[20] The text's doctrinal framework prioritizes unambiguous reports from revelation (naql) over rationalistic innovations (kalam), employing dialectical phrasing only defensively against nascent heresies, as Abu Hanifa reportedly engaged Mu'tazila in Kufa around 150 AH without originating speculative theology.[21] This mirrors the Salaf's evidentiary reliance on Quran and Sunnah, as in Imam al-Shafi'i's assertion that disputation in creed stems from bid'ah, while refuting the misconception of early creeds as unnuanced literalism by demonstrating their integrated affirmation of attributes with unequivocal denial of anthropomorphism (tashbih), as the text declares: "We do not describe Allah with anything unknown to us... nor do we liken Him to any of His creation."[12][22] Such positions, rooted in first-generation transmissions, underscore causal fidelity to prophetic exemplars over later systematizations.

Transmission and Interpretations

Early Transmissions and Attestations

The primary transmissions of al-Fiqh al-Akbar trace back to Abū Ḥanīfa (d. 150/767) through his son Ḥammād ibn Abī Ḥanīfa and his student Abū Muṭīʿ al-Balkhī (d. 199/814), with the latter's chain prominent in Transoxianan Hanafi circles including Balkh and Samarqand.[3] These chains facilitated early attestations in the 4th/10th century, such as Ibn al-Nadīm's (d. 380/990) inclusion of the text in his catalog of Abū Ḥanīfa's works in Baghdad, confirming its circulation among Hanafi scholars there.[3] In Transoxiana, Abū Manṣūr al-Māturīdī (d. 333/944) engaged the text through an ascribed commentary (Sharḥ al-Fiqh al-Akbar), indicating transmission via regional students of al-Balkhī, though the commentary's direct authorship by al-Māturīdī remains disputed among later scholars.[3][23] By the 5th/11th century, citations appear in works by Abū al-ʿAlāʾ al-Ustāwī (d. 432/1040, Nishapur) and Abū al-Yusr al-Bazdawī (d. 493/1099, Samarqand), who reference al-Balkhī's version, evidencing its integration into theological instruction amid Hanafi doctrinal standardization in madrasas like those emerging in Baghdad and Central Asia.[3] Manuscripts of these transmissions, while physically dated later (e.g., 6th/12th century copies), show minimal textual variants attributable to the creedal nature of the content, preserving core statements with high fidelity across regions.[3]

Major Commentaries and Explanations

Al-Fiqh al-Absat, transmitted by Abu Muti' al-Balkhi (d. 183 AH/799 CE), a direct pupil of Abu Hanifa, serves as an early proto-commentary expanding on core doctrines akin to those in Al-Fiqh al-Akbar, such as the nature of faith and divine unity, through dialogic exchanges that clarify foundational beliefs without introducing speculative interpretations.[24] This work prioritizes affirmation of scriptural texts on divine attributes in their apparent sense, eschewing anthropomorphic likening or figurative distortions.[3] Abu Mansur al-Maturidi (d. 333 AH/944 CE) composed Sharh al-Fiqh al-Akbar, a systematic exegesis that defends the treatise's positions using rational proofs against Mu'tazili rationalism and Jahmi negationism, while upholding the text's commitment to affirming God's attributes as described in revelation without modality (bi-la kayf) or excessive ta'wil that alters unambiguous meanings.[23] Al-Maturidi's approach integrates kalam methodology to bolster scriptural fidelity, emphasizing causal realism in theological discourse by linking divine actions to eternal attributes rather than temporal creations.[3] Subsequent Hanbali engagements, including references in al-Qadi Abu Ya'la al-Farra''s (d. 458 AH/1066 CE) creedal writings, reinforce the treatise's literalist stance on divine transcendence and establishment above the Throne, critiquing interpretive excesses while aligning with early Hanafi literalism on attributes like hand and face as real yet unlike creation.[25] Later syntheses, such as Abu al-Muntaha al-Maghnisawi's (d. 1000 AH/1592 CE) explanation incorporating Mulla Ali al-Qari's (d. 1014 AH/1605 CE) insights, maintain this balance by excerpting traditional commentaries that avoid ta'wil where texts are explicit, focusing instead on unadulterated affirmation to preserve the original's doctrinal purity. These works collectively underscore a hermeneutic prioritizing empirical scriptural data over philosophical overlays, ensuring explanations remain tethered to the text's intent.[4]

Distinction from Related Texts

Fiqh al-Akbar II: Content and Differences

Fiqh al-Akbar II constitutes a shorter recension of the theological treatise, transmitted separately from the earlier version designated as Fiqh al-Akbar I (later identified with al-Fiqh al-Absat by Abu Muti' al-Balkhi). This variant surfaced in late 4th/10th-century Bukhara, entering broader Hanafi circulation by the 8th/14th century through figures like Abd al-Aziz al-Bukhari (d. 730/1329 AH), potentially deriving from divergent pupil transmissions amid evolving doctrinal debates.[3] A primary divergence lies in its explicit affirmation of the Quran as God's uncreated speech (kalam Allah ghayr makhluq), distinct from created expressions like written or recited forms, which directly repudiates Mu'tazilite assertions of createdness; however, the phrasing incorporates 4th/10th-century technical terms (e.g., kasb for human acquisition of acts), rendering it anachronistic to Abu Hanifa's 2nd/8th-century context and indicative of potential later interpolations to address intensified polemics.[3] On faith (iman), Fiqh al-Akbar II adds emphases permitting increase via obedience and decrease via disobedience, reinforcing lexical inseparability of iman and Islam while avoiding takfir for major sins, though this formulation echoes Abu Hanifa's views with refined articulations suited to post-10th-century Hanafi-Maturidi refinements rather than pristine early transmissions. Regarding divine attributes, it upholds seven essential attributes (e.g., life, knowledge) alongside eternal active ones (e.g., creating, providing), described without modality or interpretive allegorization (ta'wil), evincing stricter affirmation akin to Athari literalism—contrasting the more qualified affirmations in Fiqh al-Akbar I—and prioritizing textual fidelity over speculative theology.[3]

Historical Misattributions and Scholarly Clarifications

Historically, the text known as al-Fiqh al-akbar II was frequently conflated with the earlier al-Fiqh al-akbar attributed to Abū Ḥanīfa (d. 150/767 CE), leading to widespread misattribution in biographical and theological works. Until the early eighth/fourteenth century, references to al-Fiqh al-akbar typically denoted a creed by Abū Muṭīʿ al-Balkhī (d. 199/814 CE), later retitled al-Fiqh al-absaṭ, rather than the later compilation labeled II. This conflation arose partly from overlapping chains of transmission (isnād), where the purported lineage for II mimicked that of al-Balkhī's work, fostering assumptions of direct authorship by Abū Ḥanīfa without verifiable evidence.[3] Medieval scholars began clarifying these distinctions through critical examination of sources. Al-Dhahabī (d. 748/1348 CE), in his biographical compilations, cited al-Balkhī's text as the authentic al-Fiqh al-akbar, excluding the content of II, which indicates an awareness of the misattribution persisting in some Hanafi circles. The popularization of II occurred later, notably through ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz al-Bukhārī's (d. 730/1329 CE) extensive quotations in Kashf al-asrār, which embedded it within mainstream Hanafi tradition despite lacking a distinct, reliable isnad. Such biographical works highlight the reliance on popular ascription over rigorous transmission scrutiny.[3] Modern textual criticism reinforces that al-Fiqh al-akbar II represents a post-Abū Ḥanīfa compilation, likely from the late fourth/tenth century, based on terminological shifts such as the use of aʿrāḍ (accidents) and kasb (acquisition), absent in second/eighth-century discourses. Scholars like A.J. Wensinck (1932) and subsequent analyses attribute authorship potentially to Abū Ḥanīfa Muḥammad b. Yūsuf al-Bukhārī, emphasizing reception history over nominal chains. These findings underscore the methodological priority of verifiable isnād and contextual linguistics in resolving attributions, distinguishing II as a derivative Hanafi creed rather than an original work.[3]

Reception and Controversies

Acceptance in Hanafi and Maturidi Traditions

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar achieved canonical status within the Hanafi madhhab as a core exposition of orthodox creed, attributed to its founder Abu Hanifa and transmitted through his student Hammad ibn Abi Hanifa.[6] This text was integrated into theological curricula alongside fiqh studies across Hanafi-dominated regions, from Transoxiana—where the madhhab flourished under early Abbasid patronage—to the Ottoman Empire, where it served as a standard against rationalist excesses like Mu'tazilite views on createdness of the Quran.[26] [27] In the Maturidi theological school, which became the predominant creed among Hanafis, al-Fiqh al-Akbar formed a foundational pillar, with Abu Mansur al-Maturidi (d. 944 CE) authoring a commentary that affirmed its positions on divine attributes, including the uncreated eternal speech of Allah distinct from His essence.[23] Maturidi scholars expanded upon the text's principles using reasoned proofs (dalil 'aqli), permitting limited kalam methodology to defend orthodoxy while rejecting speculative excesses, as seen in subsequent works like al-Nasafi's creed that echoes its affirmations of faith without innovation.[10] [28] The text's enduring acceptance is evidenced by its widespread transmission in manuscripts and commentaries within Hanafi-Maturidi circles, contributing to doctrinal unity in expansive territories under Hanafi rule, from Central Asia to Anatolia, where it countered heterodox influences through consistent scholarly endorsement.[29] This integration fostered a balanced theological framework that privileged scriptural fidelity augmented by rational defense, as historically upheld by Hanafi jurists like al-Sarakhsi and al-Tahawi.[11]

Criticisms from Salafi and Athari Perspectives

Salafi and Athari scholars frequently question the authentic attribution of Al-Fiqh al-Akbar to Imam Abu Hanifa (d. 150 AH/767 CE), arguing that it was transmitted through his student Hammad ibn Abi Hanifa without direct evidence of authorship by the Imam himself, who reportedly did not compose written treatises.[7] This skepticism stems from the chain's reliance on later narrations, with some Athari figures like Ibn Taymiyyah (d. 728 AH/1328 CE) quoting portions approvingly but not endorsing the text as an original work of Abu Hanifa.[30] [4] Instead, they prioritize creeds directly traceable to the Salaf, such as those of Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal (d. 241 AH/855 CE), viewing summarized formulations like Al-Fiqh al-Akbar as potentially introducing kalam (speculative theology) absent in early Salafi methodology.[7] A core Athari critique concerns the text's approach to divine attributes, where affirmations such as "We affirm the Names and Attributes of Allah as mentioned in the Book and the Sunnah, without distortion or resemblance" are deemed insufficiently emphatic compared to the unqualified ithbat (affirmation) of the Salaf, who simply accepted attributes like hand, face, and descent as real without qualifying phrases that could invite later ta'wil (figurative interpretation).[31] Influenced by Ibn Taymiyyah's expositions, Salafis argue that invoking bi-la kayf (without how) prematurely risks aligning with Ash'ari tendencies toward modality denial, as the Salaf passed over attributes (imrar) directly from texts without such dialectical tools, insisting on their reality befitting Allah's majesty alone.[32] This stance debunks "inclusive" readings that normalize metaphorical dilutions of attributes like speech or vision, demanding instead strict adherence to Hadith evidences—such as Allah's speech comprising letters and sounds—over creed summaries prone to interpretive drift.[21] Furthermore, Atharis contend that Al-Fiqh al-Akbar's definition of faith (iman) as tasdiq (assent) in the heart and confession by the tongue misaligns with the Salaf's holistic view, where iman integrates actions and fluctuates in degree, as evidenced in narrations from Ibn Abbas (d. 68 AH/687 CE) and others; mere verbal affirmation, they hold, echoes positions contested in early fitan (trials) like those of the Murji'ah, prioritizing instead comprehensive Hadith-based proofs over abstracted theological statements.[31] These critiques underscore a preference for unmediated scriptural texts to safeguard against rationalist encroachments, even while acknowledging orthodox elements in the treatise's tawhid affirmations.[7]

Modern Academic Debates

Contemporary scholarship on al-Fiqh al-Akbar emphasizes textual criticism and historical philology to assess its attribution to Abū Ḥanīfa (d. 150/767), distinguishing between two primary recensions: the earlier al-Fiqh al-Akbar I transmitted via Abū Muṭīʿ al-Balkhī (d. 183/799) and the later al-Fiqh al-Akbar II. Ramon Harvey's 2022 analysis traces references in theological and biographical works, demonstrating that until the early eighth/fourteenth century, the title al-Fiqh al-akbar exclusively denoted the Balkhī recension, with attributions to Abū Ḥanīfa for the second version emerging only subsequently through Hanafi-Māturīdī scholastic efforts.[3] This evolution reflects transmission dynamics in oral-writtentraditions, where creedal summaries adapted to refute emerging heresies like Muʿtazilism, rather than wholesale fabrication.[33] Scholars debate anachronisms in al-Fiqh al-Akbar II, such as formulations on divine attributes and faith that align more closely with ninth/tenth-century kalām disputes than Abū Ḥanīfa's era, as noted by early modern critics like Shiblī Nuʿmānī (d. 1914) and echoed in recent analyses.[10] Harvey counters direct authorship claims by highlighting discrepancies in early chains of transmission, yet affirms the text's rootedness in proto-Hanafī theology, likely crystallizing from Abū Ḥanīfa's reported majālis (study circles) documented in works like Kitāb al-ʿĀlim wa-l-mutaʿallim.[3] Confessional defenses, often from traditional Hanafī sources, prioritize isnād (chains) over linguistic evidence, but academic consensus privileges manuscript variants and intertextual parallels to date core content to the second/eighth century while questioning verbatim authenticity.[34] Theological debates center on whether the text's anthropomorphic leanings—affirming divine hand, face, and self without modality (bi-lā kayf)—represent Abū Ḥanīfa's baseline or later interpolations amid Atharī-Hanafī syntheses. Evidence from cross-referencing with contemporaries like Ḥammād ibn Abī Ḥanīfa (d. 120/738) supports early provenance for key affirmations of God's transcendence and unity, but posits editorial shaping by transmitters like Abū Muṭīʿ to address causal chains in predestination debates.[3] This approach underscores how creedal texts functioned as living documents in Hanafī circles, evolving through dialectical refinement rather than static authorship, with recent studies urging separation of doctrinal essence from ascriptive history to avoid anachronistic projections.[33]

Legacy and Influence

Role in Shaping Sunni Aqidah

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar, attributed to Abu Hanifa (d. 767 CE), served as one of the earliest systematic expositions of Sunni creed, articulating foundational doctrines such as the uncreated nature of the Quran and the affirmation of divine attributes without modality, thereby countering Mu'tazili rationalism that posited the Quran's createdness and denied anthropomorphic descriptions of God.[12] This text predated al-Aqida al-Tahawiyya, compiled by Abu Ja'far al-Tahawi in 933 CE, by establishing proto-Sunni positions on faith encompassing both belief and action, the eternity of God's speech, and the rejection of deterministic extremes, which later creeds echoed to standardize orthodoxy against emerging heresies.[35][36] By subsuming creedal matters under the rubric of fiqh al-akbar—the "greater jurisprudence"—the treatise bridged practical legal reasoning with theological inquiry, enabling early Hanafi traditionalists to systematize aqidah using analogical deduction and transmitted reports rather than purely speculative kalam methodologies, thus preserving scriptural fidelity while addressing dialectical challenges.[27] This approach facilitated the integration of creed into jurisprudential frameworks, allowing Sunni scholars to refute sectarian deviations through established fiqh tools like consensus (ijma') and analogy (qiyas) applied to belief, without adopting the full apparatus of later kalam schools.[37] The text's verifiable influence extended to its invocation in medieval fatwas and creedal summaries, where its affirmations of God's unity (tawhid) in essence, attributes, and actions were cited to delineate Sunni boundaries amid fractures from Jahmiyya negationism and Qadari free-will excesses, promoting doctrinal unity from the 8th to 13th centuries by prioritizing textual evidence over philosophical conjecture.[12][15] Its concise format, comprising around 60 propositions, ensured portability and repeatability in teaching, reinforcing a baseline aqidah that subsequent generations adapted to consolidate Sunni identity against rationalist and anthropomorphist poles.[35]

Enduring Impact and Contemporary Usage

Al-Fiqh al-Akbar continues to serve as a foundational text in Hanafi-Maturidi theological education, particularly in madrasas across South Asia, Turkey, and diaspora communities, where it is memorized and expounded to instill core Sunni doctrinal principles derived from Quranic imperatives and prophetic traditions without speculative embellishment.[6] Recent English translations, such as the 2007 edition with commentary facilitating access for non-Arabic speakers, have enabled its integration into global curricula, emphasizing affirmations of divine unity, attributes, and faith acts as uncreated and obligatory, respectively. These renditions, often paired with annotations rejecting anthropomorphic interpretations, support its role in clarifying orthodox positions amid diverse exegetical claims.[38] In contemporary discourse, the text is invoked by Hanafi scholars to rebut literalist tendencies associated with Wahhabi-influenced readings, which posit unnuanced divine similitude to creation, by underscoring Abu Hanifa's insistence on affirming scriptural attributes without modality or resemblance—as in statements negating divine corporeality or spatial limitation.[12] This application reinforces causal distinctions between Creator and created, countering what proponents view as reductive literalism that conflates textual rhetoric with empirical ontology. Similarly, it addresses liberal reinterpretations that relativize immutable creedal tenets, such as the eternal nature of faith and disbelief, by prioritizing evidentiary chains from primary sources over subjective accommodations to modern norms.[39] Post-2020 scholarship has reaffirmed the treatise's emphasis on non-speculative creed formulation, with analyses highlighting its alignment with empirical scriptural exegesis against both anthropomorphic and esoteric deviations, as evidenced in bibliographic surveys authenticating its transmission and doctrinal primacy in Maturidi circles.[8] These studies, drawing on manuscript evidence, position Al-Fiqh al-Akbar as a bulwark for doctrinal stability, underscoring its utility in navigating challenges like sectarian polarization and secular encroachments on traditional authority.[3]
