Les Misérables (1935 film)


Les Misérables is a 1935 American drama film directed by Richard Boleslawski, starring Fredric March as the ex-convict Jean Valjean and Charles Laughton as the obsessive Inspector Javert. Adapted from Victor Hugo's 1862 novel of the same name, it portrays Valjean's struggle for redemption after imprisonment for stealing bread, his adoption of the orphaned Cosette, and his evasion of Javert's unyielding pursuit across decades in post-Revolutionary France.[1]
Produced by Darryl F. Zanuck for 20th Century Pictures and distributed by United Artists, the film was released on April 20, 1935, with a runtime of approximately 108 minutes. Shot in 34 days at a cost nearing $1 million, production incorporated 200 actual inmates as extras to depict prison scenes authentically.[1]
Critically praised for its faithful adaptation, strong performances—particularly March's portrayal of Valjean's transformation and Laughton's menacing Javert—and technical achievements, Les Misérables received four Academy Award nominations, including Best Picture, and was selected as the sixth best English-language film of 1935 by the National Board of Review. It also achieved notable commercial success, ranking among the year's top-grossing films.[1][2]

Production

Development and Adaptation

20th Century Pictures, led by producer Darryl F. Zanuck, developed the 1935 film adaptation of Victor Hugo's 1862 novel Les Misérables during a period of heightened interest in adapting canonical literature to leverage the prestige of early sound-era cinema.[1] The studio, in its final independent year before merging with Fox Film Corporation, selected the property to capitalize on the novel's enduring dramatic scope and moral themes, aligning with Zanuck's strategy for high-profile literary projects.[3] Screenwriter W. P. Lipscomb crafted the adaptation by significantly compressing the novel's 1,400-page narrative into a 108-minute runtime, centering the script on the psychological and ethical conflict between protagonist Jean Valjean and antagonist Inspector Javert.[1] [4] This streamlining eliminated or abbreviated many peripheral subplots, such as extended explorations of secondary characters' backstories, to maintain narrative momentum within the constraints of feature-length filmmaking.[5] The scripting process occurred under the strict enforcement of the Motion Picture Production Code, implemented in July 1934, which mandated alterations to mitigate depictions of immorality, including Fantine's prostitution, widespread destitution, and the June Rebellion's violence.[6] These changes emphasized personal redemption and conscience over Hugo's critiques of institutional injustice and social upheaval, ensuring compliance while preserving the story's redemptive arc.[7]

Casting and Pre-Production

Pre-production for Les Misérables commenced in 1934 under producer Darryl F. Zanuck at 20th Century Pictures, with the studio prioritizing a prestige adaptation of Victor Hugo's novel to capitalize on themes of redemption amid the Great Depression.[8] [1] Zanuck selected director Richard Boleslawski, a Polish émigré with prior experience directing literary dramas at MGM, including The Painted Veil (1934), to helm the project and condense the expansive source material into a cohesive narrative.[8] [9] For the central role of Jean Valjean, Fredric March was cast, drawing on his versatility in dual-natured characters from films like Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (1931), for which he received the Academy Award for Best Actor. March's selection aligned with the studio's aim to feature established stars capable of conveying moral transformation and empathy in a socially resonant story.[10] Charles Laughton was chosen as Inspector Javert to provide a stark counterpoint, utilizing his established prowess in portraying unyielding authority figures, as in The Private Life of Henry VIII (1933). This pairing of March's redemptive intensity with Laughton's obsessive rigidity was intended to heighten dramatic tension and attract audiences seeking high-caliber performances in a period of economic hardship.[11] Pre-production efforts included script development by W.P. Lipscomb to streamline Hugo's 1,400-page novel into a 108-minute film, emphasizing key moral dilemmas while omitting subplots for runtime efficiency.[1] Set design focused on evoking 19th-century French locales through practical constructions at the studio, reflecting cautious budgeting typical of mid-1930s Hollywood productions amid financial recovery.[8]

Filming and Direction

Principal photography for Les Misérables commenced in 1934 at the studios of 20th Century Pictures, where elaborate sets recreating 19th-century Paris were constructed to facilitate controlled production amid the financial pressures of the Great Depression era. This studio-bound approach allowed for efficient scheduling and avoided the logistical complexities and expenses of on-location shooting, which were uncommon for major Hollywood adaptations at the time.[9] Director Richard Boleslawski, a Polish émigré with a background in theater, employed a methodical style emphasizing intimate emotional close-ups to convey character depth and psychological tension, particularly in confrontations between Jean Valjean and Javert, while maintaining a brisk pacing to adapt the novel's sprawling narrative into a 108-minute runtime.[8] His direction integrated fluid camera movements and dramatic lighting to heighten key sequences, drawing subtle influences from European cinematic traditions for sincerity and restraint.[12] Challenges arose in staging the 1832 June Rebellion's barricade scenes, where Boleslawski balanced historical spectacle with narrative focus on personal redemption, avoiding overt glorification of collective unrest to align with the Motion Picture Production Code's prohibitions on endorsing revolution or social disorder during a period of domestic economic instability and political vigilance against radicalism.[6] Production wrapped by late 1934, enabling a premiere the following spring.

Technical Production Elements

The film's cinematography was directed by Gregg Toland, who utilized deep-focus techniques and high-contrast black-and-white photography to capture the stark contrasts between light and shadow, emphasizing themes of redemption and pursuit throughout the 108-minute runtime.[1] Toland's approach, influenced by his work on other 1930s dramas, incorporated innovative shadow play to heighten the menacing presence of characters like Javert, aligning with the era's expressionistic tendencies in Hollywood sound films.[13] The musical score was composed by Alfred Newman, featuring orchestral arrangements that drew on Romantic-era influences to build dramatic tension during key sequences, such as pursuits and moral confrontations, without incorporating any pre-existing themes from Victor Hugo's novel.[1] Newman's contribution, conducted for the film's synchronized soundtrack, included swelling string sections and brass accents to underscore emotional peaks, adhering to the orchestral standards of 20th Century Pictures productions.[14] Editing was overseen by Barbara McLean, who condensed the novel's multi-decade timeline into fluid, economical sequences totaling approximately 108 minutes, earning a nomination for Best Film Editing at the 8th Academy Awards.[15] This process involved tight cross-cutting between parallel storylines, such as Valjean's transformation and Javert's obsession, to maintain narrative momentum within the constraints of early sound-era pacing.[1] Sound design followed the norms of 1935 Hollywood talkies, with synchronized dialogue and effects mixed to support the film's dramatic realism, though it received no Academy Award nomination in technical categories beyond editing and assistant direction.[8] The audio elements prioritized clear vocal delivery for the period's live-recorded tracks, enhancing the intensity of confrontational scenes without advanced post-dubbing techniques available later in the decade.[15]

Cast and Characters

Principal Roles

Fredric March portrays Jean Valjean, the film's protagonist who evolves from a desperate convict into a reformed industrialist and mayor within the adaptation's streamlined narrative.[1] His performance in the dual role, including the mistaken convict Champmathieu, earned him a nomination for the Academy Award for Best Actor at the 8th Academy Awards.[16] Charles Laughton plays Inspector Javert, rendered as a fanatical upholder of legal order whose unswerving duty drives the central conflict in the adaptation.[1] Laughton's corpulent frame and intense demeanor amplify the character's authoritarian menace.[8] Rochelle Hudson assumes the role of the adult Cosette, emphasizing her sheltered upbringing and romantic involvement to highlight themes of protection and love in the film's romantic arc.[8] John Beal depicts Marius Pontmercy, the passionate young revolutionary whose idealism intersects with Valjean's life and Cosette's affections, selected for his earnest youthful vigor.[17]

Supporting Performances

Florence Eldridge delivered a poignant performance as Fantine, the desperate mother forced to relinquish her daughter Cosette to the Thénardiers amid her descent into poverty and illness, with her scenes emphasizing quiet dignity and sacrifice rather than explicit degradation to adhere to the Motion Picture Production Code enforced since 1934.[8][6] Her brief role, culminating in Fantine's death from tuberculosis after entrusting Cosette's care to Jean Valjean, provided emotional weight to the narrative's themes of redemption without dominating the central focus on Valjean and Javert.[18] Sir Cedric Hardwicke portrayed Bishop Charles Myriel (Bienvenu), whose act of forgiving Valjean's theft of silver candlesticks and gifting him more serves as the catalyst for the protagonist's moral awakening, infusing the early sequences with a sense of compassionate authority rooted in 19th-century French clerical benevolence.[8][1] Hardwicke's restrained interpretation added ethical depth to the ensemble, highlighting institutional mercy amid societal harshness, while supporting actors in the condensed 1832 Paris uprising scenes, such as John Beal as Marius and minor revolutionaries, contributed atmospheric historical fervor through collective defiance without individualized subplots that might dilute the leads' prominence.[17] Child actress Marilyn Knowlden, aged six during production, played young Cosette in the film's harrowing inn sequences, evoking sympathy through her wide-eyed vulnerability and silent endurance of the Thénardiers' exploitation, which heightened the story's critique of child labor and neglect in a manner appealing to family audiences of the era.[18][19] Her naturalistic delivery in these pivotal moments reinforced the film's redemptive arc as Valjean rescues her, bolstering the supporting cast's role in humanizing the broader social tableau without eclipsing the principal performances.[1]

Plot Summary

Act Structure and Key Events

The film divides its narrative into three acts, condensing the source material's sprawling chronology into a 108-minute runtime focused on Jean Valjean's transformation and pursuit by Inspector Javert.[1][17] Act One establishes Valjean's origins and initial redemption. In 1800, Valjean receives a 10-year sentence as a galley slave in Favorelles for stealing bread to feed his sister's starving family, during which the young Javert rises to officer while pledging unyielding loyalty to the law.[1] After serving extended hard labor, Valjean is paroled but faces societal ostracism due to his yellow passport, finding refuge only with Bishop Bienvenu, who forgives Valjean's theft of silverware and gifts him candlesticks, inspiring moral renewal.[1][20] Valjean then reinvents himself as the prosperous factory owner and mayor M. Madeleine in another town, unaware that Javert has become the local inspector.[1] Act Two centers on familial bonds and escalating suspicion. Factory worker Fantine, dismissed after her illegitimate child Cosette is discovered, spirals into destitution and confronts Madeleine, prompting his intervention amid her arrest.[1] Following Fantine's deathbed plea, Valjean retrieves and adopts the abused Cosette from the exploitative Thénardiers, raising her in secrecy while Javert grows suspicious of Madeleine's identity, especially after Valjean confesses to authorities to exonerate an innocent man wrongly accused in his place.[20] Valjean evades recapture, fleeing with Cosette to a convent for protection as Javert intensifies the manhunt.[20] Act Three builds to revolutionary turmoil and resolution. Years later, a grown Cosette falls in love with student revolutionary Marius amid Paris unrest, drawing Valjean into the 1832 barricades where Marius leads protests.[20] Javert infiltrates the insurgents, is captured, and released by Valjean, leading to a climactic riverside confrontation that underscores the pursuer's crisis of duty.[20] The story concludes with Valjean's self-imposed exile after ensuring Cosette's union with Marius, forgoing extended aftermaths from the novel.[20]

Fidelity to Victor Hugo's Novel

Major Omissions and Alterations

The 1935 film adaptation significantly condenses Victor Hugo's expansive novel by omitting numerous subplots and secondary characters to fit a 108-minute runtime, prioritizing the central Valjean-Javert antagonism over the book's broader social tapestry.[21][13] The Thénardier family, key antagonists in the novel's underclass schemes, appears only briefly in the early inn scene where Valjean rescues Cosette, after which they vanish entirely, excluding their elaborate cons, Patron-Minette gang involvement, and ongoing manipulations.[20][21] Similarly, Fantine's arc is truncated to a cameo role, dropping her detailed descent into prostitution and related miseries that establish the novel's critique of industrial exploitation, with Valjean retrieving Cosette before her death rather than after.[3][22] Characters like Gavroche and Cosette's grandfather Gillenormand are entirely excised.[20] Structural alterations further compress the timeline and merge events for pacing. The novel's decades-spanning chronology is tightened, with Cosette and Marius's romance depicted via montage dates instead of gradual development, and Éponine recast as Marius's respectable secretary unrelated to the Thénardiers, eliminating her tragic familial ties and unrequited love subplot.[20][22] The revolutionary elements are scaled back, transforming the June Rebellion into a localized riot protesting galley slave conditions rather than a monarchical overthrow, with Marius absorbing Enjolras's leadership role as a composite figure.[20][22] The ending deviates by concluding after Javert's suicide at the barricades, followed by Valjean's retreat to England upon Cosette and Marius's reunion, omitting the novel's extended aftermath including Valjean's prolonged suffering, reconciliation efforts, and deathbed revelation.[20][3] These changes, while preserving core redemption motifs, eliminate Hugo's lengthy epilogues and digressions on history, argot, and urban underbelly to maintain narrative momentum.[21][3]

Thematic Shifts and Interpretations

The 1935 film adaptation shifts Hugo's expansive critique of systemic injustice toward a narrower emphasis on personal moral transformation, portraying Jean Valjean's redemption as the result of individual choices sparked by a singular act of mercy from the bishop, rather than broader societal overhaul.[9] This aligns with the era's economic pressures during the Great Depression, where American audiences favored narratives of self-reliance and ethical self-reform over collective upheaval, condensing the novel's voluminous social digressions into Valjean's internal struggles and acts of forgiveness.[23] Hugo's original work interweaves personal arcs with extensive commentary on poverty, law, and reform, but the screenplay by W. P. Lipscomb prioritizes causal sequences of agency—Valjean's theft of silver leading to conscience-driven restitution—highlighting redemption as achievable through personal resolve absent institutional change.[13] Inspector Javert's characterization evolves from the novel's depiction of unyielding fanaticism to a figure driven by principled adherence to duty, critiquing rigid legalism while underscoring the perils of empathy's absence without rejecting order itself.[9] Charles Laughton's performance humanizes Javert as a product of his repressive upbringing in a criminal milieu, compelled by an inflexible code that views law as absolute, yet the film frames his pursuit not as irrational zealotry but as a tragic commitment to societal stability, allowing Valjean's mercy to expose the limits of such absolutism.[24] This interpretation avoids endorsing lawlessness, instead balancing law's necessity against its potential for unchecked application, a nuance reflective of 1930s concerns over authority amid economic unrest. The film's portrayal of the 1832 June Rebellion reduces Hugo's idealistic yet doomed uprising to scenes of futile chaos, emphasizing barricade violence as disorganized and self-defeating rather than a viable path to justice.[9] In line with the novel's outcome where the insurgents perish without altering the monarchy, director Richard Boleslawski's visuals depict the revolt as mob-driven disorder, cautioning against radicalism in a manner resonant with post-World War I Red Scare sentiments lingering into the Depression era, when Hollywood adaptations often tempered revolutionary fervor to align with prevailing anti-extremist caution.[12] This depoliticizes the event, subordinating it to Valjean's personal narrative and reinforcing individual ethical action over collective insurgency as the locus of meaningful change.

Release and Commercial Performance

Premiere and Distribution

The film premiered on April 20, 1935, in the United States, with initial screenings in major cities such as Los Angeles and New York.[17] [1] Produced by 20th Century Pictures under Darryl F. Zanuck's supervision, it was handled for theatrical distribution by United Artists, the independent company that often managed releases for external productions during this period.[10] [1] This arrangement preceded the May 1935 merger of 20th Century Pictures with Fox Film Corporation to form 20th Century-Fox, allowing United Artists to leverage its established network for nationwide rollout targeting urban theaters.[1] Marketing efforts highlighted the prestige of Victor Hugo's source novel and the star power of leads Fredric March as Jean Valjean and Charles Laughton as Javert, positioning the adaptation as a thoughtful drama for mature audiences amid the era's emphasis on socially conscious films.[8] Trailers and promotional materials underscored the story's themes of redemption and justice, drawing on the actors' recent acclaimed performances—March's Oscar win for Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (1931) and Laughton's for The Private Life of Henry VIII (1933)—to attract theatergoers seeking elevated entertainment over lighter fare.[4] Internationally, distribution was more restrained, with releases in select markets including Argentina (Los miserables), Australia, Austria (Das Brandmal des Gesetzes), Brazil (Os Miseráveis), and several European countries, typically featuring subtitles or dubbed versions to accommodate non-English audiences.[25] United Artists coordinated these efforts through local partners, focusing on regions with established demand for literary adaptations rather than broad global saturation.[25]

Box Office Results

The production of Les Misérables entailed a budget of approximately $1 million, including $100,000 salaries each for leads Fredric March and Charles Laughton.[1] Domestic box office performance placed the film at #16 among 1935 releases, reflecting respectable earnings amid the Great Depression's contraction of Hollywood revenues from $720 million in 1929 to $480 million by 1933, with aggregate studio profits shifting from $54.5 million to net losses.[26][27] This outcome yielded profitability for 20th Century Pictures, bolstering its financial standing prior to the studio's merger with Fox in early 1935.[27]

Reception and Analysis

Contemporary Critical Response

The 1935 film adaptation of Les Misérables received largely favorable reviews from contemporary critics, who highlighted its emotional depth and strong performances despite the challenges of condensing Victor Hugo's voluminous novel into a 108-minute runtime.[5][28] Publications such as The New York Times deemed it a "memorable motion picture" and one of the year's notable achievements in the talking screen, praising its "unbelievably thrilling" quality and timeless resonance.[28] Variety echoed this sentiment, stating that the film would satisfy "the most exacting Victor Hugo followers" by preserving the novel's essence and flavor through W. P. Lipscomb's screenplay.[5] Critics particularly acclaimed the lead actors' portrayals as career-defining. Fredric March's Jean Valjean was described in The New York Times as "a flawless thing, strong and heartbreaking," demonstrating "enormous resource" in capturing the character's transformation from convict to benefactor.[28] Charles Laughton's Inspector Javert earned similar praise as "one of the great screen portraits," noted for its "brilliant range" in conveying relentless obsession through physical mannerisms like a "cropped head" and "hideous trembling of the lips."[28] Variety commended March for vividly rendering Valjean as a "persecuted, pitiable, admirable" figure and Laughton for a "quiet, powerful, believable" antagonist, contributing to the film's dramatic tension.[5] The adaptation's compression was generally seen as a strength rather than a flaw, with The New York Times calling Lipscomb's script "a model of telescopic rewriting" for streamlining subplots—such as eliminating Valjean's anticlimactic death—and culminating in Javert's suicide to underscore themes of mercy and justice.[28] This focus amplified the moral confrontation between the protagonists, which reviewers found resonant amid 1930s concerns over poverty and redemption, though the film's emphasis on personal struggle inherently sidelined broader social critiques present in Hugo's work, such as extended depictions of revolutionary unrest.[28] Action sequences, including the sewer chase, were highlighted as "brilliantly managed" and "breath-taking," prioritizing visceral impact over exhaustive historical scope.[5]

Awards Recognition

Les Misérables earned four nominations at the 8th Academy Awards on March 4, 1936, for films released in 1935, underscoring its competitive standing among high-profile dramas of the year.[29] The film was nominated for Outstanding Production (equivalent to Best Picture), competing against entries like Mutiny on the Bounty, which ultimately won the award as a Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer production.[29] Fredric March received a Best Actor nomination for his role as Jean Valjean, facing stiff competition from Victor McLaglen, who won for The Informer.[15] Additional technical nods included Best Assistant Director for Eric Stacey, lost to Clem Beauchamp and Paul Wing for The Lives of a Bengal Lancer, and Best Sound Recording for Franklin Hansen.[30] [15] Despite these honors, the film secured no victories, reflecting the intense rivalry in the early Academy Awards period, where dramatic prestige films vied with adventure spectacles like Mutiny on the Bounty.[29] The nominations affirmed strengths in lead performance and behind-the-scenes craftsmanship, areas of growing emphasis as the Oscars formalized categories to honor production quality.[31] No significant guild awards, such as from the Screen Actors Guild (not yet established), or international festival recognitions were accorded to the production.[15] The accolades nonetheless elevated the profiles of stars Fredric March and Charles Laughton, both already established but benefiting from the exposure in a nomination slate dominated by literary adaptations.[15]

Long-Term Evaluations

Film historians regard the 1935 Les Misérables as a superior early sound adaptation of Victor Hugo's novel, commendably condensing its expansive scope into a 108-minute narrative while preserving core dramatic tensions between law and mercy.[32][33] Retrospective analyses highlight its efficiency in prioritizing emotional arcs over the source material's digressive historical and philosophical asides, positioning it as a benchmark among pre-1950s Hollywood versions despite runtime constraints.[22] Critiques of the adaptation's thematic handling point to the Motion Picture Production Code's enforcement—effective from July 1934—as prompting a sanitization that tempered Hugo's indictment of institutional inequality and penal brutality, redirecting focus toward personal redemption and the sanctity of reformed individualism.[6][34] This shift, evident in the film's foregrounding of Jean Valjean's spiritual transformation over collective social upheaval, aligns with Code-era imperatives for moral uplift but arguably attenuates the novel's causal emphasis on systemic poverty as a driver of vice.[35] Balanced scholarly and critical assessments commend the film's strengths in character psychology, particularly Fredric March's layered depiction of Valjean's internal moral evolution and Charles Laughton's obsessive portrayal of Javert, which convey psychological depth through subtle performance rather than spectacle or elaborate sets.[13][36] These elements sustain viewer engagement in later viewings, underscoring the adaptation's enduring value as a character-driven study amid its thematic compromises.[3]

Legacy

Cultural and Historical Impact

The 1935 film adaptation of Victor Hugo's Les Misérables, directed by Richard Boleslawski and starring Fredric March as Jean Valjean and Charles Laughton as Javert, established a template for condensing the novel's sprawling narrative into a star-centric drama emphasizing personal redemption and moral confrontation over expansive social commentary. This model of narrative streamlining influenced later Hollywood versions, such as the 1952 film directed by Lewis Milestone, which similarly prioritized the Valjean-Javert antagonism and omitted much of Hugo's revolutionary subplot to maintain focus on individual character arcs.[37] Amid the Great Depression, the film's portrayal of parole, societal reintegration, and self-made success through ethical resolve struck a chord with U.S. viewers facing widespread unemployment and skepticism toward expansive government relief programs, highlighting themes of personal grit and voluntary charity—such as Valjean's factory ownership and aid to the poor—rather than critiques of institutional welfare or collective uprising.[9][38] In film studies, the adaptation has been critiqued for its conservative handling of Hugo's politically charged source material, which originally indicted systemic injustice and endorsed barricade rebellion; the 1935 version mitigates these elements to foreground apolitical uplift and law's moral tension, reflecting Hollywood's era-specific aversion to radicalism while rendering the story palatable for mainstream American consumption.[3][13]

Preservation and Modern Availability

The 1935 film Les Misérables entered the public domain in the United States due to non-renewal of its copyright, enabling unrestricted preservation and distribution efforts by archives and enthusiasts.[39] This status has allowed free online hosting, such as full uploads on the Internet Archive since 2013, where it is offered under a CC0 public domain dedication.[39] The film receives regular broadcasts on Turner Classic Movies (TCM), a cable channel dedicated to classic cinema, contributing to its ongoing visibility among audiences.[8] Physical media availability includes DVD releases from 20th Century Fox, such as the 2007 Cinema Classics Collection pairing it with the 1952 adaptation, which features digital remastering to enhance visual clarity and audio quality from original sources.[40] [41] No major theatrical-scale restorations, such as 4K scans, have been documented for this version, distinguishing it from later Hugo adaptations.[41] Modern streaming options provide periodic access, with availability on subscription services like the Criterion Channel and Kanopy, as well as rental or purchase on platforms including Amazon Prime Video.[42] [43] Free ad-supported viewing is offered on Plex.[44] These formats ensure the film's accessibility for contemporary viewers without reliance on rare prints.
