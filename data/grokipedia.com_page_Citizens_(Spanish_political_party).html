Citizens (Spanish political party)

Ciudadanos-Partido de la Ciudadanía (Cs), commonly known as Ciudadanos, is a Spanish political party established in 2006 in Catalonia by intellectuals opposing the growth of regional separatism, advocating instead for constitutional fidelity to Spain's territorial integrity and liberal economic principles.[1][2] Under the leadership of Albert Rivera, who served as party president from its early years until his resignation in November 2019 following a sharp electoral reversal, Ciudadanos transitioned from a regional platform to a national contender, emphasizing market-oriented reforms, public sector efficiency, and resistance to nationalist fragmentation.[3][4] The party experienced rapid ascent in the mid-2010s, capturing 40 seats in the Congress of Deputies in the December 2015 general election and peaking at 57 seats in April 2019, often acting as a kingmaker in coalition negotiations while refusing formal alliances with the Socialist Workers' Party.[5] However, subsequent results plummeted, with only 10 seats in the November 2019 election and complete erasure from national parliamentary representation by the July 2023 general election, alongside failure to secure European Parliament seats in 2024, attributed to voter realignment toward the conservative Popular Party and the emergence of Vox as a harder-line alternative on unity and immigration issues.[5][6] Key achievements include leading the vote in Catalonia's 2017 regional election with 25.4% support, blocking separatist majorities there temporarily, and advancing legislative pushes for dual-language education and pension sustainability at the national level.[7] Controversies encompass perceptions of ideological drift toward conservatism on Catalan matters, internal factionalism post-Rivera, and criticism for tactical abstentions that enabled minority governments, contributing to its marginalization amid Spain's polarized duopoly of PSOE and PP.[8][5]

History

Origins and Foundation (2005–2006)

Ciutadans de Catalunya, the precursor platform to Ciudadanos, emerged in response to the political dominance of Catalan nationalism in the early 2000s, particularly following the tripartite government's push for a new Statute of Autonomy that critics argued would entrench ideological conformity in education, media, and public administration. On 21 June 2005, a group of intellectuals, journalists, and professionals published the first manifesto titled "Por un nuevo partido político en Cataluña," presented at Barcelona's Teatre Tivoli, which decried the "nationalist monopoly" over Catalan society and advocated for a civic alternative prioritizing individual freedoms, constitutional pluralism, and opposition to secessionist tendencies.[9][10] The document, signed by prominent figures including journalist Arcadi Espada, playwright Albert Boadella, and jurist Francesc de Carreras, highlighted grievances such as the erosion of Spanish-language rights in schools and the exclusion of non-nationalist voices from power-sharing arrangements under Pasqual Maragall's PSC-ERC-ICV coalition.[11][12] The manifesto's release attracted over 2,000 attendees to the event and prompted widespread media coverage, signaling latent demand among urban, middle-class Catalans alienated by the perceived hegemony of ethno-nationalist politics, which had sidelined liberal and unionist perspectives since the transition to democracy. This initiative built on earlier critiques of post-Franco regional policies but crystallized amid the 2005-2006 Estatut reform process, where the proposed text expanded devolved powers in a manner seen by opponents as undermining national cohesion. In March 2006, a second manifesto reinforced these themes, urging the formation of a structured political entity to challenge the status quo ahead of regional elections.[9][13] On 8-9 July 2006, the platform convened its constituent congress in Barcelona, formally establishing Ciudadanos-Partido de la Ciudadanía (C's) as a registered political party with a liberal, anti-nationalist platform emphasizing civic citizenship over ethnic identity. Albert Rivera, a 26-year-old lawyer and water polo player who had been involved in the platform's youth outreach, was elected president, marking the transition from intellectual advocacy to electoral organization. The party's foundational documents committed to defending Spain's constitutional framework, promoting economic liberalism, and fostering bilingualism without coercion, positioning C's as a bulwark against the separatist-leaning consensus in Catalonia.[9][13] This foundation laid the groundwork for C's debut in the November 2006 Catalan parliamentary elections, where it secured three seats despite limited resources.[14]

Early Anti-Nationalist Activism (2006–2013)

Ciudadanos-Partido de la Ciudadanía was founded in Catalonia in 2006 amid opposition to the reformed Statute of Autonomy, which expanded regional powers and was criticized for embedding nationalist priorities such as linguistic preferences and historical narratives favoring Catalan identity over shared Spanish citizenship. The initiative stemmed from a 2005 manifesto urging the creation of a political alternative focused on civic values, individual rights, and rejection of ethnic-based politics. The party's inaugural congress in July 2006 elected Albert Rivera as its first president, with delegates numbering around 350 endorsing a platform emphasizing constitutional fidelity and anti-nationalist stances.[15][9] In the November 2006 Catalan regional elections, Ciudadanos positioned itself against the ruling coalition's promotion of separatism and cultural exclusivity, running a campaign that included Rivera's nude poster symbolizing the stripping away of nationalist privileges and myths. The party garnered three seats in the 135-seat Parliament of Catalonia, establishing a foothold as the primary non-nationalist opposition despite limited votes of about 3%. This entry allowed Ciudadanos to challenge policies like mandatory Catalan-language immersion in schools, which it argued discriminated against Spanish speakers and undermined bilingualism.[16][17] From 2007 to 2010, the party's parliamentary activities centered on critiquing the tripartite government's alignment with nationalist agendas, including advocacy for equal status of Spanish and Catalan languages in public life and opposition to measures portraying Catalonia as a distinct nation separate from Spain. Ciudadanos highlighted how such policies fostered division rather than cohesion, drawing on first-hand observations of linguistic coercion in education and media. Re-elected with three seats in the 2010 regional vote, the party maintained its scrutiny amid rising pro-independence rhetoric from parties like Convergència i Unió and Esquerra Republicana.[18][15] The period 2011–2013 saw intensified activism as economic downturns amplified separatist mobilization, with Ciudadanos organizing public events and media interventions to expose the impracticality of independence, citing potential disruptions to trade, finance, and EU membership. Rivera led efforts to defend Spanish cultural presence, such as protesting the marginalization of Castilian literature in curricula and supporting civic demonstrations for unity. By 2012, amid the large Diada independence marches, the party achieved electoral growth in the regional parliament, reflecting growing disillusionment with nationalist governance among urban and bilingual voters, though it remained a minority force until national outreach began.[19][20]

National Expansion and Breakthrough (2013–2015)

In October 2013, Ciudadanos, under the leadership of Albert Rivera, announced its expansion beyond Catalonia through a manifesto event titled "La conjura de Goya" held at Madrid's Teatro Goya, framing the initiative as a nationwide movement against institutional decay, economic stagnation, and territorial fragmentation.[21] The party established organizational structures in multiple regions, recruiting candidates and building local branches to contest national elections, marking a shift from its Catalan anti-nationalist roots to a broader liberal platform emphasizing constitutional unity and reform.[22] The expansion gained initial traction in the May 25, 2014, European Parliament elections, where Ciudadanos secured 3.12% of the national vote—approximately 814,000 votes—and elected two Members of the European Parliament, Javier Nart and Juan Carlos Girauta, representing its first breakthrough outside Catalonia.[23] This result positioned the party as a viable alternative to the dominant People's Party (PP) and Spanish Socialist Workers' Party (PSOE), capitalizing on voter disillusionment with corruption scandals and the economic crisis, though turnout remained low at 43.8%.[24] The May 24, 2015, municipal and regional elections represented Ciudadanos' national breakthrough, with the party obtaining 6.5% of the vote and 1,595 council seats across Spain, enabling pacts with the PP to govern in key cities like Madrid and Valencia.[25] In simultaneous regional elections in twelve autonomous communities, Ciudadanos entered all contested parliaments, winning 9 seats in Madrid, 13 in Valencia, and 40 in Catalonia—surpassing its previous regional performance and establishing itself as a kingmaker in coalition governments.[26] These gains reflected voter demand for a centrist option amid the erosion of the bipartite system, as evidenced by the PP and PSOE losing over 2,000 and 1,600 council seats respectively.[27] This momentum culminated in the December 20, 2015, general election, where Ciudadanos achieved 13.05% of the vote—over 3.5 million ballots—and 40 seats in the Congress of Deputies, becoming the fourth-largest party and a potential partner for the PP in post-election negotiations.[28] The results underscored the party's appeal to urban, educated voters prioritizing anti-corruption measures, economic liberalization, and opposition to separatism, though its reliance on Rivera as the primary figure raised questions about internal depth.[29]

Peak Electoral Success and Governance Role (2015–2019)

In the 27 September 2015 Catalan regional election, Ciudadanos achieved its strongest result to date, obtaining 25.37 percent of the vote and 36 seats in the 135-seat Parliament of Catalonia, placing second behind the pro-independence Junts pel Sí alliance.[30] This performance positioned the party as the leading anti-separatist force in the region, capitalizing on voter backlash against the independence push amid economic recovery concerns. Three months later, in the 20 December 2015 Spanish general election, Ciudadanos expanded nationally, securing 13.94 percent of the vote and 40 seats in the Congress of Deputies, emerging as the fourth-largest party and contributing to the fragmentation of the traditional two-party system dominated by the People's Party (PP) and Spanish Socialist Workers' Party (PSOE).[31][32] The June 2016 repeat general election saw a slight decline to 13.05 percent and 32 seats, yet Ciudadanos assumed a pivotal governance role by negotiating a non-binding investiture agreement with the PP on 2 August 2016, comprising 150 reform commitments on issues such as anti-corruption measures, electoral law changes, and public administration efficiency.[33] This pact enabled PP leader Mariano Rajoy to form a minority government on 31 October 2016 after two failed investiture attempts, with Ciudadanos providing external support without entering the cabinet, emphasizing its role as a centrist counterweight to both major parties. In regional contexts, the party similarly facilitated PP-led administrations in autonomous communities like the Valencian Community and Murcia following 2015 elections, where it won sufficient seats to act as a kingmaker against PSOE dominance. The October 2017 Catalan regional election, held amid the constitutional crisis triggered by the unilateral independence declaration, marked another high point: Ciudadanos topped the poll with 25.35 percent of the vote and 36 seats, becoming the largest single party despite pro-independence forces retaining a slim majority through bloc voting.[34] This outcome underscored the party's appeal as a defender of Spanish constitutional unity, drawing votes from unionist and moderate sectors alienated by separatist escalation. Nationally, Ciudadanos maintained momentum through 2017–2018 regional polls, notably in the 2 December 2018 Andalusian election, where it garnered 18.29 percent and 21 seats, enabling a PP-Ciudadanos coalition government—the first non-PSOE executive in the region since 1982—supported externally by Vox on key votes.[35] By the April 2019 general election, Ciudadanos reached its national electoral zenith with 15.86 percent of the vote and 57 seats, briefly positioning it as a potential coalition partner amid ongoing fragmentation. Throughout 2015–2019, the party's governance influence derived from its strategic alliances with the PP, enforcing policy concessions on economic liberalization and institutional reform while avoiding direct ministerial roles to preserve its outsider image; however, this positioning also exposed it to criticisms of enabling PP corruption scandals without full accountability.[2] In several autonomous communities, including Castilla y León and La Rioja by late 2019, Ciudadanos either joined coalitions or provided stability support, amplifying its role in shifting regional power dynamics away from left-wing monopolies.[19]

Decline Amid Polarization (2019–present)

In the November 2019 Spanish general election, Ciudadanos experienced a sharp electoral reversal, dropping from 57 seats in Congress obtained in the April 2019 vote to just 10 seats, with its vote share falling to approximately 6.8%.[5] This outcome, attributed in part to voter shifts toward the People's Party (PP) and the emerging Vox party amid heightened polarization over issues like Catalan separatism and economic recovery, led to the immediate resignation of party leader Albert Rivera, who also relinquished his parliamentary seat and withdrew from active politics.[36][3] Inés Arrimadas, previously the party's spokesperson in Congress and a prominent figure from its Catalan base, assumed the presidency in March 2020 following an internal selection process, marking her as the first woman to lead a major Spanish national party.[37] Under her leadership, Ciudadanos sought to refocus on liberal economic policies and national unity while navigating fragile regional coalitions, such as those with the PP in Valencia and Castilla y León. However, these efforts yielded limited success, as the party struggled to differentiate itself in a landscape dominated by bloc politics, where centrist voters increasingly consolidated behind the PP to counter the left-wing coalition government or gravitated to Vox for harder stances on immigration and separatism.[38] The decline accelerated in the May 2023 regional and municipal elections, where Ciudadanos lost nearly all its institutional presence, retaining only a handful of local council seats and failing to secure representation in most regional parliaments, including key strongholds like Catalonia.[39] Arrimadas resigned shortly thereafter, citing the need for party renewal amid what she described as a clear electoral message of diminished viability.[40] In response, the party opted not to field candidates in the ensuing July 2023 general election, a decision framed by its leadership as a strategic pause to avoid further fragmentation of the center-right vote against the PSOE-led incumbents.[41] This polarization-driven erosion reflected broader trends in Spanish politics, where the rise of Vox since 2018 siphoned culturally conservative support from Ciudadanos' right-leaning voters, while the PP absorbed its more pragmatic base, rendering the party's original anti-separatist, pro-EU centrism less competitive in a zero-sum electoral system favoring polarized alliances.[42] By 2024, Ciudadanos had shrunk to a marginal entity, with internal debates over dissolution or merger into the PP underscoring its existential crisis, as evidenced by its negligible participation in the European Parliament elections and ongoing loss of regional councilors.[39] The party's trajectory illustrates the challenges faced by liberal centrists in polarized environments, where empirical voting patterns show a premium on ideological clarity over moderation, particularly in response to persistent regional tensions and economic discontent.[42]

Ideology and Policy Positions

Core Liberal Framework

Ciudadanos positions itself as a liberal political party committed to individual freedoms, equality under the law, and the rule of law as foundational to democratic governance.[43] The party's ideology prioritizes civil liberties against state overreach, rejecting identity-based politics and emphasizing factual truth over ideological imposition in public discourse.[43] This framework draws from classical liberal tenets, advocating for equal opportunities for all citizens irrespective of origin or background, while opposing redistributive policies that undermine meritocracy or personal responsibility.[43] Economic aspects underscore support for middle-class prosperity through market-driven growth, balanced environmental policies that avoid hindering development, and rejection of degrowth ideologies in favor of sustainable expansion.[43] In response to perceived crises in liberal democracy, Ciudadanos outlined a "Liberal Refoundation" in 2022, comprising 10 principles to revitalize these core values amid challenges like authoritarian tendencies and economic stagnation.[43] Key among them are defenses of personal freedom, universal equality, and responsible policymaking focused on problem-solving rather than symbolic gestures.[43] The framework also integrates a commitment to civic patriotism, national unity, and European integration, viewing Spain's constitutional order as a bulwark for liberal principles against fragmentation or external threats.[43] This approach critiques both left-wing collectivism and populist excesses, prioritizing individual agency and institutional seriousness to foster societal progress.[43] The party's liberalism extends to foreign policy, supporting Spain's role in NATO and the European Union to advance strategic autonomy and liberal democratic standards globally, while maintaining a balanced stance on issues like environmental protection that aligns development with human needs.[43] Overall, Ciudadanos' framework seeks to counter polarization by reaffirming liberalism's emphasis on truth, responsibility, and opportunity as antidotes to ideological extremes.[43]

Stance on National Unity and Separatism

Ciudadanos originated as a civic platform in Catalonia in 2005, explicitly opposing the perceived nationalist drift and promotion of separatism within the region's political institutions, which it viewed as undermining democratic pluralism and Spanish constitutional order.[44] The party's founding manifesto emphasized a "nation of citizens" bound by shared civic values rather than ethnic or regional identities, positioning it as a bulwark against peripheral nationalisms that prioritize self-determination over national cohesion.[45] This anti-separatist foundation propelled Ciudadanos to gain representation in the Catalan Parliament in 2006, where it consistently voted against independence initiatives and advocated for adherence to the Spanish Constitution's indivisibility clause in Article 2.[17] Under former leader Albert Rivera, Ciudadanos championed "constitutional patriotism" as a counter to ethnic nationalism, defining it as loyalty to Spain's democratic institutions, rule of law, and equal citizenship across territories, rather than symbolic or historical allegiances.[46] Rivera articulated this in 2015, presenting it as essential to resisting both nationalism and populism, and reiterated it during the 2017 Catalan independence crisis, where the party demanded the activation of Article 155 of the Constitution to suspend autonomous powers and restore legality after the unilateral declaration of independence.[47][48] Ciudadanos opposed any negotiated referendums on self-determination outside the constitutional framework, arguing they illegitimately fragmented national sovereignty, and supported judicial interventions against separatist leaders for sedition and rebellion.[17] In its electoral programs, such as the 2019 general elections platform, Ciudadanos proposed constitutional reforms to reinforce central state competencies in education, justice, and fiscal policy, aiming to prevent separatist encroachments while preserving autonomies within a unified Spain.[49] The party has extended this stance beyond Catalonia, critiquing Basque and other peripheral nationalisms for similar threats to unity, and pledged to combat "separatist blackmail" in national politics by refusing pacts that concede to independence demands.[50] This position, rooted in liberal universalism, contrasts with both conservative unionism and progressive federalism, prioritizing empirical defense of institutional integrity over concessions that could incentivize further fragmentation.[51]

Economic Liberalism

Ciudadanos has positioned itself as a proponent of economic liberalism, emphasizing free-market principles, labor market flexibility, and incentives for entrepreneurship to foster growth and reduce unemployment. The party's programs advocate for reducing government intervention through deregulation, lower taxes, and streamlined public administration, drawing on models like Austria's flexicurity system to balance worker protections with employer needs. This approach aims to address Spain's structural economic challenges, such as high youth unemployment and a segmented labor market, by prioritizing private sector dynamism over expansive welfare spending.[52][53] A cornerstone of their labor policy is the replacement of temporary contracts with a single indefinite contract, supplemented by individual worker accounts for severance ("mochila austriaca"), where contributions accumulate for dismissal compensation rather than rigid employer liabilities. This measure, proposed in their 2015 and 2019 platforms, seeks to eliminate the dual labor market—where insiders enjoy protections while newcomers face precarity—by introducing graduated indemnities based on seniority and reducing social security contributions for stable hires, including full bonuses for under-30 workers in their first year. Complementary initiatives include training vouchers for the long-term unemployed and a "second chance" law for debt restructuring, allowing full market-value dation in payment to encourage risk-taking without fear of permanent insolvency.[52][53][7] On taxation, Ciudadanos favors reductions to stimulate investment and consumption, including lowering the top IRPF rate to 44%, a 100% bonus on inheritance and gift taxes for direct relatives, and corporate tax cuts to 15% for SMEs reinvesting profits. They have implemented inheritance tax eliminations in regions under their influence, such as Andalusia and Madrid coalitions, arguing that high levies discourage savings and family businesses while proposing a guaranteed annual salary supplement for low-income workers as a targeted alternative to broad redistribution. These policies extend to entrepreneurship, with proposals for a Startups Law enabling tax-free operations for two years, a flat €30 monthly self-employment rate for two years in rural areas, and company creation via mobile app in 24 hours to cut bureaucratic hurdles.[52][54] Public finances under Ciudadanos' vision prioritize efficiency and innovation, committing to raise R&D spending to 3% of GDP by 2030 through a National Industry Pact, while simplifying procurement and subsidies to combat corruption and waste. Their 2015 economic blueprint, coordinated by economist Luis Garicano, shifted emphasis from public works to knowledge-based growth, critiquing overreliance on infrastructure as inefficient amid Spain's post-crisis recovery. In practice, during 2016-2018 national budget support to the PP government, Ciudadanos backed fiscal consolidation measures that aligned with deficit reduction targets, though they critiqued excessive rigidity in EU-imposed austerity.[52][53][55]

Social and Cultural Policies

Ciudadanos supports regulated access to abortion within the framework of the 2010 Organic Law, emphasizing counseling and alternatives for women while opposing unrestricted expansion.[52] The party has not proposed repealing the law but advocates for enhanced support services, such as mandatory psychological assistance and promotion of adoption options prior to termination.[52] On end-of-life issues, Ciudadanos prioritizes comprehensive palliative care through a proposed Law on Rights and Guarantees at the End of Life, aiming to ensure nationwide access to pain management and hospice services before considering assisted dying.[52] In 2018, the party conditioned parliamentary support for euthanasia legislation on prior approval of robust palliative frameworks, reflecting a cautious stance favoring symptom relief over active termination.[56] The party endorses equality for LGBTQ individuals via a proposed Law on Sexual Diversity and Gender, seeking to eliminate discrimination in employment, housing, and services while promoting inclusion without quotas.[52] It supports same-sex marriage, legalized in 2005, and extends rights through a national Law on De Facto Partnerships for equal inheritance, pension, and parental benefits.[52] Altruistic surrogacy is favored for all couples, including same-sex pairs, with strict bans on commercial arrangements to prevent exploitation.[52] Family policies focus on reversing demographic decline, with proposals for a Law on Protection and Support for Families offering annual benefits of €1,200 for households with two or more children (€2,400 for three or more) and free childcare for ages 0-3.[52] Parental leave would extend to 16 weeks per parent, with incentives for shared responsibility.[52] Gender equality measures target wage gaps via transparency mandates and incentives for women in STEM and leadership, without mandating parity lists.[52] Immigration policy emphasizes legal pathways and integration, introducing a points-based visa system prioritizing skills, education, and language proficiency to attract researchers and professionals.[52] Illegal entries would face stricter enforcement, including international cooperation to dismantle trafficking networks, alongside requirements for immigrants to demonstrate self-sufficiency and cultural adaptation.[52] In education, Ciudadanos calls for a National Education Pact to standardize quality across Spain's regions, rejecting fragmented systems that undermine unity.[52] Key reforms include free English immersion programs, a unified university entrance exam, teacher merit-based selection via MIR system, and free textbooks in public schools to promote meritocracy and equal opportunity.[52] Culturally, the party promotes Spanish heritage through a Plan to Disseminate Spanish History countering negative narratives like the "Black Legend," and an internationalization strategy bolstering institutions such as the Real Academia Española and Instituto Cervantes to advance the language globally.[52] Policies stress bilingual education in co-official language regions to preserve national cohesion without suppressing regional identities.[52]

Foreign Affairs and European Integration

Ciudadanos positions itself as Spain's most pro-European political formation, emphasizing the defense of democratic values and European unity against nationalist and populist challenges. As a member of the Renew Europe group in the European Parliament, the party supports advancing toward a more integrated Union, including reforms to enhance transparency in fund management, such as the Next Generation EU recovery instrument, where its representatives contributed to design and implementation efforts to foster economic transformation in Spain.[57] The party advocates for a strong, value-based Europe rooted in liberal principles, viewing deeper integration as essential to counter authoritarian threats and promote open societies and economies.[58] On broader foreign affairs, Ciudadanos promotes an Atlanticist orientation, supporting NATO's expansion to bolster collective defense; its parliamentary representatives voted in favor of admitting Sweden and Finland to the alliance in 2022, aligning with transatlantic security priorities. The party favors robust EU trade policies to expand opportunities for citizens and businesses, having led negotiations for agreements like the EU-Mercosur pact with provisions for environmental and labor standards. In the Middle East, Ciudadanos condemns Hamas's use of terror and human shields while urging Israel, as the region's sole democracy, to adhere to international humanitarian law, rejecting unchecked actions by either side and endorsing a two-state solution contingent on concrete steps toward peace rather than rhetoric alone.[59][60][61] The party's approach prioritizes pragmatic realism, favoring EU leadership in global crises while maintaining alliances like NATO to address threats to European borders and democratic norms. Ciudadanos critiques overly ideological foreign policies, advocating instead for adult, serious engagement that upholds liberal internationalism without granting carte blanche to aggressors or violators of norms. This stance reflects a commitment to causal accountability, where support for integration and alliances is tied to verifiable advancements in security, prosperity, and human rights.[57][60]

Internal Organization

Leadership Succession

Albert Rivera served as the president of Ciudadanos from July 2006 until his resignation on November 11, 2019, a period during which the party transitioned from a regional Catalan anti-nationalist platform to a national centrist force.[62] His departure followed the party's loss of 47 seats in the Congress of Deputies during the November 2019 general election, dropping from 57 to 10 seats.[63][5] The party then held primaries on March 15, 2020, electing Inés Arrimadas as president; she secured victory over Francisco Igea, receiving over 77% of the vote among participating militants.[64] Arrimadas' tenure, lasting until June 1, 2023, was characterized by attempts to reposition the party amid ongoing electoral erosion, including abstaining from the investiture of Pedro Sánchez in January 2020.[38] Arrimadas resigned after Ciudadanos suffered a near-total collapse in the May 28, 2023, municipal and regional elections, failing to retain any of the six regional presidencies it previously held and losing representation in most local councils.[65][40] In the ensuing leadership transition, Edmundo Bal acted as parliamentary spokesperson, but no immediate presidential election occurred.[66] Carlos Pérez-Nievas assumed the role of secretary general in March 2024 following Adrián Vázquez's resignation, positioning him as the party's de facto leader without a formal presidential contest.[67] A former deputy in the Parliament of Navarra and education counselor, Pérez-Nievas has focused on revitalizing the party's liberal profile and critiquing political polarization.[67] As of February 2025, he remains the leading figure, advocating for a "new, attractive" project amid the party's diminished parliamentary presence of three seats.[68]

Party Structure and Affiliations

Ciudadanos maintains a structured internal organization as defined in its statutes, approved by the party's General Assembly on January 14–15, 2023. The Asamblea General functions as the supreme decision-making body, comprising delegates (compromisarios) elected by affiliates in good standing; it convenes every four years to deliberate on statutes, ideological principles, strategic orientations, and major programmatic decisions. Between assemblies, authority resides with the Consejo General, a body of 125 members elected by the Asamblea General through open lists, responsible for supervision, budget approval, and interim strategy setting. The Comité Nacional, the primary executive organ with a maximum of 24 ex officio members plus additional elected representatives, directs daily operations, financial management, and policy execution under the leadership of the Secretary General. A Comité Permanente, as a subset of the Comité Nacional, ensures continuity in governance.[69] Leadership centers on the Secretary General, elected directly by party affiliates via universal suffrage among those with at least 12 months of membership and paid dues; this position entails representing the party externally, chairing the Comité Nacional and Comité Permanente, and exercising tie-breaking votes in collegial bodies. Territorial organization follows a decentralized model with Comités Autonómicos (one per autonomous community), Comités Provinciales, and Comités Locales or Agrupaciones Locales (requiring at least 15 affiliates), enabling localized coordination of activities, candidate selection, and electoral participation while aligning with national directives. Guarantee mechanisms include the Comisión de Garantías for appeals, a Comisión Disciplinaria for sanctions, and a Gabinete de Cumplimiento for ethical oversight. Membership requires individuals aged 18 or older, possessing full legal capacity, and either EU citizenship or equivalent residency rights, with acquisition via application and adherence to party principles; rights include voting in primaries and assemblies, subject to dues payment and no outstanding sanctions.[69][70] At the European level, Ciudadanos holds membership in the Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe Party (ALDE Party), aligning with its pro-European, liberal orientation and commitment to countering nationalism and populism within a united European framework. From 2014 to 2019, the party's Members of the European Parliament affiliated with the ALDE group (later rebranded Renew Europe in 2019), but following electoral declines, it currently lacks representation in the European Parliament as of the 2024 elections. No formal affiliations with international or other continental organizations beyond ALDE are stipulated in the statutes, though the party extends operations to EU territories and may pursue international extensions.[1]

Electoral Record

General and Regional Elections

Ciudadanos first contested national general elections in December 2015, securing 3,514,846 votes (13.05 percent of the valid vote) and 40 seats in the Congress of Deputies, establishing itself as the fourth-largest party and contributing to the fragmentation of Spain's traditional two-party system.[71][72] In the June 2016 repeat election, the party obtained 3,141,919 votes (13.23 percent) and 32 seats, reflecting a modest decline amid voter fatigue and coalition negotiations that positioned it as a potential kingmaker for a center-right government.[73][74] The April 2019 general election marked Ciudadanos' electoral zenith, with 4,150,397 votes (15.86 percent) translating to 57 seats, its strongest national performance driven by appeals to anti-separatist voters and economic liberalism amid Catalonia's independence crisis.[75] However, the November 2019 repeat saw a sharp reversal, yielding 1,634,283 votes (6.80 percent) and only 10 seats, as disillusioned supporters migrated to the People's Party (PP) and Vox amid perceptions of ideological ambiguity and failed coalition strategies.[76][77] By the July 2023 election, Ciudadanos collapsed to 414,122 votes (1.23 percent), failing to secure any congressional seats due to the 3 percent provincial threshold, signaling its effective marginalization at the national level.[78]

European Parliament Elections

Ciudadanos first contested the European Parliament elections on 25 May 2014, garnering 495,114 votes or 3.16% of the valid votes, which translated to three seats under the d'Hondt method applied to Spain's single national constituency allocating 54 mandates.[81] The elected members—Javier Nart, Juan Carlos Girauta, and Carolina Punset—affiliated with the Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe (ALDE) group, aligning the party with pro-European liberal forces emphasizing economic liberalization and federalist reforms.[82] By the 26 May 2019 elections, amid heightened national visibility from its anti-separatist stance and centrist positioning, Ciudadanos expanded to seven seats with approximately 12% vote share, led by candidate Luis Garicano, an economist advocating market-oriented policies and EU integration.[6] This result positioned the party as a key player in the Renew Europe group (successor to ALDE), where its MEPs contributed to debates on digital single market rules and migration pacts, though internal shifts toward more conservative alliances strained group cohesion. The gain reflected temporary voter flight from traditional parties post-financial crisis but foreshadowed volatility tied to national polarization.

In the 9 June 2024 elections, Ciudadanos, headed by Jordi Cañas, collapsed to 120,000 votes (0.6%), below the effective threshold for representation amid voter consolidation toward PP and Vox on the center-right, resulting in zero seats and complete loss of EP presence.[6][83] This outcome mirrored the party's broader electoral erosion, exacerbated by leadership instability and failure to differentiate from larger conservative blocs, leaving no institutional foothold for its liberal-unionist platform in Brussels.

Municipal and Local Results

In the 2015 municipal elections held on 24 May, Citizens expanded beyond its Catalan origins to secure representation in city councils across Spain, particularly in urban centers like Madrid, Barcelona, and Valencia, where it positioned itself as a pivotal partner for People's Party (PP) administrations through post-election pacts.[84] This marked the party's initial national breakthrough at the local level, emphasizing anti-corruption and unionist stances that resonated in areas affected by regional separatism.[85] Citizens reached its peak municipal influence in the 2019 elections on 26 May, obtaining 2,789 councillor seats nationwide.[86] The party subsequently entered governing coalitions in about 400 municipalities via alliances, primarily with the PP, and directly led approximately 200 town halls, enhancing its local executive presence.[87] The 2023 municipal elections on 28 May represented a severe collapse, with Citizens securing only 392 councillors and 302,302 votes, an 86% reduction in seats from 2019 and exclusion from all provincial capitals.[88][89] Remaining strongholds were confined to smaller localities, where the party retained absolute majorities in 62 municipalities, often in rural or mid-sized towns in regions like Valencia and Murcia.[90] This outcome reflected voter shifts toward the PP and Vox on the center-right spectrum, amid Citizens' national decline.[79]

Performance Trends and Analysis

Ciudadanos experienced a rapid ascent in national elections from its national debut in 2015, securing 40 seats in the Congress of Deputies with 13.05% of the vote (3,514,557 votes) in the December election, positioning itself as the fourth-largest party amid voter dissatisfaction with the traditional PSOE-PP duopoly.[91] In the June 2016 repeat election, it maintained momentum with 32 seats and 13.05% (3,141,919 votes), again fourth place, though turnout and fragmentation affected gains.[73] The party's peak came in the April 2019 election, yielding 57 seats and 15.09% (4,153,085 votes), nearly overtaking the PP as the main center-right force, driven by anti-separatist appeals and economic liberal messaging.[92] However, the November 2019 election marked a sharp reversal, dropping to 10 seats and 6.77% (1,671,486 votes), falling to sixth place as voters shifted to Vox and PP.[93] By 2023, Ciudadanos opted not to contest national elections, effectively ceding representation after dismal local and regional showings.[41]

Regional performances mirrored national volatility but with earlier declines in core areas like Catalonia, where Ciudadanos won 36 seats (18.3%) in 2015 parliamentary elections as the leading anti-independence force, only to plummet to 18 seats (5.6%) by 2021 amid separatist resurgence and voter fatigue. In Valencia, it briefly governed in coalition with PP post-2015, but lost all seats in 2023 regional polls. Similar erosion occurred in Andalusia (from 11% in 2018 to under 5% in 2022) and Madrid, where it failed to retain influence after 2021 alliances. European Parliament results tracked the pattern: 7.14% and no seats in 2014, surging to 12.18% and 7 MEPs in 2019, before collapsing to 2.7% and zero in 2024. Municipal results showed parallel decay, with councilors dropping from over 2,000 in 2015 to fewer than 400 by 2023. Analytically, Ciudadanos' trajectory reflects the challenges of sustaining a centrist-liberal niche in Spain's polarized landscape, where the 2015-2019 surge capitalized on post-crisis anti-establishment sentiment and Catalan separatism but faltered due to ideological ambiguity and tactical errors. The party's abstention in Mariano Rajoy's 2016 investiture, while enabling a PP minority government, preserved short-term stability but blurred distinctions from the right, eroding its unique anti-corruption, pro-EU appeal.[94] A graver misstep was the 2019 abstention facilitating Pedro Sánchez's PSOE investiture, alienating conservative voters who viewed it as enabling left-wing policies, including amnesty overtures to separatists, and accelerating flight to Vox's harder-line conservatism.[95] Competition intensified as PP under Feijóo consolidated center-right space, absorbing moderate liberals, while Vox captured nationalist discontent Ciudadanos once monopolized. Internal factors, including leadership transitions from Rivera to Arrimadas and failure to build grassroots infrastructure beyond urban elites, compounded voter disconnection, with polls showing preference for established parties in fragmented coalitions. Empirical data indicate the center's squeeze in majoritarian systems favors poles, as evidenced by Ciudadanos' vote share halving post-2019 amid rising abstention among its base.[42] By 2023, systemic irrelevance prompted withdrawal, underscoring how unanchored centrism yields to ideological clarity in causal political dynamics.[96]

Controversies and Public Scrutiny

Internal Conflicts and Leadership Crises

In June 2019, Ciudadanos experienced a significant internal rift following the party's decision to reject alliances with the PSOE and pivot toward cooperation with the PP and Vox, prompting resignations from key figures critical of the strategic shift. Toni Roldán, the party's economic spokesperson, resigned on June 24, 2019, citing the abandonment of the party's centrist principles in favor of a rightward orientation that he argued undermined its ability to address Spain's economic challenges.[97][98] Javier Nart, a member of the executive, also stepped down the same day, while other affiliates like Juan Vázquez abandoned their roles, exacerbating tensions over leadership decisions under Albert Rivera.[99][100] These departures highlighted fractures between the party's founding liberal base and its electoral pragmatists, with regional disputes—such as candidate selection conflicts in Castellón—further fueling discontent.[101] The crisis intensified after the November 10, 2019, general elections, where Ciudadanos plummeted from 57 to 10 seats, prompting Rivera to resign as party president and deputy on November 11, 2019, and call for an extraordinary congress to select a successor.[102][103] Inés Arrimadas, the parliamentary spokesperson, emerged as the frontrunner with broad internal backing, including from former critics like Roldán who temporarily reconciled, and assumed leadership in early 2020 amid hopes of stabilizing the party.[104][105] However, ongoing electoral declines eroded unity, culminating in 2022 leadership primaries where Edmundo Bal, the deputy spokesperson, challenged Arrimadas on December 2, framing his bid as a necessary transition to rebuild, amid disputes over policy stances like the "solo sí es sí" law and broader strategic direction.[106][107] A third candidacy from grassroots affiliates added to the fragmentation.[108] By 2023, the party's collapse after the May 28 regional and municipal elections—losing nearly all representation—led to the decision against contesting the July general elections, triggering Arrimadas' exit from politics on June 1, 2023.[65][109] Bal and Francisco Igea faced expulsion on September 28, 2023, for launching an alternative platform and publicly opposing the non-participation, underscoring a leadership vacuum and fratricidal struggles as the party splintered without a viable path forward.[110][111] These episodes reflected deeper causal tensions between ideological purity, electoral opportunism, and organizational cohesion, contributing to Ciudadanos' marginalization.

Media Portrayals and Ideological Mischaracterizations

Ciudadanos, self-described as a liberal party emphasizing individual freedoms, economic liberalism, and opposition to ethnic nationalism, has frequently been portrayed in Spanish media as shifting toward right-wing conservatism, particularly following its national expansion after 2015 and alliances with the Partido Popular (PP) against Catalan independence efforts. Left-leaning outlets, such as El País, have depicted the party's evolution from a Catalan anti-secessionist platform—initially blending liberal and social-democratic elements—into a more unified Spanish force as a departure from its cosmopolitan origins, framing its constitutionalism as akin to traditionalist defenses of the status quo. This portrayal often overlooks Ciudadanos' consistent pro-European Union stance, support for regulated market economies, and social policies like endorsement of same-sex marriage and limited abortion access, which align with classical liberalism rather than social conservatism.[112] A notable mischaracterization emerged in comparative narratives equating Ciudadanos with Podemos, the left-populist party, by dubbing it the "Podemos of the right." This analogy, advanced in analyses from outlets like openDemocracy, implies a mirror-image populism driven by anti-establishment fervor, yet Ciudadanos has historically advocated incremental reforms within existing institutions, pro-NATO positions, and fiscal responsibility measures post-2008 crisis, contrasting sharply with Podemos' more radical redistribution and anti-austerity rhetoric. Such framing, while highlighting the party's rapid polling surge to leading positions in 2018, serves to delegitimize its critique of separatist movements by associating it with ideological extremes, despite empirical policy divergences like Ciudadanos' rejection of coalition pacts with the far-right Vox party in 2019 regional elections.[17] In Spain's polarized media environment, where left-leaning publications dominate cultural commentary and exhibit systemic biases toward framing center-right actors as threats to pluralism, Ciudadanos' opposition to identity-based politics has been recast as regressive nationalism. For instance, coverage in regional outlets has noted the party's transition from perceived centrism to right-wing labeling, attributing this to its hardline stance on territorial unity amid the 2017 Catalan referendum crisis, even as the party maintained liberal economic proposals like tax reductions and labor market deregulation. This selective emphasis contributes to mischaracterizations that conflate defense of legal uniformity with authoritarianism, ignoring data from electoral platforms showing Ciudadanos' voter base drawn from urban professionals favoring meritocracy over quota systems. Right-leaning media, conversely, have amplified its anti-corruption credentials without ideological scrutiny, but overall, the asymmetry in critical portrayals underscores how institutional left-wing tilts in journalism—evident in underreporting of separatist media's own biases—affect balanced depiction of non-left alternatives.[113]

Relations with Other Political Forces

Ciudadanos has primarily pursued pragmatic alliances with the Partido Popular (PP) to facilitate center-right governance and oppose socialist-led administrations and regional separatist forces. In October 2016, the party abstained in the investiture vote for Mariano Rajoy's minority PP government, enabling its formation after repeated election deadlocks, with Ciudadanos securing commitments on anti-corruption reforms and economic liberalization in exchange.[94][114] This abstention reflected Ciudadanos' strategic positioning as a moderating influence on the PP rather than a direct coalition partner at the national level. Similarly, following the December 2018 Andalusian regional elections, Ciudadanos entered a formal coalition with the PP, ending 36 years of PSOE dominance in the region; the agreement included 90 policy measures on education, taxation, and employment, with Ciudadanos' Juan Marín serving as vice president.[115] Relations with the PSOE have been predominantly adversarial, with Ciudadanos framing itself as a liberal alternative to the socialists' interventionist policies and accommodations with peripheral nationalists. The party consistently opposed PSOE investitures, such as Pedro Sánchez's in 2018 and 2019, prioritizing fiscal responsibility and constitutional unity over cross-aisle pacts. Limited local or regional abstentions occurred in exceptional cases, but these were tactical and did not extend to national support, underscoring ideological divergences on issues like labor reform and Catalan separatism. Ciudadanos has explicitly rejected alliances with Vox, distinguishing itself through liberal economic stances and aversion to what it terms Vox's "extremism" on cultural and immigration matters. In November 2019, party lawmakers voted to reprove Vox figures in Madrid and opposed cooperation in Catalonia, risking PP-Vox majorities to maintain distance. This stance contributed to electoral competition, as Vox captured voters disillusioned with Ciudadanos' perceived moderation, accelerating the latter's decline post-2019.[116] Against regional separatist parties like ERC and Junts, Ciudadanos adopted a firm unionist posture, particularly in Catalonia, where it originated as an anti-nationalist platform; this included legal challenges to independence processes and support for PP-led interventions under Article 155 of the Spanish Constitution. Under leaders like Inés Arrimadas and Edmundo Bal through 2023, the party sought to revive center-right unity without Vox, though its parliamentary irrelevance after the July 2023 general elections limited influence. Bal's subsequent departure to form the center-oriented Cree in 2024 highlighted ongoing tensions with both PSOE's governance model and PP's occasional Vox pacts.[117][118]

Funding and Ethical Questions

Ciudadanos' primary sources of funding have consisted of public subsidies allocated by the Spanish state and regional governments based on electoral performance, alongside private donations from individuals and, historically, affiliate contributions or low-interest loans solicited from supporters. Public funding, governed by Organic Law 8/2007 and subsequent reforms, provides parties with fixed amounts per vote obtained and additional allocations for parliamentary representation; for instance, the party received €34.6 million in electoral subsidies in 2019, reflecting its peak national support, but these dwindled to zero for state-level activities by 2024 following electoral declines below the threshold.[119][120] Private donations are capped at €100,000 annually per donor under Spanish law, with Ciudadanos reporting modest inflows, such as 47 ordinary donors averaging €401.75 each in 2023, none exceeding the €25,000 disclosure threshold for detailed public reporting.[120] The party has promoted a model of financial independence by funding early campaigns through citizen loans rather than corporate or banking sources, avoiding potential influence from large private entities.[121] Ethical questions surrounding Ciudadanos' finances have been limited compared to systemic issues in other major Spanish parties, with no convictions for illegal financing recorded against the organization or its leadership. In April 2016, a group of dissident affiliates and former members filed a complaint with the Anti-Corruption Prosecutor's Office alleging possible irregular funding practices, including undeclared contributions tied to the party's expansion; however, the allegations stemmed from internal factional disputes and did not yield formal charges or judicial findings of wrongdoing, as subsequent investigations by the Court of Accounts found no evidence of systemic violations.[122][123] The party's statutes and ethical code mandate transparency, including annual financial audits published on its website and a requirement for elected officials to resign upon accusation of corruption-related offenses, positioning Ciudadanos as adhering to stricter internal accountability than mandated by law.[120] Critics from left-leaning outlets have occasionally speculated on opaque early funding linked to its Catalan origins or European alliances, such as the 2009 Libertas venture, but these claims lack substantiation from official probes and appear motivated by ideological opposition rather than empirical irregularities.[124] Overall, Ciudadanos' financial practices have emphasized compliance with Spain's post-2015 financing reforms, which enhanced donor disclosure and banned anonymous contributions, resulting in audited accounts showing cleared debts (e.g., €5.2 million in loans resolved by 2019) and reliance on verifiable public allocations over time.[120] This approach contrasts with documented illegal slush funds in parties like the PP (Gürtel case) or PSOE (ERE scandal), underscoring Ciudadanos' relative insulation from the corruption endemic critiqued in broader analyses of Spanish political funding.[125][126]

Altercations and Public Incidents

In July 2019, during the Orgullo LGTBI parade in Madrid, Ciudadanos members including congressional spokesperson Inés Arrimadas were confronted by activists who blocked their path, hurled ice cubes, and chanted "No pasarán" and "Con Vox no se pacta" in reference to the party's exploratory talks with Vox. Police intervened to escort the group out amid the harassment, which the party described as attempted aggression and intimidation. A subsequent Madrid police report, however, concluded there were no physical assaults, only verbal confrontations and minor thrown objects, contradicting Ciudadanos' initial claims of violence.[127][128][129] Earlier incidents in Catalonia involved more direct physical elements. On November 13, 2008, during a Barcelona demonstration against the Ley de Educación de Cataluña, nationalists attacked Ciudadanos activists by throwing lighters and other objects while shouting "Fuera nazis, fuera españoles," forcing Mossos d'Esquadra to provide custody and protection. The party reported multiple injuries from the assaults, framing them as targeted violence against non-nationalist voices.[130] Broader patterns of political violence in Catalonia have frequently targeted Ciudadanos as a constitutionalist party opposing independence. Independentist groups perpetrated over 90% of documented incidents against such parties between 2017 and 2021, including vandalism of offices, threats, and physical confrontations at public events, though specific Ciudadanos cases often involved disputed escalations from verbal to physical. For instance, former leader Albert Rivera faced repeated public heckling, such as boos and insults during a September 2019 visit to Vic, accompanied by Inés Arrimadas, highlighting ongoing tensions with local nationalists. In parliamentary settings, heated verbal clashes occurred, including a 2016 Congress session where Podemos deputies refused to stand for a minute of silence, prompting uproar from Ciudadanos and others, though without physical contact.[131][132][133][134]
