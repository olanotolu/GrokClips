Elizabeth Hesselblad

Maria Elisabeth Hesselblad, OSsS (4 June 1870 – 24 April 1957), was a Swedish religious sister who revived the Bridgettine Order by founding its active Roman branch in 1911 and re-establishing it in Sweden, while also sheltering Jews in her Rome convent during the Holocaust, for which she was posthumously honored as Righteous Among the Nations.[1][2] Born into a Lutheran family in the village of Faglavik as the fifth of thirteen children, she emigrated to the United States in her youth, trained as a nurse at Roosevelt Hospital in New York, and converted to Catholicism in 1902 after experiencing a profound spiritual calling.[1][3] Returning to Europe, Hesselblad professed vows as a Bridgettine in 1906 and dedicated her life to ecumenism, prayer for Christian unity, and charitable works, including founding convents across Italy and beyond.[1] During World War II, as superior of the Casa di Santa Brigida in Rome, she hid around 60 Jews for several months amid Nazi occupation, coordinating their protection through networks including the Swedish embassy.[2][4] Canonized by Pope Francis on 5 June 2016—the first canonization Mass over which he personally presided—she became the second saint from modern Sweden after the medieval St. Bridget, celebrated for her fidelity amid religious persecution and her intercession in verified miracles.[5][6]

Early Life and Background

Childhood and Family Influences

Maria Elisabeth Hesselblad was born on 4 June 1870 in the village of Faglavik, Älvsborg Province, Sweden, as the fifth of thirteen children to parents Augusto Roberto Hesselblad and Cajsa Pettesdotter Dag.[1] [7] She was raised in a devout Lutheran household of the Reformed Church of Sweden, baptized in the Hundene parish, where the family's Protestant faith emphasized religious observance.[1] [8] Economic difficulties prompted multiple family relocations during her childhood, shaping her experience in modest circumstances and fostering early resilience.[1] [7] This environment, marked by parental piety, cultivated her initial interest in religious matters and moral inclinations toward charity.[8]

Education and Move to America

Elizabeth Hesselblad, born the fifth of thirteen children in a poor Lutheran family in Fåglavik, Sweden, on June 4, 1870, received only limited formal education due to the family's financial constraints and large size, completing her basic schooling by age eighteen.[1][3] To contribute to her family's support amid intermittent poverty, she began working locally in Sweden around 1886, first in Karlshorg, before seeking greater opportunities abroad.[1][4] In 1888, at age eighteen, Hesselblad emigrated to the United States primarily for employment prospects, arriving in New York on July 9 of that year.[9][10] She promptly enrolled in nursing training at Roosevelt Hospital in Manhattan, where she completed her formal education in the profession and began working as a nurse starting in 1888.[11][7] During her time in America, Hesselblad endured experiences of poverty and demanding labor, often providing home care to the sick, elderly, and poor immigrants, including many Catholics among New York's working-class population.[7][3] Her duties frequently involved tending to injured laborers and those in dire conditions, which sharpened her practical nursing skills through hands-on immersion in challenging urban environments.[9][12]

Conversion to Catholicism

Spiritual Quest and Key Influences

Hesselblad, raised in the Lutheran Reformed Church of Sweden, experienced growing dissatisfaction with Protestant doctrines during her nursing career in the United States, where she emigrated in 1888 and encountered Catholic patients whose faith practices ignited her quest for deeper truth.[1] This exposure prompted intensive personal study of Church history and theology, alongside periods of fervent prayer, leading her to perceive the Catholic Church as embodying a more complete sacramental and doctrinal realism absent in her Lutheran upbringing.[1][13] Key external influences included interactions with Catholic clergy, notably Jesuit priest Fr. Giovani Giorgio Hagen, who provided spiritual guidance, and the writings and example of St. Bridget of Sweden, whose mystical insights resonated with Hesselblad's aspirations for ecclesiastical unity and reform.[1][13] Internally, a profound devotion to the Virgin Mary deepened her discernment, fostering a rejection of doctrinal relativism in favor of the Catholic Church's authoritative tradition, as evidenced by her documented reflections on the Eucharist and apostolic continuity.[1] Her spiritual deliberations occurred amid chronic health challenges, including frailty from exhaustive nursing duties, yet these trials reinforced her resolve, as she credited divine graces for sustaining her pursuit of certainty over ambiguity in matters of faith.[1] This period of reflection, spanning the late 1890s to early 1900s, culminated in a conviction grounded in empirical encounters with Catholic devotion and historical continuity, rather than abstract sentiment.[13]

Formal Conversion and Initial Challenges

On August 15, 1902, Elizabeth Hesselblad received conditional baptism at the Convent of the Visitation in Washington, D.C., administered by Jesuit priest Father Giovanni Giorgio Hagen, who subsequently served as her spiritual director.[1] This rite marked her formal entry into the Catholic Church, during which she adopted the religious name Maria Elisabeth to signify her deepened commitment to the faith.[13] The conditional nature of the baptism accounted for the validity of her prior infant baptism in the Lutheran Church of Sweden, ensuring sacramental integrity amid her transition from Protestantism.[14] Her conversion elicited strong opposition from her Lutheran family in Sweden, who viewed Catholicism as a deviation from their established Protestant traditions. Hesselblad's mother, in particular, reacted with anguish upon learning of the decision, imploring her not to publicize what she deemed shameful news, reflecting the deep-seated familial loyalty to Lutheranism.[8] This resistance was compounded by broader Swedish cultural skepticism toward Catholicism, often perceived as an imported "innovation" incompatible with the nation's post-Reformation heritage, where Catholic practices had been suppressed for centuries and converts were rare.[3] In the immediate aftermath, Hesselblad grappled with personal isolation and material hardship as she sought to integrate into Catholic circles while estranged from her Protestant roots. Living as a nurse in the United States with limited resources, she faced emotional solitude amid her family's disapproval and the challenges of forging new spiritual ties in an unfamiliar ecclesiastical environment.[15] These trials tested her resolve, yet she proceeded swiftly to Rome later in 1902, embarking on a pilgrimage that underscored her determination despite ongoing financial precarity and health vulnerabilities.[13]

Nursing Career and Religious Vocation

Professional Work as a Nurse

Hesselblad immigrated to the United States in 1888 at age 18 and enrolled as a nursing student at Roosevelt Hospital in New York City, completing her training there by the early 1890s.[1] She worked as a hospital nurse before shifting to private duty and home care, attending to sick immigrants and the destitute in their residences, often traveling long distances by train for days at a time.[1] Her practice emphasized hygiene protocols and direct patient support, which aligned with emerging standards in early 20th-century nursing that reduced infection rates through sanitation and isolation measures, contributing to tangible health improvements among underserved populations.[11] In 1896, she left hospital employment for independent private nursing, serving affluent families like the Cisneros while maintaining a focus on charitable cases among the poor.[1] This work involved administering treatments, monitoring vital signs, and providing palliative care in resource-limited settings, where basic interventions like wound cleaning and nutritional support often proved decisive in recovery outcomes. Encounters with chronic illness and poverty in these roles highlighted the efficacy of methodical caregiving, reinforcing service to the afflicted as a core expression of human solidarity.[16] Her professional diligence earned recognition for conscientiousness, as she balanced medical duties with familial support remittances to Sweden.[16]

Discernment of a Religious Calling

Following her conversion to Catholicism on August 15, 1902, in Washington, D.C., Elisabeth Hesselblad experienced a deepening attraction to consecrated religious life, discerning that vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience would enable a total self-gift to God beyond the limitations of her lay nursing profession.[1] She viewed perpetual celibacy and communal living as essential for sustained spiritual fruitfulness and effective service to the Church, rejecting secular alternatives such as marriage or independent apostolic work, which she believed diluted one's focus amid worldly distractions.[1] This conviction stemmed from her observation of historical religious communities' proven capacity to preserve and propagate faith amid adversity, a pattern she contrasted with the fragmentation of individual lay efforts.[17] By early 1903, Hesselblad's sense of vocation intensified during travels in Europe, prompting initial attempts to enter existing convents despite her age (approaching 33) and chronic health issues, including bleeding ulcers that rendered her physically frail.[3] She applied to the Carmelite convent at Rome's Casa di Santa Brigida in 1904, receiving the habit as a novice under the name Maria Elisabeth and beginning formation, though her condition limited full integration.[3] These efforts highlighted her resolve to embrace enclosure and stability for deeper prayer and charity, informed by consultations with Catholic spiritual directors who affirmed the authenticity of her call while cautioning on practical obstacles.[3] In 1906, at age 36, Hesselblad discerned a "call within a call" to a distinct form of religious dedication, leading her to seek approval from ecclesiastical authorities for private profession under a traditional rule.[17] With guidance from Jesuit priest Johann Georg Hagen, who had instructed her pre-conversion, she petitioned Pope Pius X, who granted dispensation on June 25, 1906, allowing her to don the habit of the Order of the Most Holy Savior and profess solemn vows as a spiritual daughter of the Bridgettines, bypassing standard novitiate requirements due to her health.[1][3] This step marked the culmination of her discernment, affirming vows as the causal foundation for a life oriented toward evangelical poverty, communal witness, and intercessory mission over solitary or temporal pursuits.[1]

Revival of the Bridgettine Order

Inspiration from St. Bridget of Sweden

Maria Elizabeth Hesselblad, born in Sweden in 1870, developed a profound spiritual affinity for St. Bridget of Sweden (1303–1373), the medieval mystic and founder of the Bridgettine Order, whose revelations emphasized ecclesiastical reform, devotion to the Eucharist, and Christian unity.[18] Hesselblad studied Bridget's visions—compiled in the Revelations—and the order's rule, discerning parallels between her own Swedish heritage and Bridget's calls for renewal amid 14th-century corruption and division, much as Hesselblad sought to address modern spiritual fragmentation in a post-Reformation Scandinavia.[18] [1] She recognized the Bridgettine Order's sharp decline following the Protestant Reformation, which dismantled nearly all its Northern European monasteries, including those in Sweden, through state-enforced suppressions and confiscations that targeted Catholic monastic traditions.[19] This historical rupture, occurring in the 16th century under figures like King Gustav Vasa, left the order's contemplative-active charism dormant in its homeland, motivating Hesselblad's vision of restoration as a counter to ongoing Protestant dominance and secular drifts.[20] [18] In 1903, during a visit to St. Bridget's church and house in Rome, Hesselblad experienced a personal locution interpreted as a divine directive: "It is in this place that I want you to serve me," which she discerned through prolonged prayer as an urging to revive Bridget's work for Scandinavian reconciliation with the Catholic Church.[1] This insight, verified via ecclesiastical counsel and her deepening Eucharistic devotion, aligned with Bridget's ecumenical aspirations, prompting Hesselblad to foster explicit veneration of the saint while adapting her rule to contemporary apostolic needs without diluting its monastic essence.[1] [21]

Founding Efforts and Establishment

Following encouragement from Pope Pius X, who in 1906 granted her permission to assume the Bridgettine habit and profess vows as an oblate, Hesselblad sought to revive the dormant women's branch of the Order of the Most Holy Savior of Saint Bridget, adapting its contemplative roots to active apostolic works for Church unity, particularly among Scandinavians.[1] Her efforts faced significant obstacles, including fruitless visits to existing European Bridgettine monasteries that yielded no practical support or recruits, compounded by her own health struggles, yet she persevered through prayer and determination to establish a new branch faithful to Saint Bridget's original charism.[1][19] In 1911, Hesselblad acquired and restored the historic house of Saint Bridget at Piazza Farnese in Rome—previously occupied by Carmelites—as the order's first convent and motherhouse, marking the initial concrete step in reestablishment on September 8.[19] This location, tied to Saint Bridget's 19-year residence, symbolized continuity with the order's medieval origins while enabling a Roman base for outreach.[1] On November 9, 1911, Hesselblad formally refounded the order by welcoming three young English postulants into the community at the Casa di Santa Brigida, initiating communal life focused on eucharistic adoration, prayer for Christian unity, and charitable apostolates.[1] This small group formed the nucleus, overcoming initial isolation and resource scarcity through shared vocation and fidelity to the founder’s vision.[19] Hesselblad personally drafted the order's constitutions, blending Saint Bridget's rigorous asceticism and devotion to the Passion with provisions for active ministry in education, nursing, and ecumenical dialogue, tailored to 20th-century needs while awaiting Holy See approval—initially provisional in 1911 and definitive on December 2, 1940.[18][19] This framework emphasized perseverance in trials, reflecting her own experience of surmounting institutional indifference and personal frailty to resurrect the order.[1]

Life in Rome and Charitable Activities

Convent Life and Daily Ministry

Upon establishing the Bridgettine convent in Rome on 8 September 1911 at the historic house of Saint Bridget, Maria Elisabeth Hesselblad instituted a routine that harmonized contemplation with apostolic service. The sisters dedicated significant portions of each day to Eucharistic adoration, emphasizing prolonged exposure to the Blessed Sacrament as a core element of their spiritual life, alongside the communal recitation of the Divine Office to fulfill the order's liturgical obligations.[22][23] This structure drew directly from the Bridgettine tradition, prioritizing prayer in remembrance of Christ's Passion while fostering an environment for intercessory supplication on behalf of the Church and Christian unity.[24] Hospitality formed a vital aspect of daily ministry, with the convent serving as a refuge for pilgrims, retreatants, and those in need of spiritual guidance, mirroring Saint Bridget's own practices of welcoming guests and offering counsel. The sisters engaged in practical acts of charity, such as providing meals, lodging, and opportunities for private prayer, which extended the contemplative focus into active outreach without compromising enclosure.[25][1] From this Roman base, Hesselblad oversaw the order's expansion into additional European foundations, including establishments in Sweden, Poland, England, and Switzerland by the mid-20th century, where convents often incorporated retreat facilities and charitable initiatives to support local communities.[25] Despite her persistent health frailties stemming from earlier tuberculosis, she personally directed this growth through correspondence, visitations, and formation of new members, embodying ascetic discipline in her unremitting commitment to the order's mission.[22][1]

Heroic Actions During World War II

During the German occupation of Rome from September 1943 to June 1944, Mother Maria Elisabeth Hesselblad, as superior of the Bridgettine convent at Piazza Farnese, concealed Jewish families within the premises to evade Nazi roundups and deportations to concentration camps.[4] Her convent served as a sanctuary, housing persecuted individuals for approximately six months amid heightened risks from SS searches and informants.[26] Hesselblad's initiatives reportedly protected dozens of Jews, with accounts varying from at least 12 individuals, including children like Piero Piperno, to over 60 in total across multiple families.[27] [28] [4] These efforts involved direct oversight of hiding arrangements, reflecting calculated defiance against totalitarian enforcement of racial policies. In recognition of her role in preserving Jewish lives during the Holocaust, Yad Vashem awarded her the title Righteous Among the Nations in 2004.[26][4]

Spiritual Practices and Writings

Personal Devotion and Eucharistic Focus

Hesselblad's spiritual life centered on the Eucharist as the foundational source of grace and vitality for her religious community, emphasizing daily participation in Mass and prolonged periods of adoration before the Blessed Sacrament. She instituted adoration as one of the three primary goals for her Bridgettine sisters—alongside contemplation and reparation—viewing it as essential for sustaining the order's mission amid challenges.[9] This practice, modeled after the contemplative life of Nazareth, involved silent prayer and exposition of the Eucharist, which she regarded as nourishing the soul's union with Christ and countering spiritual fragmentation in modern society.[1] Her devotion extended to acts of reparation and strict obedience, which she cultivated as antidotes to individualism by submitting personal will to divine authority and ecclesial discipline. Hesselblad exemplified obedience through her religious profession on 25 March 1906 and ongoing adherence to the Rule of St. Bridget, integrating reparative prayer to atone for offenses against God and the Church.[1] These practices reinforced communal discipline, prioritizing sacrificial fidelity over self-assertion.[9] From the 1920s onward, Hesselblad united her chronic health sufferings—stemming from lifelong fragility exacerbated by earlier illnesses like diphtheria, scarlet fever, and stomach ailments—to Christ's Passion, offering them during Mass as reparative acts for the Church's renewal.[9] [7] Despite physical tolls from her demanding apostolate, she embraced these trials with fortitude, seeing them as channels of grace that fortified her obedience and deepened Eucharistic reparation.[1]

Visions, Prayers, and Ecumenical Aspirations

Hesselblad reported interior locutions that shaped her commitment to Church unity. While visiting the church of St. Bridget in Rome, she heard a divine call: "It is in this place that I want you to serve me," which she interpreted as a directive to revive the Bridgettine charism there.[1] Earlier, during a eucharistic procession involving a monstrance carried by a bishop, she experienced the presence and voice of God affirming: "I am he whom you seek," marking a pivotal moment in her conversion and discernment.[29] These reported mystical communications, verified through her cause for beatification, emphasized service through prayer for ecclesiastical restoration rather than personal acclaim.[1] Her prayers centered on achieving full communion by urging the return of Protestant communities, particularly in Scandinavia, to the Catholic Church, avoiding dilutions of doctrine in favor of orthodox reintegration.[30] Upon her conditional baptism on 15 August 1902, she described an infusion of divine love that intensified this focus, leading her to found the revived Order of the Most Holy Saviour of St. Bridget on 9 November 1911 explicitly to intercede for Scandinavian Christians' unity with Rome, stating it as "the prime goal of our vocation."[1] This aspiration drew from Scandinavia's historical Catholic foundations, which predominated until the 16th-century Reformation imposed Lutheranism under state authority, prompting her to view reconversion as a restoration of pre-schism fidelity supported by contemplative reparation.[30] In adapting St. Bridget's revelations—centered on Christ's Passion, eucharistic devotion, and calls for reform—Hesselblad applied them to 20th-century renewal, promoting a balanced life of "prayer, work, sacrifice" to foster genuine unity without syncretism.[1] She instructed her sisters to prioritize ardent love for God and neighbor, writing in 1904: "Dear Lord, I do not ask to see the path. In darkness, in anguish and in fear, I will hang on tightly to your hand," and later: "The Lord has called us from different nations, but we must be united with one heart and one soul."[1] These emphases, rooted in Bridget's prophetic visions rather than modern ecumenical dilutions, underscored causal links between personal sanctity, communal prayer, and broader ecclesial healing, as affirmed in her canonization process.[5]

Final Years and Death

Health Decline and Last Works

In the years following World War II, Hesselblad experienced a progressive decline in health, marked by chronic cardiac weakness that intensified in her final months.[9] Despite these afflictions, she persisted in her role as superior of the Bridgettine Order, providing maternal spiritual direction to her sisters through emphases on Eucharistic devotion and Marian contemplation, which she viewed as essential for their formation amid post-war challenges.[17] Her endurance reflected a faith-rooted conviction that bodily frailty amplified opportunities for redemptive suffering, aligning with her lifelong pattern of uniting personal trials to Christ's Passion.[9] As her condition worsened ahead of a planned canonical visitation of the order in 1957, Hesselblad intensified her prayer life, offering her pains explicitly for the community's stability and the Church's unity.[1] In this period, she composed reflective prayers expressing total trust in divine providence amid suffering, such as invoking Christ's hand in moments of anguish and fear to sustain her soul's spousal bond with him.[1] These final spiritual exhortations and acts of oblation underscored her preparation for eternity, prioritizing intercession for ecumenical reconciliation and the order's perseverance over physical recovery.[9]

Death and Immediate Aftermath

Maria Elisabeth Hesselblad died on 24 April 1957 in the early hours at the House of St. Bridget in Rome, surrounded by her Bridgettine sisters, following a prolonged decline in health marked by physical suffering and spiritual preparation through prayer.[1] Her body was buried at the same convent, where she had spent much of her later life directing the order's activities.[3] [31] In the immediate aftermath, tributes from members of the Bridgettine order highlighted her exemplary life of humility, charity, and faith, with contemporaries noting the profound impact of her leadership and devotion.[1] Her preexisting reputation for holiness, built on decades of convent ministry and ecumenical efforts, intensified rapidly among those who knew her, fostering an atmosphere of reverence within the community shortly after her passing.[1]

Path to Sainthood

Investigation and Beatification

The cause for beatification of Maria Elisabeth Hesselblad was initiated by the Vicariate of Rome shortly after her death on April 24, 1957, reflecting her widespread reputation for sanctity and exemplary Christian life.[1] The diocesan phase involved gathering testimonies and documents attesting to her virtues, subjected to the Congregation for the Causes of Saints' scrutiny to ensure compliance with canonical norms requiring proof of heroic degrees of theological and cardinal virtues. On March 26, 1999, Pope John Paul II promulgated the decree affirming Hesselblad's exercise of heroic virtues, elevating her to the title of Venerable; this step demanded exhaustive review of her writings, actions, and witness accounts by theologians and historians to verify theological orthodoxy, prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance, faith, hope, and charity.[12] The requisite miracle for beatification—an inexplicable healing attributed to her intercession, investigated diocesanally in 1996 and ratified by the Congregation on October 17, 1998—involved rigorous medical and theological examinations to exclude natural explanations or fraud, aligning with Vatican protocols for scientific inexplicability and moral certainty.[32] Hesselblad was solemnly beatified by Pope John Paul II on April 9, 2000, during a ceremony in Saint Peter's Square, where her intercessory role in the verified cure was publicly acknowledged as fulfilling the evidentiary threshold for Blessed status.[1]

Canonization Process and Miracles

The canonization process for Maria Elisabeth Hesselblad advanced following her beatification in 2000, requiring verification of a second miracle attributed to her intercession. On December 14, 2015, Pope Francis promulgated a decree approving this miracle, which involved the inexplicable healing of Carlos Miguel Valdés Rodríguez, a young boy from Santa Clara, Cuba, diagnosed with a brain tumor. Medical examinations confirmed the tumor's disappearance after prayers invoking Hesselblad's intercession, with no scientific explanation provided by attending physicians.[33][34] This miracle underwent rigorous scrutiny by the Congregation for the Causes of Saints, including theological and medical investigations to establish its supernatural character. The healing occurred post-beatification and was deemed complete, instantaneous, and permanent, fulfilling the Vatican's criteria for canonization. Hesselblad's intercession was specifically invoked by the boy's family during his critical condition following unsuccessful treatments.[35][36] Canonization occurred on June 5, 2016, during a Mass in Saint Peter's Square, where Pope Francis declared Hesselblad a saint alongside Stanisław Papczyński. In his homily, Francis highlighted Hesselblad's life as an exemplary witness to Christ's resurrection, emphasizing her deep union with Jesus' passion through charity and ecumenical efforts. Her path as a convert and restorer of the Bridgettine Order was affirmed as a valid model for modern discipleship, rooted in eucharistic devotion and service to the marginalized.[5][37]

Legacy and Recognition

Enduring Impact on the Church and Order

The branch of the Bridgettine Order revived by Hesselblad, known as the Bridgettines of the Most Holy Savior, has expanded from its re-foundation in Rome on September 8, 1911, to encompass approximately 787 sisters across 19 countries as of 2024, including contemplative monasteries and apostolic communities dedicated to prayer, retreats, and spiritual renewal.[38] [24] This growth, from a single house to dozens worldwide, reflects sustained vocations averaging around 30 annually in earlier decades, enabling the order to maintain its charism of Eucharistic adoration and intercession amid modern secular challenges.[39] Hesselblad's model as a Lutheran convert to Catholicism in 1902 has positioned the order as an exemplar for Protestant-to-Catholic transitions, emphasizing rigorous orthodoxy, perpetual adoration, and service without compromise on doctrine, thereby fostering traditional piety in regions with declining religious observance.[40] The order's apostolate continues through retreat houses, such as those in the United States and India, where sisters provide spiritual guidance and host pilgrims, perpetuating Hesselblad's vision of contemplative life intertwined with apostolic outreach.[41] [42] Her emphasis on prayer for Christian unity, particularly bridging Catholic-Lutheran divides through persistent intercession rather than doctrinal dilution, has influenced broader ecclesiastical priorities on ecumenism grounded in shared devotion to the Eucharist and saints like Bridget of Sweden.[40] This approach aligns with papal calls for unity via holiness and service, as evidenced by the order's global footprint supporting retreats that draw diverse pilgrims, including those seeking reconciliation across confessional lines, without eroding Catholic distinctives.[24]

Posthumous Honors and Distinctions

In 2004, Israel's Yad Vashem Holocaust Remembrance Center posthumously honored Elisabeth Hesselblad as one of the Righteous Among the Nations for her efforts in sheltering approximately 60 Jews in the Bridgettine convent at Piazza Farnese in Rome during the Nazi occupation from October 1943 to June 1944, providing them refuge, forged documents, and even facilitating a makeshift synagogue.[2] [4] [26] Her canonization by Pope Francis on June 5, 2016, established her as Sweden's first canonized saint since Bridget of Sweden in 1391, highlighting her role in reviving the Bridgettine Order and contributing to Catholic renewal in a historically Protestant nation.[4] [6] The Catholic Church observes her liturgical memorial on June 4, the date of her baptism, in recognition of her conversion from Lutheranism and lifelong devotion to Eucharistic prayer and ecumenical unity.[7] [43]
