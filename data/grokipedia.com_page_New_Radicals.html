New Radicals

The New Radicals were an American pop rock band formed in 1997 in Los Angeles, centered on singer-songwriter Gregg Alexander and vocalist Danielle Brisebois, who functioned more as a studio project than a traditional touring ensemble.[1][2] The group released a single studio album, Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too, on October 20, 1998, via MCA Records, which drew from 1970s rock and soul influences and earned gold certification in the United States for 500,000 units sold.[3][4] Their lead single, "You Get What You Give", peaked at number 36 on the Billboard Hot 100, reached number 5 on the UK Singles Chart, and topped charts in Canada and New Zealand, noted for its anthemic chorus and lyrics critiquing corporate greed, health insurance practices, and celebrity culture.[5][6] Despite initial success, the band disbanded in early 1999 after a short tour, as Alexander rejected the demands of sustained performance to prioritize songwriting and production, later contributing to hits like Santana's "The Game of Love", which won a Grammy Award.[1] The New Radicals briefly reunited in 2021 to perform "You Get What You Give" at U.S. President Joe Biden's inauguration, highlighting the song's lasting motivational appeal.[1]

History

Formation and early development (1997)

The New Radicals were formed in Los Angeles in 1997 by Gregg Alexander, a Michigan-born singer-songwriter and multi-instrumentalist who had previously released two commercially unsuccessful solo albums on major labels in 1989 and 1992.[7] Alexander conceived the project as a vehicle for delivering pointed social commentary through pop music, viewing the band as a "Trojan horse" to smuggle revolutionary rhetoric critiquing corporate greed, consumer culture, and youth apathy into mainstream audiences.[8] The group operated as a revolving-door ensemble with no fixed members beyond Alexander, though keyboardist and percussionist Danielle Briseboisâ€”formerly a child actress and session musicianâ€”served as the sole other consistent collaborator.[9] In late 1997, Alexander secured a recording deal with MCA Records specifically for the New Radicals, marking a shift from his solo career amid frustration with industry commercialization and personal disillusionment after years of obscurity.[7] He quickly assembled a loose collective of Los Angeles-based session players to begin developing material, focusing on anthemic, Motown- and 1970s rock-influenced songs that blended upbeat melodies with lyrical disdain for societal complacency.[8] Early sessions emphasized Alexander's songwriting and production, laying the groundwork for the debut album Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too, with initial demos capturing his intent to provoke awareness rather than conform to prevailing alt-pop trends.[7] This period reflected Alexander's strategic pivot: leveraging major-label resources to amplify outsider critiques, unburdened by the rigid structures of traditional band dynamics.[8]

Rise to mainstream success and album release (1998â€“1999)

The New Radicals rose to mainstream prominence in 1998 through their debut single "You Get What You Give," which became an international hit following its release as the lead track from their sole album. The song debuted on the US Billboard Hot 100 on December 5, 1998, eventually peaking at number 36.[10] Internationally, it reached number 1 on the charts in Canada and New Zealand, number 5 in the United Kingdom, and top 5 positions in Ireland and Scotland.[11] [12] This breakthrough single, characterized by its anthemic pop-rock sound and optimistic lyrics, garnered significant radio play and MTV exposure, propelling the band from obscurity to widespread recognition.[13] The success of "You Get What You Give" directly facilitated the release of the band's debut and only studio album, Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too, on October 20, 1998, via MCA Records.[14] The album, produced primarily by frontman Gregg Alexander, featured a blend of 1970s-inspired rock and soul influences, with the hit single anchoring its commercial performance. It achieved sales exceeding 1.15 million copies worldwide, including strong performance in Canada where it peaked at number 5 on the albums chart.[15] Despite not entering the US Billboard 200 top 100, the record's momentum from the single's airplay and the band's limited promotional tour in late 1998 and early 1999 solidified their brief stint in the mainstream pop landscape.[16] In 1999, the band issued a second single, "Someday We'll Know," which received moderate airplay but failed to replicate the chart success of its predecessor, peaking outside the top 40 in most markets.[9] This period marked the height of the New Radicals' visibility, with performances on major television programs and festival appearances, though internal motivations soon led to their dissolution later that year.[1]

Disbandment and motivations (1999)

On July 12, 1999, New Radicals leader Gregg Alexander issued a press release announcing the band's immediate disbandment, stating that the group "will no longer be a recording, promoting, or performing entity" after having achieved its creative objectives with the debut album Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too.[17][18] The decision came amid rising success from the lead single "You Get What You Give," which peaked at number 36 on the Billboard Hot 100 in early 1999, but just before the planned release of the follow-up single "Someday We'll Know."[1] Alexander, who conceived the band as a vehicle for his songwriting vision rather than a permanent ensemble, cited exhaustion from the promotional and touring obligations inherent to pop stardom as the core impetus.[19] In a 2018 interview, he described the prospect of extended touring as "terrifying," emphasizing his preference for studio-based composition over the "merchandise/food chain" of live performances and media appearances.[19] He further articulated a deliberate rejection of prolonged fame, noting, "I had no interest in 50 years of being in a band and doing the same thing over and over," and clarifying that he "didnâ€™t want to be famous... I didnâ€™t want to be a celebrity."[19] This aversion extended to disillusionment with industry structures, including the influence of corporate entities on artistic output, which Alexander found incompatible with his goals after turning 30.[6] The press release itself framed the experience as "intense and wonderful" yet complete, with Alexander declaring, "I accomplished all of my goals with this record, and Iâ€™m ready to move on and make the next step in my career."[1] Consequently, the project dissolved without further commitments, allowing Alexander to pivot toward behind-the-scenes production and songwriting for other artists.[19]

Post-disbandment careers of principal members

Following the New Radicals' disbandment in 1999, Gregg Alexander transitioned to a career focused on songwriting and production, eschewing the spotlight of performing. He co-wrote "The Game of Love" for Santana featuring Michelle Branch, which reached number one on the Billboard Adult Top 40 chart in 2002.[20] Alexander also penned "Life Is a Rollercoaster" for Ronan Keating, peaking at number two on the UK Singles Chart in 2000, and contributed to tracks for artists including Texas and Michelle Branch.[20] His production work earned a Grammy Award, and he later composed songs for the 2013 film Begin Again, including the Oscar-nominated "Lost Stars."[21] [22] Danielle Brisebois, Alexander's primary collaborator in the band, pursued songwriting and released her solo album Portable Life in 2000, produced by Alexander, though it received limited commercial release.[9] She co-wrote "Lost Stars" with Alexander for Begin Again, earning an Academy Award nomination for Best Original Song in 2015.[23] Brisebois continued contributing to Alexander's projects and maintained a lower-profile career in music composition thereafter.[23] Other touring members, such as drummer Gary Ashworth, did not achieve notable public visibility in subsequent music endeavors, with available records indicating no major releases or credits post-1999.

Reunions and political engagements (2021â€“2024)

On January 20, 2021, the New Radicals reunited after 22 years of inactivity to perform "You Get What You Give" during the virtual "Parade Across America," a televised event celebrating Joe Biden's inauguration as president.[24][25] Frontman Gregg Alexander announced the one-off reunion on January 17, stating the performance fulfilled a pre-election pledge to play the song if Biden won, citing the track's adoption as a Biden family anthem.[26][11] The live rendition featured original members Alexander, Gary Yang, and Danielle Brisebois, streamed without an in-person audience due to pandemic restrictions.[24] No further live performances or full-band activities occurred until August 2024, when the group released its first new recordings in 25 years: covers of "Lost Stars" (from the 2014 film Begin Again, written by Alexander) and "Murder on the Dancefloor" (originally by Sophie Ellis-Bextor, co-written by Alexander).[27][28] These tracks, produced by Alexander, were issued to "rally the cause of democracy and encourage all artists to get out the vote" ahead of the Democratic National Convention, framing the releases as opposition to perceived threats to democratic norms.[29] Accompanying the music was an open letter from Alexander addressed to Doug Emhoff, husband of then-presidential candidate Kamala Harris, which critiqued "toxic politics," border policy influences ("BPD 'biz con abuse bros'"), COVID isolation, and mental health crises while urging civic engagement.[28][27] The letter referenced the band's earlier Biden-era performance and positioned the output as a call for artists to counter authoritarianism, without specifying partisan endorsements beyond the democratic support context.[29]

Musical style, influences, and lyrical content

Genre classification and sonic elements

The New Radicals' music is classified as pop/rock, with core styles encompassing alternative pop/rock, adult alternative pop/rock, and alternative/indie rock.[30] This positioning reflects the band's fusion of accessible pop structures with rock instrumentation and a late-1990s alternative edge, evident in their sole album Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too (released October 20, 1998), which blends melodic hooks and rhythmic drive akin to contemporaries in the power pop and alternative scenes.[14] Sonically, the band's output features prominent electric guitars delivering crunchy riffs and chord progressions, layered with keyboards and synthesizers for buoyant, melodic accents, as handled by co-founder Danielle Brisebois.[31] Drums provide a vibrant, propulsive backbone with big snare reverbs and steady beats, while Gregg Alexander's lead vocalsâ€”processed with quarter-note delaysâ€”add a soaring, echoed quality that amplifies anthemic choruses.[32] Production emphasizes dynamic builds, from intros mimicking ascending effects (such as the airplane-like synth swell in "You Get What You Give") to expansive mixes that prioritize energy and optimism without heavy distortion.[33] This combination yields a polished, radio-friendly sound marked by upbeat tempos around 100-120 BPM, harmonious backing vocals, and a focus on verse-chorus dynamics that evoke resilience and immediacy, distinguishing the New Radicals from grittier alternative acts of the era.[34]

Key influences and production approach

The New Radicals' music was predominantly influenced by 1970s rock and soul, blending these with elements of 1960s classic rock to create an alternative pop-rock sound characterized by energetic piano riffs, soaring choruses, and socially conscious lyrics.[35][36] Gregg Alexander, the band's primary songwriter, cited early Motown records such as "Jimmy Mack" by Martha and the Vandellas, as well as '70s soul acts like Little Anthony & the Imperials, alongside '80s pop, as formative inspirations shaping the album's melodic hooks and rhythmic drive.[37] Specific tracks reflected narrower nods, including Todd Rundgren's production style in the anthemic "You Get What You Give" and Prince's funk-infused chord progressions and piano arrangements in songs like "I Hope I Never" and "Someday We'll Know."[38][39] Later cuts, such as "Flowers," incorporated mid-1990s alternative rock influences akin to the Smashing Pumpkins' introspective "1979."[40] The production of their sole album, Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too (released October 20, 1998), centered on Alexander's hands-on role as producer, writer, and multi-instrumentalist, emphasizing live-band energy over polished studio effects to evoke the raw vitality of '70s influences.[41][42] He collaborated with co-producer and backing vocalist Danielle Brisebois on select tracks, while enlisting session musicians like drummer Josh Freese and guitarist Rusty Anderson for organic instrumentation, including guitars, synthesizers, and layered harmonies recorded at studios in Los Angeles.[41][43] This approach prioritized concise song structuresâ€”most tracks under four minutesâ€”and avoided excessive overdubs, allowing Alexander's raspy vocals and piano to dominate, as in the hit single's buildup from sparse verses to explosive refrains, fostering a sense of immediacy that contrasted with contemporaneous electronica-heavy pop production.[44]

Lyrical themes and social critiques

The lyrics of New Radicals, primarily penned by Gregg Alexander, recurrently explore themes of empowerment amid societal disillusionment, urging listeners to reject passive conformity and embrace authentic aspiration. In the band's signature track "You Get What You Give" (1998), Alexander posits a karmic reciprocityâ€”"You get what you give"â€”as a counter to existential inertia, framing personal agency as a bulwark against uncontrollable external forces like economic instability or cultural vapidity.[45] Alexander described the song as a call to "fly high and be completely off your head" in defiance of such chaos, emphasizing resilience over resignation.[45] Social critiques permeate the work, targeting media-driven superficiality and institutional failures. The album Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too (1998) indicts pervasive cultural indoctrination, with its title evoking how excessive television consumptionâ€”averaging five hours daily in the late 1990sâ€”fosters unrealistic standards of beauty, materialism, and distraction from substantive concerns.[18] Alexander explicitly linked this "brainwashing" to societal prioritization of pop culture ephemera over pressing realities, as seen in "You Get What You Give"'s dismissal of celebrity icons (e.g., references to Beck, Hanson, Marilyn Manson, and Courtney Love) as emblematic of hollow fame.[46] These barbs critique the entertainment industry's role in diverting attention from systemic inequities, including health insurance denials and regulatory overreach by bodies like the FDA and FBI, woven into the song's bridge as urgent rallying cries.[33] Broader album tracks extend this scrutiny to corruption, greed, and eroding social safety nets, juxtaposing personal vices like drug use and fleeting romance against institutional bankruptcy, such as the inadequacies of the American welfare system.[18] Songs like "Someday We'll Know" reflect wistful inquiry into human folly, while "I Hope I Didn't Just Give Away the Ending" laments lost innocence amid commodified relationships, underscoring a causal link between individual complacency and collective decay.[47] Alexander's aversion to industry commodification informed these narratives, leading him to disband the group post-album to evade the very mechanisms he lambasted, prioritizing artistic integrity over commercial perpetuation.[1] This stance aligns with first-principles skepticism of elite-driven narratives, though Alexander attributed no overt political ideology, focusing instead on universal anti-conformism.[19]

Band members and personnel

The New Radicals were founded by Gregg Alexander as the primary creative force, handling lead vocals, guitar, songwriting, and production, alongside Danielle Brisebois on keyboards, percussion, and backing vocals as the consistent core duo.[9][2] The project utilized a revolving lineup of session musicians for recordings, including guitarist Rusty Anderson and bassist John Pierce on select tracks.[48] For live performances supporting their 1998 album Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too, the band expanded to include touring members Brad Fernquist on guitar, Jim McGorman on keyboards, Stuart Johnson on drums, and Sasha Krivtsov on bass.[9][2] This lineup performed during their brief promotional tour in late 1998 and early 1999 before disbanding.[9]

Discography

Studio albums

The New Radicals released one studio album, Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too, on October 20, 1998, via MCA Records.[44] Primarily written and produced by band leader Gregg Alexander, the record features 12 original tracks blending pop rock elements with retro influences from 1970s soul and rock.[49] Key singles from the album include "You Get What You Give," which propelled initial commercial attention, and "Someday We'll Know."[44] No additional studio albums followed, as the band disbanded shortly after its release.[30]

Singles and promotional releases

The New Radicals' primary singles were drawn from their sole studio album, Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too (1998). The lead single, "You Get What You Give", was released on November 10, 1998, in various formats including CD and cassette, featuring remixes and B-sides such as "You Get What You Give (Beck Acid Wash Remix)".[50][51] This track served as the band's breakthrough, emphasizing upbeat pop-rock with social commentary. The second single, "Someday We'll Know", followed on May 3, 1999, amid the band's impending disbandment, which limited promotional efforts; it appeared on CD with additional mixes like the "Alpine 7" version and non-album tracks.[52][53] Formats included maxi-singles distributed primarily in Europe and the US. "Mother We Just Can't Get Enough" was issued as a single in 1999, available in both promotional one-track formats and four-track commercial CDs containing remixes (e.g., "Sunday Night at the Cafe Remix") and live elements; its release aligned with the album's Japanese edition but saw minimal international push due to the group's dissolution.[54][55] No further official singles were released during the band's active period, reflecting Gregg Alexander's aversion to extensive touring and promotion.[2]

Recent cover versions and one-off recordings

In August 2024, the New Radicals released two recordings after a 25-year hiatus from new material: a version of "Murder on the Dancefloor," originally co-written by frontman Gregg Alexander for Sophie Ellis-Bextor's 2001 album Read My Lips and intended for the band's scrapped second album, and a rendition of "Lost Stars," the Oscar-nominated track from the 2013 film Begin Again also co-penned by Alexander.[56][27] These singles were issued via Flatiron Recordings in support of pro-democracy initiatives, coinciding with Doug Emhoff's selection of the band's 1998 hit "You Get What You Give" as his campaign walk-on song during the U.S. presidential election cycle.[57][27] The releases marked a rare activation of the band's dormant catalog, with "Murder on the Dancefloor" gaining renewed attention due to its viral resurgence via TikTok and the 2023 film Saltburn, though the New Radicals' take predated Ellis-Bextor's hit single.[56] Proceeds from streams and sales were directed toward nonprofit organizations promoting democratic participation, reflecting Alexander's longstanding pattern of tying music to activist causes without full band reformation.[27] Notable recent covers of New Radicals' material by other artists include the Hindley Street Country Club's live rendition of "You Get What You Give" recorded in January 2025, Keith Urban's acoustic performance of the same track during a June 2025 concert at PNC Bank Arts Center, and Better Joy's indie-pop version released in June 2025.[58][59][60] These interpretations highlight the song's enduring appeal in live and alternative genres, often emphasizing its anthemic chorus amid broader nostalgia for late-1990s pop-rock.

Reception, legacy, and cultural impact

Commercial performance and chart achievements

The album Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too, released on October 20, 1998, debuted and peaked at number 41 on the US Billboard 200 chart in early 1999.[61] It received RIAA certification for gold status (500,000 units shipped) on February 8, 1999, followed by platinum certification (1,000,000 units) on October 14, 1999.[62] In the United Kingdom, the album reached number 10 on the UK Albums Chart.[1] The lead single "You Get What You Give", issued on November 3, 1998, drove much of the album's commercial momentum despite not being released as a commercial single in the US, where it peaked at number 36 on the Billboard Hot 100 and number 8 on the Modern Rock Tracks chart.[5] It achieved number-one positions in Canada and New Zealand, while reaching number 5 on the UK Singles Chart and top-five status in Ireland and Scotland.[6] The track's airplay success, including a number 30 peak on Billboard Hot 100 Airplay, contributed significantly to the album's sales trajectory.[33] Subsequent singles like "Someday We'll Know" generated additional radio play and charted modestly in markets including the UK, but failed to replicate the lead single's peaks or sustain broader commercial impact.[63] Overall, the New Radicals' chart achievements were concentrated in late 1998 and 1999, with the platinum-selling album reflecting sustained interest fueled by the flagship single amid the band's abrupt dissolution.[62]

Critical assessments and retrospective views

The debut album Maybe You've Been Brainwashed Too (1998) garnered generally favorable reviews upon release, with critics highlighting its infectious pop-rock energy, melodic hooks, and Gregg Alexander's impassioned songwriting. Aggregated critic scores placed it at 69 out of 100 based on nine professional reviews, reflecting appreciation for tracks blending optimism with cynicism, though some noted the album's occasionally overblown choruses as a stylistic choice that prioritized impact over subtlety.[49] Retrospective analyses have elevated the album's standing, portraying it as an underrated gem that anticipated trends like yacht rock revival and indie rock's eclectic influences, while distinguishing itself from late-1990s alt-rock conformity. The lead single "You Get What You Give" is frequently cited as enduringly vital, blending anti-consumerist critiqueâ€”such as name-drops of celebrities like Beck, Hanson, and Marilyn Mansonâ€”with uplifting resolve, and aging better than many contemporaries through its proto-social media-style urgency on issues like apathy and spiritual void.[64] Alexander's lyrics across the album, addressing financial precarity, environmental concerns, and societal brainwashing, are now viewed as prescient, with the full work demonstrating greater depth beyond its one-hit status, including soulful ballads like "I Don't Wanna Die Anymore" and politically charged tracks akin to those later covered by U2.[16] The band's abrupt disbandment in 1999, just months after the album's release, has been retrospectively praised as a principled stand against the music industry's demands for touring and endless promotion, enabling Alexander to thrive as a behind-the-scenes songwriter (e.g., contributing to hits for Madonna and an Oscar-nominated track in Begin Again). This choice is often framed not as a failure but as intentional integrity, preserving the project's anti-corporate ethos and allowing the album to stand as a complete artistic statement rather than diluted follow-ups.[64] A brief 2020 reunion performance for COVID-19 relief efforts underscored the lingering goodwill toward the group's legacy, reinforcing views of it as a fleeting but potent cultural artifact.[16]

Usage in media, sports, and popular culture

"You Get What You Give" featured in the end credits of the 2006 film Click, directed by Frank Coraci and starring Adam Sandler as a workaholic who discovers a universal remote that controls time.[65] The track also appeared in the soundtrack of the 2012 drama The Perks of Being a Wallflower, adapted from Stephen Chbosky's novel and emphasizing themes of youth and resilience that align with the song's motivational lyrics.[65] Another New Radicals song, "Mother, We Just Can't Get Enough," was included in the 2002 romantic drama A Walk to Remember, directed by Adam Shankman and based on Nicholas Sparks' novel, where it underscored scenes of emotional connection and aspiration.[66] The band's music has appeared in various television programs, including episodes of Dawson's Creek during its late 1990s run, contributing to the show's portrayal of teen angst and optimism, and in the 2022 Hulu series Pam & Tommy, which dramatized the 1990s sex tape scandal involving Pamela Anderson and Tommy Lee.[67] "You Get What You Give" has been licensed for commercials and promotional content, such as a PBS bumper promoting educational programming in 2022, and a re-recorded version featuring New Zealand singer Benee aired as part of MTV's campaign for the 2025 Video Music Awards on September 3, 2025, updating the original with contemporary production while retaining its core anthem structure.[68][5] In sports media, "You Get What You Give" has been performed or featured in broadcasts like Friday Night Football, a program covering American high school and college football games since 1983, often evoking themes of perseverance suitable for athletic contexts.[66] The song's uplifting tempo has led to its informal use as a motivational track in sports settings, though specific event integrations remain sporadic. Beyond media, the New Radicals' sole major hit has permeated popular culture through high-profile reunions; the band, dormant since 1999, reconvened on January 20, 2021, to perform "You Get What You Give" live at the "Celebrating America" television special marking Joe Biden's presidential inauguration, marking their first public appearance in 22 years and highlighting the track's enduring appeal as a symbol of hope amid national transition.[69] This performance, viewed by millions, reinforced the song's status as a cultural touchstone for resilience, occasionally referenced in discussions of 1990s nostalgia and one-hit wonders that transcend their era.

Political interpretations, associations, and debates

The lyrics of "You Get What You Give," the New Radicals' signature 1998 single, critique corporate greed, regulatory overreach, and celebrity culture, with specific references to "health insurance rip off lying FDA," "big bankers buying," and "fashion shoots with Beck and Hanson, Courtney Love and Marilyn Manson."[70][45] Frontman Gregg Alexander described these as "one of the more political lyrics in a long, long" time, framing the song as an experiment in blending pop accessibility with social commentary against systemic exploitation.[71] The track's chorusâ€”"You get what you give"â€”promotes personal agency and reciprocity as antidotes to institutional failures, resonating with anti-establishment sentiments that emphasize individual resilience over collective dependence.[45] Alexander's political associations have aligned predominantly with Democratic figures and causes. The song became a personal "theme song" for Joe Biden, who cited it in his memoir Promise Me, Dad (2017) for its motivational impact during his son's illness.[6] In November 2020, Alexander licensed it for a Biden campaign get-out-the-vote advertisement produced by Coda Media.[72] The band reunited briefly on January 20, 2021, to perform at Biden's presidential inauguration, marking their first live appearance in over two decades.[6] Further ties include admiration from the Obama family and support for the 2024 Democratic National Convention, where the group released newly recorded versions of "Lost Stars" and "Murder on the Dancefloor" on August 23, 2024, explicitly to bolster democratic institutions amid perceived threats.[73][27] Alexander penned an open letter to Doug Emhoff, husband of Kamala Harris, on August 22, 2024, reiterating refusals to alter lyrics for pharmaceutical interests and endorsing progressive electoral efforts.[74] Debates surrounding these associations highlight tensions between the song's rebellious tone and its adoption by establishment Democrats. Critics noted irony in performing lyrics decrying "fakes" and vowing to "kick your ass in" at a high-profile political ceremony, especially with name-drops of figures like Marilyn Manson, whose inclusion drew scrutiny for clashing with the event's decorum.[70] Some observers argued the track's anti-corporate edge, including jabs at the FDA and bankers, aligns more with populist critiques than partisan loyalty, potentially diluting its original intent when repurposed for campaigns.[1] Alexander addressed such concerns in a 1998 interview, advocating strategic navigation of societal structures for meaningful change rather than outright rejection, though he has consistently rejected commercial alterations to the lyrics.[8][74] No major conservative endorsements or reinterpretations have emerged, with the song's evolving Democratic linkage contrasting its broadly libertarian-leaning critiques of power.[1]
