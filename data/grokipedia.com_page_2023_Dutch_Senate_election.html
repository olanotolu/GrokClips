2023 Dutch Senate election

The 2023 Dutch Senate election was an indirect election held on 30 May 2023 to select the 75 members of the Senate, the upper house of the States General, for a four-year term beginning 13 June 2023.[1][2] The members were elected by 572 delegates from the twelve provincial councils—elected three months earlier on 15 March 2023—along with representatives from electoral colleges for the Caribbean Netherlands, using a system of weighted votes proportional to each council's size.[1][3] The election produced a fragmented outcome, with the newly formed Farmer–Citizen Movement (BBB) achieving a breakthrough by winning 16 seats and becoming the largest party, followed by the People's Party for Freedom and Democracy (VVD) with 10 seats, Labour Party (PvdA) and GroenLinks each with 7 seats, and Christian Democratic Appeal (CDA) with 6 seats.[1] The results reflected the parallel provincial elections, where BBB's strong performance among rural voters stemmed from opposition to the Rutte IV cabinet's nitrogen reduction plans, which aimed to cut agricultural emissions to comply with EU directives but were criticized for relying on models that projected disproportionate farm closures without adequate economic compensation or alternatives.[4][1] The incumbent coalition of VVD, Democrats 66 (D66), CDA, and Christian Union (CU) lost its outright majority, dropping to 24 seats from 32 previously, which forced reliance on opposition support—such as from PvdA/GroenLinks—for passing legislation and highlighted deepening rural-urban divides over environmental policy enforcement.[4] Other shifts included heavy losses for Forum for Democracy (down to 2 seats) and gains for JA21 (3 seats) and Volt (2 seats), underscoring volatility in the indirect electoral process tied to provincial sentiments.[1][4]

Background

Governing coalition instability

The Rutte IV cabinet, comprising the liberal-conservative VVD, progressive-liberal D66, Christian-democratic CDA, and orthodox-Christian CU, was installed on January 10, 2022, following the longest post-World War II formation period of 299 days, which highlighted deep ideological divisions among the partners and foreshadowed governance challenges.[5] The coalition's diverse composition—spanning market-oriented pragmatism to environmental progressivism—exacerbated tensions over policy implementation, particularly in reconciling economic growth with stringent EU-derived environmental mandates.[6] A primary source of instability emerged from the nitrogen emissions crisis, stemming from a 2019 court ruling mandating a 50% reduction in agricultural nitrogen deposits by 2030 to protect Natura 2000 sites. The cabinet's proposed measures, including livestock reductions and voluntary farm buyouts near protected areas, provoked widespread farmers' protests beginning in 2021 and intensifying through 2022, with blockades of distribution centers, highways, and ministerial residences.[7] Internal disagreements within the coalition hampered progress: D66 advocated aggressive cuts aligned with green priorities, while CU and CDA, representing rural constituencies, resisted measures perceived as overly punitive to farmers, leading to delays in formulating a coherent agricultural transition plan.[8] These fractures culminated in the resignation of Agriculture Minister Henk Staghouwer (CU) on September 5, 2022, the first departure from Rutte IV, after he failed to secure cabinet consensus on nitrogen reduction targets and admitted delays in delivering a viable future strategy for the sector, deeming himself "the wrong man for the job."[9] The vacancy underscored the coalition's inability to bridge urban-rural divides and environmental-economic trade-offs, further eroding public confidence amid ongoing protests that disrupted logistics and heightened political polarization.[6] Polling in late 2022 reflected declining support for coalition parties, with the nitrogen impasse fueling the rise of the agrarian BoerBurgerBeweging (BBB), positioning the March 2023 provincial elections—which determine Senate composition—as a de facto referendum on the government's handling of the crisis.[10]

Key policy failures and public discontent

The Dutch government's nitrogen reduction policies, initiated to comply with European Union environmental directives following a 2019 court ruling, mandated significant cuts in agricultural emissions, primarily ammonia from livestock farming, which accounted for approximately 46% of national nitrogen oxide deposits. These measures proposed closing or scaling back up to 30% of farms by 2030, prompting widespread farmer protests from 2019 onward, including tractor blockades and demonstrations that escalated into violence in 2022. Farmers argued that prior voluntary reductions had already lowered emissions substantially, yet the policies disproportionately burdened rural communities without adequate alternatives, fueling perceptions of governmental overreach and economic sabotage.[11][12][13] A chronic housing shortage exacerbated public frustration, with over 390,000 units needed by 2025 amid stagnant construction rates averaging below 70,000 annually since 2013, despite population growth driven by net migration exceeding 100,000 yearly. Critics attributed this failure to regulatory hurdles, zoning restrictions, and prioritization of sustainability mandates over supply expansion, leaving young families and low-income households facing rents 20-30% above inflation-adjusted norms. Immigration inflows, particularly asylum seekers numbering around 50,000 in 2022, intensified competition for scarce affordable housing, amplifying resentment toward policies perceived as lax on border controls and integration.[14][15] Rising living costs, including energy prices spiked by the 2022 Ukraine crisis and persistent inflation hovering at 10% in early 2023, compounded discontent with the long-serving Rutte IV coalition, in power since 2017 and criticized for incrementalism over structural reforms. Surveys indicated broad dissatisfaction, with over 60% of respondents viewing the government as ineffective on core issues like agriculture and migration, eroding trust after repeated scandals such as the childcare benefits affair. This culminated in the March 15, 2023, provincial elections, where the agrarian BoerBurgerBeweging (BBB) secured 17 seats—surpassing established parties—and shifted the Senate balance, stripping the coalition of its majority for the first time since 1918.[16][17][13]

Provincial elections as precursor

The Dutch Senate, consisting of 75 members, is elected indirectly by the members of the twelve provincial councils, who serve as electors in a proportional representation system based on the combined seats across all provinces. Provincial council elections occur every four years and directly determine the composition of this electoral college, with Senate elections required to be held within three months of the provincial vote to ensure timely renewal of the upper house.[3] This mechanism links regional representation to national legislative oversight, allowing provincial outcomes to influence the Senate's balance of power.[18] Provincial council elections took place nationwide on 15 March 2023, coinciding with water board and select island council votes. Voter turnout was approximately 62.7%, reflecting moderate engagement amid national debates over agricultural policy and environmental regulations. The elections saw the entry of the Farmer–Citizen Movement (BBB), founded in 2019 to challenge government nitrogen reduction targets that threatened farm closures and rural livelihoods; BBB campaigned on decentralizing environmental decisions and prioritizing food security.[19][20] BBB achieved breakthrough success, winning seats in all twelve provinces and becoming the largest party in the effective electoral college for Senate selection, with strong rural support driving gains at the expense of established parties. This outcome eroded the majority held by the governing coalition (VVD, D66, CDA, and ChristenUnie), which had controlled 35 Senate seats prior to 2023; post-election projections indicated a drop to around 29 seats, stripping the coalition of its upper house dominance and complicating legislative passage. The shift highlighted causal tensions between centralized EU-mandated emission cuts and provincial economic realities, foreshadowing broader political instability that contributed to the government's eventual resignation in July 2023.[21][22][23]

Electoral system

Electors and their selection

The electors for the Dutch Senate, known as the Eerste Kamer, consist of the members of the twelve provincial assemblies (Provinciale Staten) and the members of four electoral colleges representing the special municipalities of the Caribbean Netherlands and Dutch citizens residing abroad. These electors cast votes to select the 75 senators using party lists, with each elector's vote weighted according to the population of their respective province or electoral college to ensure proportionality relative to the Dutch population.[24][3] The members of the provincial assemblies are selected through direct elections held every four years among eligible voters in each province, employing proportional representation via the largest remainder method with the Hare quota. The 2023 provincial elections occurred on 15 March 2023, resulting in 572 assembly members across the twelve provinces who served as electors for the subsequent Senate election on 30 May 2023.[25][3] The four electoral colleges provide representation for areas without provincial assemblies. Three colleges—for Bonaire, Sint Eustatius, and Saba—are elected directly by residents of these special municipalities on the same date as the provincial elections, yielding a total of 19 members. Introduced by constitutional amendment effective for the 2023 cycle, the fourth college for non-residents comprises 25 members elected via postal voting by Dutch nationals living abroad who registered in advance, marking the first inclusion of expatriate influence in Senate composition.[26][27][28]

Voting procedure and proportionality

The 75 members of the Senate are elected indirectly through a system of electors consisting of all members of the twelve provincial councils, totaling 570 individuals, supplemented by members of a special electoral college representing Dutch nationals residing abroad, introduced following a 2022 constitutional amendment.[29][30] These electors, whose composition is determined by the outcomes of the provincial council elections held on 15 March 2023, convene separately in each province and the electoral college to cast votes on a designated date, which for the 2023 election was 30 May 2023.[2] Each elector casts a single vote for one national candidate list submitted by a participating political party, with lists ranked by preference order and no preference voting allowed, ensuring party discipline in seat assignment.[31] Votes are tallied nationally, aggregating all elector ballots into a single constituency.[29] Seat allocation follows the Hare-Niemeyer method of proportional representation, dividing the total valid votes by 75 to establish the electoral quotient, with each party receiving full quotients allocated corresponding seats.[32] Any remaining seats are assigned to parties based on the highest remainders after the initial quotient divisions, prioritizing proportionality over strict majoritarian outcomes.[32] Absent an electoral threshold—unlike the 0.67% effective barrier in House elections—this system permits high fidelity to elector preferences, enabling even minor parties to claim seats if their aggregated votes exceed the quotient for at least one.[31] The nationwide list-based approach, while indirect, mirrors the direct proportional system used for the House of Representatives, minimizing vote-seat distortions and reflecting the aggregated will of provincial representatives as proxies for territorial interests.[32] Empirical outcomes in 2023 demonstrated this proportionality, with seat shares closely tracking elector vote proportions across the spectrum of participating lists.[2]

Participating parties

Major parties and their positions

The Farmer-Citizen Movement (BBB), a party established in 2019 amid protests against agricultural regulations, emerged as a major force by opposing the Dutch government's nitrogen emissions reduction program, which required mandatory farm buyouts and closures to comply with EU environmental directives originating from a 2019 court ruling. BBB advocated alternative strategies emphasizing technological innovation, voluntary participation by farmers, and exemptions for high-performing livestock operations, while criticizing the policy as overly punitive toward rural economies without sufficient evidence of proportional environmental gains. The party also blended agrarian priorities with broader appeals, including lower excise taxes on fuels, restrictions on non-EU immigration to alleviate housing pressures, and support for universal public health insurance, positioning itself as a populist counter to perceived urban-centric governance.[33][34][20] The People's Party for Freedom and Democracy (VVD), the senior partner in the ruling coalition, defended the nitrogen policy as necessary for legal compliance and long-term ecological sustainability, though it emphasized housing development targets of 900,000 new units by 2030 to address shortages exacerbated by population growth and regulatory delays. VVD prioritized economic liberalism, EU integration, and pragmatic environmentalism, arguing that targeted reductions in agricultural emissions—projected to cut nitrogen deposition by 50% by 2030—would enable construction permits stalled by pollution thresholds, but faced voter backlash for perceived inflexibility in rural areas.[33][34] The Party for Freedom (PVV), led by Geert Wilders, focused on curbing immigration, proposing asylum suspensions and deportation expansions to reduce net migration below 50,000 annually, linking high inflows to strained housing markets and welfare costs rather than directly to nitrogen issues. While supportive of farmers' resistance to emission mandates, PVV critiqued the EU's broader green policies as sovereignty-eroding, advocating nitrogen targets met through border controls and urban densification over rural sacrifices.[33] The GroenLinks–PvdA alliance, combining environmentalists and social democrats, endorsed accelerated nitrogen cuts, including livestock reductions and nature restoration investments totaling €25 billion, to fulfill judicial mandates and combat biodiversity loss documented in government reports showing excess deposition at 74% of protected sites. The alliance pushed for progressive taxation to fund transitions, affordable housing via green zoning reforms, and opposition to farm subsidies without emission compliance, prioritizing urban sustainability over agrarian exemptions.[33]

Emerging opposition forces

The Farmer–Citizen Movement (BBB), founded in 2019 amid protests against the Dutch government's nitrogen emissions regulations, emerged as a significant opposition force in the March 15, 2023, provincial elections. The party, emphasizing rural interests, agricultural viability, and resistance to mandatory farm reductions, secured seats in all 12 provincial councils, topping the vote in several including Friesland, Overijssel, and Gelderland.[20] [21] This breakthrough reflected widespread discontent with central government policies perceived as disproportionately burdening farmers to meet EU environmental targets, without adequate compensation or alternatives. BBB's performance deprived the ruling coalition of a Senate majority, positioning the party as a key veto player in upper-house legislation.[33] Other right-wing opposition parties, such as the Party for Freedom (PVV) led by Geert Wilders, also gained provincial seats, increasing from 9 in 2019 to 16 across councils, bolstering anti-immigration and Euroskeptic voices outside the traditional establishment. However, parties like Forum for Democracy (FvD) and JA21 experienced declines or stagnation, underscoring BBB's unique ascent as the primary new contender challenging the status quo on agrarian and regional autonomy issues.[23] These shifts highlighted a fragmentation of opposition, with BBB drawing support from voters disillusioned by the coalition's handling of housing shortages, bureaucracy, and perceived urban bias in policy-making.[35]

Pre-election dynamics

Campaign issues and voter mobilization

The primary campaign issue in the lead-up to the March 15, 2023, provincial elections—which determined the composition of the electors for the subsequent Senate vote on May 30—was the government's nitrogen emissions reduction policy, initiated following 2019 court rulings mandating compliance with EU habitat directives to curb pollution from agriculture, industry, and transport. This policy proposed significant cuts to livestock farming, including up to 30% herd reductions in some areas, prompting widespread farmers' protests since 2019, as rural communities viewed it as an existential threat to their livelihoods and an unfair concentration of regulatory burdens on agriculture despite nitrogen originating from multiple sectors.[11][34] The ruling coalition under Prime Minister Mark Rutte, comprising VVD, D66, CDA, and ChristenUnie, defended the measures as necessary for environmental protection and halting new construction permits stalled by emission limits, but critics, including opposition parties, argued the approach lacked proportionality and ignored alternatives like technological innovation or targeting urban sources more aggressively.[33] Secondary issues included broader government discontent, fueled by scandals such as the 2019 childcare benefits affair involving wrongful fraud accusations against thousands of parents, the prolonged Groningen gas field extraction controversy causing earthquakes, and perceptions of urban-centric policymaking neglecting rural infrastructure and economic viability.[33] Housing shortages and rising living costs also featured, though subordinated to the nitrogen debate, with parties framing the provincial vote as a de facto referendum on the national coalition's Senate majority, given the indirect electoral link. Opposition forces, particularly the newly formed Farmer-Citizen Movement (BBB), positioned the election as a chance to block further "top-down" policies, while left-leaning alliances like GroenLinks-PvdA emphasized climate imperatives alongside social equity.[36][33] Voter mobilization centered on rural discontent, with BBB leveraging ongoing farmers' tractor protests—peaking in 2022 with blockades of distribution centers and government buildings—to rally support, achieving a 20% national vote share through anti-elite messaging that resonated beyond farmers to include suburban and disillusioned conservative voters shifting from VVD, CDA, and PVV.[21][33] The party, led by Caroline van der Plas, conducted grassroots campaigns emphasizing rural-urban divides and direct appeals via social media and local events, capitalizing on a turnout increase to 77.7%—the highest since 1982—driven by protest energy rather than traditional party machinery.[34] Coalition parties relied on established networks but suffered from low enthusiasm, with VVD focusing on economic stability pledges, while post-provincial lobbying targeted the 570 newly elected councilors as Senate electors, though BBB's provincial gains ensured opposition control without needing extensive direct Senate campaigning.[33][36]

Polling and seat projections

Polls for the 2023 Dutch Senate election focused on voter intentions for the concurrent provincial council elections, as Senate seats are allocated proportionally by provincial electors based on those results.[37] Pollsters such as Ipsos, in collaboration with EenVandaag, conducted surveys translating provincial vote shares into projected Senate seats (out of 75), accounting for regional variations and elector behavior.[37] These projections consistently forecasted the loss of the ruling coalition's (VVD, D66, CDA, ChristenUnie) majority, driven by gains for BoerBurgerBeweging (BBB) amid public backlash against nitrogen emission policies affecting farmers.[38] In the final Ipsos poll (March 10–14, 2023, n=2,002, online representative sample, weighted by demographics and region), BBB led projections with 13 seats, followed closely by a combined GroenLinks–PvdA at 14 seats (GL 8, PvdA 6).[37] The coalition was projected at 23 seats total (VVD 12, CDA 5, D66 4, CU 2), down from 32 in 2019, falling short of the 38 needed for control.[37][38] Other notable shifts included PVV at 8 seats (up from 5), SP at 5, and a collapse for Forum voor Democratie to 2 from 12.[37] Margins of error were approximately ±2 seats for larger parties.[37]

Results

Seat allocation by party

The 2023 Dutch Senate election, held on 30 May 2023, allocated 75 seats among 16 parties through an indirect vote by the members of the 12 provincial councils (elected in March 2023) and electoral colleges for Dutch nationals abroad and in Caribbean territories.[1] [4] The official results, certified by the Kiesraad on 1 June 2023, reflected the proportional distribution based on weighted votes, including 68 initial seats and 7 remainder seats awarded via the largest remainder method.[1] The Farmer–Citizen Movement (BBB) emerged as the largest party with 16 seats, benefiting from its strong performance in the underlying provincial elections amid farmer protests against nitrogen emission policies.[1] [41] The ruling coalition parties—VVD, CDA, D66, and ChristenUnie—collectively held 24 seats, falling short of a majority (38 seats required).[4] Opposition parties, including left-leaning PvdA and GroenLinks (each with 7 seats), and right-wing PVV (4 seats), gained ground, while Forum for Democracy (FVD) dropped to 2 seats from 7 in 2019.[1] [41]

Shifts from 2019 election

The 2023 Dutch Senate election produced marked shifts in party seat shares relative to the 2019 results, reflecting voter discontent expressed through the March 2023 provincial elections that determined the electoral college. The governing coalition of VVD, CDA, D66, and ChristenUnie, which held 32 seats in 2019, fell to 24 seats, losing its previous blocking minority influence.[42][1] The most prominent gain was by the BoerBurgerBeweging (BBB), a new agrarian protest party formed in 2019 in response to government nitrogen reduction policies affecting farmers, which captured 16 seats from zero, becoming the largest group.[1] Forum voor Democratie (FvD), which had surged to 12 seats in 2019 amid anti-establishment sentiment, collapsed to 2 seats, attributable to internal scandals, leadership turmoil under Thierry Baudet, and splintering that birthed JA21.[4][43] The Partij voor de Vrijheid (PVV) entered with 4 seats after securing none in 2019 due to its boycott of provincial elections.[1][42]

Aftermath

Immediate governmental consequences

The election of the Senate on 30 May 2023 resulted in the ruling Rutte IV coalition—comprising the People's Party for Freedom and Democracy (VVD), Democrats 66 (D66), Christian Democratic Appeal (CDA), and Christian Union (CU)—losing its majority, with the four parties collectively securing 24 seats out of 75.[23] [33] This shortfall from the 38 seats required for a majority stemmed directly from the Farmer–Citizen Movement (BBB)'s strong performance in the preceding provincial council elections on 15 March 2023, where it captured approximately 22% of the vote and translated that into 16 Senate seats as the largest single party.[23] [33] The shift reflected widespread rural discontent, particularly over nitrogen emissions regulations that threatened agricultural viability, compelling the government to negotiate with opposition groups like BBB or the combined GroenLinks–PvdA bloc (14 seats) for legislative passage.[23] [35] This minority status in the upper house immediately hampered the cabinet's ability to advance key policies without concessions, as the Senate reviews and can amend or reject bills passed by the House of Representatives.[23] For instance, contentious environmental and farming reforms faced heightened scrutiny and required ad hoc alliances, exacerbating internal coalition tensions already strained by prior crises such as the 2019 child benefits scandal.[23] [35] Prime Minister Mark Rutte acknowledged the challenges, stating that the government would prioritize dialogue with the Senate to maintain functionality, though analysts noted this dependency prolonged uncertainty over budget approvals and EU-mandated compliance measures.[35] The weakened legislative position contributed to the cabinet's collapse on 7 July 2023, when D66 and CU withdrew support amid irreconcilable differences on tightening asylum policies, a decision influenced by the broader inability to secure reliable cross-party backing in the Senate.[23] In the interim, the government operated in a caretaker capacity, deferring major decisions and paving the way for snap House of Representatives elections on 22 November 2023 to resolve the impasse.[23] This episode underscored the indirect electoral system's role in amplifying provincial voter sentiments into national gridlock, as the Senate's composition now better reflected anti-establishment pressures absent in the House.[33]

Broader political realignments

The 2023 Senate election underscored a pronounced shift towards agrarian populism, with the Farmer–Citizen Movement (BBB) emerging as the largest party by capturing 16 seats out of 75, up from zero in the previous term. This surge was driven by rural voters' opposition to the Rutte IV cabinet's nitrogen reduction measures, enacted to fulfill European Court of Justice requirements on emissions but resulting in mandatory farm closures and buyouts that jeopardized agricultural livelihoods. The BBB's success highlighted a deepening rural-urban cleavage, where peripheral regions prioritized economic viability and local autonomy over centralized environmental mandates.[41][33] Concurrently, the ruling coalition of VVD, D66, CDA, and ChristenUnie relinquished its Senate majority, dropping to 24 seats collectively—VVD to 10 (-2), D66 to 5 (-2), CDA to 6 (-3), and ChristenUnie to 3 (-1)—forcing reliance on opposition pacts for legislative passage. This fragmentation exacerbated governance instability, with electoral volatility reaching 29%, the highest since 1983, signaling erosion of the post-war consensus on technocratic, pro-EU centrism. Traditional pillars like Christian democracy and social democracy further weakened, as evidenced by combined GL-PvdA holdings at 14 seats amid broader anti-establishment currents.[41][33] These dynamics presaged national trends, amplifying right-leaning opposition forces like PVV (4 seats, -1 from 2019) and foreshadowing the cabinet's collapse in July 2023 over asylum policy disputes. The election thus illustrated causal pressures from policy-induced economic distress fueling populist realignments, challenging urban-centric elites and EU-aligned priorities in favor of sovereignty-focused, sector-specific advocacy.[33][23]

Criticisms of the indirect electoral process

The indirect electoral process for the Dutch Senate, whereby its 75 members are elected by the members of the twelve provincial councils and a separate electoral college for non-resident Dutch citizens, has faced longstanding criticism for conferring a weak democratic mandate on senators compared to the directly elected House of Representatives (Tweede Kamer). Critics argue that this double layer of indirection—voters first electing provincial councilors, who then select senators through internal party mechanisms—distances the Senate from the direct expression of popular will, resulting in senators who represent interest groups or party elites rather than ordinary voters or regional interests.[44][45] In the context of the 2023 Senate election, held following the March 15 provincial elections, provincial councilors like Tjeerd van der Zwan, mayor of Heerenveen, described the process as a "relict uit oude tijden" (relic from old times) unfit for contemporary democracy, lacking a clear modern function and echoing earlier calls, such as the Labour Party's 1983 push for abolition due to perceived overreach. Similarly, Sijbe Knol of the Frisian National Party criticized national parties for treating provincial elections as a de facto national referendum on government performance—exemplified by the 2023 vote's focus on immigration and housing—rather than provincial matters, leading to attempts at "vote-buying" where national leaders lobbied provincial councilors to secure favorable Senate outcomes. This distortion, critics contend, undermines the Senate's intended role as a check on hasty legislation by prioritizing national partisan dynamics over balanced regional representation.[46] Further critiques highlight the process's propensity for internal party selection of senators, often drawing from established administrators in sectors like education or finance, which reinforces elite capture and erodes public accountability, as senators face no direct electoral scrutiny. Historical patterns reinforce these concerns: since the 19th century, the indirect system has been accused of causing legislative delays and impasses when Senate majorities diverge from the House of Representatives, with post-1999 debates questioning its legitimacy amid calls to revert to pre-1983 models or enhance direct elements. Proponents of reform, including suggestions to merge the chambers or allocate additional regional seats in the House of Representatives, argue that the current setup lacks sufficient democratic legitimacy to wield veto power over bills, potentially leading to political gridlock without corresponding public endorsement.[44][45][47]
